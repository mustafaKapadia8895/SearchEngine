<p>The <b>1953 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was the first time an organized list of female names was used to name Atlantic storms. It officially began on June 15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and lasted until November 15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> although activity occurred both before and after the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">season</a>'s limits. The season was active with fourteen total storms, six of which developed into hurricanes; four of the hurricanes attained <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane</a> status, or a Category 3 or greater on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson scale</a>.</p>
<p>The strongest hurricane of the season was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol_(1953)" title="Hurricane Carol (1953)">Carol</a>, although by the time it struck <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> it was much weaker. Both hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Barbara_(1953)" title="Hurricane Barbara (1953)">Barbara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Florence_(1953)" title="Hurricane Florence (1953)">Florence</a> struck the United States; the former crossed the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> and impacted much of the east coast, and Florence struck a sparsely populated region of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> without causing much damage. Bermuda was threatened by three hurricanes within two weeks. In addition to the hurricanes, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alice_(1953)" title="Tropical Storm Alice (1953)">Tropical Storm Alice</a> developed in late May and left several fatalities in Cuba. The final named storm of the season, Hazel, produced additional rainfall in Florida after previous flooding conditions. There were several unnamed storms, the last of which dissipated on December 9.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alice_(1953)" title="Tropical Storm Alice (1953)">Tropical Storm Alice (1953)</a></div>
<p>A tropical storm developed east of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on May 25, executing a counterclockwise loop over <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. After weakening over land, Alice re-intensified over the western Caribbean, moving over western Cuba on May 21 with winds of 50 mph (80 km/h). On June 1, it entered the Gulf of Mexico, and later executed another loop off the northwest coast of Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> Alice quickly weakened due to a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and advisories were discontinued by June 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> While near Cuba, Alice produced drought-breaking rainfall that caused flooding and several unofficial drowning deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After passing near Cuba, Alice turned to the north and restrengthened to peak winds of 70 mph (110 km/h) (in fact, it is possible Alice briefly reached hurricane intensity but data was inconclusive). It again weakened before making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Panama_City_Beach,_Florida" title="Panama City Beach, Florida">Panama City Beach</a> on June 6 as a minimal tropical storm, and Alice dissipated shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> Alice brought heavy rainfall to Florida, peaking at 13.48 inches (342 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid,_Florida" title="Lake Placid, Florida">Lake Placid</a>. Near where it made landfall, the storm dropped light rainfall, and there were no reports of damage in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_Rain_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Florida_Rain-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> Alice was the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">North Atlantic tropical cyclone</a> to have a female name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was also one of 22 tropical or subtropical cyclones on record in the month of May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Barbara">Hurricane Barbara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hurricane Barbara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> developed into Tropical Storm Barbara over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> on August 11. It intensified as it moved north-northwestward, becoming a hurricane by the next day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paper_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-paper-9">[9]</a></sup> and reaching peak winds of 90 mph (150 km/h) just south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a> on August 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Barbara moved over the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>, passing between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morehead_City" title="Morehead City">Morehead City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocracoke,_North_Carolina" title="Ocracoke, North Carolina">Ocracoke</a>, and it turned and accelerated to the northeast. Steadily weakening and losing tropical characteristics, the hurricane transitioned into an extratropical cyclone late on August 15. It turned northward, crossing eastern <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> and dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> on August 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paper_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-paper-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before Barbara struck the Outer Banks, officials ordered evacuations for a few islands, and several thousand tourists voluntarily left the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap813_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap813-10">[10]</a></sup> Wind gusts reached 90 mph (140 km/h) at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hatteras,_NC" title="Hatteras, NC">Hatteras</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nags_Head,_NC" title="Nags Head, NC">Nags Head</a>. Torrential rainfall fell across the state and extended northward into <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paper_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-paper-9">[9]</a></sup> peaking at 11.1 in (280 mm) near <a href="/wiki/Onley,_Virginia" title="Onley, Virginia">Onley</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_of_Virginia" title="Eastern Shore of Virginia">Eastern Shore of Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Across the region, the hurricane left flooding and downed trees,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap813_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap813-10">[10]</a></sup> some of which survived the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Atlantic_Hurricane_of_1944" title="Great Atlantic Hurricane of 1944">Great Atlantic Hurricane of 1944</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paper_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-paper-9">[9]</a></sup> Monetary damage from Barbara was estimated around $1.3 million (1953 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $11.6 million 2017 USD),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mi-12">[12]</a></sup> mostly from the crop damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dt_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dt-13">[13]</a></sup> Newspaper reports indicated there were seven deaths in the country;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dt_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-dt-13">[13]</a></sup> two additional deaths occurred offshore <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> when a dory sunk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol_(1953)" title="Hurricane Carol (1953)">Hurricane Carol (1953)</a></div>
<p>On August 28, a tropical wave developed into a tropical depression near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. After moving west-southwestward, it turned to the northwest, intensifying into a tropical storm on August 31 and into a hurricane on September 2. Passing northeast of the Lesser Antilles, Carol rapidly intensified to Category 5 intensity, reaching peak winds of 160 mph (260 km/h) on September 3, making it the strongest hurricane of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It gradually weakened, bypassing <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on September 6 and producing high waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Carol later turned to the north-northeast, brushing <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> and causing boating accidents across New England. Four people were killed in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Fishing losses totaled around $1 million (1953 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $8.95 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After bypassing New England, Carol brushed western <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> before moving ashore near <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John, New Brunswick</a> as a minimal hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada2-17">[17]</a></sup> As it moved ashore, it produced hurricane conditions in eastern Maine, one of only six Atlantic hurricanes to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In Nova Scotia, several boats were wrecked or washed ashore, with one drowning death reported. High seas caused coastal flooding, while strong winds downed large areas of trees. Heavy losses to the apple crop occurred in <a href="/wiki/Annapolis_Valley" title="Annapolis Valley">Annapolis Valley</a>, totaling $1 million (1950 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a>, $9.26 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada2_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canada2-17">[17]</a></sup> Carol later dissipated southwest of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> on September 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>On August 29, a tropical storm developed near <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isla de la Juventud</a>, south of the western tip of Cuba. It moved erratically, first quickly northwestward through Cuba, followed by a turn to the east across southern Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> While moving through the state, the storm dropped heavy rainfall, reaching 4.67 in (119 mm) at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale Beach</a>; similar rainfall was observed in the northern Bahamas. Although one Weather Bureau forecaster described the system as "wide and flat", the storm gradually organized over the western Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On August 31, after turning to the northwest, the storm reached peak winds of 40 mph (65 km/h), although it weakened to a tropical depression before moving ashore near <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a> on September 1. It turned to the north over land, and dissipated over <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> on September 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Dolly">Hurricane Dolly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hurricane Dolly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The origins of Dolly were from a tropical wave that moved through the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> producing 10 in (250 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">Saint Thomas</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>. The rains closed schools and government buildings around <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>, and flooding was reported in <a href="/wiki/Guayama,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guayama, Puerto Rico">Guayama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yabucoa,_Puerto_Rico" title="Yabucoa, Puerto Rico">Yabucoa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Patillas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Patillas, Puerto Rico">Patillas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On September 8, a tropical storm developed north of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, which moved slowly west-southwestward before turning to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It quickly intensified, and after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> reported an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, Dolly reached hurricane status on September 9. At that time, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warnings</a> were issued in the Bahamas, although the storm turned away from the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mi_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-mi-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It intensified to peak winds of 75 mph (121 km/h) on September 10, as reported by aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dolly weakened as it accelerated northeastward, although it still threatened to strike Bermuda with strong winds. As a result, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> ordered all of the planes on the island to fly to the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> After continued weakening, Dolly passed over the island on September 11, producing only <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">gale</a>-force winds, rains, and little to no damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bem_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-bem-22">[22]</a></sup> It deteriorated into a tropical storm on September 12 and transitioned into an extratropical cyclone later that day. The remnants of Dolly later turned eastward, dissipating just west of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> on September 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Shortly behind Dolly, another tropical wave spawned a tropical depression over the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> on September 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> As it moved through the region, it produced unsettled conditions across the northeast Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Edna quickly intensified as it tracked northwestward, attaining hurricane status on September 15 and peak winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) the next day. After peaking, the hurricane turned to the northeast and maintained most of its intensity for a few days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> It passed just north of Bermuda early on September 18 with winds of 115 mph (185 km/h), before beginning a steady weakening trend as it accelerated. By September 19, Edna completed the transition into an extratropical cyclone, lasting another day before dissipating west of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before Edna struck Bermuda, the islanders were well-prepared due to being previously impacted by hurricanes Carol and Dolly, and they boarded up their homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The hurricane caused "considerable damage", with wind gusts reaching 120 mph (190 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> The winds downed trees, blocking roads, and also caused disruptions to the power and water services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> During its passage, Edna produced heavy rainfall and also damaged several roofs. There were three injuries on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bem_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-bem-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical depression formed in the western <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> on September 14 and moved eastward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm produced heavy seas across the region, which damaged two small boats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> After turning to the northeast, the storm reached peak winds of 70 mph (110 km/h), followed by executing a clockwise loop from September 16 through September 18. <p>Florence developed on September 23 near <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> from a tropical wave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> While passing that island, it produced heavy rainfall that blocked roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It intensified to hurricane status on September 24 while passing through the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Channel" title="Yucatán Channel">Yucatán Channel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> and while doing so left heavy damage in western Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-en-28">[28]</a></sup> After turning north and entering the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, Florence quickly intensified to peak winds of 115 mph (185 km/h). It gradually weakened before making landfall on September 26 as a minimal hurricane in a sparsely populated region of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before Florence hit the United States Gulf Coast, about 10,000 people evacuated <a href="/wiki/Panama_City,_Florida" title="Panama City, Florida">Panama City, Florida</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-pc-29">[29]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">Weather Bureau</a> issued timely warnings that was credited in preventing any deaths or major injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> Winds reached 84 mph (135 km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Eglin_Air_Force_Base" title="Eglin Air Force Base">Eglin Air Force Base</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up927_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-up927-30">[30]</a></sup> and the heaviest rainfall was 14.71 in (374 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Lockhart,_Alabama" title="Lockhart, Alabama">Lockhart, Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-31">[31]</a></sup> The combination of winds and heavy rainfall left crop damage in the Florida panhandle and southeastern Alabama, although coastal damage was not severe. Overall, 421 houses were damaged and another three were destroyed, with monetary damage estimated around $200,000 (1953 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $1.79 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> After landfall, Florence quickly transitioned into an extratropical cyclone, and as it continued across the southeastern United States it produced moderately heavy rainfall. It dissipated on September 28 southeast of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-rain-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-16"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>On October 2, a tropical wave spawned a tropical depression about 815 mi (1310 km) west-southwest of the Cape Verde islands. The depression quickly intensified into Tropical Storm Gail, and the next day, a ship encountered the storm, reporting a minimum pressure of 986 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inch_of_Mercury" title="Inch of Mercury">inHg</a>). As the ship crossed through the center, it reported winds of 80 mph (130 km/h), indicating Gail attained hurricane status. Initially believed to have dissipated and reformed as a new unnamed storm on October 5, in reality Gail continued northwest and then meandered around the central Atlantic before being absorbed by a front on October 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-17"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>On October 3, a tropical storm developed near the southern coast of Cuba. It moved northwestward, crossing the island before turning to the northeast. Early on October 5, the storm brushed southeastern Florida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-18"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> producing gusty winds and rainfall. The storm caused two deaths after it wrecked a boat in <a href="/wiki/Broad_Cove,_Nova_Scotia" title="Broad Cove, Nova Scotia">Broad Cove, Nova Scotia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Later, the cyclone passed south of Greenland before dissipating southwest of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> on October 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-20"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Hazel">Hurricane Hazel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hurricane Hazel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The twelfth tropical storm and the final hurricane of the season formed in the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Channel" title="Yucatán Channel">Yucatán Channel</a> on October 7. Given the name Hazel, the storm tracked north-northeastward, then northeastward through the Gulf of Mexico while gradually intensifying. On October 9, Hazel strengthened into a hurricane made landfall just north of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers,_Florida" title="Fort Myers, Florida">Fort Myers, Florida</a> at its peak intensity of 85 mph (140 km/h). Peak wind gusts were 80 mph (130 km/h), with a minimum pressure of 987.0 mb (29.15 inHg) reported in <a href="/wiki/Okeechobee,_Florida" title="Okeechobee, Florida">Okeechobee City</a>. The storm crossed the state in about six hours, during which it weakened slightly back to a tropical storm. Over the western Atlantic Ocean, Hazel re-intensified to its peak winds, although by late on October 10 it transitioned into an extratropical storm between North Carolina and Bermuda. The remnants continued northeastward, dissipating southeast of Newfoundland on October 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-21"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Sable_Island" title="Sable Island">Sable Island</a>, the storm produced heavy winds and rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the fairly light winds across Florida, damage from Hazel was minor, estimated around $250,000 (1953 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $2.24 million 2017 USD). During its passage, the storm spawned a tornado in <a href="/wiki/St._James_City,_Florida" title="St. James City, Florida">St. James City</a> that destroyed several homes, and there were indications of another tornado in Okeechobee City. The primary impact from Hazel was from its rainfall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup> peaking at 10.53 inches (267 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">Daytona Beach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The rains added to previous flooding conditions across the state, causing a record flood stage along the <a href="/wiki/St._Johns_River" title="St. Johns River">St. Johns River</a> that flooded 6 mi (9.7 km) of highway. Overall flooding damage was estimated up to $10 million (1953 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $89.5 million 2017 USD), but it was impossible to determine how much was due to Hazel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_3-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The final tropical cyclone of the season developed on December 7 about 705 mi (1735 km) east-northeast of the Lesser Antilles as a tropical depression. It tracked generally westward, briefly intensifying into a tropical storm on December 8. The next day it dissipated about 270 mi (435 km) north of the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-23"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_names">Storm names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Storm names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These names were used to name storms during the 1953 season. The list was the same for the <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954 season</a> as well. Initially, all female names were used; it was not until the <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">1979 season</a> that male and female names were used in alternating order. Names that were not assigned are marked in <span style="color:#666666">gray</span>. All storm names were named for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-gp-36">[36]</a></sup> No names were <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a>.</p>
