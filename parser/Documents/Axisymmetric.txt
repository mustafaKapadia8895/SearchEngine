The <a href="/wiki/Triskelion" title="Triskelion">triskelion</a> appearing on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Flag of the Isle of Man">Isle of Man flag</a> has rotational symmetry because it appears the same when rotated by one third of a full turn about its center. <p><b>Rotational symmetry</b>, also known as <b>radial symmetry</b> in biology, is the property a shape has when it looks the same after some rotation by a partial turn. An object's degree of rotational symmetry is the number of distinct orientations in which it looks the same.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_treatment">Formal treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_symmetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formal treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rotational_invariance" title="Rotational invariance">Rotational invariance</a></div>
<p>Formally the rotational symmetry is <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a> with respect to some or all <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotations</a> in <i>m</i>-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>. Rotations are <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_group#Direct_and_indirect_isometries" title="Euclidean group">direct isometries</a>, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Isometry" title="Isometry">isometries</a> preserving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orientation_(mathematics)" title="Orientation (mathematics)">orientation</a>. Therefore a <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> of rotational symmetry is a subgroup of <i>E</i><sup>+</sup>(<i>m</i>) (see <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_group" title="Euclidean group">Euclidean group</a>).</p>
<p>Symmetry with respect to all rotations about all points implies <a href="/wiki/Translational_symmetry" title="Translational symmetry">translational symmetry</a> with respect to all translations, so space is homogeneous, and the symmetry group is the whole <i>E</i>(<i>m</i>). With the <a href="/wiki/Symmetry#Mathematical_model_for_symmetry" title="Symmetry">modified notion of symmetry for vector fields</a> the symmetry group can also be <i>E</i><sup>+</sup>(<i>m</i>).</p>
<p>For symmetry with respect to rotations about a point we can take that point as origin. These rotations form the special <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_group" title="Orthogonal group">orthogonal group</a> SO(<i>m</i>), the group of <i>m</i>×<i>m</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_matrices" title="Orthogonal matrices">orthogonal matrices</a> with determinant 1. For <span class="texhtml"><i>m</i> = 3</span> this is the <a href="/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3)" title="Rotation group SO(3)">rotation group SO(3)</a>.</p>
<p>In another meaning of the word, the rotation group <i>of an object</i> is the symmetry group within <i>E</i><sup>+</sup>(<i>n</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_group" title="Euclidean group">group of direct isometries</a>; in other words, the intersection of the full symmetry group and the group of direct isometries. For <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(mathematics)" title="Chirality (mathematics)">chiral</a> objects it is the same as the full symmetry group.</p>
<p>Laws of physics <a href="/wiki/Isotropy" title="Isotropy">are SO(3)-invariant</a> if they do not distinguish different directions in space. Because of <a href="/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem" title="Noether's theorem">Noether's theorem</a>, rotational symmetry of a physical system is equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> conservation law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discrete_rotational_symmetry">Discrete rotational symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_symmetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discrete rotational symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Rotational symmetry of order <i>n</i></b>, also called <b><i>n</i>-fold rotational symmetry</b>, or <b>discrete rotational symmetry of the <i>n</i>th order</b>, with respect to a particular point (in 2D) or axis (in 3D) means that rotation by an angle of 360°/n (180°, 120°, 90°, 72°, 60°, 51 <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>7</sub></span>°, etc.) does not change the object. Note that "1-fold" symmetry is no symmetry (all objects look alike after a rotation of 360°).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Crystal_system#Overview_of_point_groups_by_crystal_system" title="Crystal system">notation</a> for <i>n</i>-fold symmetry is <i><b>C<sub>n</sub></b></i> or simply "<i>n</i>". The actual <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> is specified by the point or axis of symmetry, together with the <i>n</i>. For each point or axis of symmetry, the abstract group type is <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_group" title="Cyclic group">cyclic group</a> of order <i>n</i>, Z<sub><i>n</i></sub>. Although for the latter also the notation <i>C</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> is used, the geometric and abstract <i>C</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> should be distinguished: there are other symmetry groups of the same abstract group type which are geometrically different, see <a href="/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions#Cyclic_symmetry_groups_in_3D_cyclic_symmetry_groups" title="Point groups in three dimensions">cyclic symmetry groups in 3D</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_domain" title="Fundamental domain">fundamental domain</a> is a sector of 360°/n.</p>
<p>Examples without additional <a href="/wiki/Reflection_symmetry" title="Reflection symmetry">reflection symmetry</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>n</i> = 2, 180°: the <i>dyad</i>; among the <a href="/wiki/Quadrilateral" title="Quadrilateral">quadrilaterals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parallelogram" title="Parallelogram">parallelograms</a>, which have this as a fully identifying (necessary and sufficient) feature; letters Z, N, S; the outlines, albeit not the colors, of the <a href="/wiki/Yin_and_yang" title="Yin and yang">yin and yang</a> symbol; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Flag" title="Union Flag">Union Flag</a> (as divided along the flag's diagonal and rotated about the flag's center point)</li>
<li><i>n</i> = 3, 120°: <i>triad</i>, <a href="/wiki/Triskelion" title="Triskelion">triskelion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borromean_rings" title="Borromean rings">Borromean rings</a>; sometimes the term <i>trilateral symmetry</i> is used;</li>
<li><i>n</i> = 4, 90°: <i>tetrad</i>, <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a></li>
<li><i>n</i> = 6, 60°: <i>hexad</i>, <a href="/wiki/Star_of_David" title="Star of David">Star of David</a></li>
<li><i>n</i> = 8, 45°: <i>octad</i>, Octagonal <a href="/wiki/Muqarnas" title="Muqarnas">muqarnas</a>, computer-generated (CG), ceiling</li>
</ul>
<p><i>C</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> is the rotation group of a regular <i>n</i>-sided <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> in 2D and of a regular <i>n</i>-sided <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramid</a> in 3D.</p>
<p>If there is e.g. rotational symmetry with respect to an angle of 100°, then also with respect to one of 20°, the <a href="/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor" title="Greatest common divisor">greatest common divisor</a> of 100° and 360°.</p>
<p>A typical 3D object with rotational symmetry (possibly also with perpendicular axes) but no mirror symmetry is a <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_symmetry_axes_through_the_same_point">Multiple symmetry axes through the same point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_symmetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Multiple symmetry axes through the same point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Discrete_symmetry" title="Discrete symmetry">discrete symmetry</a> with multiple symmetry axes through the same point, there are the following possibilities:</p>
<ul>
<li>In addition to an <i>n</i>-fold axis, <i>n</i> perpendicular 2-fold axes: the <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_group" title="Dihedral group">dihedral groups</a> <i>D</i><sub>n</sub> of order 2<i>n</i> (<span class="texhtml"><i>n</i> ≥ 2</span>). This is the rotation group of a regular <a href="/wiki/Prism_(geometry)" title="Prism (geometry)">prism</a>, or regular <a href="/wiki/Bipyramid" title="Bipyramid">bipyramid</a>. Although the same notation is used, the geometric and abstract <i>D</i><sub>n</sub> should be distinguished: there are other symmetry groups of the same abstract group type which are geometrically different, see <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_group#Dihedral_symmetry_groups_in_3D" title="Dihedral group">dihedral symmetry groups in 3D</a>.</li>
<li>4×3-fold and 3×2-fold axes: the rotation group <i>T</i> of order 12 of a regular <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron" title="Tetrahedron">tetrahedron</a>. The group is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomorphic" title="Isomorphic">isomorphic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alternating_group" title="Alternating group">alternating group</a> <i>A</i><sub>4</sub>.</li>
<li>3×4-fold, 4×3-fold, and 6×2-fold axes: the rotation group <i>O</i> of order 24 of a <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a> and a regular <a href="/wiki/Octahedron" title="Octahedron">octahedron</a>. The group is isomorphic to <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_group" title="Symmetric group">symmetric group</a> <i>S</i><sub>4</sub>.</li>
<li>6×5-fold, 10×3-fold, and 15×2-fold axes: the rotation group <i>I</i> of order 60 of a <a href="/wiki/Dodecahedron" title="Dodecahedron">dodecahedron</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">icosahedron</a>. The group is isomorphic to alternating group <i>A</i><sub>5</sub>. The group contains 10 versions of <i>D<sub>3</sub></i> and 6 versions of <i>D<sub>5</sub></i> (rotational symmetries like prisms and antiprisms).</li>
</ul>
<p>In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Platonic_solid" title="Platonic solid">Platonic solids</a>, the 2-fold axes are through the midpoints of opposite edges, and the number of them is half the number of edges. The other axes are through opposite vertices and through centers of opposite faces, except in the case of the tetrahedron, where the 3-fold axes are each through one vertex and the center of one face.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotational_symmetry_with_respect_to_any_angle">Rotational symmetry with respect to any angle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_symmetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rotational symmetry with respect to any angle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rotational symmetry with respect to any angle is, in two dimensions, <a href="/wiki/Circular_symmetry" title="Circular symmetry">circular symmetry</a>. The fundamental domain is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)#Ray" title="Line (mathematics)">half-line</a>.</p>
<p>In three dimensions we can distinguish <b>cylindrical symmetry</b> and <b>spherical symmetry</b> (no change when rotating about one axis, or for any rotation). That is, no dependence on the angle using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinates_(elementary_mathematics)#Cylindrical_coordinates" title="Coordinates (elementary mathematics)">cylindrical coordinates</a> and no dependence on either angle using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinates_(elementary_mathematics)#Spherical_coordinates" title="Coordinates (elementary mathematics)">spherical coordinates</a>. The fundamental domain is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-plane" title="Half-plane">half-plane</a> through the axis, and a radial half-line, respectively. <b>Axisymmetric</b> or <b>axisymmetrical</b> are <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a> which refer to an object having cylindrical symmetry, or <b>axisymmetry</b>. An example of approximate spherical symmetry is the Earth (with respect to density and other physical and chemical properties).</p>
<p>In 4D, continuous or discrete rotational symmetry about a plane corresponds to corresponding 2D rotational symmetry in every perpendicular plane, about the point of intersection. An object can also have rotational symmetry about two perpendicular planes, e.g. if it is the <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_product" title="Cartesian product">Cartesian product</a> of two rotationally symmetry 2D figures, as in the case of e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Duocylinder" title="Duocylinder">duocylinder</a> and various regular <a href="/wiki/Duoprism" title="Duoprism">duoprisms</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotational_symmetry_with_translational_symmetry">Rotational symmetry with translational symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_symmetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rotational symmetry with translational symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>2-fold rotational symmetry together with single <a href="/wiki/Translational_symmetry" title="Translational symmetry">translational symmetry</a> is one of the <a href="/wiki/Frieze_group" title="Frieze group">Frieze groups</a>. There are two rotocenters per <a href="/wiki/Primitive_cell" title="Primitive cell">primitive cell</a>.</p>
<p>Together with double translational symmetry the rotation groups are the following <a href="/wiki/Wallpaper_group" title="Wallpaper group">wallpaper groups</a>, with axes per primitive cell:</p>
<ul>
<li>p2 (2222): 4×2-fold; rotation group of a <a href="/wiki/Parallelogram" title="Parallelogram">parallelogrammic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rectangle" title="Rectangle">rectangular</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhombus" title="Rhombus">rhombic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lattice_(group)" title="Lattice (group)">lattice</a>.</li>
<li>p3 (333): 3×3-fold; <i>not</i> the rotation group of any lattice (every lattice is upside-down the same, but that does not apply for this symmetry); it is e.g. the rotation group of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tilings_of_regular_polygons#Regular_tilings" title="Tilings of regular polygons">regular triangular tiling</a> with the equilateral triangles alternatingly colored.</li>
<li>p4 (442): 2×4-fold, 2×2-fold; rotation group of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_(geometry)" title="Square (geometry)">square</a> lattice.</li>
<li>p6 (632): 1×6-fold, 2×3-fold, 3×2-fold; rotation group of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal" title="Hexagonal">hexagonal</a> lattice.</li>
<li>2-fold rotocenters (including possible 4-fold and 6-fold), if present at all, form the translate of a lattice equal to the translational lattice, scaled by a factor 1/2. <p>Scaling of a lattice divides the number of points per unit area by the square of the scale factor. Therefore the number of 2-, 3-, 4-, and 6-fold rotocenters per primitive cell is 4, 3, 2, and 1, respectively, again including 4-fold as a special case of 2-fold, etc.</p>
<p>3-fold rotational symmetry at one point and 2-fold at another one (or ditto in 3D with respect to parallel axes) implies rotation group p6, i.e. double translational symmetry and 6-fold rotational symmetry at some point (or, in 3D, parallel axis). The translation distance for the symmetry generated by one such pair of rotocenters is 2√3 times their distance.</p>
