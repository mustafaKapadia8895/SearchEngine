<p>The <b>Sahara</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">الصحراء الكبرى</span>‎‎, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">aṣ-ṣaḥrāʾ al-kubrā</span></i> , 'the Greatest Desert') is the largest <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">hot desert</a> and the third largest <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> in the world after <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geology.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-geology.com-1">[1]</a></sup> Its area of 9,200,000 square kilometres (3,600,000 sq mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is comparable to the area of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> or the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>The desert comprises much of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, excluding the fertile region on the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> coast, the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" title="Nile Valley">Nile Valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>. It stretches from the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> in the east and the Mediterranean in the north to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> in the west, where the landscape gradually changes from desert to coastal plains. To the south, it is bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>, a belt of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_grasslands,_savannas,_and_shrublands" title="Tropical and subtropical grasslands, savannas, and shrublands">semi-arid tropical savanna</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">Niger River</a> valley and the <a href="/wiki/Sudan_(region)" title="Sudan (region)">Sudan Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>.</p>
<p>The Sahara can be divided into several regions including: the western Sahara, the central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahaggar_Mountains" title="Ahaggar Mountains">Ahaggar Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tibesti_Mountains" title="Tibesti Mountains">Tibesti Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/A%C3%AFr_Mountains" title="Aïr Mountains">Aïr Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A9n%C3%A9r%C3%A9" title="Ténéré">Ténéré</a> desert, and the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Desert" title="Libyan Desert">Libyan Desert</a>.</p>
<p>The name 'Sahara' is derived from <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">ṣaḥārā</span></i> (‏<span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%B5%D8%AD%D8%A7%D8%B1%D9%89#Arabic" title="wikt:صحارى">صحارى</a></span>‎, pronounced <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic" title="Help:IPA/Arabic">/ˈsˤaħaːraː/</a></span>), the plural of the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> word for "desert".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Sahara covers large parts of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. It covers 9 million square kilometres (3,500,000 sq mi), amounting to 31% of Africa. If all areas with a mean annual precipitation of less than 250 mm were included, the Sahara would be 11 million square kilometres (4,200,000 sq mi). It is one of three distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiographic_provinces" title="Physiographic provinces">physiographic provinces</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Physiographic_regions_of_the_world#Compendium_table_of_the_worlds_physiographic_regions" title="Physiographic regions of the world">African massive physiographic division</a>.</p>
<p>The Sahara is mainly rocky <a href="/wiki/Hamada" title="Hamada">hamada</a> (stone plateaus), <a href="/wiki/Erg_(landform)" title="Erg (landform)">Ergs</a> (sand seas - large areas covered with sand <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a>) form only a minor part, but many of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_dune" title="Sand dune">sand dunes</a> are over 180 metres (590 ft) high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">Wind</a> or rare rainfall shape the desert features: sand dunes, dune fields, sand seas, stone plateaus, gravel plains (<i>reg</i>), <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">dry valleys</a> (<i>wadi</i>), <a href="/wiki/Dry_lake" title="Dry lake">dry lakes</a> (<i>oued</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Salt_pan_(geology)" title="Salt pan (geology)">salt flats</a> (<i>shatt</i> or <i>chott</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PA1327_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PA1327-8">[8]</a></sup> Unusual landforms include the <a href="/wiki/Richat_Structure" title="Richat Structure">Richat Structure</a> in Mauritania.</p>
<p>Several deeply dissected mountains, many volcanic, rise from the desert, including the <a href="/wiki/A%C3%AFr_Mountains" title="Aïr Mountains">Aïr Mountains</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahaggar_Mountains" title="Ahaggar Mountains">Ahaggar Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saharan_Atlas" title="Saharan Atlas">Saharan Atlas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibesti_Mountains" title="Tibesti Mountains">Tibesti Mountains</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrar_des_Iforas" title="Adrar des Iforas">Adrar des Iforas</a>, and the Red Sea hills. The highest peak in the Sahara is <a href="/wiki/Emi_Koussi" title="Emi Koussi">Emi Koussi</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">shield volcano</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibesti" title="Tibesti">Tibesti</a> range of northern Chad.</p>
<p>The central Sahara is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperarid" title="Hyperarid">hyperarid</a>, with sparse vegetation. The northern and southern reaches of the desert, along with the highlands, have areas of sparse grassland and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_shrub" title="Desert shrub">desert shrub</a>, with trees and taller shrubs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wadis" title="Wadis">wadis</a>, where moisture collects. In the central, hyperarid region, there are many subdivisions of the great desert: <a href="/wiki/Tanezrouft" title="Tanezrouft">Tanezrouft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A9n%C3%A9r%C3%A9" title="Ténéré">Ténéré</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Desert" title="Libyan Desert">Libyan Desert</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Desert" title="Eastern Desert">Eastern Desert</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nubian_Desert" title="Nubian Desert">Nubian Desert</a> and others. These extremely arid areas often receive no rain for years.</p>
<p>To the north, the Sahara skirts the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> in Egypt and portions of Libya, but in <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, the Sahara borders the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_forest,_woodland,_and_scrub" title="Mediterranean forest, woodland, and scrub">Mediterranean forest, woodland, and scrub</a> eco-regions of northern Africa, all of which have a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> characterized by hot summers and cool and rainy winters. According to the botanical criteria of <a href="/wiki/Frank_White_(botanist)" title="Frank White (botanist)">Frank White</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and geographer Robert Capot-Rey,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove1958_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grove1958-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bisson2003_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bisson2003-11">[11]</a></sup> the northern limit of the Sahara corresponds to the northern limit of <a href="/wiki/Date_palm" title="Date palm">date palm</a> cultivation and the southern limit of the range of <a href="/wiki/Esparto" title="Esparto">esparto</a>, a grass typical of the Mediterranean climate portion of the Maghreb and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberia" title="Iberia">Iberia</a>. The northern limit also corresponds to the 100 mm (3.9 in) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isohyet</a> of annual precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walton2007_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walton2007-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>To the south, the Sahara is bounded by the Sahel, a belt of dry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_savanna" title="Tropical savanna">tropical savanna</a> with a summer rainy season that extends across Africa from east to west. The southern limit of the Sahara is indicated botanically by the southern limit of <i><a href="/wiki/Cornulaca_monacantha" title="Cornulaca monacantha">Cornulaca monacantha</a></i> (a drought-tolerant member of the <a href="/wiki/Chenopodiaceae" title="Chenopodiaceae">Chenopodiaceae</a>), or northern limit of <i><a href="/wiki/Cenchrus_biflorus" title="Cenchrus biflorus">Cenchrus biflorus</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grass</a> typical of the Sahel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove1958_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grove1958-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bisson2003_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bisson2003-11">[11]</a></sup> According to climatic criteria, the southern limit of the Sahara corresponds to the 150 mm (5.9 in) isohyet of annual precipitation (this is a long-term average, since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> varies annually).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walton2007_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walton2007-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Important cities located in the Sahara include <a href="/wiki/Nouakchott" title="Nouakchott">Nouakchott</a>, the capital of Mauritania; <a href="/wiki/Tamanrasset" title="Tamanrasset">Tamanrasset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ouargla" title="Ouargla">Ouargla</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9char" title="Béchar">Béchar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hassi_Messaoud" title="Hassi Messaoud">Hassi Messaoud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gharda%C3%AFa" title="Ghardaïa">Ghardaïa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/El_Oued" title="El Oued">El Oued</a> in Algeria; <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> in Mali; <a href="/wiki/Agadez" title="Agadez">Agadez</a> in Niger; <a href="/wiki/Ghat,_Libya" title="Ghat, Libya">Ghat</a> in Libya; and <a href="/wiki/Faya-Largeau" title="Faya-Largeau">Faya-Largeau</a> in Chad.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sahara_pump_theory" title="Sahara pump theory">Sahara pump theory</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Sahara" title="Green Sahara">Green Sahara</a></div>
<p>The Sahara is the world's largest low-latitude <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_desert" title="Hot desert">hot desert</a>. The area is located in the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>, a significant belt of semi-permanent subtropical warm-core high pressure where the air from upper levels of the troposphere tends to sink towards the ground. This steady descending airflow causes a warming and a drying effect in the upper troposphere. The sinking air prevents evaporating water from rising and, therefore, prevents the adiabatic cooling, which makes cloud formation extremely difficult to nearly impossible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The permanent dissolution of clouds allows unhindered light and thermal radiation. The <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability#Stable_atmosphere" title="Atmospheric instability">stability of the atmosphere</a> above the desert prevents any convective overturning, thus making rainfall virtually non-existent. As a consequence, the weather tends to be sunny, dry and stable with a minimal risk of rainfall. Subsiding, diverging, dry air masses associated with subtropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_system" title="High-pressure system">high-pressure systems</a> are extremely unfavorable for the development of convectional showers. The subtropical ridge is the predominate factor that explains the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_desert_climate" title="Hot desert climate">hot desert climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>BWh</i>) of this vast region. The lowering of air is the strongest and the most effective over the eastern part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Desert" title="Great Desert">Great Desert</a>, in the Libyan Desert which is the sunniest, driest and the most nearly "rain-less" place on the planet rivaling the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>, lying in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>.</p>
<p>The rainfall inhibition and the dissipation of cloud cover are most accentuated over the eastern section of the Sahara rather than the western. The prevailing <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air mass</a> lying above the Sahara is the continental tropical (cT) air mass, which is hot and dry. Hot, dry air masses primarily form over the North-African desert from the heating of the vast continental land area, and it affects the whole desert during most of the year. Because of this extreme heating process, a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">thermal low</a> is usually noticed near the surface, and is the strongest and the most developed during the summertime. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sahara_High&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sahara High (page does not exist)">Sahara High</a> represents the eastern continental extension of the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Azores High</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> centered over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a>. The subsidence of the Sahara High nearly reaches the ground during the coolest part of the year while it is confined to the upper troposphere during the hottest periods.</p>
<p>The effects of local surface low pressure are extremely limited because upper-level subsidence still continues to block any form of air ascent. Also, to be protected against rain-bearing weather systems by the atmospheric circulation itself, the desert is made even drier by his geographical configuration and location. Indeed, the extreme aridity of the Sahara can not be only explained by the subtropical high pressure. The <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a>, found in Algeria, Morocco and Tunisia also help to enhance the aridity of the northern part of the desert. These major mountain ranges act as a barrier causing a strong <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> effect on the leeward side by dropping much of the humidity brought by atmospheric disturbances along the <a href="/wiki/Polar_front" title="Polar front">polar front</a> which affects the surrounding Mediterranean climates.</p>
<p>The primary source of rain in the Sahara is the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>, a continuous belt of low-pressure systems near the equator which bring the brief, short and irregular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainy_season" title="Rainy season">rainy season</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> and southern Sahara. Rainfall in this giant desert has to overcome the physical and atmospheric barriers that normally prevent the production of precipitation. The harsh climate of the Sahara is characterized by: extremely low, unreliable, highly erratic rainfall; extremely high sunshine duration values; high temperatures year-round; negligible rates of <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a>; a significant <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal temperature variation</a>; and extremely high levels of <a href="/wiki/Potential_evaporation" title="Potential evaporation">potential evaporation</a> which are the highest recorded worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span></h3>
<p>The sky is usually clear above the desert and the <a href="/wiki/Sunshine_duration" title="Sunshine duration">sunshine duration</a> is extremely high everywhere in the Sahara. Most of the desert enjoys more than 3,600 h of bright sunshine annually or over 82% of the time, and a wide area in the eastern part experiences in excess of 4,000 h of bright sunshine a year or over 91% of the time. The highest values are very close to the theoretical maximum value. A value of 4,300 h or 98% of the time would be recorded in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxor" title="Luxor">Luxor</a>) and in the Nubian Desert (<a href="/wiki/Wadi_Halfa" title="Wadi Halfa">Wadi Halfa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.fr_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.fr-15">[15]</a></sup> The annual average <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">direct solar irradiation</a> is around 2,800 kWh/(m<sup>2</sup> year) in the Great Desert. The Sahara has a huge potential for solar energy production.</p>

<p>The constantly high position of the sun, the extremely low relative humidity, and the lack of <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> and rainfall make the Great Desert the hottest continuously large area worldwide, and the hottest place on Earth during summer in some spots. The average high temperature exceeds 38 to 40 °C or 100.4 to 104.0 °F during the hottest month nearly everywhere in the desert except at very high altitudes. The highest officially recorded average high temperature<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Average over what time period? (April 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> was 47 °C or 116.6 °F in a remote desert town in the <a href="/wiki/Algerian_Desert" title="Algerian Desert">Algerian Desert</a> called <a href="/wiki/Bou_Bernous" title="Bou Bernous">Bou Bernous</a> with an elevation of 378 metres (1,240 ft) meters above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.fr_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.fr-15">[15]</a></sup> It is the world’s highest recorded average high temperature<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Average over what time period? (April 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_Valley,_California" title="Death Valley, California">Death Valley, California</a> rivals it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Other hot spots in Algeria such as <a href="/wiki/Adrar,_Algeria" title="Adrar, Algeria">Adrar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timimoun" title="Timimoun">Timimoun</a>, <a href="/wiki/In_Salah" title="In Salah">In Salah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ouallene" title="Ouallene">Ouallene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aoulef" title="Aoulef">Aoulef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reggane" title="Reggane">Reggane</a> with an elevation between 200 and 400 metres (660 and 1,310 ft) above sea level get slightly lower summer average highs around 46 °C or 114.8 °F during the hottest months of the year. Salah, well known in Algeria for its extreme heat, has average high temperatures of 43.8 °C or 110.8 °F, 46.4 °C or 115.5 °F, 45.5 °C or 113.9 °F and 41.9 °C or 107.4 °F in June, July, August and September respectively. There are even hotter spots in the Sahara, but they are located in extremely remote areas, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Azalai" title="Azalai">Azalai</a>, lying in northern Mali. The major part of the desert experiences around three to five months when the average high strictly exceeds 40 °C or 104 °F. The southern central part of the desert experiences up to six or seven months when the average high temperature strictly exceeds 40 °C or 104 °F which shows the constancy and the length of the really hot season in the Sahara. Some examples of this are: <a href="/wiki/Bilma" title="Bilma">Bilma</a>, Niger and Faya-Largeau, Chad. The annual average daily temperature exceeds 20 °C or 68 °F everywhere and can approach 30 °C or 86 °F in the hottest regions year-round. However, most of the desert has a value in excess of 25 °C or 77 °F.</p>
<p>Sand and ground temperatures are even more extreme. During daytime, the sand temperature is extremely high as it can easily reach 80 °C or 176 °F or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-17">[17]</a></sup> A sand temperature of 83.5 °C (182.3 °F) has been recorded in <a href="/wiki/Port_Sudan" title="Port Sudan">Port Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-17">[17]</a></sup> Ground temperatures of 72 °C or 161.6 °F have been recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Adrar_Plateau" title="Adrar Plateau">Adrar of Mauritania</a> and a value of 75 °C (167 °F) has been measured in <a href="/wiki/Borkou" title="Borkou">Borkou</a>, northern Chad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to lack of cloud cover and very low humidity, the desert usually features high diurnal temperature variations between days and nights. However, it is a myth that the nights are cold after extremely hot days in the Sahara. The average diurnal temperature range is typically between 13 and 20 °C or 23.4 and 36.0 °F. The lowest values are found along the coastal regions due to high humidity and are often even lower than 10 °C or 18 °F, while the highest values are found in inland desert areas where the humidity is the lowest, mainly in the southern Sahara. Still, it is true that winter nights can be cold as it can drop to the freezing point and even below, especially in high-elevation areas. The frequency of subfreezing winter nights in the Sahara is strongly influenced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a> (NAO), with warmer winter temperatures during negative NAO events and cooler winters with more frosts when the NAO is positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This is because the weaker clockwise flow around the eastern side of the subtropical anticyclone during negative NAO winters, although too dry to produce more than negligible precipitation, does reduce the flow of dry, cold air from higher latitudes of Eurasia into the Sahara significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation">Precipitation</span></h3>
<p>The average annual rainfall ranges from very low in the northern and southern fringes of the desert to nearly non-existent over the central and the eastern part. The thin northern fringe of the desert receives more winter cloudiness and rainfall due to the arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_system" title="Low pressure system">low pressure systems</a> over the Mediterranean Sea along the polar front, although very attenuated by the rain shadow effects of the mountains and the annual average rainfall ranges from 100 millimetres (4 in) to 250 millimetres (10 in). For example, <a href="/wiki/Biskra" title="Biskra">Biskra</a>, Algeria and <a href="/wiki/Ouarzazate" title="Ouarzazate">Ouarzazate</a>, Morocco are found in this zone. The southern fringe of the desert along the border with the Sahel receives summer cloudiness and rainfall due to the arrival of the Intertropical Convergence Zone from the south and the annual average rainfall ranges from 100 millimetres (4 in) to 250 millimetres (10 in). For example, Timbuktu, Mali and <a href="/wiki/Agadez" title="Agadez">Agadez</a>, Niger are found in this zone. The vast central hyper-arid core of the desert is virtually never affected by northerly or southerly atmospheric disturbances and permanently remains under the influence of the strongest anticyclonic weather regime, and the annual average rainfall can drop to less than 1 millimetre (0.04 in). In fact, most of the Sahara receives less than 20 millimetres (0.8 in). Of the 9,000,000 square kilometres (3,500,000 sq mi) of desert land in the Sahara, an area of about 2,800,000 square kilometres (1,100,000 sq mi) (about 31% of the total area) receives an annual average rainfall amount of 10 millimetres (0.4 in) or less, while some 1,500,000 square kilometres (580,000 sq mi) (about 17% of the total area) receives an average of 5 millimetres (0.2 in) or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-20">[20]</a></sup> The annual average rainfall is virtually zero over a wide area of some 1,000,000 square kilometres (390,000 sq mi) in the eastern Sahara comprising deserts of: Libya, Egypt and Sudan (<a href="/wiki/Tazirbu" title="Tazirbu">Tazirbu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kufra" title="Kufra">Kufra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dakhla_Oasis" title="Dakhla Oasis">Dakhla</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kharga" title="Kharga">Kharga</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farafra" title="Farafra">Farafra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siwa_Oasis" title="Siwa Oasis">Siwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asyut" title="Asyut">Asyut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sohag" title="Sohag">Sohag</a>, Luxor, Aswan, <a href="/wiki/Abu_Simbel_temples" title="Abu Simbel temples">Abu Simbel</a>, Wadi Halfa) where the long-term mean approximates 0.5 millimetres (0.02 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-20">[20]</a></sup> Rainfall is very unreliable and erratic in the Sahara as it may vary considerably year by year. In full contrast to the negligible annual rainfall amounts, the annual rates of potential evaporation are extraordinarily high, roughly ranging from 2,500 millimetres (100 in) per year to more than 6,000 millimetres (240 in) per year in the whole desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Nowhere else on Earth has air been found as dry and evaporative as in the Sahara region. However, at least two instances of snowfall have been recorded in Sahara, in February 1979 and December 2016, both in the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ain_Sefra" title="Ain Sefra">Ain Sefra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecoregions">Ecoregions</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Sahara comprises several distinct <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a>. With their variations in temperature, rainfall, elevation, and soil, these regions harbor distinct communities of plants and animals.</p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_coastal_desert" title="Atlantic coastal desert">Atlantic coastal desert</a></b> is a narrow strip along the Atlantic coast where fog generated offshore by the cool <a href="/wiki/Canary_Current" title="Canary Current">Canary Current</a> provides sufficient moisture to sustain a variety of <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Succulent_plant" title="Succulent plant">succulents</a>, and shrubs. It covers an area of 39,900 square kilometers (15,400 sq mi) in the south of Morocco and Mauritania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/North_Saharan_steppe_and_woodlands" title="North Saharan steppe and woodlands">North Saharan steppe and woodlands</a></b> is along the northern desert, next to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_forests,_woodlands,_and_scrub" title="Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub">Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub</a> ecoregions of the northern Maghreb and Cyrenaica. Winter rains sustain shrublands and dry woodlands that form a transition between the Mediterranean climate regions to the north and the hyper-arid Sahara proper to the south. It covers 1,675,300 square kilometers (646,840 sq mi) in Algeria, Egypt, Libya, Mauritania, Morocco, and Tunisia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert_(ecoregion)" title="Sahara Desert (ecoregion)">Sahara Desert ecoregion</a></b> covers the hyper-arid central portion of the Sahara where rainfall is minimal and sporadic. Vegetation is rare, and this ecoregion consists mostly of sand dunes (<i>erg, chech, raoui</i>), stone plateaus (<i>hamadas</i>), gravel plains (<i>reg</i>), dry valleys (<i>wadis</i>), and salt flats. It covers 4,639,900 square kilometres (1,791,500 sq mi) of: Algeria, Chad, Egypt, Libya, Mali, Mauritania, Niger, and Sudan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PA1327_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-PA1327-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/South_Saharan_steppe_and_woodlands" title="South Saharan steppe and woodlands">South Saharan steppe and woodlands</a></b> ecoregion is a narrow band running east and west between the hyper-arid Sahara and the Sahel savannas to the south. Movements of the equatorial Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) bring summer rains during July and August which average 100 to 200 mm (4 to 8 in) but vary greatly from year to year. These rains sustain summer pastures of grasses and herbs, with dry woodlands and shrublands along seasonal watercourses. This ecoregion covers 1,101,700 square kilometres (425,400 sq mi) in Algeria, Chad, Mali, Mauritania, and Sudan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <b><a href="/wiki/West_Saharan_montane_xeric_woodlands" title="West Saharan montane xeric woodlands">West Saharan montane xeric woodlands</a></b>, several volcanic highlands provide a cooler, moister environment that supports Saharo-Mediterranean woodlands and shrublands. The ecoregion covers 258,100 square kilometres (99,650 sq mi), mostly in the <a href="/wiki/Tassili_n%27Ajjer" title="Tassili n'Ajjer">Tassili n'Ajjer</a> of Algeria, with smaller enclaves in the Aïr of Niger, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhar_Adrar" title="Dhar Adrar">Dhar Adrar</a> of Mauritania, and the Adrar des Iforas of Mali and Algeria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Tibesti-Jebel_Uweinat_montane_xeric_woodlands" title="Tibesti-Jebel Uweinat montane xeric woodlands">Tibesti-Jebel Uweinat montane xeric woodlands</a></b> ecoregion consists of the Tibesti and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jebel_Uweinat" title="Jebel Uweinat">Jebel Uweinat</a> highlands. Higher and more regular rainfall and cooler temperatures support woodlands and shrublands of Date palm, <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myrtus" title="Myrtus">myrtle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oleander" title="Oleander">oleander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamarix" title="Tamarix">tamarix</a>, and several rare and endemic plants. The ecoregion covers 82,200 square kilometres (31,700 sq mi) in the Tibesti of Chad and Libya, and Jebel Uweinat on the border of Egypt, Libya, and Sudan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>Saharan halophytics</b> is an area of seasonally flooded saline depressions which is home to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halophytic" title="Halophytic">halophytic</a> (salt-adapted) plant communities. The Saharan halophytics cover 54,000 square kilometres (21,000 sq mi) including: the <a href="/wiki/Qattara_Depression" title="Qattara Depression">Qattara</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siwa_Depression" title="Siwa Depression">Siwa</a> depressions in northern Egypt, the <a href="/wiki/Tunisian_salt_lakes" title="Tunisian salt lakes">Tunisian salt lakes</a> of central Tunisia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chott_Melghir" title="Chott Melghir">Chott Melghir</a> in Algeria, and smaller areas of Algeria, Mauritania, and the southern part of Morocco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>Tanezrouft</b> is one of the harshest regions on Earth as well as one of the hottest and driest parts of the Sahara, with no vegetation and very little life. It is along the borders of Algeria, Niger]] and Mali, west of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahaggar_Mountains" title="Ahaggar Mountains">Hoggar</a> mountains.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span></h2>
<p>The flora of the Sahara is highly diversified based on the bio-geographical characteristics of this vast desert. <a href="/wiki/Floristics" title="Floristics">Floristically</a>, the Sahara has three zones based on the amount of rainfall received – the Northern (Mediterranean), Central and Southern Zones. There are two transitional zones – the Mediterranean-Sahara transition and the Sahel transition zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mares1999_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mares1999-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Saharan flora comprises around 2800 species of <a href="/wiki/Vascular_plant" title="Vascular plant">vascular plants</a>. Approximately a quarter of these are <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a>. About half of these species are common to the flora of the Arabian deserts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hou.C3.A9rou2008_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hou.C3.A9rou2008-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The central Sahara is estimated to include five hundred species of plants, which is extremely low considering the huge extent of the area. Plants such as acacia trees, palms, succulents, spiny shrubs, and grasses have adapted to the arid conditions, by growing lower to avoid water loss by strong winds, by storing water in their thick stems to use it in dry periods, by having long roots that travel horizontally to reach the maximum area of water and to find any surface moisture, and by having small thick leaves or needles to prevent water loss by <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>. Plant leaves may dry out totally and then recover.</p>

<p>Several species of fox live in the Sahara including: the <a href="/wiki/Fennec_fox" title="Fennec fox">fennec fox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pale_fox" title="Pale fox">pale fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_fox" title="Rüppell's fox">Rüppell's fox</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Addax" title="Addax">addax</a>, a large white <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a>, can go nearly a year in the desert without drinking. The <a href="/wiki/Dorcas_gazelle" title="Dorcas gazelle">dorcas gazelle</a> is a north African gazelle that can also go for a long time without water. Other notable gazelles include the <a href="/wiki/Rhim_gazelle" title="Rhim gazelle">rhim gazelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dama_gazelle" title="Dama gazelle">dama gazelle</a>.</p>
<p>The Saharan <a href="/wiki/Cheetah" title="Cheetah">cheetah</a> (<a href="/wiki/Northwest_African_cheetah" title="Northwest African cheetah">northwest African cheetah</a>) lives in Algeria, <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a>, Niger, Mali, <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>. There remain fewer than 250 mature cheetahs, which are very cautious, fleeing any human presence. The cheetah avoids the sun from April to October, seeking the shelter of shrubs such as <a href="/wiki/Balanites" title="Balanites">balanites</a> and acacias. They are unusually pale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The other cheetah subspecies (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_African_cheetah" title="Northeast African cheetah">northeast African cheetah</a>) lives in Chad, Sudan and the eastern region of Niger. However, it is currently extinct in the wild in Egypt and Libya. There are approximately 2000 mature individuals left in the wild.</p>

<p>Other animals include the <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">monitor lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyrax" title="Hyrax">hyrax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerastes_(genus)" title="Cerastes (genus)">sand vipers</a>, and small populations of <a href="/wiki/African_wild_dog" title="African wild dog">African wild dog</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-33">[33]</a></sup> in perhaps only 14 countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borrell_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borrell-34">[34]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-necked_ostrich" title="Red-necked ostrich">red-necked ostrich</a>. Other animals exist in the Sahara (birds in particular) such as <a href="/wiki/African_silverbill" title="African silverbill">African silverbill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-faced_firefinch" title="Black-faced firefinch">black-faced firefinch</a>, among others. There are also small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_crocodile" title="Desert crocodile">desert crocodiles</a> in Mauritania and the <a href="/wiki/Ennedi_Plateau" title="Ennedi Plateau">Ennedi Plateau</a> of Chad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Deathstalker" title="Deathstalker">deathstalker</a> scorpion can be 10 cm (3.9 in) long. Its venom contains large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Agitoxin" title="Agitoxin">agitoxin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scyllatoxin" title="Scyllatoxin">scyllatoxin</a> and is very dangerous; however, a sting from this scorpion rarely kills a healthy adult. The <a href="/wiki/Saharan_silver_ant" title="Saharan silver ant">Saharan silver ant</a> is unique in that due to the extreme high temperatures of their habitat, and the threat of predators, the ants are active outside their nest for only about ten minutes per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WehnerEtAl1992_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-WehnerEtAl1992-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dromedary" title="Dromedary">Dromedary</a> camels and <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goats</a> are the domesticated animals most commonly found in the Sahara. Because of its qualities of endurance and speed, the dromedary is the favourite animal used by <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomads</a>.</p>
<p>Human activities are more likely to affect the habitat in areas of permanent water (oases) or where water comes close to the surface. Here, the local pressure on natural resources can be intense. The remaining populations of large mammals have been greatly reduced by hunting for food and recreation. In recent years development projects have started in the deserts of Algeria and Tunisia using irrigated water pumped from underground aquifers. These schemes often lead to soil degradation and <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">salinization</a>.</p>
<p>Researchers from <a href="/wiki/Hacettepe_University" title="Hacettepe University">Hacettepe University</a> (Yücekutlu, N. et al., 2011) have reported that Saharan soil may have bio-available iron and also some essential macro and micro nutrient elements suitable for use as fertilizer for growing wheat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YTSB2011_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-YTSB2011-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>People lived on the edge of the desert thousands of years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-Ice_Age_Sahara_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post-Ice_Age_Sahara-38">[38]</a></sup> since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>. The Sahara was then a much wetter place than it is today. Over 30,000 <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a> of river animals such as <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crocodiles_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crocodiles-39">[39]</a></sup> survive, with half found in the Tassili n'Ajjer in southeast Algeria. <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossils</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Afrovenator" title="Afrovenator">Afrovenator</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jobaria" title="Jobaria">Jobaria</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ouranosaurus" title="Ouranosaurus">Ouranosaurus</a></i>, have also been found here. The modern Sahara, though, is not lush in vegetation, except in the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> Valley, at a few <a href="/wiki/Oasis" title="Oasis">oases</a>, and in the northern highlands, where Mediterranean plants such as the <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a> tree are found to grow. It was long believed that the region had been this way since about 1600 BCE, after shifts in the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">Earth's axis</a> increased temperatures and decreased precipitation, which led to the abrupt desertification of North Africa about 5,400 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_Daily_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science_Daily-40">[40]</a></sup> However, this theory has recently been called into dispute, when samples taken from several 7 million year old sand deposits led scientists to reconsider the timeline for desertification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Publishing_Group_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Publishing_Group-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nubians">Nubians</span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright">

<a href="/wiki/M%27zab" title="M'zab">Beni Isguen</a>, a holy city surrounded by thick walls in the Algerian Sahara.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> Era, before the onset of <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> around 9500 BCE, the central Sudan had been a rich environment supporting a large population ranging across what is now barren desert, like the Wadi el-Qa'ab. By the 5th millennium BCE, the people who inhabited what is now called <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>, were full participants in the "agricultural revolution", living a settled lifestyle with domesticated plants and animals. Saharan rock art of cattle and herdsmen suggests the presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_cult" title="Cattle cult">cattle cult</a> like those found in Sudan and other pastoral societies in Africa today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">Megaliths</a> found at <a href="/wiki/Nabta_Playa" title="Nabta Playa">Nabta Playa</a> are overt examples of probably the world's first known <a href="/wiki/Archaeoastronomy" title="Archaeoastronomy">archaeoastronomy</a> devices, predating <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> by some 2,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This complexity, as observed at Nabta Playa, and as expressed by different levels of authority within the society there, likely formed the basis for the structure of both the Neolithic society at Nabta and the Old Kingdom of Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egyptians">Egyptians</span></h3>
<p>By 6000 BCE <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predynastic_Egypt" title="Predynastic Egypt">predynastic Egyptians</a> in the southwestern corner of Egypt were herding cattle and constructing large buildings. Subsistence in organized and permanent settlements in predynastic Egypt by the middle of the 6th millennium BCE centered predominantly on cereal and animal agriculture: cattle, goats, pigs and sheep. Metal objects replaced prior ones of stone. Tanning of animal skins, pottery and weaving were commonplace in this era also. There are indications of seasonal or only temporary occupation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Fayyum" title="Al Fayyum">Al Fayyum</a> in the 6th millennium BCE, with food activities centering on fishing, hunting and food-gathering. Stone <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead" title="Arrowhead">arrowheads</a>, knives and scrapers from the era are commonly found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fayum_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fayum-45">[45]</a></sup> Burial items included pottery, jewelry, farming and hunting equipment, and assorted foods including dried meat and fruit. Burial in desert environments appears to enhance Egyptian preservation rites, and the dead were buried facing due west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Predynastic_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Predynastic-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 3400 BCE, the Sahara was as dry as it is today, due to reduced precipitation and higher temperatures resulting from a shift in the Earth's orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_Daily_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Science_Daily-40">[40]</a></sup> As a result of this aridification, it became a largely impenetrable barrier to humans, with the remaining settlements mainly being concentrated around the numerous oases that dot the landscape. Little trade or commerce is known to have passed through the interior in subsequent periods, the only major exception being the Nile Valley. The Nile, however, was impassable at several <a href="/wiki/Cataracts_of_the_Nile" title="Cataracts of the Nile">cataracts</a>, making trade and contact by boat difficult.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Western_Sahara" title="History of Western Sahara">History of Western Sahara</a></div>
<p>The people of <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicia</a>, who flourished from 1200–800 BCE, created a confederation of kingdoms across the entire Sahara to Egypt. They generally settled along the Mediterranean coast, as well as the Sahara, among the people of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">ancient Libya</a>, who were the ancestors of people who speak <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber languages</a> in North Africa and the Sahara today, including the <a href="/wiki/Tuareg_people" title="Tuareg people">Tuareg</a> of the central Sahara.</p>

<p>The Phoenician alphabet seems to have been adopted by the ancient Libyans of north Africa, and <a href="/wiki/Tifinagh" title="Tifinagh">Tifinagh</a> is still used today by Berber-speaking Tuareg camel herders of the central Sahara.</p>
<p>Sometime between 633 BCE and 530 BCE, <a href="/wiki/Hanno_the_Navigator" title="Hanno the Navigator">Hanno the Navigator</a> either established or reinforced Phoenician colonies in Western Sahara, but all ancient remains have vanished with virtually no trace.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greeks">Greeks</span></h3>
<p>By 500 BCE, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> arrived in the desert. Greek traders spread along the eastern coast of the desert, establishing trading colonies along the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthaginians</a> explored the Atlantic coast of the desert, but the turbulence of the waters and the lack of markets caused a lack of presence further south than modern Morocco. Centralized states thus surrounded the desert on the north and east; it remained outside the control of these states. Raids from the nomadic Berber people of the desert were of constant concern to those living on the edge of the desert.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_civilization">Urban civilization</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ghardaia01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2196" data-file-width="3375" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Ghardaia01.jpg/220px-Ghardaia01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Ghardaia01.jpg/330px-Ghardaia01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Ghardaia01.jpg/440px-Ghardaia01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ghardaia01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Market on the main square of <a href="/wiki/Gharda%C3%AFa" title="Ghardaïa">Ghardaïa</a> (1971)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An urban civilization, the <a href="/wiki/Garamantes" title="Garamantes">Garamantes</a>, arose around 500 BCE in the heart of the Sahara, in a valley that is now called the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wadi_al-Ajal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wadi al-Ajal (page does not exist)">Wadi al-Ajal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fezzan" title="Fezzan">Fezzan</a>, Libya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakes_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakes-47">[47]</a></sup> The Garamantes achieved this development by digging tunnels far into the mountains flanking the valley to tap <a href="/wiki/Fossil_water" title="Fossil water">fossil water</a> and bring it to their fields. The Garamantes grew populous and strong, conquering their neighbors and capturing many slaves (who were put to work extending the tunnels). The ancient Greeks and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> knew of the Garamantes and regarded them as uncivilized nomads. However, they traded with them, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_bath" title="Roman bath">Roman bath</a> has been found in the Garamantes' capital of Garama. <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">Archaeologists</a> have found eight major towns and many other important settlements in the Garamantes' territory. The Garamantes' civilization eventually collapsed after they had depleted available water in the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> and could no longer sustain the effort to extend the tunnels further into the mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keys_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keys-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber people</a> occupied (and still occupy) much of the Sahara. The Garamantes Berbers built a prosperous empire in the heart of the desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The Tuareg nomads continue to inhabit and move across wide Sahara surfaces to the present day.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_expansion">Islamic expansion</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade" title="Trans-Saharan trade">Trans-Saharan trade</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamization_of_Sudan" title="Islamization of Sudan">Islamization of Sudan</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> ruled the northern shores of the Sahara from the 5th to the 7th centuries. After the Muslim conquest of Arabia, specifically the Arabian peninsula, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_North_Africa" title="Muslim conquest of North Africa">Muslim conquest of North Africa</a> began in the mid-7th to early 8th centuries and Islamic influence expanded rapidly on the Sahara. By the end of 641 all of Egypt was in Muslim hands. Trade across the desert intensified, and a significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> crossed the desert. It has been estimated that from the 10th to 19th centuries some 6,000 to 7,000 slaves were transported north each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_Turkish_era">Ottoman Turkish era</span></h3>
<p>In the 16th century the northern fringe of the Sahara, such as coastal regencies in present-day <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Algeria" title="Ottoman Algeria">Algeria</a> and Tunisia, as well as some parts of present-day Libya, together with the semi-autonomous kingdom of Egypt, were occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. From 1517 Egypt was a valued part of the Ottoman Empire, ownership of which provided the Ottomans with control over the Nile Valley, the east Mediterranean and North Africa. The benefit of the Ottoman Empire was the freedom of movement for citizens and goods. Traders exploited the Ottoman land routes to handle the spices, gold and silk from the East, manufactured goods from Europe, and the slave and gold traffic from Africa. Arabic continued as the local language and Islamic culture was much reinforced. The Sahel and southern Sahara regions were home to several independent states or to roaming Tuareg clans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_colonialism">European colonialism</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/European_colonialism" title="European colonialism">European colonialism</a> in the Sahara began in the 19th century. <a href="/wiki/July_Monarchy" title="July Monarchy">France</a> conquered the regency of Algiers from the Ottomans in 1830, and French rule spread south from Algeria and eastwards from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> into the upper <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">Niger</a> to include present-day Algeria, Chad, Mali then <a href="/wiki/French_Sudan" title="French Sudan">French Sudan</a> including Timbuktu, Mauritania, Morocco (1912), Niger, and Tunisia (1881). By the beginning of the 20th century, the trans-Saharan trade had clearly declined because goods were moved through more modern and efficient means, such as airplanes, rather than across the desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French Colonial Empire was the dominant presence in the Sahara. It established regular air links from <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a> (HQ of famed <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale</a>), to <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">Oran</a> and over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoggar" title="Hoggar">Hoggar</a> to Timbuktu and West to <a href="/wiki/Bamako" title="Bamako">Bamako</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, as well as trans-Sahara bus services run by La Companie Transsaharienne (est. 1927).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wauthier1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-wauthier1-52">[52]</a></sup> A remarkable film shot by famous aviator Captain <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Wauthier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="René Wauthier (page does not exist)">René Wauthier</a> documents the first crossing by a large truck convoy from Algiers to Tchad, across the Sahara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reconnaissance_saharienne_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reconnaissance_saharienne-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Egypt, under <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali</a> and his successors, conquered Nubia in 1820–22, founded <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a> in 1823, and conquered <a href="/wiki/Darfur" title="Darfur">Darfur</a> in 1874. Egypt, including the Sudan, became a British protectorate in 1882. Egypt and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">Britain</a> lost control of the Sudan from 1882 to 1898 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Mahdist_War" title="Mahdist War">Mahdist War</a>. After its capture by British troops in 1898, the Sudan became an Anglo-Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Condominium_(international_law)" title="Condominium (international law)">condominium</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Restoration_(Spain)" title="Restoration (Spain)">Spain</a> captured present-day <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> after 1874, although Rio del Oro remained largely under <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_people" title="Sahrawi people">Sahrawi</a> influence. In 1912, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Italy</a> captured parts of what was to be named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Libya" title="Ottoman Libya">Libya</a> from the Ottomans. To promote the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> religion in the desert, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pope Pius IX</a> appointed a delegate Apostolic of the Sahara and the Sudan in 1868; later in the 19th century his jurisdiction was reorganized into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicariate_Apostolic_of_Sahara" title="Vicariate Apostolic of Sahara">Vicariate Apostolic of Sahara</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakup_of_the_empires_and_afterwards">Breakup of the empires and afterwards</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Egypt became independent of Britain in 1936, although the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Egyptian_treaty_of_1936" title="Anglo-Egyptian treaty of 1936">Anglo-Egyptian treaty of 1936</a> allowed Britain to keep troops in Egypt and to maintain the British-Egyptian condominium in the Sudan. British military forces were withdrawn in 1954.</p>
<p>Most of the Saharan states achieved independence after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>: Libya in 1951; Morocco, Sudan, and Tunisia in 1956; Chad, Mali, Mauritania, and Niger in 1960; and Algeria in 1962. Spain withdrew from Western Sahara in 1975, and it was partitioned between Mauritania and Morocco. Mauritania withdrew in 1979; Morocco continues to hold the territory.</p>
<p>In the post-World War II era, several <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a> and communities have developed to utilize the desert's natural resources. These include large deposits of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> in Algeria and Libya, and large deposits of <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a> in Morocco and Western Sahara.</p>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Trans-African_Highway_network" title="Trans-African Highway network">Trans-African highways</a> have been proposed across the Sahara, including the <a href="/wiki/Cairo%E2%80%93Dakar_Highway" title="Cairo–Dakar Highway">Cairo–Dakar Highway</a> along the Atlantic coast, the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Sahara_Highway" title="Trans-Sahara Highway">Trans-Sahara Highway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a> on the Mediterranean to <a href="/wiki/Kano" title="Kano">Kano</a> in Nigeria, the <a href="/wiki/Tripoli_%E2%80%93_Cape_Town_Highway" title="Tripoli – Cape Town Highway">Tripoli – Cape Town Highway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a> in Libya to <a href="/wiki/N%27Djamena" title="N'Djamena">N'Djamena</a> in Chad, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cairo_%E2%80%93_Cape_Town_Highway" title="Cairo – Cape Town Highway">Cairo – Cape Town Highway</a> which follows the Nile. Each of these highways is partially complete, with significant gaps and unpaved sections.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People_and_languages">People and languages</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arabslavers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="378" data-file-width="556" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Arabslavers.jpg/220px-Arabslavers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Arabslavers.jpg/330px-Arabslavers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Arabslavers.jpg/440px-Arabslavers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arabslavers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 19th-century engraving of an Arab <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">slave-trading</a> caravan transporting black African slaves across the Sahara.</div>

</div>
<p>The people of the Sahara are of various origins. Among them the Amaziɣ including the Turūq, various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabized" title="Arabized">Arabized</a> Amaziɣ groups such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hassaniya" title="Hassaniya">Hassaniya</a>-speaking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahrawis" title="Sahrawis">Sahrawis</a>, whose populations include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Znaga" title="Znaga">Znaga</a>, a tribe whose name is a remnant of the pre-historic <a href="/wiki/Zenaga_language" title="Zenaga language">Zenaga language</a>. Other major groups of people include the: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toubou" title="Toubou">Toubou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nubians" title="Nubians">Nubians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zaghawa_people" title="Zaghawa people">Zaghawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanuri_people" title="Kanuri people">Kanuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hausa_people" title="Hausa people">Hausa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Songhai_people" title="Songhai people">Songhai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beja_people" title="Beja people">Beja</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fula_people" title="Fula people">Fula/Fulani</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Peul</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Fula_language" title="Fula language">Fula</a>: <span lang="ff" xml:lang="ff"><i>Fulɓe</i></span>).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_dialects" title="Arabic dialects">Arabic dialects</a> are the most widely spoken languages in the Sahara. Arabic, Berber and its variants now regrouped under the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a> (which includes the <a href="/wiki/Guanche_language" title="Guanche language">Guanche language</a> spoken by the original Berber inhabitants of the Canary Islands) and Beja languages are part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic" title="Afro-Asiatic">Afro-Asiatic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamito-Semitic" title="Hamito-Semitic">Hamito-Semitic</a> family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike neighboring <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> and the central governments of the states that comprise the Sahara, the French language bears little relevance to inter-personal discourse and commerce within the region, its people retaining staunch ethnic and political affiliations with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuareg" title="Tuareg">Tuareg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berber</a> leaders and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The legacy of the French <a href="/wiki/French_North_Africa" title="French North Africa">colonial era</a> administration is primarily manifested in the territorial reorganization enacted by the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">Third</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Fourth_Republic" title="French Fourth Republic">Fourth</a> republics, which engendered artificial political divisions within a hitherto isolated and porous region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Diplomacy with local clients was conducted primarily in Arabic, which was the traditional language of bureaucratic affairs. Mediation of disputes and inter-agency communication was served by interpreters contracted by the French government, who, according to Keenan, "documented a space of intercultural mediation," contributing much to preserving the indigenous cultural identities in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
