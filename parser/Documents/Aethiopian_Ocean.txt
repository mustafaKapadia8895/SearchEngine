<p><b>Aethiopian Sea</b>, <b>Æthiopian Ocean</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>Æthiopic Ocean</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Æthiopicum Mare</i></span> or <i>Oceanus Æthiopicus</i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">البحر الأثيوبي</span>‎‎) was the name given to the southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> in classical geographical works. The name appeared in maps from ancient times to the turn of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aethiopian_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Aethiopia" title="Aethiopia">Aethiopia</a></div>
<p>The Ethiopian Sea, Ethiopic Ocean or Ethiopian Ocean (Okeanos Aithiopos), is an old name for what is now called the South Atlantic Ocean. It is separated from the North Atlantic Ocean by a narrow region between <a href="/wiki/Natal,_Rio_Grande_do_Norte" title="Natal, Rio Grande do Norte">Natal, Brazil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monrovia,_Liberia" title="Monrovia, Liberia">Monrovia, Liberia</a>. The term Ethiopian Ocean appeared until the mid-19th century, for example on the map <i>Accuratissima Totius Africae in Lucem Producta</i>, engraved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_Homann" title="Johann Baptist Homann">Johann Baptist Homann</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_de_Wit" title="Frederick de Wit">Frederick de Wit</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_von_Sandrart" title="Jacob von Sandrart">Jacob von Sandrart</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Nürnberg">Nürnberg</a> in 1702.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name Aethiopian Sea to refer to the ocean was related to referring to large swathes of the continent of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> as <a href="/wiki/Aethiopia" title="Aethiopia">Aethiopia</a>. Nowadays the classical use of the term has become obsolete, as the modern nation of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> is located in the northeastern area of Africa and not near the actual Ethiopic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> historians <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diodorus" title="Diodorus">Diodorus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palaephatus" title="Palaephatus">Palaephatus</a> mentioned that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorgons" title="Gorgons">Gorgons</a> lived in the <a href="/wiki/Gorgades" title="Gorgades">Gorgades</a>, islands in the Aethiopian Sea. The main island was called Cerna and, according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_T._Riley" title="Henry T. Riley">Henry T. Riley</a>, these islands may correspond to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16th century maps, the name of the Northern Atlantic Ocean was <i>Sinus Occidentalis</i>, while the central Atlantic, southwest of present-day <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, appeared as <i>Sinus Atlanticus</i> and the Southern Atlantic as <i>Mare Aethiopicum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 17th century <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Seller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Seller (page does not exist)">John Seller</a> divided the Atlantic Ocean in two parts by means of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. He named the northern portion of the Atlantic "Mar del Nort" and the southern part "Oceanus Æthiopicus" in his <i>Atlas Maritimus</i> published in 1672.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Wright_(mathematician)" title="Edward Wright (mathematician)">Edward Wright</a> did not label the North Atlantic at all but called the portion south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> the "Aethiopian Sea" in a map that was published posthumously in 1683.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Thornton_(cartographer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Thornton (cartographer) (page does not exist)">John Thornton</a> used the term in "A New Map of the World" from 1703.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decades after the terms Ethiopian Ocean or Ethiopian Sea had fallen into disuse to refer to the Southern Atlantic Ocean, botanist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Albert_Setchell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Albert Setchell (page does not exist)">William Albert Setchell</a> (1864–1943) used the term for the sea around certain islands close to <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
