<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">High pressure</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the related geology term, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultrahigh-pressure_metamorphism" title="Ultrahigh-pressure metamorphism">Ultrahigh-pressure metamorphism</a>. For the meteorology term, see <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">High-pressure area</a>. For the 1932 film, see <a href="/wiki/High_Pressure_(film)" title="High Pressure (film)">High Pressure (film)</a>. For the Red Garland album, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Pressure_(Red_Garland_album)" title="High Pressure (Red Garland album)">High Pressure (Red Garland album)</a>.</div>
<p><b>High pressure</b> in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> is studying the effects of high pressure on materials and the design and construction of devices, such as a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell" title="Diamond anvil cell">diamond anvil cell</a>, which can create high <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. By high pressure it is usually meant pressures of thousands (kilo<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bars</a>) or millions (megabars) of times <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> (about 1 bar or 100,000 Pa).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_overview">History and overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Percy_Williams_Bridgman" title="Percy Williams Bridgman">Percy Williams Bridgman</a> received a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in 1946 for advancing this area of physics by several magnitudes of pressure (400 MPa to 40,000 MPa). The list of founding fathers of this field includes also the names of <a href="/wiki/Harry_George_Drickamer" title="Harry George Drickamer">Harry George Drickamer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Hall" title="Tracy Hall">Tracy Hall</a>, Francis P. Bundy, Leonid F. Vereschagin, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_M._Stishov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sergey M. Stishov (page does not exist)">Sergey M. Stishov</a>.</p>
<p>It was by applying high pressure as well as high <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> that man-made <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_diamond" title="Synthetic diamond">diamonds</a> were first produced as well as many other interesting discoveries. Almost any material when subjected to high pressure will compact itself into a denser form, for example, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a> will first adopt a denser form known as <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a>, then upon application of even higher pressure, form <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a>. These two forms of silica were first discovered by high-pressure experimenters, but then found in nature at the site of a meteor impact.</p>
<p>Chemical bonding is likely to change under high pressure, when the P*V term in the free energy becomes comparable to the energies of typical chemical bonds - i.e. at around 100 GPa. Among the most striking changes are metallization of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> at 96 GPa (rendering oxygen a superconductor), and transition of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> from a nearly-free-electron metal to a transparent insulator at ~200 GPa. At ultimately high compression, however, all materials will metallize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>High-pressure experimentation has led to the discovery of the types of minerals which are believed to exist in the deep mantle of the Earth, such as <a href="/wiki/Silicate_perovskite" title="Silicate perovskite">silicate perovskite</a>, which is thought to make up half of the Earth's bulk, and <a href="/wiki/Post-perovskite" title="Post-perovskite">post-perovskite</a>, which occurs at the core-mantle boundary and explains many anomalies inferred for that region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Pressure "landmarks": typical pressures reached by large-volume presses are up to 30-40 GPa, pressures that can be generated inside <a href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell" title="Diamond anvil cell">diamond anvil cells</a> are ~1000 GPa,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> pressure in the center of the Earth is 364 GPa, and highest pressures ever achieved in shock waves are over 100,000 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
