<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Freezing level</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iso0.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="250" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Iso0.png/220px-Iso0.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3f/Iso0.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iso0.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 0 °C isotherm under normal conditions</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>freezing level</b>, or <b>0 °C (zero-degree) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherm</a></b>, represents the <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> in which the temperature is at 0 °C (the freezing point of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>) in a free atmosphere (i.e. allowing reflection of the sun by snow, etc.). Any given measure is valid for only a short period of time, often less than a day.</p>
<p>Above the freezing altitude, the temperature of the air is below freezing. Below it, the temperature is above freezing. The profile of this frontier, and its variations, are studied in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, and are used for a variety of forecasts and predictions. Whilst not given on general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_forecasts" title="Weather forecasts">weather forecasts</a>, it is used on bulletins giving forecasts for mountainous areas.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring">Measuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freezing_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measuring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer,_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="906" data-file-width="1101" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer%2C_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png/220px-Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer%2C_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer%2C_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png/330px-Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer%2C_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer%2C_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png/440px-Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer%2C_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dual-polarization_radar_image_of_an_easily_identifiable_freezing_layer,_denoted_by_the_yellow_ring_around_the_radar.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
<p>There are several different methods to examine the structure of the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> attached to a <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a> is the oldest and most common method used. Each area releases two balloons a day in locations hundreds of kilometres apart.</li>
<li>Measuring devices attached to commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airliners" title="Airliners">airliners</a> permit reporting the isotherm, and its height, to aerial traffic.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_satellites" title="Weather satellites">Weather satellites</a> are equipped with sensors which scan the atmosphere and measure the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> it emits.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Weather radar</a> detects <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar#Bright_band" title="Weather radar">bright bands</a>, which are radar echoes produced just underneath the isotherm caused by the melting of snow in the layer above 0 °C.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Wind_profiler" title="Wind profiler">wind profiler</a>, an upward pointing radar, can detect the speed of precipitation, which is different for rain, snow, and melting snow.</li>
</ul>
<p>Depending on the <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensor_resolution" title="Sensor resolution">resolution</a> at which these readings are taken, these methods can report the isotherm with greater or lesser precision. Radiosondes, for example, only report a reading twice daily and provide very rough information. Weather radar can detect a variation every five to ten minutes if there is precipitation, and can scan a radius of up to two kilometres.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_in_the_isotherm">Variations in the isotherm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freezing_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations in the isotherm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The isotherm can be very stable over a large area. It varies under two conditions:</p>
<ol>
<li>A change in the density of air due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">weather fronts</a>. This changes the isotherm gradually, over tens of kilometres for a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, and hundreds for a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a>.</li>
<li>Local levels can be changed by <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, reflection of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> level. These factors can cause the isotherm to change rapidly over several kilometres, in both winter and summer. Also, <a href="/wiki/Subsidence#Atmosphere" title="Subsidence">atmospheric subsidence</a> and ascendence can contribute to variations in the isotherm.</li>
</ol>
<p>These conditions imply that the 0 °C isotherm varies globally.</p>
