<p>The <b>Drake Passage</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Pasaje de Drake</i></span>) or <b>Mar de Hoces</b>—Sea of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Hoces" title="Francisco de Hoces">Hoces</a>—is the body of water between <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. It connects the southwestern part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scotia_Sea" title="Scotia Sea">Scotia Sea</a>) with the southeastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and extends into the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drake_Passage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The passage receives its English-language name from the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateer</a> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Sir Francis Drake</a>. Drake's <a href="/wiki/Golden_Hind" title="Golden Hind">only remaining ship</a>, after having passed through the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a>, was blown far south in September 1578. This incident implied an open connection between the Atlantic and Pacific oceans.</p>
<p>Half a century earlier, after a gale had pushed them south from the entrance of the Strait of Magellan, the crew of the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> navigator <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Hoces" title="Francisco de Hoces">Francisco de Hoces</a> thought they saw a land's end and possibly inferred this passage in 1525.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oyarzun_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oyarzun-1">[1]</a></sup> For this reason, some Spanish and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin American</a> <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a> and sources call it <b>Mar de Hoces</b> after Francisco de Hoces.</p>
<p>The first recorded voyage through the passage was that of <i>Eendracht</i>, captained by the Dutch navigator <a href="/wiki/Willem_Schouten" title="Willem Schouten">Willem Schouten</a> in 1616, naming Cape Horn in the process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drake_Passage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 800-kilometre (500 mi) wide passage between Cape Horn and <a href="/wiki/Livingston_Island" title="Livingston Island">Livingston Island</a> is the shortest crossing from Antarctica to any other landmass. The boundary between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans is sometimes taken to be a line drawn from Cape Horn to <a href="/wiki/Snow_Island_(South_Shetland_Islands)" title="Snow Island (South Shetland Islands)">Snow Island</a> (130 kilometres (81 mi) north of mainland Antarctica). Alternatively, the meridian that passes through Cape Horn may be taken as the boundary. Both boundaries lie entirely within the Drake Passage.</p>
<p>The other two passages around the extreme southern part of South America (though not going around Cape Horn as such), <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel" title="Beagle Channel">Beagle Channel</a>, are very narrow, leaving little room for a ship. They can also become icebound, and sometimes the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> blows so strongly no <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> vessel can make headway against it. Hence most sailing ships preferred the Drake Passage, which is open water for hundreds of miles, despite very rough conditions. The small <a href="/wiki/Diego_Ram%C3%ADrez_Islands" title="Diego Ramírez Islands">Diego Ramírez Islands</a> lie about 100 kilometres (62 mi) south-southwest of Cape Horn.</p>
<p>There is no significant land anywhere around the world at the <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> of Drake Passage, which is important to the unimpeded flow of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a> which carries a huge volume of water (about 600 times the flow of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a>) through the Passage and around Antarctica.</p>
<p>Ships in the Passage are often good platforms for the sighting of <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a> and seabirds including <a href="/wiki/Giant_petrel" title="Giant petrel">giant petrels</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drake_Passage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The passage is known to have been closed until around 41 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> according to a chemical study of <a href="/wiki/Shark_tooth" title="Shark tooth">fish teeth</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>. Before the passage opened, the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans were separated entirely with Antarctica being much warmer and having no <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice cap</a>. The joining of the two great oceans started the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a> and cooled the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> significantly.</p>
