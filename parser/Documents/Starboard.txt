<p><b>Port</b> and <b>starboard</b> are <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_nautical_terms" title="Glossary of nautical terms">nautical</a> and aeronautical terms for left and right, respectively. Port is the left-hand side of a vessel or aircraft, facing forward. Starboard is the right-hand side, facing forward. Since port and starboard never change, they are unambiguous references that are not <a href="/wiki/Relative_direction" title="Relative direction">relative</a> to the observer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rmg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rmg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>starboard</i> derives from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">Old English</a> <i>steorbord</i>, meaning the side on which the ship is steered. Before ships had <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudders</a> on their centrelines, they were steered with a <a href="/wiki/Steering_oar" title="Steering oar">steering oar</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a> of the ship and, because more people are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-handed" title="Right-handed">right-handed</a>, on the right-hand side of it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The term is cognate with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> <i>stýri</i> (rudder) and <i>borð</i> (side of a ship).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Since the steering oar was on the right side of the boat, it would tie up at the <a href="/wiki/Wharf" title="Wharf">wharf</a> on the other side. Hence the left side was called <i>port</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Formerly, <i>larboard</i> was used instead of <i>port</i>. This is from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle-English" title="Middle-English">Middle-English</a> <i>ladebord</i> and the term <i>lade</i> is related to the modern <i>load</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rmg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rmg-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Larboard</i> sounds similar to <i>starboard</i> and in 1844 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> ordered that <i>port</i> be used instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie1847_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norie1847-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (February 2017)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The United States Navy followed suit in 1846.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_navy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-us_navy-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Larboard</i> continued to be used well into the 1850s by <a href="/wiki/Whaler" title="Whaler">whalers</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In Old English the word was <i>bæcbord</i>, of which cognates are used in other European languages, for example as the present <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>bakboord</i>, the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>backbord</i> and the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> term <i>bâbord</i> (derived in turn from <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The navigational treaty convention, the <a href="/wiki/International_Regulations_for_Preventing_Collisions_at_Sea" title="International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea">International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea</a>—for instance, as appears in the <i>Merchant Shipping (Distress Signals and Prevention of Collisions) Regulations of 1996</i> (and comparable US documents from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Coast_Guard" title="US Coast Guard">US Coast Guard</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLREGS_UK_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-COLREGS_UK-7">[7]</a></sup>—sets forth the rights of way for maritime vessels, whether by sail or powered, and whether a vessel is overtaking, approaching head-on, or crossing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLREGS_UK_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-COLREGS_UK-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11-12</sup> To set forth these navigational rules, the terms starboard and port are absolutely essential, and to aid in <i>in situ</i> decision-making, the two sides of each vessel are marked, dusk to dawn, by <a href="/wiki/Navigation_light" title="Navigation light">navigation lights</a>, the vessel's starboard side by green and its port side by red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLREGS_UK_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-COLREGS_UK-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:15</sup> Aircraft are lit in the same way.</p>
