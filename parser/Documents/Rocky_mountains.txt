<p>The <b>Rocky Mountains</b>, commonly known as the <b>Rockies</b>, are a major <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a> in western <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The Rocky Mountains stretch more than 3,000 miles (4,800 km) from the northernmost part of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, in western <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, to <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a>. Within the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Cordillera" title="North American Cordillera">North American Cordillera</a>, the Rockies are somewhat distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Ranges" title="Pacific Coast Ranges">Pacific Coast Ranges</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, which all lie further to the west.</p>
<p>The Rocky Mountains were initially formed from 80 million to 55 million years ago during the <a href="/wiki/Laramide_orogeny" title="Laramide orogeny">Laramide orogeny</a>, in which a number of plates began to slide underneath the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_plate" title="North American plate">North American plate</a>. The angle of <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> was shallow, resulting in a broad belt of mountains running down western North America. Since then, further tectonic activity and erosion by glaciers have sculpted the Rockies into dramatic peaks and valleys. At the end of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">last ice age</a>, humans started to inhabit the mountain range. After Europeans, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Alexander_Mackenzie" title="Sir Alexander Mackenzie">Sir Alexander Mackenzie</a>, and Americans, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark_expedition" title="Lewis and Clark expedition">Lewis and Clark expedition</a>, started to explore the range, minerals and furs drove the initial economic exploitation of the mountains, although the range itself never became densely populated.</p>
<p>Much of the mountain range is protected by public parks and forest lands and is a popular tourist destination, especially for <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camping" title="Camping">camping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">mountaineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain biking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Snowboarding" title="Snowboarding">snowboarding</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The name of the mountains is a translation of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindian" title="Amerindian">Amerindian</a> name that is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Cree" title="Cree">Cree</a> name "<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Cree transliteration">as-sin-wati</span>", is given as, <i>when seen from across the prairies, they looked like a rocky mass</i>. The first mention of their present name by a European was in the journal of <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Legardeur_de_Saint-Pierre" title="Jacques Legardeur de Saint-Pierre">Jacques Legardeur de Saint-Pierre</a> in 1752, where they were called "<span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">Montagnes de Roche</span>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_States_Rocky_Mountain_System" title="Geography of the United States Rocky Mountain System">Geography of the United States Rocky Mountain System</a></div>
<p>The Rocky Mountains are commonly defined as stretching from the <a href="/wiki/Liard_River" title="Liard River">Liard River</a> in British Columbia south to the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a> in New Mexico. Other mountain ranges continue beyond those two rivers, including the <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_Mountains" title="Selwyn Mountains">Selwyn Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Range" title="Brooks Range">Brooks Range</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">Sierra Madre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, but those are not part of the Rockies, though they are part of the <a href="/wiki/American_Cordillera" title="American Cordillera">American Cordillera</a>. The United States definition of the Rockies includes the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Mountains" title="Cabinet Mountains">Cabinet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salish_Mountains" title="Salish Mountains">Salish Mountains</a> of Idaho and Montana. Their counterparts north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kootenai_River" title="Kootenai River">Kootenai River</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Mountains" title="Columbia Mountains">Columbia Mountains</a>, are considered a separate system in Canada, lying to the west of the huge <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Trench" title="Rocky Mountain Trench">Rocky Mountain Trench</a>. This runs the length of British Columbia from its beginnings in the middle <a href="/wiki/Flathead_River" title="Flathead River">Flathead River</a> valley in western Montana to the south bank of the Liard River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Rockies vary in width from 70 to 300 miles (110 to 480 kilometers). Also west of the Rocky Mountain Trench, farther north and facing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muskwa_Range" title="Muskwa Range">Muskwa Range</a> across the trench, are the <a href="/wiki/Stikine_Ranges" title="Stikine Ranges">Stikine Ranges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omineca_Mountains" title="Omineca Mountains">Omineca Mountains</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Interior_Mountains" title="Interior Mountains">Interior Mountains</a> system of British Columbia. A small area east of <a href="/wiki/Prince_George,_British_Columbia" title="Prince George, British Columbia">Prince George, British Columbia</a> on the eastern side of the Trench, the <a href="/wiki/McGregor_Plateau" title="McGregor Plateau">McGregor Plateau</a>, resembles the Rockies but is considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Interior_Plateau" title="Interior Plateau">Interior Plateau</a>.</p>

<p>The eastern edge of the Rockies rises dramatically above the <a href="/wiki/Interior_Plains" title="Interior Plains">Interior Plains</a> of central North America, including the <a href="/wiki/Sangre_de_Cristo_Mountains" title="Sangre de Cristo Mountains">Sangre de Cristo Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Front_Range" title="Front Range">Front Range</a> of Colorado, the <a href="/wiki/Wind_River_Range" title="Wind River Range">Wind River Range</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Horn_Mountains" title="Big Horn Mountains">Big Horn Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Absaroka_Range" title="Absaroka Range">Absaroka</a>-<a href="/wiki/Beartooth_Mountains" title="Beartooth Mountains">Beartooth</a> ranges and <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Front" title="Rocky Mountain Front">Rocky Mountain Front</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Clark_Range_(Canada)" title="Clark Range (Canada)">Clark Range</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>. In Canada geographers define three main groups of ranges: the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Ranges" title="Continental Ranges">Continental Ranges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hart_Ranges" title="Hart Ranges">Hart Ranges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muskwa_Ranges" title="Muskwa Ranges">Muskwa Ranges</a> (the latter two flank the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_River_(Canada)" title="Peace River (Canada)">Peace River</a>, the only river to pierce the Rockies, and are collectively referred to as the Northern Rockies). The Muskwa and Hart Ranges together comprise what is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Rockies" title="Northern Rockies">Northern Rockies</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_Mountains" title="Mackenzie Mountains">Mackenzie Mountains</a> north of the Liard River are sometimes referred to as being part of the Rocky Mountains but this is an unofficial designation).</p>

<p>The western edge of the Rockies includes ranges such as the <a href="/wiki/Wasatch_Range" title="Wasatch Range">Wasatch</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_Utah" title="Salt Lake City, Utah">Salt Lake City</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bitterroot_Range" title="Bitterroot Range">Bitterroots</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>-Montana border. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbia_River_Plateau" title="Columbia River Plateau">Columbia River Plateau</a> separate these sub-ranges from distinct ranges further to the west, most prominent among which are the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coast_Mountains" title="Coast Mountains">Coast Mountains</a>. The Rockies do not extend into the Yukon or Alaska, or into central British Columbia, where the <b>Rocky Mountain System</b> (but not the Rocky Mountains) includes the Columbia Mountains, the southward extension of which is considered part of the Rockies in the United States. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_States_Rocky_Mountain_System" title="Geography of the United States Rocky Mountain System">Rocky Mountain System</a> within the United States is a <a href="/wiki/United_States_physiographic_region" title="United States physiographic region">United States physiographic region</a>; the Rocky Mountain System is known in Canada as the Eastern System.</p>
<p>The Rocky Mountains are notable for containing the highest peaks in central North America. The range's highest peak is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Elbert" title="Mount Elbert">Mount Elbert</a> located in Colorado at 14,440 feet (4,401 m) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Robson" title="Mount Robson">Mount Robson</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, at 12,972 feet (3,954 m), is the highest peak in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Rockies" title="Canadian Rockies">Canadian Rockies</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Continental_Divide_of_the_Americas" title="Continental Divide of the Americas">Continental Divide of the Americas</a> is located in the Rocky Mountains and designates the line at which waters flow either to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> Oceans. <a href="/wiki/Triple_Divide_Peak_(Montana)" title="Triple Divide Peak (Montana)">Triple Divide Peak</a> (8,020 feet (2,440 m)) in <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a> is so named because water that falls on the mountain reaches not only the Atlantic and Pacific, but <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> as well. Farther north in Alberta, the <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_River" title="Athabasca River">Athabasca</a> and other rivers feed the basin of the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a>, which has its outlet on the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Sea" title="Beaufort Sea">Beaufort Sea</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivers_of_the_Rocky_Mountains" title="Rivers of the Rocky Mountains">Rivers of the Rocky Mountains</a> for a list of rivers.</p>
<p>Human population is not very dense in the Rocky Mountains, with an average of four people per square kilometer and few cities with over 50,000 people. However, the human population grew rapidly in the Rocky Mountain states between 1950 and 1990. The 40-year statewide increases in population range from 35% in Montana to about 150% in Utah and Colorado. The populations of several mountain towns and communities have doubled in the last 40 years. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jackson_Hole,_Wyoming" title="Jackson Hole, Wyoming">Jackson Hole, Wyoming</a>, increased 260%, from 1,244 to 4,472 residents, in 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Rocky_Mountains" title="Geology of the Rocky Mountains">Geology of the Rocky Mountains</a></div>
<p>The rocks in the Rocky Mountains were formed before the mountains were raised by tectonic forces. The oldest rock is <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rock</a> that forms the core of the North American continent. There is also Precambrian sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Argillite" title="Argillite">argillite</a>, dating back to 1.7 billion years ago. During the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a>, western North America lay underneath a shallow sea, which deposited many kilometers of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gadd_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-gadd-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the southern Rocky Mountains, near present-day Colorado, these ancestral rocks were disturbed by mountain building approximately 300 Ma, during the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvanian_(geology)" title="Pennsylvanian (geology)">Pennsylvanian</a>. This mountain building produced the Ancestral Rocky Mountains. They consisted largely of Precambrian metamorphic rock forced upward through layers of the limestone laid down in the shallow sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The mountains eroded throughout the late Paleozoic and early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesozoic_Era" title="Mesozoic Era">Mesozoic</a>, leaving extensive deposits of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terranes" title="Terranes">Terranes</a> started to collide with the western edge of North America in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippian_age" title="Mississippian age">Mississippian</a> (approximately 350 million years ago), causing the <a href="/wiki/Antler_orogeny" title="Antler orogeny">Antler orogeny</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakely_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blakely-7">[7]</a></sup> For 270 million years, the effects of plate collisions were focused very near the edge of the North American plate boundary, far to the west of the Rocky Mountain region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakely_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blakely-7">[7]</a></sup> It was not until 80 Ma that these effects began to reach the Rockies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-English-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The current Rocky Mountains were raised in the <a href="/wiki/Laramide_orogeny" title="Laramide orogeny">Laramide orogeny</a> from between 80 and 55 Ma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-English-8">[8]</a></sup> For the Canadian Rockies, the mountain building is analogous to a rug being pushed on a hardwood floor:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gadd_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-gadd-5">[5]</a></sup> the rug bunches up and forms wrinkles (mountains). In Canada, the terranes and subduction are the foot pushing the rug, the ancestral rocks are the rug, and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">Canadian Shield</a> in the middle of the continent is the hardwood floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gadd_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-gadd-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further south, the growth of the Rocky Mountains in the United States was probably caused by an unusual subduction, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farallon_plate" title="Farallon plate">Farallon plate</a> dove at a shallow angle below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_plate" title="North American plate">North American plate</a>. This low angle moved the focus of melting and mountain building much farther inland than the normal 200 to 300 miles (300 to 500 km). It is postulated that the shallow angle of the subducting plate greatly increased the friction and other interactions with the thick continental mass above it. Tremendous <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrusts</a> piled sheets of crust on top of each other, building the extraordinarily broad, high Rocky Mountain range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The current southern Rockies were forced upwards through the layers of Pennsylvanian and <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> sedimentary remnants of the Ancestral Rocky Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Such sedimentary remnants were often tilted at steep angles along the flanks of the modern range; they are now visible in many places throughout the Rockies, and are prominently shown along the <a href="/wiki/Dakota_Hogback" title="Dakota Hogback">Dakota Hogback</a>, an early Cretaceous sandstone formation that runs along the eastern flank of the modern Rockies.</p>
<p>Immediately after the Laramide orogeny, the Rockies were like <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>: a high plateau, probably 6,000 metres (20,000 ft) above sea level. In the last 60 million years, <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> stripped away the high rocks, revealing the ancestral rocks beneath, and forming the current landscape of the Rockies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gadd_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-gadd-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Periods of glaciation occurred from the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> Epoch (1.8 million – 70,000 years ago) to the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> Epoch (fewer than 11,000 years ago). These ice ages left their mark on the Rockies, forming extensive <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> landforms, such as U-shaped valleys and <a href="/wiki/Cirque" title="Cirque">cirques</a>. Recent glacial episodes included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull_Lake_Glaciation" title="Bull Lake Glaciation">Bull Lake Glaciation</a> that began about 150,000 years ago and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinedale_Glaciation" title="Pinedale Glaciation">Pinedale Glaciation</a> that probably remained at full glaciation until 15,000–20,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pierce79_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pierce79-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>All of the geological processes, above, have left a complex set of rocks exposed at the surface. For example, volcanic rock from the <a href="/wiki/Paleogene" title="Paleogene">Paleogene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> periods (66 million – 2.6 million years ago) occurs in the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Mountains" title="San Juan Mountains">San Juan Mountains</a> and in other areas. Millennia of severe erosion in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wyoming_Basin" title="Wyoming Basin">Wyoming Basin</a> transformed intermountain basins into a relatively flat terrain. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetons" title="Tetons">Tetons</a> and other north-central ranges contain folded and faulted rocks of Paleozoic and <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> age draped above cores of <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> igneous and metamorphic rocks ranging in age from 1.2 billion (e.g., Tetons) to more than 3.3 billion years (Beartooth Mountains).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_climate">Ecology and climate</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecology_of_the_Rocky_Mountains" title="Ecology of the Rocky Mountains">Ecology of the Rocky Mountains</a></div>
<p>There are a wide range of environmental factors in the Rocky Mountains. The Rockies range in latitude between the Liard River in British Columbia (at 59° N) and the Rio Grande in New Mexico (at 35° N). Prairie occurs at or below 1,800 feet (550 m), while the highest peak in the range is Mount Elbert at 14,440 feet (4,400 m). Precipitation ranges from 10 inches (250 mm) per year in the southern valleys<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> to 60 inches (1,500 mm) per year locally in the northern peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Average January temperatures can range from 20 °F (−7 °C) in Prince George, British Columbia, to 43 °F (6 °C) in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad,_Colorado" title="Trinidad, Colorado">Trinidad, Colorado</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheridan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheridan-14">[14]</a></sup> Therefore, there is not a single monolithic ecosystem for the entire Rocky Mountain Range.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alpine_tundra_Copper_Mountain_Colorado.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tundra in the Rocky Mountains of Colorado</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Instead, ecologists divide the Rocky Mountain into a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_zones" title="Life zones">biotic zones</a>. Each zone is defined by whether it can support trees, and the presence of one or more <a href="/wiki/Indicator_species" title="Indicator species">indicator species</a>. Two zones that do not support trees are the Plains and the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">Alpine tundra</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> lie to the east of the Rockies, and is characterized by prairie grasses (below roughly 1,800 feet (550 m)). Alpine tundra occurs in regions above the treeline for the Rocky Mountains, which varies from 12,000 feet (3,700 m) in New Mexico to 2,500 feet (760 m) at the northern end of the Rocky Mountains (near the Yukon).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheridan_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sheridan-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The USGS defines ten forested zones in the Rocky Mountains. Zones in more southern, warmer, or drier areas are defined by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Pinyon_pine" title="Pinyon pine">pinyon pines</a>/<a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">junipers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ponderosa_pine" title="Ponderosa pine">ponderosa pines</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oaks</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>. In more northern, colder, or wetter areas, zones are defined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas-fir" title="Douglas-fir">Douglas-firs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascade_range" title="Cascade range">Cascadian</a> species (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_hemlock" title="Western hemlock">western hemlock</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lodgepole_pine" title="Lodgepole pine">lodgepole pines</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaking_aspen" title="Quaking aspen">quaking aspens</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">firs</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>. Near treeline, zones can consist of white pines (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitebark_pine" title="Whitebark pine">whitebark pine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bristlecone_pine" title="Bristlecone pine">bristlecone pine</a>); or a mixture of white pine, fir, and spruce that appear as shrub-like <a href="/wiki/Krummholz" title="Krummholz">krummholz</a>. Finally, rivers and canyons can create a unique forest zone in more arid parts of the mountain range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2430" data-file-width="3680" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg/220px-Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg/330px-Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg/440px-Bighorn_lamb_Alberta.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Bighorn_sheep" title="Bighorn sheep">Bighorn sheep</a> (such as this lamb in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>) have declined dramatically since European-American settlement of the Rocky Mountains.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Rocky Mountains are an important habitat for a great deal of well-known wildlife, such as <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">moose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mule_deer" title="Mule deer">mule</a> and <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_deer" title="White-tailed deer">white-tailed deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pronghorn" title="Pronghorn">pronghorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_goat" title="Mountain goat">mountain goats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bighorn_sheep" title="Bighorn sheep">bighorn sheep</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Badgers" title="Badgers">badgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wolverine" title="Wolverine">wolverines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> For example, North America's largest herds of moose is in the <a href="/wiki/Alberta-British_Columbia_foothills_forests" title="Alberta-British Columbia foothills forests">Alberta-British Columbia foothills forests</a>.</p>
<p>The status of most species in the Rocky Mountains is unknown, due to incomplete information. European-American settlement of the mountains has adversely impacted native species. Examples of some species that have declined include <a href="/wiki/Western_toad" title="Western toad">western toads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenback_cutthroat_trout" title="Greenback cutthroat trout">greenback cutthroat trout</a>, <a href="/wiki/White_sturgeon" title="White sturgeon">white sturgeon</a>, <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_ptarmigan" title="White-tailed ptarmigan">white-tailed ptarmigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trumpeter_swan" title="Trumpeter swan">trumpeter swan</a>, and bighorn sheep. In the United States portion of the mountain range, <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a> such as grizzly bears and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray_wolves" title="Gray wolves">gray wolves</a> had been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extirpated" title="Extirpated">extirpated</a> from their original ranges, but have partially recovered due to conservation measures and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf_reintroduction_in_Yellowstone" title="Wolf reintroduction in Yellowstone">reintroduction</a>. Other recovering species include the <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">bald eagle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_people">Indigenous people</span></h3>
<p>Since the last great ice age, the Rocky Mountains were home first to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Apache" title="Apache">Apache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arapaho" title="Arapaho">Arapaho</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bannock_(tribe)" title="Bannock (tribe)">Bannock</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackfoot" title="Blackfoot">Blackfoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne" title="Cheyenne">Cheyenne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coeur_d%27Alene_people" title="Coeur d'Alene people">Coeur d'Alene</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalispel" title="Kalispel">Kalispel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow_Nation" title="Crow Nation">Crow Nation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confederated_Salish_and_Kootenai_Tribes_of_the_Flathead_Nation" title="Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes of the Flathead Nation">Flathead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shoshone" title="Shoshone">Shoshone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Sioux</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ute_Tribe" title="Ute Tribe">Ute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ktunaxa_Kinbasket_Tribal_Council" title="Ktunaxa Kinbasket Tribal Council">Kutenai</a> (Ktunaxa in Canada), <a href="/wiki/Sekani" title="Sekani">Sekani</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunne-za" title="Dunne-za">Dunne-za</a>, and others. Paleo-Indians hunted the now-extinct <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_bison" title="Ancient bison">ancient bison</a> (an animal 20% larger than modern bison) in the foothills and valleys of the mountains. Like the modern tribes that followed them, Paleo-Indians probably migrated to the plains in fall and winter for bison and to the mountains in spring and summer for fish, deer, elk, roots, and berries. In Colorado, along the crest of the Continental Divide, rock walls that Native Americans built for driving game date back 5,400–5,800 years. A growing body of scientific evidence indicates that indigenous people had significant effects on mammal populations by hunting and on vegetation patterns through deliberate burning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_exploration">European exploration</span></h3>
<p>Recent human history of the Rocky Mountains is one of more rapid change. The Spanish explorer <a href="/wiki/Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado" title="Francisco Vázquez de Coronado">Francisco Vázquez de Coronado</a>—with a group of soldiers, missionaries, and African slaves—marched into the Rocky Mountain region from the south in 1540.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS2001_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS2001-16">[16]</a></sup> The introduction of the horse, metal tools, rifles, new diseases, and different cultures profoundly changed the Native American cultures. Native American populations were extirpated from most of their historical ranges by disease, warfare, habitat loss (eradication of the bison), and continued assaults on their culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1739, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur traders</a> Pierre and Paul Mallet, while journeying through the Great Plains, discovered a range of mountains at the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/Platte_River" title="Platte River">Platte River</a>, which local <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">American Indian</a> tribes called the "Rockies", becoming the first Europeans to report on this uncharted mountain range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_(c.1800).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="529" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_%28c.1800%29.jpg/170px-Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_%28c.1800%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_%28c.1800%29.jpg/255px-Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_%28c.1800%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_%28c.1800%29.jpg/340px-Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_%28c.1800%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_MacKenzie_by_Thomas_Lawrence_(c.1800).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sir Alexander MacKenzie in 1800</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mackenzie_(explorer)" title="Alexander Mackenzie (explorer)">Sir Alexander MacKenzie</a> (1764 – March 11, 1820) became the first European to cross the Rocky Mountains in 1793.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrincetonNWPass_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-PrincetonNWPass-18">[18]</a></sup> He found the upper reaches of the Fraser River and reached the Pacific coast of what is now Canada on July 20 of that year, completing the first recorded transcontinental crossing of North America north of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAHistPlace_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAHistPlace-19">[19]</a></sup> He arrived at <a href="/wiki/Bella_Coola,_British_Columbia" title="Bella Coola, British Columbia">Bella Coola, British Columbia</a>, where he first reached saltwater at South Bentinck Arm, an inlet of the Pacific Ocean.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark_Expedition" title="Lewis and Clark Expedition">Lewis and Clark Expedition</a> (1804–1806) was the first scientific reconnaissance of the Rocky Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPSScientificEncounters_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPSScientificEncounters-20">[20]</a></sup> Specimens were collected for contemporary botanists, zoologists, and geologists. The expedition was said to have paved the way to (and through) the Rocky Mountains for European-Americans from the East, although Lewis and Clark met at least 11 European-American mountain men during their travels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mountain men, primarily French, Spanish, and British, roamed the Rocky Mountains from 1720 to 1800 seeking mineral deposits and furs. The fur-trading <a href="/wiki/North_West_Company" title="North West Company">North West Company</a> established <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_House" title="Rocky Mountain House">Rocky Mountain House</a> as a trading post in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Foothills" title="Rocky Mountain Foothills">Rocky Mountain Foothills</a> of present-day Alberta in 1799, and their business rivals the <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">Hudson's Bay Company</a> established Acton House nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PC2012_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-PC2012-21">[21]</a></sup> These posts served as bases for most European activity in the Canadian Rockies in the early 19th century. Among the most notable are the expeditions of <a href="/wiki/David_Thompson_(explorer)" title="David Thompson (explorer)">David Thompson (explorer)</a>, who followed the Columbia River to the Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwda2006_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nwda2006-22">[22]</a></sup> On his 1811 expedition, he camped at the junction of the Columbia River and the Snake River and erected a pole and notice claiming the area for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and stating the intention of the North West Company to build a fort at the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oldham2003_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oldham2003-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-American_Convention_of_1818" title="Anglo-American Convention of 1818">Anglo-American Convention of 1818</a>, which established the <a href="/wiki/49th_parallel_north" title="49th parallel north">49th parallel north</a> as the international boundary west from <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_the_Woods" title="Lake of the Woods">Lake of the Woods</a> to the "Stony Mountains";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDS2007_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-USDS2007-24">[24]</a></sup> the UK and the USA agreed to what has since been described as "joint occupancy" of lands further west to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Resolution of the territorial and treaty issues, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oregon_dispute" title="Oregon dispute">Oregon dispute</a>, was deferred until a later time.</p>
<p>In 1819, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> ceded their rights north of the 42nd Parallel to the United States, though these rights did not include possession and also included obligations to Britain and Russia concerning their claims in the same region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement">Settlement</span></h3>
<p>After 1802, American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fur_trader" title="Fur trader">fur traders</a> and explorers ushered in the first widespread <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_race" title="Caucasian race">Caucasian</a> presence in the Rockies south of the 49th parallel. The more famous of these include Americans <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Ashley" title="William Henry Ashley">William Henry Ashley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bridger" title="Jim Bridger">Jim Bridger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kit_Carson" title="Kit Carson">Kit Carson</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Colter" title="John Colter">John Colter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fitzpatrick_(trapper)" title="Thomas Fitzpatrick (trapper)">Thomas Fitzpatrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Henry_(fur_trader)" title="Andrew Henry (fur trader)">Andrew Henry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jedediah_Smith" title="Jedediah Smith">Jedediah Smith</a>. On July 24, 1832, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Bonneville" title="Benjamin Bonneville">Benjamin Bonneville</a> led the first <a href="/wiki/Wagon_train" title="Wagon train">wagon train</a> across the Rocky Mountains by using <a href="/wiki/South_Pass_(Wyoming)" title="South Pass (Wyoming)">South Pass</a> in the present State of Wyoming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup> Similarly, in the wake of Mackenzie's 1793 expedition, fur trading posts were established west of the Northern Rockies in a region of the northern Interior Plateau of British Columbia which came to be known as <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia_(Canada)" title="New Caledonia (Canada)">New Caledonia</a>, beginning with <a href="/wiki/McLeod_Lake" title="McLeod Lake">Fort McLeod</a> (today's community of McLeod Lake) and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Fraser,_British_Columbia" title="Fort Fraser, British Columbia">Fort Fraser</a>, but ultimately focused on Stuart Lake Post (today's <a href="/wiki/Fort_St._James" title="Fort St. James">Fort St. James</a>).</p>
<p>Negotiations between the United Kingdom and the United States over the next few decades failed to settle upon a compromise boundary and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oregon_Dispute" title="Oregon Dispute">Oregon Dispute</a> became important in geopolitical diplomacy between the British Empire and the new American Republic. In 1841 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Sinclair_(fur_trapper)" title="James Sinclair (fur trapper)">James Sinclair</a>, Chief Factor of the Hudson's Bay Company, guided some 200 settlers from the <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Colony" title="Red River Colony">Red River Colony</a> west to bolster settlement around <a href="/wiki/Fort_Vancouver" title="Fort Vancouver">Fort Vancouver</a> in an attempt to retain the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_District" title="Columbia District">Columbia District</a> for Britain. The party crossed the Rockies into the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Valley" title="Columbia Valley">Columbia Valley</a>, a region of the Rocky Mountain Trench near present-day <a href="/wiki/Radium_Hot_Springs" title="Radium Hot Springs">Radium Hot Springs</a>, British Columbia, then traveled south. Despite such efforts, in 1846, Britain ceded all claim to Columbia District lands south of the 49th parallel to the United States; as resolution to the Oregon boundary dispute by the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Treaty" title="Oregon Treaty">Oregon Treaty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSPN_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSPN-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cherokee_Pass2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1869" data-file-width="2640" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Cherokee_Pass2.jpg/220px-Cherokee_Pass2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Cherokee_Pass2.jpg/330px-Cherokee_Pass2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Cherokee_Pass2.jpg/440px-Cherokee_Pass2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cherokee_Pass2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cherokee Trail near <a href="/wiki/Fort_Collins,_Colorado" title="Fort Collins, Colorado">Fort Collins, Colorado</a>, from a sketch taken 7 June 1859.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Thousands passed through the Rocky Mountains on the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Trail" title="Oregon Trail">Oregon Trail</a> beginning in the 1840s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMotic_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMotic-26">[26]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mormon" title="Mormon">Mormons</a> began to settle near the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a> in 1847.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNL_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNL-27">[27]</a></sup> From 1859 to 1864, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> was discovered in Colorado, Idaho, Montana, and British Columbia, sparking several <a href="/wiki/Gold_rush" title="Gold rush">gold rushes</a> bringing thousands of prospectors and miners to explore every mountain and canyon and to create the Rocky Mountains' first major industry. The Idaho gold rush alone produced more gold than the California and Alaska gold rushes combined and was important in the financing of the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Transcontinental_railroad" title="Transcontinental railroad">transcontinental railroad</a> was completed in 1869,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UC2012_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-UC2012-28">[28]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a> was established as the world's first national park in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPS_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-29">[29]</a></sup> Meanwhile, a transcontinental railroad in Canada was originally promised in 1871. Though political complications pushed its completion to 1885, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific Railway</a> eventually followed the <a href="/wiki/Kicking_Horse_Pass" title="Kicking Horse Pass">Kicking Horse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Pass_(British_Columbia)" title="Rogers Pass (British Columbia)">Rogers</a> Passes to the Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StanfordCPR_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-StanfordCPR-30">[30]</a></sup> Canadian railway officials also convinced <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Canada" title="Parliament of Canada">Parliament</a> to set aside vast areas of the Canadian Rockies as <a href="/wiki/Jasper_National_Park" title="Jasper National Park">Jasper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banff_National_Park" title="Banff National Park">Banff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yoho_National_Park" title="Yoho National Park">Yoho</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waterton_Lakes_National_Park" title="Waterton Lakes National Park">Waterton Lakes</a> National Parks, laying the foundation for a tourism industry which thrives to this day. Glacier National Park (MT) was established with a similar relationship to tourism promotions by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_Railway_(U.S.)" title="Great Northern Railway (U.S.)">Great Northern Railway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PennState2011_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-PennState2011-31">[31]</a></sup> While settlers filled the valleys and mining towns, conservation and preservation ethics began to take hold. U.S. <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">President Harrison</a> established several forest reserves in the Rocky Mountains in 1891–92. In 1905, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> extended the <a href="/wiki/Medicine_Bow_%E2%80%93_Routt_National_Forest" title="Medicine Bow – Routt National Forest">Medicine Bow Forest Reserve</a> to include the area now managed as <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_National_Park" title="Rocky Mountain National Park">Rocky Mountain National Park</a>. Economic development began to center on <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>, as well as on the service industries that support them. Tents and camps became ranches and farms, forts and train stations became towns, and some towns became cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_and_development">Industry and development</span></h3>
<p>Economic resources of the Rocky Mountains are varied and abundant. <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">Minerals</a> found in the Rocky Mountains include significant deposits of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, gold, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>. The Wyoming Basin and several smaller areas contain significant reserves of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Climax,_Colorado" title="Climax, Colorado">Climax</a> mine, located near <a href="/wiki/Leadville,_Colorado" title="Leadville, Colorado">Leadville</a>, Colorado, was the largest producer of molybdenum in the world. Molybdenum is used in heat-resistant steel in such things as cars and planes. The Climax mine employed over 3,000 workers. The <a href="/wiki/Coeur_d%27Alene,_Idaho" title="Coeur d'Alene, Idaho">Coeur d'Alene</a> mine of northern Idaho produces silver, lead, and zinc. Canada's largest coal mines are near <a href="/wiki/Fernie,_British_Columbia" title="Fernie, British Columbia">Fernie, British Columbia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sparwood,_British_Columbia" title="Sparwood, British Columbia">Sparwood, British Columbia</a>; additional coal mines exist near <a href="/wiki/Hinton,_Alberta" title="Hinton, Alberta">Hinton</a>, Alberta, and in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Rocky_Mountains" title="Northern Rocky Mountains">Northern Rockies</a> surrounding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumbler_Ridge,_British_Columbia" title="Tumbler Ridge, British Columbia">Tumbler Ridge, British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abandoned mines with their wakes of mine tailings and toxic wastes dot the Rocky Mountain landscape. In one major example, eighty years of zinc mining profoundly polluted the river and bank near <a href="/wiki/Eagle_River_(Colorado)" title="Eagle River (Colorado)">Eagle River</a> in north-central Colorado. High concentrations of the metal carried by spring runoff harmed <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> populations. An economic analysis of mining effects at this site revealed declining property values, degraded water quality, and the loss of recreational opportunities. The analysis also revealed that cleanup of the river could yield $2.3 million in additional revenue from recreation. In 1983, the former owner of the zinc mine was sued by the Colorado Attorney General for the $4.8 million cleanup costs; five years later, ecological recovery was considerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandt93_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandt93-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Rocky Mountains contain several <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basins</a> that are rich in <a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane" title="Coalbed methane">coalbed methane</a>. Coalbed methane is natural gas that arises from coal, either through bacterial action, or through exposure to high temperature. Coalbed methane supplies 7 percent of the natural gas used in the United States. The largest coalbed methane sources in the Rocky Mountains are in the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Basin" title="San Juan Basin">San Juan Basin</a> in New Mexico and Colorado and the <a href="/wiki/Powder_River_Basin" title="Powder River Basin">Powder River Basin</a> in Wyoming. These two basins are estimated to contain 38 trillion cubic feet of gas. Coalbed methane can be recovered by dewatering the coal bed, and separating the gas from the water; or injecting water to fracture the coal to release the gas (so-called <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agriculture and forestry are major industries. Agriculture includes dryland and irrigated farming and <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> grazing. Livestock are frequently moved between high-elevation summer <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastures</a> and low-elevation winter pastures, a practice known as <a href="/wiki/Transhumance" title="Transhumance">transhumance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>See also:</i> <a href="/wiki/List_of_ski_areas_and_resorts_in_the_United_States#Rocky_Mountains" title="List of ski areas and resorts in the United States">List of U.S. Rocky Mountain ski resorts</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_ski_areas_and_resorts_in_Canada#Alberta" title="List of ski areas and resorts in Canada">List of Alberta ski resorts</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_ski_areas_and_resorts_in_Canada#British_Columbia" title="List of ski areas and resorts in Canada">List of B.C. ski resorts</a></p>
<p>Every year the scenic areas and recreational opportunities of the Rocky Mountains draw millions of tourists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-4">[4]</a></sup> The main language of the Rocky Mountains is <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>. But there are also linguistic pockets of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and indigenous languages.</p>
<p>People from all over the world visit the sites to hike, camp, or engage in mountain sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In the summer season, examples of tourist attractions are:</p>
<p>In the United States:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pikes_Peak" title="Pikes Peak">Pikes Peak</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Royal_Gorge" title="Royal Gorge">Royal Gorge</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cumbres_and_Toltec_Scenic_Railroad" title="Cumbres and Toltec Scenic Railroad">Cumbres and Toltec Scenic Railroad</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_National_Park" title="Rocky Mountain National Park">Rocky Mountain National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grand_Teton_National_Park" title="Grand Teton National Park">Grand Teton National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sawtooth_National_Recreation_Area" title="Sawtooth National Recreation Area">Sawtooth National Recreation Area</a></li>
</ul>
<p>In Canada, the mountain range contains these <a href="/wiki/National_Parks_of_Canada" title="National Parks of Canada">national parks</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Banff_National_Park" title="Banff National Park">Banff National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jasper_National_Park" title="Jasper National Park">Jasper National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kootenay_National_Park" title="Kootenay National Park">Kootenay National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Waterton_Lakes_National_Park" title="Waterton Lakes National Park">Waterton Lakes National Park</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yoho_National_Park" title="Yoho National Park">Yoho National Park</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Glacier National Park in Montana and Waterton Lakes National Park in Alberta border each other and collectively are known as <a href="/wiki/Waterton-Glacier_International_Peace_Park" title="Waterton-Glacier International Peace Park">Waterton-Glacier International Peace Park</a>. (See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Peace_Park" title="International Peace Park">International Peace Park</a>.)</p>
<p>In the winter, <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a> is the main attraction. A list of the major ski resorts can be found at <a href="/wiki/List_of_ski_areas_and_resorts_in_the_United_States#Rocky_Mountains" title="List of ski areas and resorts in the United States">List of U.S. Rocky Mountain ski resorts</a>.</p>
<p>The adjacent Columbia Mountains in British Columbia contain major resorts such as <a href="/wiki/Panorama_Mountain_Village" title="Panorama Mountain Village">Panorama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kicking_Horse_Resort" title="Kicking Horse Resort">Kicking Horse</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Revelstoke_National_Park" title="Mount Revelstoke National Park">Mount Revelstoke National Park</a> and Glacier National Park.</p>
<p>There are numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_provincial_parks_in_British_Columbia" title="List of provincial parks in British Columbia">provincial parks</a> in the British Columbia Rockies, the largest and most notable being <a href="/wiki/Mount_Assiniboine_Provincial_Park" title="Mount Assiniboine Provincial Park">Mount Assiniboine Provincial Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Robson_Provincial_Park" title="Mount Robson Provincial Park">Mount Robson Provincial Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Rocky_Mountains_Provincial_Park" title="Northern Rocky Mountains Provincial Park">Northern Rocky Mountains Provincial Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kwadacha_Wilderness_Provincial_Park" title="Kwadacha Wilderness Provincial Park">Kwadacha Wilderness Provincial Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stone_Mountain_Provincial_Park" title="Stone Mountain Provincial Park">Stone Mountain Provincial Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muncho_Lake_Provincial_Park" title="Muncho Lake Provincial Park">Muncho Lake Provincial Park</a>.</p>
