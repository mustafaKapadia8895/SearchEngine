<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">North American Monsoon</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>North American monsoon</b>, variously known as the <b>Southwest <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a></b>, the <b>Mexican monsoon</b>, the <b>New Mexican monsoon</b>, or the <b>Arizona monsoon</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams-1">[1]</a></sup> is a pattern of pronounced increase in thunderstorms and rainfall over large areas of the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">southwestern United States</a> and northwestern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, typically occurring between July and mid September. During the monsoon, <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> are fueled by daytime heating and build up during the late afternoon-early evening. Typically, these storms dissipate by late night, and the next day starts out fair, with the cycle repeating daily. The monsoon typically loses its energy by mid-September when drier and cooler conditions are reestablished over the region. Geographically, the North American monsoon precipitation region is centered over the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">Sierra Madre Occidental</a> in the Mexican states of <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa" title="Sinaloa">Sinaloa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">Durango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chihuahua_(state)" title="Chihuahua (state)">Chihuahua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The North American Monsoon is not as strong or persistent as its <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">Indian counterpart</a>, mainly because the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Plateau" title="Mexican Plateau">Mexican Plateau</a> is not as high or as large as the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> in Asia. However, the North American Monsoon shares most of the basic characteristics of its Indian counterpart. There is a shift in wind patterns in summer which occurs as Mexico and the southwest U.S. warm under intense solar heating. As this happens, the flow reverses. The prevailing winds start to flow from moist ocean areas into dry land areas.</p>
<p>The North American monsoon is associated with an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">high pressure</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> that moves northward during the summer months and a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">thermal low</a> (a trough of low pressure which develops from intense surface heating) over the Mexican Plateau and the Desert Southwest of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The monsoon begins in late May to early June in southern Mexico and quickly spreads along the western slopes of the Sierra Madre Occidental, reaching <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> in early July. The monsoon extends into the southwest United States as it matures in mid-July, when an area of high pressure, called the monsoon or subtropical ridge, develops in the upper atmosphere over the <a href="/wiki/Four_Corners" title="Four Corners">Four Corners</a> region, creating wind flow aloft from the east or southeast.</p>
<p>Pulses of low level moisture are transported primarily from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>. The Gulf of California, a narrow body of water surrounded by mountains, is particularly important for low-level moisture transport into Arizona and Sonora. Upper level moisture is also transported into the region, mainly from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> by easterly winds aloft. Once the forests of the Sierra Madre Occidental green up from the initial monsoon rains, evaporation and <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">plant transpiration</a> can add additional moisture to the atmosphere which will then flow into Arizona. Finally, if the southern Plains of the U.S. are unusually wet and green during the early summer months, that area can also serve as a moisture source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As precipitable water values rise in early summer, brief but often torrential thunderstorms can occur, especially over mountainous terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This activity is occasionally enhanced by the passage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_waves" title="Tropical waves">tropical waves</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(meteorology)" title="Entrainment (meteorology)">entrainment</a> of the remnants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Monsoons play a vital role in managing <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> threat by providing moisture at higher elevations and feeding desert streams. Heavy monsoon rain can lead to excess winter plant growth, in turn a summer wildfire risk. A lack of monsoon rain can hamper summer seeding, reducing excess winter plant growth but worsening drought.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">Flash flooding</a> is a serious danger during the monsoon. Dry washes can become raging rivers in an instant, even when no storms are visible as a storm can cause a flash flood tens of miles away; it is therefore wise to avoid camping in a dry wash during the monsoon. Lightning strikes are also a significant danger. Because it is dangerous to be caught in the open when these storms suddenly appear, many golf courses in Arizona have thunderstorm warning systems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patterns_of_rainfall">Patterns of rainfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Monsoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Patterns of rainfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Rainfall during the monsoon is not continuous. It varies considerably, depending on a variety of factors. There are usually distinct "burst" periods of heavy rain during the monsoon, and "break" periods with little or no rain. Monsoon precipitation, however, accounts for a substantial portion of annual precipitation in northwest Mexico and the Southwest U.S. Most of these areas receive over half their annual precipitation from the monsoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The North American Monsoon circulation pattern typically develops in late May or early June over southwest Mexico. By mid to late summer, <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> increase over the "core" region of the southwest U.S. and northwest Mexico, including the U.S. and Mexican states of Arizona, New Mexico, Sonora, Chihuahua, Sinaloa and Durango.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-4">[4]</a></sup> The monsoon typically arrives in mid to late June over northwest Mexico, and early July over the southwest U.S. Once the monsoon is underway, mountain ranges, including the Sierra Madre Occidental and the <a href="/wiki/Mogollon_Rim" title="Mogollon Rim">Mogollon Rim</a> provide a focusing mechanism for the daily development of thunderstorms. Thus much of the monsoon rainfall occurs in mountainous terrain. For example, monsoon rainfall in the Sierra Madre Occidental typically ranges from 10 to 15 inches. Since the southwest U.S. is at the northern fringe of the monsoon, precipitation is less and tends to be more variable. Areas further west of the core monsoon region, namely California and Baja California, typically receive only spotty monsoon-related rainfall. In those areas, the intense solar heating is not strong enough to overcome a <a href="/wiki/California_Current" title="California Current">continual supply of cold water from the North Pacific Ocean moving down the west coast of North America</a>. Winds do turn toward the land in these areas, but the cool moist air actually stabilizes the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-4">[4]</a></sup> The monsoon pushes as far west as the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Ranges" title="Peninsular Ranges">Peninsular Ranges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Ranges" title="Transverse Ranges">Transverse Ranges</a> of southern California, but rarely reaches the coastal strip. As shown in the panorama below, a wall of thunderstorms, only a half-hour's drive away, is a common sight from the sunny skies along the coast during the monsoon.</p>

<p>This monsoon ridge is almost as strong as the one which develops over Asia during the summer. However, since the lower level moisture flow is not as persistent as in the Indian monsoon, the upper level steering pattern and disturbances around the ridge are critical for influencing where thunderstorms develop on any given day. The exact strength and position of the subtropical ridge also governs how far north the tropical easterly winds aloft can spread. If the ridge is too close to a particular area, the sinking air at its center suppresses thunderstorms and can result in a significant monsoon "break." If the ridge is too far away or too weak, the east winds around the high are inadequate to bring tropical moisture into the mountains of Mexico and southwest U.S. However, if the ridge sets up in a few key locations, widespread and potentially severe thunderstorms can develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variability">Variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Monsoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike the Pacific region of the United States and the west coast of Baja California, precipitation in the NA monsoon is not associated with large-scale mid-latitude <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a>, but with <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> which have very different spatial/temporal distribution characteristics. The difficulty in understanding the variability of summertime convective activity in the southwestern U.S. and northwestern Mexico results from the complex interactions between atmospheric circulation features at both the <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> (100 to 1000 km spatially, 1 day to 1 week, temporally) and <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> (several km to 100 km, hours to one day temporally) and the extremely varied topography. The larger-scale atmospheric motions may control the distribution of water vapor and the general stability or instability (that is, the tendency to form storms) in the atmosphere; nevertheless, local topographic effects are critical to the geographic and even temporal distribution of convective activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
