<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Benue Trough</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Benue Trough</b> is a major geological formation underlying a large part of <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and extending about 1,000Â km northeast from the <a href="/wiki/Bight_of_Benin" title="Bight of Benin">Bight of Benin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Chad" title="Lake Chad">Lake Chad</a>. It is part of the broader Central African Rift System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineNigeria_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineNigeria-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benue_Trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Benue_Trough.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="358" data-file-width="327" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Benue_Trough.svg/300px-Benue_Trough.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Benue_Trough.svg/450px-Benue_Trough.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Benue_Trough.svg/600px-Benue_Trough.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Benue_Trough.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sketch map of Benue Trough</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The trough has its southern limit at the northern boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Niger_Delta" title="Niger Delta">Niger Delta</a>, where it dips down and is overlaid with <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> and more recent sediments. It extends in a northeasterly direction to the <a href="/wiki/Chad_Basin" title="Chad Basin">Chad Basin</a>, and is about 150Â km wide. The trough is arbitrarily divided into lower, middle and upper regions, and the upper region is further divided into the Gongola and Yola arms. The <a href="/wiki/Anambra_Basin" title="Anambra Basin">Anambra Basin</a> in the west of the lower region is more recent than the rest of the trough, being formed during a later period of compression, but is considered part of the formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-George2009-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rifting_and_sedimentation">Rifting and sedimentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benue_Trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rifting and sedimentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Benue Trough was formed by rifting of the central West African basement, beginning at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[fn 1]</a></sup> At first, the trough accumulated sediments deposited by rivers and lakes. During the Late Early to Middle Cretaceous, the basin subsided rapidly and was covered by the sea. Sea floor sediment accumulated, especially in the southern <a href="/wiki/Abakaliki" title="Abakaliki">Abakaliki</a> Rift, under oxygen-deficient bottom conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineNigeria_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineNigeria-1">[1]</a></sup> In the Upper Cretaceous, the Benue Trough probably formed the main link between the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Ocean</a> (predecessor of the Mediterranean Sea) via the <a href="/wiki/Chad_Basin" title="Chad Basin">Chad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iullemmeden_Basin" title="Iullemmeden Basin">Iullemmeden Basins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright1985_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wright1985-4">[3]</a></sup> Towards the end of this period the basin rose above sea level, and extensive coal forming swamps developed, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Anambra_Basin" title="Anambra Basin">Anambra Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineNigeria_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineNigeria-1">[1]</a></sup> The trough is estimated to contain 5,000Â m of Cretaceous sediments and volcanic rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adighije1979_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adighije1979-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common explanation of the trough's formation is that it is an <a href="/wiki/Aulacogen" title="Aulacogen">aulacogen</a>, an abandoned arm of a three-armed radial rift system. The other two arms continued to spread during the break-up of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>, as South America separated from Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petters1978_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Petters1978-6">[5]</a></sup> The two continents seem to have started to split apart at what are now their southern tips, with the rift extending up the modern coastlines to the Benue Trough, then later split along what is now the southern coast of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> and the north eastern coast of South America. As the continents were wedged apart, the trough opened up. When separation was complete, the southern part of Africa swung back to some extent, with the sediments in the Benue Trough compressed and folded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright1968_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wright1968-7">[6]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Santonian" title="Santonian">Santonian</a> age, around 84 million years ago, the basin underwent intense compression and folding, forming over 100 <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">synclines</a>. The deposits in the Benue Trough were displaced westwards at this time, causing subsidence of the <a href="/wiki/Anambra_Basin" title="Anambra Basin">Anambra Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-George2009-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mantle_plume_theory">Mantle plume theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benue_Trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mantle plume theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Benue_Trough_faults.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="411" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Benue_Trough_faults.svg/300px-Benue_Trough_faults.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Benue_Trough_faults.svg/450px-Benue_Trough_faults.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Benue_Trough_faults.svg/600px-Benue_Trough_faults.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Benue_Trough_faults.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Benue Trough and related Atlantic fracture zones</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A refinement to the model involves the rise of a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a>, where abnormal heat leads to melting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_mantle" title="Upper mantle">upper mantle</a>, thinning and stretching the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, followed by rifting of the weakened crust. This may have been repeated several times, with the Benue Trough deformed between rifting episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ofoegbu1984_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ofoegbu1984-8">[7]</a></sup> The same plume may be responsible for the line of volcanoes in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Shear_Zone" title="Central African Shear Zone">Central African Shear Zone</a> and for the volcanic island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Helena" title="St. Helena">St. Helena</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulon1996_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coulon1996-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magmatic" title="Magmatic">magmatic</a> activity (volcanic action) have been identified, 147â106 Ma, 97â81 Ma and 68â49 Ma. The first is prominent in the north of the trough, and contemporary with magmatism in Brazil, probably occurring during a period of <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">crustal extension</a> before the Atlantic started to open. The second is found only in the south of the trough, and may belong to a period when the extension of the Atlantic had slowed down, ending with a period of compression. The third and last period is also found only in the south of the trough, and may be related to an <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> response to the earlier crustal thinning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maluski1995_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maluski1995-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mantle plume activity was probably limited in its effect, with most of the basins in the trough being created from a combination of extension and strike-slip faults. The faults extend into the ocean with the Chain and Charcot fault zones, and have their counterparts in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robert2009-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_importance">Economic importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benue_Trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> is rich in coal deposits derived from terrestrial organic matter, most of which lie in the Benue Trough. These are mined in <a href="/wiki/Enugu_State" title="Enugu State">Enugu State</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jauro_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jauro-12">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Enugu" title="Enugu">Enugu</a> deposits formed in brackish marshes during the Late <a href="/wiki/Campanian" title="Campanian">Campanian</a> - early <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> ages, around 70 Ma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George2009_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-George2009-2">[2]</a></sup> During this time, the <a href="/wiki/Anambra_Basin" title="Anambra Basin">Anambra Basin</a> became silted up with thick vegetation growing in low-lying marshes on a broad delta fan deposited by rivers from the interior. Later, the deep layer of vegetation became buried under coarse sands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright1985_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wright1985-4">[3]</a></sup> Coal is potentially a source for oil and natural gas. An exploratory well drilled in the Gongola Basin of the upper region in 2003 found no oil, although there was a narrow layer of coal between 4,710Â ft and 4,770Â ft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obaje2004_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obaje2004-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
