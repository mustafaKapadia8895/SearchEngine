<p><i><b>Nephrops norvegicus</b></i>, known variously as the <b>Norway lobster</b>, <b>Dublin Bay prawn</b>, <i><b><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">langoustine</span></b></i> (compare <a href="/wiki/Langostino" title="Langostino">langostino</a>) or <i><b><a href="/wiki/Scampi" title="Scampi">scampi</a></b></i>, is a slim, orange-pink <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a> which grows up to 25 cm (10 in) long, and is "the most important commercial crustacean in Europe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> It is now the only extant species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nephrops" title="Nephrops">Nephrops</a></i>, after several other species were moved to the closely related genus <i><a href="/wiki/Metanephrops" title="Metanephrops">Metanephrops</a></i>. It lives in the north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, and parts of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, but is absent from the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. Adults emerge from their <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a> at night to feed on <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> has the typical body shape of a <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a>, albeit narrower than the large <i><a href="/wiki/Homarus" title="Homarus">Homarus</a></i> genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> It is pale orange in colour, and grows to a typical length of 18–20 centimetres (7–8 in), or exceptionally 25 cm (10 in) long, including the tail and <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">claws</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marlin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marlin-4">[4]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> covers the animal's <a href="/wiki/Cephalothorax" title="Cephalothorax">cephalothorax</a>, while the abdomen is long and segmented, ending in a broad tail fan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marlin_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marlin-4">[4]</a></sup> The first three pairs of legs bear claws, of which the first are greatly elongated and bear ridges of <a href="/wiki/Spine_(zoology)" title="Spine (zoology)">spines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marlin_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marlin-4">[4]</a></sup> Of the two pairs of <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a>, the second is the longer and thinner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marlin_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Marlin-4">[4]</a></sup> There is a long, spinous <a href="/wiki/Rostrum_(anatomy)" title="Rostrum (anatomy)">rostrum</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_eye" title="Compound eye">compound eyes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>-shaped, providing the name of the genus, from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">roots</a> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BD%CE%B5%CF%86%CF%81%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:νεφρός">νεφρός</a></span> (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nephros" title="wikt:nephros">nephros</a></i>, "kidney") and <i>ops</i> ("eye").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> is found in the north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> as far north as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and south to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. It is not common in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> except in the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> notably the north Adriatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is absent from both the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to its ecological demands for particular sediments, <i>N. norvegicus</i> has a very patchy distribution, and is divided into over 30 populations. These populations are separated by inhospitable terrain, and adults rarely travel distances greater than a few hundred metres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> adults prefer to inhabit muddy seabed sediments, with more than 40 percent silt and clay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> Their <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a> are semi-permanent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and vary in structure and size. Typical burrows are 20 to 30 centimetres (8 to 12 in) deep, with a distance of 50 to 80 centimetres (20 to 31 in) between the front and back entrances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> Norway lobsters spend most of their time either lying in their burrows or by the entrance, only leaving their shelters to forage or mate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenger</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predator</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> that makes short foraging excursions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> mainly during periods of subdued light. They feed on active prey, including <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which they capture with their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheliped" title="Cheliped">chelipeds</a> and walking legs, and food is conveyed to the mouth using the anterior walking legs, assisted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxilliped" title="Maxilliped">maxillipeds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites_and_symbionts">Parasites and symbionts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parasites and symbionts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a> to a number of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbionts</a>. A number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessility_(zoology)" title="Sessility (zoology)">sessile</a> organisms attach to the <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> of <i>N. norvegicus</i>, including the <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacle</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Balanus_crenatus" title="Balanus crenatus">Balanus crenatus</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminiferan</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclogyra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cyclogyra (page does not exist)">Cyclogyra</a></i>, but overall <i>Nephrops</i> suffers fewer infestations of such <a href="/wiki/Epibiont" title="Epibiont">epibionts</a> than other decapod crustaceans do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stentiford_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stentiford-12">[12]</a></sup> In December 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Commensalism" title="Commensalism">commensal</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Symbion_pandora" title="Symbion pandora">Symbion pandora</a></i> was discovered attached to the <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_mouthparts" title="Arthropod mouthparts">mouthparts</a> of <i>Nephrops norvegicus</i>, and was found to be the first member of a new <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symbion" title="Symbion">Cycliophora</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a finding described by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Conway_Morris" title="Simon Conway Morris">Simon Conway Morris</a> as "the zoological highlight of the decade".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>S. pandora</i> has been found in many populations of <i>N. norvegicus</i>, both in the north Atlantic and in the Mediterranean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Individuals may be found on most segments of the lobster's mouthparts, but are generally concentrated on the central parts of the larger mouthparts, from the <a href="/wiki/Mandible_(arthropod_mouthpart)" title="Mandible (arthropod mouthpart)">mandible</a> to the third <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxilliped" title="Maxilliped">maxilliped</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most significant parasite of <i>N. norvegicus</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellate</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Hematodinium" title="Hematodinium">Hematodinium</a></i>, which has caused <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a> infection in fished populations of <i>N. norvegicus</i> since the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stentiford_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stentiford-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Hematodinium</i> is a genus that contains major pathogens of a wide variety of decapod crustaceans, although its internal taxonomy is poorly resolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stentiford_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stentiford-12">[12]</a></sup> The species which attacks <i>N. norvegicus</i> causes a <a href="/wiki/Syndrome" title="Syndrome">syndrome</a> originally described as "post-moult syndrome", in which the <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> turns opaque and becomes highly pigmented, the <a href="/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">haemolymph</a> becomes milky white, and the animal appears <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/moribund" title="wikt:moribund">moribund</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stentiford_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stentiford-12">[12]</a></sup> Other parasites of <i>N. norvegicus</i> include the <a href="/wiki/Gregarinasina" title="Gregarinasina">gregarine</a> <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoan</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Porospora_nephropis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Porospora nephropis (page does not exist)">Porospora nephropis</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Trematoda" title="Trematoda">trematode</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Stichocotyle" title="Stichocotyle">Stichocotyle nephropis</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaete</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Histriobdella_homari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Histriobdella homari (page does not exist)">Histriobdella homari</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stentiford_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stentiford-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1623" data-file-width="2611" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG/220px-Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG/330px-Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG/440px-Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nephrops_norvegicus_with_roe.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Adult female <i>N. norvegicus</i> with eggs</div>

<p>The typical <a href="/wiki/Longevity" title="Longevity">life span</a> of <i>N. norvegicus</i> is 5–10 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> reaching 15 years in exceptional cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reproductive_cycle" title="Reproductive cycle">reproductive cycle</a> varies depending on geographical position: "the periods of hatching and spawning, and the length of the incubation period, vary with latitude and the breeding cycle changes from annual to biennial as one moves from south to north".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> Incubation of eggs is <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>-dependent, and in colder climates, the duration of the incubation period increases. This means that, by the time hatching occurs, it may be too late for the females to take part in that year's breeding cycle. In warmer climates, the combined effects of recovery from <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moulting</a> and ovary maturation mean that spawning can become delayed. This, in turn, has the effect of the female missing out a year of egg carrying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adult male <i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moult</a> once or twice a year (usually in late winter or spring) and adult females moult up to once a year (in late winter or spring, after hatching of the eggs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup> In annual breeding cycles, mating takes place in the spring or winter, when the females are in the soft, post-moult state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The ovaries mature throughout the spring and summer months, and egg-laying takes place in late summer or early autumn. After spawning, the berried (egg-carrying) females return to their burrows and remain there until the end of the incubation period. Hatching takes place in late winter or early spring. Soon after hatching, the females moult and mate again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the planktonic larval stage (typically 1 to 2 months in duration) the nephrops larvae exhibit a diel vertical migration behaviour as they are dispersed by the local currents. This complex biophysical interaction determines the fate of the larvae; the overlap between advective pathway destination and spatial distributions of suitable benthic habitats must be favourable in order for the larvae to settle and reach maturity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg/220px-Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg/330px-Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg/440px-Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Norway_Lobster_Scotland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Freshly caught <i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a></div>

<p>The muscular tail of <i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> is frequently eaten, and its meat is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Scampi" title="Scampi">scampi</a></i>. The <i>N. norvegicus</i> is eaten only on special occasions in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and Portugal, where it is less expensive than the common lobster, <i><a href="/wiki/Homarus_gammarus" title="Homarus gammarus">Homarus gammarus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>N. norvegicus</i> is an important species for <a href="/wiki/Lobster_fishing" title="Lobster fishing">fisheries</a>, being caught mostly by <a href="/wiki/Trawling" title="Trawling">trawling</a>. Around 60,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> are caught annually, half of it in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">waters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides the established trawling fleets, a significant number of fleets using <a href="/wiki/Creel_(basket)" title="Creel (basket)">lobster creels</a> have developed. The better size and condition of lobsters caught by this method yield prices three to four times higher than animals netted by trawling. Creel fishing was found to have a reduced impact on the seafloor, require lower fuel consumption, and allow fishermen with smaller boats to participate in this high-value fishery. It has therefore been described as a reasonable alternative to demersal towed gears, and the allocation of additional fishing rights for this type of take has been suggested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The North East Atlantic individual biological stocks of Nephrops are identified as functional units. A number of functional units make up the sea areas over which a total allowable catch (TAC) is set annually by the EU Council of Ministers. For example, the TAC set for North Sea Nephrops is based on the aggregate total tonnage of removals recommended by science for nine separate functional unit areas. This method has attracted criticism because it can promote the over-exploitation of a specific functional unit even though the overall TAC is under-fished. In 2016, the UK implemented a package of emergency technical measures aimed at recovering the Nephrop stock in the Farn(e) Deeps off North East England which is close to collapse. A 2017 assessment performed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_the_Exploration_of_the_Sea" title="International Council for the Exploration of the Sea">International Council for the Exploration of the Sea</a> (ICES) indicates the beginning of biomass expansion, but that controls on fishing effort will need to continue at least until the biomass reaches a size that is sustainable when measured against the level of fishing activity by all fishers wanting to target the stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Discards from <i>Nephrops</i> fishery may account for up to 37% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_energetics" title="Ecological energetics">energy requirements</a> of certain marine <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hagfish" title="Hagfish">hagfish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Myxine_glutinosa" title="Myxine glutinosa">Myxine glutinosa</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Boats involved in <i>Nephrops</i> fishery also catch a number of fish species such as <a href="/wiki/European_plaice" title="European plaice">plaice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_sole" title="Common sole">sole</a>, and it is thought that without that revenue, <i>Nephrops</i> fishery would be economically unviable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> was one of the species included by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></span></i>, the starting point for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoological_nomenclature" title="Zoological nomenclature">zoological nomenclature</a>. In that work, it was listed as <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Cancer Norvegicus</span></i>, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> of <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">in Mari Norvegico</span></i> ("in the Norwegian sea").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In choosing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lectotype" title="Lectotype">lectotype</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipke_Holthuis" title="Lipke Holthuis">Lipke Holthuis</a> restricted the type locality to the <a href="/wiki/Kattegat" title="Kattegat">Kattegat</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Kullaberg" title="Kullaberg">Kullen Peninsula</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Nephrops_norvegicus&amp;params=56_18_N_12_28_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">56°18′N</span> <span class="longitude">12°28′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">56.300°N 12.467°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">56.300; 12.467</span></span></span></a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLW_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MLW-2">[2]</a></sup> Two <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonyms</a> of the species have been published<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLW_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-MLW-2">[2]</a></sup> – "<i>Astacus rugosus</i>", described by the eccentric zoologist <a href="/wiki/Constantine_Samuel_Rafinesque" title="Constantine Samuel Rafinesque">Constantine Samuel Rafinesque</a> in 1814 from material collected in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and "<i>Nephropsis cornubiensis</i>", described by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Spence_Bate" title="Charles Spence Bate">Charles Spence Bate</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Brooking_Rowe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joshua Brooking Rowe (page does not exist)">Joshua Brooking Rowe</a> in 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As new genera were erected, the species was moved, reaching its current position in 1814, when <a href="/wiki/William_Elford_Leach" title="William Elford Leach">William Elford Leach</a> erected the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nephrops" title="Nephrops">Nephrops</a></i> to hold this species alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLW_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-MLW-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Seven <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> species have since been described in the genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grave_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grave-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Populations in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> are sometimes separated as "<i>Nephrops norvegicus</i> var. <i>meridionalis</i> Zariquiey, 1935", although this taxon is not universally considered valid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
