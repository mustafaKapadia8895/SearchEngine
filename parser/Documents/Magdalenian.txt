<p>The <b>Magdalenian</b> (also <b>Madelenian</b>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i>Magdalénien</i>) refers to one of the later <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_culture" title="Archaeological culture">cultures</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">western Europe</a>, dating from around 17,000 to 12,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leseyzies_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-leseyzies-1">[1]</a></sup> It is named after the <a href="/wiki/Type_site" title="Type site">type site</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Madeleine_(prehistoric_site)" title="La Madeleine (prehistoric site)">La Madeleine</a>, a rock shelter located in the Vézère valley, commune of <a href="/wiki/Tursac" title="Tursac">Tursac</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Dordogne" title="Dordogne">Dordogne</a> department of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p>Originally termed <i>L'âge du renne</i> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Reindeer" title="Age of the Reindeer">Age of the Reindeer</a>) by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lartet" title="Édouard Lartet">Édouard Lartet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Christy" title="Henry Christy">Henry Christy</a>, the first systematic excavators of the type site, in their publication of 1875, the Magdalenian is synonymous in many people's minds with reindeer hunters, although Magdalenian sites also contain extensive evidence for the hunting of red deer, horses, and other large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a> present in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> toward the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>. The culture was geographically widespread, and later Magdalenian sites have been found from Portugal in the west to Poland in the east. It is the third <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabriel_de_Mortillet" title="Gabriel de Mortillet">Gabriel de Mortillet's</a> cave chronology system, corresponding roughly to the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Period_biology">Period biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdalenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Period biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Magdalenian epoch was a long one, represented by numerous stations, whose contents show progress in the arts and general culture. It was characterized by a cold and dry climate, the existence of humans in association with the reindeer, and the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a>. The use of bone and ivory for various implements, already begun in the preceding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solutrian_epoch" title="Solutrian epoch">Solutrian epoch</a>, was much increased, and the period is essentially a bone period. The bone instruments are quite varied: spear-points, <a href="/wiki/Harpoon" title="Harpoon">harpoon</a>-heads, borers, hooks, and needles.</p>
<p>Most remarkable is the evidence La Madeleine affords of prehistoric art. Numbers of bones, reindeer antlers, and animal teeth were found, with rude pictures carved or etched on them of seals, fishes, reindeer, mammoths, and other creatures. The best of these are a mammoth engraved on a fragment of its own ivory;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Magdalenian#Dubious" title="Talk:Magdalenian">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> a dagger of reindeer antler, with a handle in form of a reindeer; a cave-bear cut on a flat piece of <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a>; a seal on a bear's tooth; a fish drawn on a reindeer antler; and a complete picture, also on reindeer antler, showing horses, an <a href="/wiki/Aurochs" title="Aurochs">aurochs</a>, trees, and a snake biting a man's leg. The man is naked, which, together with the snake, suggests a warm climate in spite of the presence of the reindeer.</p>
<p>The fauna of the Magdelenian epoch seems, indeed, to have included tigers and other tropical species side by side with reindeer, blue foxes, Arctic hares, and other polar creatures. Magdelenian humans appear to have been of low stature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolichocephalic" title="Dolichocephalic">dolichocephalic</a>, with a low retreating forehead and prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brow_ridge" title="Brow ridge">brow ridges</a>.</p>

<p>The culture spans from approximately 17,000 to 12,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>, toward the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. The Magdalenian tool culture is characterised by regular <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">blade</a> industries struck from <a href="/wiki/Carinate" title="Carinate">carinated</a> cores. Typologically, the Magdalenian is divided into six phases which are generally agreed to have chronological significance. The earliest phases are recognised by the varying proportion of blades and specific varieties of scrapers, the middle phases marked by the emergence of a microlithic component (particularly the distinctive <a href="/wiki/Denticulate_tool" title="Denticulate tool">denticulated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microliths" title="Microliths">microliths</a>), and the later phases by the presence of uniserial (phase<span class="nowrap"> </span>5) and biserial 'harpoons' (phase<span class="nowrap"> </span>6) made of bone, antler, and ivory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is extensive debate about the precise nature of the earliest Magdalenian assemblages, and it remains questionable whether the Badegoulian culture is, in fact, the earliest phase of the Magdalenian. Similarly, finds from the forest of Beauregard near Paris often have been suggested as belonging to the earliest Magdalenian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The earliest Magdalenian sites are all found in France. The <a href="/wiki/Epigravettian" title="Epigravettian">Epigravettian</a> is a similar culture appearing at the same time in Italy, the Balkans, Moldavia, Romania, and Russia.</p>
<p>The later phases of the Magdalenian are also synonymous with the human re-settlement of north-western Europe after the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Glacial_Maximum" title="Late Glacial Maximum">Late Glacial Maximum</a>. Research in Switzerland, southern Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Belgium <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> has provided AMS <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> to support this. Being hunter gatherers, Magdalenians did not simply re-settle permanently in north-west Europe, however, as they often followed herds and moved depending on seasons.</p>
<p>By the end of the Magdalenian, the lithic technology shows a pronounced trend toward increased microlithisation. The bone harpoons and points have the most distinctive chronological markers within the typological sequence. As well as flint tools, the Magdalenians are best known for their elaborate worked bone, antler, and <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> that served both functional and aesthetic purposes, including <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A2ton_de_commandement" title="Bâton de commandement">perforated batons</a>. Examples of Magdalenian portable art include batons, <a href="/wiki/Figurines" title="Figurines">figurines</a>, and intricately engraved projectile points, as well as items of personal adornment including sea shells, perforated <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivore</a> teeth (presumably necklaces), and fossils.</p>
<p>The sea shells and fossils found in Magdalenian sites may be sourced to relatively precise areas of origin, and so have been used to support hypothesis of Magdalenian hunter-gatherer seasonal ranges, and perhaps trade routes. Cave sites such as the world famous <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux</a> contain the best known examples of Magdalenian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_art" title="Cave art">cave art</a>. The site of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altamira_(cave)" title="Altamira (cave)">Altamira</a> in Spain, with its extensive and varied forms of Magdalenian <a href="/wiki/Portable_art" title="Portable art">mobiliary art</a> has been suggested to be an agglomeration site where many small groups of Magdalenian hunter-gatherers congregated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In northern Spain and south-west France this tool culture was superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Azilian" title="Azilian">Azilian</a> culture. In northern Europe it was followed by different variants of the <a href="/wiki/Federmesser_culture" title="Federmesser culture">Tjongerian</a> techno-complex. It has been suggested that key Late-glacial sites in south-western Britain also may be attributed to the Magdalenian, including the famous site of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kent%27s_Cavern" title="Kent's Cavern">Kent's Cavern</a>, although this remains open to debate.</p>
<p>Besides La Madeleine, the chief stations of the epoch are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Les_Eyzies" title="Les Eyzies">Les Eyzies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laugerie-Basse" title="Laugerie-Basse">Laugerie-Basse</a>, and Gorges d'Enfer in the <a href="/wiki/Dordogne" title="Dordogne">Dordogne</a>; Grotte du Placard in <a href="/wiki/Charente" title="Charente">Charente</a> and others in south-west France.</p>
