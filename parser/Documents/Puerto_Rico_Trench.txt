<h2><span id="Coordinates:_19°50′9″N_66°45′16″W﻿_/_﻿19.83583°N_66.75444°W﻿_/_19.83583;_-66.75444"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordinates:_19.C2.B050.E2.80.B29.E2.80.B3N_66.C2.B045.E2.80.B216.E2.80.B3W.EF.BB.BF_.2F_.EF.BB.BF19.83583.C2.B0N_66.75444.C2.B0W.EF.BB.BF_.2F_19.83583.3B_-66.75444"><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Puerto_Rico_Trench&amp;params=19_50_9_N_66_45_16_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">19°50′9″N</span> <span class="longitude">66°45′16″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">19.83583°N 66.75444°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">19.83583; -66.75444</span></span></span></a></span></span></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rico_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Coordinates: 19°50′9″N 66°45′16″W﻿ / ﻿19.83583°N 66.75444°W﻿ / 19.83583; -66.75444">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <b>Puerto Rico Trench</b> is located on the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trench</a> is associated with a complex transition between the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles_subduction_zone" title="Lesser Antilles subduction zone">Lesser Antilles subduction zone</a> to the south and the major <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform fault</a> zone or plate boundary, which extends west between <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cayman_Trench" title="Cayman Trench">Cayman Trench</a> to the coast of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. The trench is 800 kilometres (497 mi) long and has a maximum depth of 8,648 metres (28,373 ft) or 5.373 miles at <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Deep" title="Milwaukee Deep">Milwaukee Deep</a>, which is the deepest point in the Atlantic Ocean and the deepest point not in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.</p>
<p>Scientific studies have concluded that an earthquake occurring along this fault zone could generate a significant <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-1">[1]</a></sup> The island of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> which lies immediately to the south of the fault zone and the trench, suffered a destructive tsunami soon after the <a href="/wiki/1918_San_Ferm%C3%ADn_earthquake" title="1918 San Fermín earthquake">1918 San Fermín earthquake</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rico_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Puerto Rico Trench is located at a boundary between two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">plates</a> that pass each other along a transform boundary with only a small component of <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Plate" title="Caribbean Plate">Caribbean Plate</a> is moving to the east while the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a> is moving to the west. The North American Plate is being subducted by the Caribbean Plate obliquely at the trench while to the southeast, the South American Plate is being more directly subducted along the Lesser Antilles subduction zone. This subduction zone explains the presence of active <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> over the southeastern part of the Caribbean Sea. Volcanic activity is frequent along the island arc southeast from Puerto Rico to the coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.</p>
<p>Puerto Rico, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> do not have active volcanoes; however, they are at risk from <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a>. The Puerto Rico Trench is capable of producing earthquakes greater than magnitude 8.0.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_awareness">Public awareness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rico_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public awareness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Knowledge of the earthquake and tsunami risks has not been widespread among the general public of the islands located near the trench. Since 1988, the Puerto Rican Seismic Society has been trying to use the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican</a> media to inform people about a future earthquake that could result in a catastrophic tragedy.</p>
<p>Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">2004 tsunami</a> that affected more than forty countries in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, many more people now fear the consequences that such an event would bring to the Caribbean. Local governments have begun emergency planning. In the case of Puerto Rico and the U.S. Virgin Islands, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> government has been studying the problem for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-2">[2]</a></sup> It is increasing its seismic investigations and developing <a href="/wiki/Tsunami_warning_system" title="Tsunami warning system">tsunami warning systems</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquake_history">Earthquake history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rico_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earthquake history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PRtrenchlocation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="600" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/PRtrenchlocation.jpg/300px-PRtrenchlocation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/PRtrenchlocation.jpg/450px-PRtrenchlocation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/PRtrenchlocation.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PRtrenchlocation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>On 11 October 1918, the western coast of the island was hit by a <a href="/wiki/1918_San_Ferm%C3%ADn_earthquake" title="1918 San Fermín earthquake">major earthquake</a> which caused a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a>. The 1918 earthquake was caused by an old left-lateral strike-slip fault near the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Passage" title="Mona Passage">Mona Passage</a>. In 1953, <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, was affected by the Santo Domingo earthquake. The actual subduction zone (Puerto Rico Trench) has not ruptured in over 200 years, which is a major concern to geophysicists, as they believe it may be due for a major event.</p>
<p>Puerto Rico has always been an area of concern to earthquake experts because, apart from the 1918 episode, there are frequent tremors in and around the island, indicating activity. A 1981 tremor was felt across the island, while another in 1985 was felt in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Cayey,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cayey, Puerto Rico">Cayey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salinas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Salinas, Puerto Rico">Salinas</a>.</p>
<p>"The January 13, 2014 M 6.4 earthquake north of Puerto Rico occurred as a result of oblique-thrust faulting. Preliminary faulting mechanisms for the event indicate it ruptured either a structure dipping shallowly to the south and striking approximately east-west, or a near-vertical structure striking northwest-southeast. At the location of this earthquake, the North America plate moves west-southwest with respect to the Caribbean plate at a velocity of approximately 20 mm/yr, and subducts beneath the Caribbean plate at the Puerto Rico Trench. The location, depth and mechanism of the earthquake are consistent with the event occurring on this subduction zone interface."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rico_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several exploration cruises carried out by USGS in the Puerto Rico Trench have for the first time mapped the entire trench using ship mounted multibeam bathymetry (see references and external links below).</p>
<p>The seafloor was visited for the first time by a robotic vehicle in 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>.The most conspicuous aspect of the footage was the swarm of benthic amphipods. Some of these amphipods were collected by bait bags attached to the vehicle and were brought to the surface for further analysis. The samples recovered were <i>Scopelocherius</i> <i>schellenbergi</i>, a species of lysianassoid amphipod that have so far only been found in ultradeep trenches in the Pacific<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>. Two invertebrate creatures were also observed in the video. One soft dark individual, estimated to be 10-20 centimeters long, has been identified by Dr. Stace E. Beaulieu of Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution as a sea cucumber, tentatively assigned to genus <i>Peniagone</i>. The other individual, a small crustacean, is tentatively identified as a <i>munnposid</i> <i>isopod</i>, based on morphology and similar walking and jumping movements observed for other hadal <i>munnopsid</i> <i>isopods</i>. Because these individual were not collected, it is not possible to obtain species-level identifications. However, these sightings likely exceed the deepest known records for genus <i>Peniagone</i> and family <i>Munnopsidae</i>.  </p>
