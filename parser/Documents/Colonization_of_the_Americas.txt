<p>The <b>European <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a></b> describes the history of the settlement and establishment of control of the continents of the Americas by various European powers. Starting in either the 10th or 11th century, when <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">West Norse</a> sailors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">explored and briefly settled</a> on the shores of present-day <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_Sagas" title="Icelandic Sagas">Icelandic Sagas</a>, violent conflicts with the indigenous population ultimately led to the Norse abandoning those settlements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:America_1794.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1270" data-file-width="988" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/America_1794.png/220px-America_1794.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/America_1794.png/330px-America_1794.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/America_1794.png/440px-America_1794.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:America_1794.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Political map of the Americas in 1794</div>

<p>European colonization began in 1492, when a Spanish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbus%27s_first_voyage" title="Columbus's first voyage">expedition</a> headed by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> sailed west to find a new trade route to the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a> but inadvertently landed in what came to be known to Europeans as the "<a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>". Running aground on the northern part of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> on 5 December 1492, which the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">Taino people</a> had inhabited since the 7th century, the site became the first European settlement in the Americas. European conquest, large-scale exploration and colonization soon followed. Columbus's first two voyages (1492–93) reached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a> islands, including Hispaniola, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. In 1498, sailing from <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> landed on the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> coast, and a year later, Columbus's <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus#Third_voyage" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">third voyage</a> reached the South American coast. As the sponsor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus%27s_voyages" title="Christopher Columbus's voyages">Christopher Columbus's voyages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spain</a> was the first European power to settle and colonize the largest areas, from North America and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> to the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.</p>
<p>Other powers such as <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">France</a> also founded colonies in the Americas: in eastern North America, a number of Caribbean islands and small coastal parts of South America. <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> colonized <a href="/wiki/History_of_Brazil" title="History of Brazil">Brazil</a>, tried colonizing the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">eastern coasts</a> of present-day Canada and settled for extended periods northwest (on the east bank) of the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">River Plate</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_Exploration" title="Age of Exploration">Age of Exploration</a> was the beginning of territorial expansion for several European countries. Europe had been <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Late_Middle_Ages" title="Crisis of the Late Middle Ages">preoccupied with internal wars</a> and was slowly recovering from the loss of population caused by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>; thus the rapid rate at which it grew in wealth and power was unforeseeable in the early 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2001-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eventually, most of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a> came under the control of European governments, leading to changes to its landscape, population, and plant and animal life. In the 19th century over 50 million people left Europe for the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The post-1492 era is known as the period of the <a href="/wiki/Columbian_Exchange" title="Columbian Exchange">Columbian Exchange</a>, a dramatically widespread exchange of animals, plants, culture, human populations (including <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">slaves</a>), ideas and <a href="/wiki/Native_American_disease_and_epidemics" title="Native American disease and epidemics">communicable disease</a> between the American and Afro-Eurasian hemispheres following Columbus's voyages to the Americas.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> journeys to Greenland and Canada are supported by historical and archaeological evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Price2015_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Price2015-4">[4]</a></sup> A Norse colony in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> was established in the late 10th century, and lasted until the mid 15th century, with court and parliament assemblies (<i><a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">þing</a></i>) taking place at <a href="/wiki/Brattahl%C3%AD%C3%B0" title="Brattahlíð">Brattahlíð</a> and a bishop located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gardar,_Greenland" title="Gardar, Greenland">Garðar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WurmM.C3.BChlh.C3.A4usler1996_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WurmM.C3.BChlh.C3.A4usler1996-5">[5]</a></sup> The remains of a Norse settlement at <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, Canada, were discovered in 1960 and were dated to around the year 1000 (carbon dating estimate 990–1050 CE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CordellLightfoot2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CordellLightfoot2008-6">[6]</a></sup> L'Anse aux Meadows is the only site widely accepted as evidence of pre-Columbian trans-oceanic contact. It was named a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" title="World Heritage site">World Heritage site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen1997_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen1997-7">[7]</a></sup> It is also notable for its possible connection with the attempted colony of <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, established by <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">Leif Erikson</a> around the same period or, more broadly, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_exploration_of_the_Americas" title="Norse exploration of the Americas">West Norse colonization of the Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristinsson2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristinsson2010-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Early_conquests,_claims_and_colonies"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_conquests.2C_claims_and_colonies">Early conquests, claims and colonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early conquests, claims and colonies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Early explorations and conquests were made by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese</a> immediately following their own final <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">reconquest</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberia" title="Iberia">Iberia</a> in 1492. In the 1494 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">Treaty of Tordesillas</a>, ratified by the Pope, these two kingdoms divided the entire non-European world into two areas of exploration and colonization, with a north to south boundary that cut through the Atlantic Ocean and the eastern part of present-day Brazil. Based on this treaty and on early claims by Spanish explorer <a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Núñez de Balboa">Vasco Núñez de Balboa</a>, discoverer of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> in 1513, the Spanish conquered large territories in North, Central and South America.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%22America%22_(Engraving)_Nova_reperta_(Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638).tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2312" data-file-width="3162" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/%22America%22_%28Engraving%29_Nova_reperta_%28Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638%29.tif/lossless-page1-220px-%22America%22_%28Engraving%29_Nova_reperta_%28Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638%29.tif.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/%22America%22_%28Engraving%29_Nova_reperta_%28Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638%29.tif/lossless-page1-330px-%22America%22_%28Engraving%29_Nova_reperta_%28Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638%29.tif.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/%22America%22_%28Engraving%29_Nova_reperta_%28Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638%29.tif/lossless-page1-440px-%22America%22_%28Engraving%29_Nova_reperta_%28Speculum_diuersarum_imaginum_speculatiuarum_1638%29.tif.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Spanish conquistador <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> took over the <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Empire" title="Aztec Empire">Aztec Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a> conquered the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a>. As a result, by the mid-16th century, the Spanish Crown had gained control of much of western South America, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, in addition to its earlier <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> territories. Over this same timeframe, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal" title="History of Portugal">Portugal</a> claimed lands in North America (Canada) and colonized much of eastern South America, naming it <a href="/wiki/History_of_Brazil" title="History of Brazil">Santa Cruz and Brazil</a>.</p>
<p>Other <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> nations soon disputed the terms of the Treaty of Tordesillas. England and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime_in_France" title="Ancien Régime in France">France</a> attempted to plant colonies in the Americas in the 16th century, but these failed. England and France succeeded in establishing permanent colonies in the following century, along with the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a>. Some of these were on Caribbean islands, which had often already been conquered by the Spanish or depopulated by disease, while others were in eastern North America, which had not been colonized by Spain north of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Florida" title="History of Florida">Florida</a>.</p>
<p>Early European possessions in North America included <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Florida" title="Spanish Florida">Spanish Florida</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_New_Mexico" title="Spanish New Mexico">Spanish New Mexico</a>, the English colonies of <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Virginia</a> (with its <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_in_the_Atlantic_Ocean" title="List of islands in the Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic</a> offshoot, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>) and <a href="/wiki/New_England_Colonies" title="New England Colonies">New England</a>, the French colonies of <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada,_New_France" title="Canada, New France">Canada</a>, the Swedish colony of <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a>, and the Dutch <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>. In the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">Denmark–Norway</a> revived its former colonies in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> gained a foothold in <a href="/wiki/Russian_America" title="Russian America">Alaska</a>. Denmark-Norway would later make several claims in the Caribbean, starting in the 1600s.</p>
<p>As more nations gained an interest in the colonization of the Americas, competition for territory became increasingly fierce. Colonists often faced the threat of attacks from neighboring colonies, as well as from indigenous tribes and <a href="/wiki/Piracy_in_the_Caribbean" title="Piracy in the Caribbean">pirates</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_state-sponsored_colonists">Early state-sponsored colonists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early state-sponsored colonists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Age_of_Discovery" title="Portugal in the Age of Discovery">Portugal in the Age of Discovery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization of the Americas</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_European_colonization_wave_(15th_century%E2%80%9319th_century)" title="First European colonization wave (15th century–19th century)">First European colonization wave (15th century–19th century)</a></div>
<p>The first phase of well-financed European activity in the Americas began with the Atlantic Ocean crossings of Christopher Columbus (1492–1504), sponsored by Spain, whose original attempt was to find a new route to India and China, known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indies" title="Indies">the Indies</a>". He was followed by other explorers such as <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a>, who was sponsored by England and reached Newfoundland. <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral" title="Pedro Álvares Cabral">Pedro Álvares Cabral</a> reached Brazil and claimed it for Portugal.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a>, working for Portugal in voyages from 1497 to 1513, established that Columbus had reached a new set of continents. <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">Cartographers</a> still use a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latinization_(literature)" title="Latinization (literature)">Latinized</a> version of his first name, <i>America</i>, for the two continents. Other explorers included <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">Giovanni da Verrazzano</a>, sponsored by France in 1524; the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Vaz_Corte-Real" title="João Vaz Corte-Real">João Vaz Corte-Real</a> in Newfoundland; <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Fernandes_Lavrador" title="João Fernandes Lavrador">João Fernandes Lavrador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Corte-Real" title="Gaspar Corte-Real">Gaspar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Corte-Real" title="Miguel Corte-Real">Miguel Corte-Real</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_%C3%81lvares_Fagundes" title="João Álvares Fagundes">João Álvares Fagundes</a>, in Newfoundland, Greenland, Labrador, and Nova Scotia (from 1498 to 1502, and in 1520); <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cartier" title="Jacques Cartier">Jacques Cartier</a> (1491–1557), <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hudson" title="Henry Hudson">Henry Hudson</a> (1560s–1611), and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a> (1567–1635), who explored the region of Canada he reestablished as <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>.</p>
<p>In 1513, <a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Núñez de Balboa">Vasco Núñez de Balboa</a> crossed the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> and led the first European expedition to see the Pacific Ocean from the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_west_coast_of_North_America" title="History of the west coast of North America">west coast of the New World</a>. In an action with enduring historical import, Balboa claimed the Pacific Ocean and all the lands adjoining it for the Spanish Crown. It was 1517 before another expedition, from <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, visited Central America, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Spanish colonization of the Yucatán">landing on the coast of Yucatán</a> <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_Spanish_New_World_colonies" title="Slavery in the Spanish New World colonies">in search of slaves</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Philip_II%27s_realms_in_1598.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>These explorations were followed, notably in the case of Spain, by a phase of conquest: The Spaniards, having just finished the <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></i> of Spain from Muslim rule, were the first to colonize the Americas, applying the same model of governing their European holdings to their territories of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.</p>
<p>Ten years after Columbus's discovery, the administration of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> was given to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicolás de Ovando">Nicolás de Ovando</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Alc%C3%A1ntara" title="Order of Alcántara">Order of Alcántara</a>, founded during the <i>Reconquista</i>. As in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Taíno people">inhabitants of Hispaniola</a> were given new landmasters, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_religious_order" title="Roman Catholic religious order">religious orders</a> handled the local administration. Progressively the <i><a href="/wiki/Encomienda" title="Encomienda">encomienda</a></i> system, which granted tribute (access to indigenous labor and taxation) to European settlers, was set in place.</p>
<p>A relatively common misconception is that a small number of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquistadores" title="Conquistadores">conquistadores</a></i> conquered vast territories, aided only by disease epidemics and their powerful <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">caballeros</a>. In fact, recent archaeological excavations have suggested a vast Spanish-Indian alliance numbering in the hundreds of thousands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> eventually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_Mexico" title="Spanish conquest of Mexico">conquered Mexico</a> with the help of <a href="/wiki/Tlaxcala" title="Tlaxcala">Tlaxcala</a> in 1519–1521, while the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Inca_Empire" title="Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire">conquest of the Incas</a> was carried out by some 40,000 Incan renegades led by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a> in between 1532 and 1535.</p>
<p>Over the 1st century and a half after Columbus's voyages, the native population of the Americas plummeted by an estimated 80% (from around 50 million in 1492 to eight million in 1650<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Histoire_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Histoire-10">[10]</a></sup>), mostly by outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">diseases</a>.</p>
<p>In 1532, <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a> sent a vice-king to Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Mendoza" title="Antonio de Mendoza">Antonio de Mendoza</a>, in order to prevent Cortes' independentist drives, who definitively returned to Spain in 1540. Two years later, Charles V signed the <a href="/wiki/New_Laws" title="New Laws">New Laws</a> (which replaced the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Burgos" title="Laws of Burgos">Laws of Burgos</a> of 1512) prohibiting slavery and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Repartimientos" title="Repartimientos">repartimientos</a></i>, but also claiming as his own all the American lands and all of the indigenous people as his own subjects.</p>

<p>When in May 1493, the Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_VI" title="Alexander VI">Alexander VI</a> issued the <i><a href="/wiki/Inter_caetera" title="Inter caetera">Inter caetera</a></i> bull granting the new lands to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Spain" title="Kingdom of Spain">Kingdom of Spain</a>, he requested in exchange an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church_and_colonialism" title="Roman Catholic Church and colonialism">evangelization of the people</a>. Thus, during <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Columbus</a>'s second voyage, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> friars accompanied him, along with twelve other priests. As <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_medieval_Europe" title="Slavery in medieval Europe">slavery</a> was prohibited between Christians, and could only be imposed in non-Christian prisoners of war or on men already sold as slaves, the debate on Christianization was particularly acute during the 16th century. In 1537, the papal bull <i><a href="/wiki/Sublimis_Deus" title="Sublimis Deus">Sublimis Deus</a></i> definitively recognized that Native Americans possessed souls, thus prohibiting their enslavement, without putting an end to the debate. Some claimed that a native who had rebelled and then been captured could be enslaved nonetheless.</p>
<p>Later, the <a href="/wiki/Valladolid_debate" title="Valladolid debate">Valladolid debate</a> between the Dominican priest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_de_Las_Casas" title="Bartolomé de Las Casas">Bartolomé de Las Casas</a> and another Dominican <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gin%C3%A9s_de_Sep%C3%BAlveda" title="Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda">Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda</a> was held, with the former arguing that Native Americans were beings doted with souls, as all other human beings, while the latter argued to the contrary and justified their enslavement.</p>
<p>The process of Christianization was at first violent: when the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscans</a> arrived in Mexico in 1524, they burned the places dedicated to pagan cult, alienating much of the local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Histoire2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Histoire2-11">[11]</a></sup> In the 1530s, they began to adapt Christian practices to local customs, including the building of new churches on the sites of ancient places of worship, leading to a mix of Old World Christianity with local religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Histoire2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Histoire2-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Spain" title="Roman Catholicism in Spain">Spanish Roman Catholic Church</a>, needing the natives' labor and cooperation, evangelized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_language" title="Quechua language">Quechua</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuatl_language" title="Nahuatl language">Nahuatl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_language" title="Guaraní language">Guaraní</a> and other Native American languages, contributing to the expansion of these <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">indigenous languages</a> and equipping some of them with writing systems. One of the first primitive schools for Native Americans was founded by Fray <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Gante" title="Pedro de Gante">Pedro de Gante</a> in 1523.</p>
<p>To reward their troops, the <i>Conquistadores</i> often allotted Indian towns to their troops and officers. Black African slaves were introduced to substitute for Native American labor in some locations—including the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, where the indigenous population was nearing extinction on many islands.</p>
<p>During this time, the Portuguese gradually switched from an initial plan of establishing trading posts to extensive <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a> of what is now Brazil. They imported millions of slaves to run their plantations. The Portuguese and Spanish royal governments expected to rule these settlements and collect at least 20% of all treasure found (the <i><a href="/wiki/Quinto_real" title="Quinto real">quinto real</a></i> collected by the <i><a href="/wiki/Casa_de_Contrataci%C3%B3n" title="Casa de Contratación">Casa de Contratación</a></i>), in addition to collecting all the taxes they could. By the late 16th century American <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> accounted for one-fifth of Spain's total budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the 16th century perhaps 240,000 Europeans entered American ports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_search_for_riches">The search for riches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The search for riches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="423" data-file-width="600" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg/220px-Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg/330px-Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg/440px-Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fur_traders_in_canada_1777.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fur traders in Canada, trading with Indians, 1777</div>

<p>Inspired by the Spanish riches from colonies founded upon the conquest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztecs" title="Aztecs">Aztecs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incas" title="Incas">Incas</a>, and other large Native American populations in the 16th century, the first Englishmen to settle permanently in America hoped for some of the same rich discoveries when they established their first permanent settlement in <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown, Virginia</a> in 1607. They were sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Common_stock" title="Common stock">common stock</a> companies such as the chartered <a href="/wiki/London_Company" title="London Company">Virginia Company</a> financed by wealthy Englishmen who exaggerated the economic potential of this new land. The main purpose of this colony was the hope of finding gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2001-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It took strong leaders, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Smith_of_Jamestown" title="John Smith of Jamestown">John Smith</a>, to convince the colonists of Jamestown that searching for gold was not taking care of their immediate needs for food and shelter and the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">biblical</a> principle that "he who will not work shall not eat" (see <a href="/wiki/2_Thessalonians_3" title="2 Thessalonians 3">2 Thessalonians 3</a>). The lack of <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> leading to extremely high mortality rate was quite distressing and cause for despair among the colonists. To support the Colony, numerous <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_supply_missions" title="Jamestown supply missions">supply missions</a> were organized. <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">Tobacco</a> later became a cash crop, with the work of <a href="/wiki/John_Rolfe" title="John Rolfe">John Rolfe</a> and others, for export and the sustaining economic driver of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and the neighboring colony of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>.</p>
<p>From the beginning of Virginia's settlements in 1587 until the 1680s, the main source of labor and a large portion of the immigrants were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_servants" title="Indentured servants">indentured servants</a> looking for new life in the overseas colonies. During the 17th century, indentured servants constituted three-quarters of all European immigrants to the Chesapeake region. Most of the indentured servants were teenagers from England with poor economic prospects at home. Their fathers signed the papers that gave them free passage to America and an unpaid job until they became of age. They were given food, clothing, housing and taught farming or household skills. American landowners were in need of laborers and were willing to pay for a laborer’s passage to America if they served them for several years. By selling passage for five to seven years worth of work they could then start out on their own in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Many of the migrants from England died in the first few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_2001_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2001-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Economic advantage also prompted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darien_Scheme" title="Darien Scheme">Darien Scheme</a>, an ill-fated venture by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a> to settle the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> in the late 1690s. The Darien Scheme aimed to control trade through that part of the world and thereby promote Scotland into a world trading power. However, it was doomed by poor planning, short provisions, weak leadership, lack of demand for trade goods, and devastating disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The failure of the Darien Scheme was one of the factors that led the Kingdom of Scotland into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" title="Act of Union 1707">Act of Union 1707</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> creating the united <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> and giving Scotland commercial access to English, now British, colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the French colonial regions, the focus of economy was on sugar plantations in Caribbean. In Canada the <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a> with the natives was important. About 16,000 French men and women became colonizers. The great majority became subsistence farmers along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a>. With a favorable disease environment and plenty of land and food, their numbers grew exponentially to 65,000 by 1760. Their colony was taken over by Britain in 1760, but social, religious, legal, cultural and economic changes were few in a society that clung tightly to its recently formed traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_immigration">Religious immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religious immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="299" data-file-width="602" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg/220px-William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg/330px-William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg/440px-William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:William_Penn_and_the_Indians.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Penn_Treaty_Park" title="Penn Treaty Park">Penn's Treaty</a> with the Indians</div>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a> were the first major religious group to immigrate to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, as settlers in the colonies of Portugal and Spain (and later, France) belonged to that faith. English and Dutch colonies, on the other hand, tended to be more religiously diverse. Settlers to these colonies included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglicans</a>, Dutch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvinists" title="Calvinists">Calvinists</a>, English <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritans</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconformists" title="Nonconformists">nonconformists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Toleration_Act" title="Maryland Toleration Act">English Catholics</a>, Scottish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterians</a>, French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a>, German and Swedish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutherans" title="Lutherans">Lutherans</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mennonites" title="Mennonites">Mennonites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Moravians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> of various nationalities.</p>
<p>Many groups of colonists went to the Americas searching for the right to practice their religion without <a href="/wiki/Religious_persecution" title="Religious persecution">persecution</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> of the 16th century broke the unity of Western <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a> and led to the formation of numerous new religious sects, which often faced persecution by governmental authorities. In England, many people came to question the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> by the end of the 16th century. One of the primary manifestations of this was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> movement, which sought to "purify" the existing Church of England of its many residual Catholic rites that they believed had no mention in the Bible.</p>
<p>A strong believer in the notion of rule by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine_Right_of_Kings" title="Divine Right of Kings">divine right</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>, King of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a>, persecuted religious dissenters. Waves of repression led to the migration of about 20,000 Puritans to <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> between 1629 and 1642, where they founded multiple colonies. Later in the century, the new <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> colony was given to <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a> in settlement of a debt the king owed his father. Its government was set up by William Penn in about 1682 to become primarily a refuge for persecuted English Quakers; but others were welcomed. Baptists, <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>, German and Swiss Protestants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptists" title="Anabaptists">Anabaptists</a> flocked to Pennsylvania. The lure of cheap land, religious freedom and the right to improve themselves with their own hand was very attractive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forced_immigration_and_enslavement">Forced immigration and enslavement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Forced immigration and enslavement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">Slavery</a> was a common practice in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> prior to the arrival of Europeans, as different American Indian groups captured and held other tribes' members as slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Many of these captives were forced to undergo <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">human sacrifice</a> in Amerindian civilizations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztecs" title="Aztecs">Aztecs</a>. In response to some enslavement of natives in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> during the early years, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Crown" title="Spanish Crown">Spanish Crown</a> passed a series of <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Burgos" title="Laws of Burgos">laws prohibiting slavery</a> as early as 1512. A new stricter set of laws was passed in 1542, called the <i>New Laws of the Indies for the Good Treatment and Preservation of Indians</i>, or simply <a href="/wiki/New_Laws" title="New Laws">New Laws</a>. These were created to prevent the exploitation of the indigenous peoples by the <i><a href="/wiki/Encomienda" title="Encomienda">encomenderos</a></i> or landowners, by strictly limiting their power and dominion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This helped curb Indian slavery considerably, though not completely. Later, with the arrival of other European colonial powers in the New World, the enslavement of native populations increased, as these empires lacked legislation against slavery until decades later. The population of indigenous peoples declined (mostly from European diseases, but also from forced exploitation and atrocities). Later, native workers were replaced by Africans imported through a large commercial slave trade.</p>
<p>By the 18th century, the overwhelming number of black slaves was such that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindian" title="Amerindian">Amerindian</a> slavery was less commonly used. Africans, who were taken aboard slave ships to the Americas, were primarily obtained from their African homelands by coastal tribes who captured and sold them. Europeans traded for slaves with the slave capturers of the local native African tribes in exchange for rum, guns, gunpowder, and other manufactures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> to islands in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, Mexico and to the United States is estimated to have involved 12 million Africans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The vast majority of these slaves went to sugar colonies in the Caribbean and to Brazil, where life expectancy was short and the numbers had to be continually replenished. At most about 600,000 African slaves were imported into the U.S., or 5% of the 12 million slaves brought across from Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Life expectancy was much higher in the U.S. (because of better food, less disease, lighter work loads, and better medical care) so the numbers grew rapidly by excesses of births over deaths, reaching 4 million by the 1860 Census. From 1770 until 1860, the rate of natural growth of North American slaves was much greater than for the population of any nation in Europe, and was nearly twice as rapid as that of England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<li>1701–1760 – 189,000</li>

<li>Total – 597,000</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease_and_indigenous_population_loss">Disease and indigenous population loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disease and indigenous population loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Disease_in_colonial_America" title="Disease in colonial America">Disease in colonial America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Native_American_disease_and_epidemics" title="Native American disease and epidemics">Native American disease and epidemics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="640" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg/220px-FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg/330px-FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg/440px-FlorentineCodex_BK12_F54_smallpox.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The European lifestyle included a long history of sharing close quarters with domesticated animals such as cows, pigs, sheep, goats, horses, dogs and various domesticated <a href="/wiki/Fowl" title="Fowl">fowl</a>, from which many diseases originally stemmed. Thus, in contrast to the indigenous people, Europeans had developed a richer endowment of antibodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The large-scale contact with Europeans after 1492 introduced novel germs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">indigenous people of the Americas</a>.</p>
<p>Epidemics of <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> (1518, 1521, 1525, 1558, 1589), <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a> (1546), <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a> (1558), <a href="/wiki/Diphtheria" title="Diphtheria">diphtheria</a> (1614) and <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> (1618) swept the Americas subsequent to European contact,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> killing between 10 million and 100 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> people, up to 95% of the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous population</a> of the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The cultural and political instability attending these losses appears to have been of substantial aid in the efforts of various colonists in New England and Massachusetts to acquire control over the great wealth in land and resources of which indigenous societies had customarily made use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Such diseases yielded human mortality of an unquestionably enormous gravity and scale – and this has profoundly confused efforts to determine its full extent with any true precision. Estimates of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_history_of_American_indigenous_peoples" title="Population history of American indigenous peoples">pre-Columbian population of the Americas</a> vary tremendously.</p>
<p>Others have argued that significant variations in population size over pre-Columbian history are reason to view higher-end estimates with caution. Such estimates may reflect historical population maxima, while indigenous populations may have been at a level somewhat below these maxima or in a moment of decline in the period just prior to contact with Europeans. Indigenous populations hit their ultimate lows in most areas of the Americas in the early 20th century; in a number of cases, growth has returned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_colonial_land_ownership_on_long-term_development">Impact of colonial land ownership on long-term development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impact of colonial land ownership on long-term development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geographic differences between the colonies played a large determinant in the types of political and economic systems that later developed. In their paper on institutions and long-run growth, economists <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daron_Acemo%C4%9Flu" title="Daron Acemoğlu">Daron Acemoğlu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Johnson_(economist)" title="Simon Johnson (economist)">Simon Johnson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_A._Robinson_(economist)" title="James A. Robinson (economist)">James A. Robinson</a> argue that certain natural endowments gave rise to distinct colonial policies promoting either smallholder or coerced labor production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Densely settled populations, for example, were more easily exploitable and profitable as slave labor. In these regions, landowning elites were economically incentivized to develop forced labor arrangements such as the Peru <a href="/wiki/Mit%27a" title="Mit'a">mit'a</a> system or Argentinian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latifundias" title="Latifundias">latifundias</a> without regard for democratic norms. French and British colonial leaders, conversely, were incentivized to develop capitalist markets, property rights, and democratic institutions in response to natural environments that supported smallholder production over forced labor.</p>
<p>James Mahoney, a professor at Northwestern University, proposes that colonial policy choices made at critical junctures regarding land ownership in coffee-rich Central America fostered enduring <a href="/wiki/Path_dependence" title="Path dependence">path dependent</a> institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Coffee economies in Guatemala and El Salvador, for example, were centralized around large plantations that operated under coercive labor systems. By the 19th century, their political structures were largely authoritarian and militarized. In Colombia and Costa Rica, conversely, liberal reforms were enacted at critical junctures to expand commercial agriculture, and they ultimately raised the bargaining power of the middle class. Both nations eventually developed more democratic and egalitarian institutions than their highly concentrated landowning counterparts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions_and_collections">Exhibitions and collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Exhibitions and collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_American_History" title="National Museum of American History">National Museum of American History</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Historical_Society" title="Virginia Historical Society">Virginia Historical Society</a> (VHS) co-organized a traveling exhibition to recount the strategic alliances and violent conflict between European empires (<a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a>) and the Native people living in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The exhibition was presented in three languages and with multiple perspectives. Artifacts on display included rare surviving Native and European artifacts, maps, documents, and ceremonial objects from museums and royal collections on both sides of the Atlantic. The exhibition opened in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> on March 17, 2007, and closed at the Smithsonian International Gallery on October 31, 2009.</p>
<p>The related online exhibition explores the international origins of the societies of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and commemorates the 400th anniversary of three lasting settlements in <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown</a> (1607), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Québec">Québec</a> (1608), and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe</a> (1609). The site is accessible in three languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
