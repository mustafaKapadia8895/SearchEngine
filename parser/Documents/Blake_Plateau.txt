<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blake Plateau</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Blake Plateau</b> lies in the western <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> off the southeastern United States coasts of North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia and Florida. The Blake Plateau lies between the North American <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_basin" title="Oceanic basin">deep ocean basin</a> extending about 145 kilometers (90 miles) east and west by 170 kilometers (106 miles) north and south, with a depth of about 500 meters (1,640 feet) inshore sloping to about 1,000 meters (3,281 feet) about 375 kilometers (233 miles) off shore, where the <b>Blake Escarpment</b> drops steeply to the deep basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Blake Plateau, associated <b>Blake Ridge</b> and <a href="/wiki/Blake_Basin" title="Blake Basin">Blake Basin</a> are named for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Geodetic_Survey" title="U.S. National Geodetic Survey">U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey</a> steamer <a href="/wiki/USC%26GS_George_S._Blake" title="USC&amp;GS George S. Blake"><i><b>George S. Blake</b></i></a>, in service 1874-1905,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> that first used steel cable for oceanographic operations and pioneered deep ocean and <a class="external text" href="http://oceancurrents.rsmas.miami.edu/atlantic/gulf-stream.html" rel="nofollow">Gulf Stream</a> exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Survey lines of the steamer <i>Blake</i> first defined the plateau that now bears the ship's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 1880 <i>Blake</i> under the command of Commander John R. Bartlett, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[Note 1]</a></sup> U.S.N., was working with sounding gear designed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dwight_Sigsbee" title="Charles Dwight Sigsbee">Lieutenant Commander Sigsbee</a> in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Agassiz" title="Alexander Agassiz">Alexander Agassiz</a> collecting biological samples and examining the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> running eastward from <a class="external text" href="https://maps.google.com/maps?q=Cape+Romain,+Charleston,+South+Carolina&amp;hl=en&amp;sll=38.846224,-77.306373&amp;sspn=0.098935,0.149174&amp;oq=Cape+Romain,+South+Carolina&amp;hnear=Cape+Romain&amp;t=h&amp;z=12" rel="nofollow">Cape Romain</a> when, in taking frequent soundings eastward, "depths on this line were unexpectedly small, the axis of the Gulf Stream being crossed before a depth of three hundred fathoms (1,800 feet/549 meters) was found" with a bottom of "hard coral" and little life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> This was early indication of the plateau that would in the future carry the ship's name. In 1882 Commander Bartlett described the plateau:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Instead of a deep channel in the course of the Stream as reported by Lieutenants <a href="/wiki/John_Newland_Maffitt_(privateer)" title="John Newland Maffitt (privateer)">Maffit</a> and Craven, and published in the Coast Survey Reports, our later soundings show an extensive and nearly level plateau, extending from a point to the eastward of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Bahama_Banks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Little Bahama Banks (page does not exist)">Little Bahama Banks</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a>—off <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> nearly 200 miles wide, and gradually contracting in width to the northward until reaching Hatteras, where the depth is more than 1000 fathoms within thirty miles of shore. This plateau has a general depth of 400 fathoms, suddenly dropping off on its eastern edge to over 2000 fathoms.<i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Bartlett reported the scouring effect of the current on the plateau, noting that on each side of the current the sounding cylinder, a device for sampling the nature of the bottom with the sounding, brought up ooze. Within the current the "bottom was washed nearly bare", with particles being small and broken pieces of coral rock and so hard the sharp edge of the brass cylinder was bent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to unusual features of the plateau, particularly scouring by the <a class="external text" href="http://oceancurrents.rsmas.miami.edu/atlantic/florida.html" rel="nofollow">Florida Current</a> and the <a class="external text" href="http://oceancurrents.rsmas.miami.edu/atlantic/antilles.html" rel="nofollow">Antilles Current</a> that merge over the plateau to form the Gulf Stream,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> mineral deposits, particularly <a href="/wiki/Manganese_nodule" title="Manganese nodule">manganese nodules</a>, have long been of interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" title="Methane hydrate">Methane and other gas hydrates</a> are also found on the plateau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dover_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dover-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Blake Plateau, although described as a "bleak, current-swept plain," has biological communities including <i><a href="/wiki/Lophelia" title="Lophelia">Lophelia pertusa</a></i> reefs that support communities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> as well as communities supported by gas hydrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dover_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dover-13">[12]</a></sup> Commercial fishermen have begun exploiting deep sea fish on the plateau with studies being undertaken on the viability as these fish, although large, grow slowly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Biological sampling of the deep, hard bottom is difficult under the Gulf Stream with the consequence that the fauna is relatively poorly known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
