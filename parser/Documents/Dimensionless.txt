<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dimensionless quantity</h1>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>, a <b>dimensionless quantity</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a> to which no <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">physical dimension</a> is applicable. It is also known as a <b>bare number</b> or a <b>quantity of dimension one</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the corresponding unit of measurement in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> is <b>one</b> (or <b>1</b>) unit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and it is not explicitly shown. Dimensionless quantities are widely used in many fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. By contrast, examples of quantities with dimensions are <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a>, <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a>, which are measured in dimensional units, such as <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">metre per second</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis#History" title="Dimensional analysis">Dimensional analysis § History</a></div>
<p>Quantities having dimension <b>1</b>, habitually called <i>dimensionless quantities</i>, regularly occur in sciences, and are formally treated within the field of <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>. In the nineteenth century, <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> physicist <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> led significant developments in the modern concepts of <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_(measurement)" title="Unit (measurement)">unit</a>. Later work by <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> physicists <a href="/wiki/Osborne_Reynolds" title="Osborne Reynolds">Osborne Reynolds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Rayleigh" title="Lord Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> contributed to the understanding of dimensionless numbers in physics. Building on Rayleigh's method of dimensional analysis, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Buckingham" title="Edgar Buckingham">Edgar Buckingham</a> proved the <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_%CF%80_theorem" title="Buckingham π theorem"><span class="texhtml">π</span> theorem</a> (independent of French mathematician <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bertrand" title="Joseph Bertrand">Joseph Bertrand</a>'s previous work) to formalize the nature of these quantities. Numerous other dimensionless numbers, mostly ratios, were coined in the early 1900s, particularly in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">fluid mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>. Measuring <i>ratios</i> in the (derived) unit <i>dB</i> (<a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">decibel</a>) finds widespread use nowadays. In the early <a href="/wiki/2000s_(decade)" title="2000s (decade)">2000s</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> discussed naming the unit of 1 as the 'uno', but the idea of just introducing a new SI-name for <b>1</b> was dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_numbers">Pure numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pure numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All <b><a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">pure numbers</a></b> are dimensionless quantities, for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_(number)" title="1 (number)">1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi"><span class="texhtml">π</span></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler%27s_number" title="Euler's number"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">e</span></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Golden_ratio" title="Golden ratio"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">φ</span></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Units of number such as the <a href="/wiki/Dozen" title="Dozen">dozen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gross_(unit)" title="Gross (unit)">gross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Googol" title="Googol">googol</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_number" title="Avogadro's number">Avogadro's number</a> may also be considered dimensionless.</p>
<h2><span id="Ratios,_proportions,_and_angles"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratios.2C_proportions.2C_and_angles">Ratios, proportions, and angles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ratios, proportions, and angles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dimensionless quantities are often obtained as <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratios</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a> that are not dimensionless, but whose dimensions cancel out in the mathematical operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Examples include calculating <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slopes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Conversion_of_units" title="Conversion of units">unit conversion factors</a>. A more complex example of such a ratio is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_strain" title="Engineering strain">engineering strain</a>, a measure of physical deformation defined as a change in length divided by the initial length. Since both quantities have the dimension <i>length</i>, their ratio is dimensionless. Another set of examples is <a href="/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)" title="Mass fraction (chemistry)">mass fractions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fractions</a> often written using <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts-per notation</a> such as ppm (= 10<sup>−6</sup>), ppb (= 10<sup>−9</sup>), and ppt (= 10<sup>−12</sup>), or more confusingly as ratios of two identical units (<a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>/kg or <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>/mol). For example, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>, which characterizes the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverage</a>, could be written as mL / 100 mL.</p>
<p>Other common proportions are percentages <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%25" title="%">%</a> (= 0.01),  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_mil" title="Per mil">‰</a> (= 0.001) and angle units such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radians" title="Radians">radians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gradian" title="Gradian">grads</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> the <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_variation" title="Coefficient of variation">coefficient of variation</a> is the ratio of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">mean</a> and is used to measure the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_dispersion" title="Statistical dispersion">dispersion</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_data" title="Statistical data">data</a>.</p>
<h2><span id="Buckingham_π_theorem"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Buckingham_.CF.80_theorem">Buckingham <span class="texhtml">π</span> theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Buckingham π theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_%CF%80_theorem" title="Buckingham π theorem">Buckingham <span class="texhtml">π</span> theorem</a> indicates that validity of the laws of physics does not depend on a specific unit system. A statement of this theorem is that any physical law can be expressed as an <a href="/wiki/Identity_(mathematics)" title="Identity (mathematics)">identity</a> involving only dimensionless combinations (ratios or products) of the variables linked by the law (e. g., pressure and volume are linked by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boyle%27s_Law" title="Boyle's Law">Boyle's Law</a> – they are inversely proportional). If the dimensionless combinations' values changed with the systems of units, then the equation would not be an identity, and Buckingham's theorem would not hold.</p>
<p>Another consequence of the theorem is that the <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">functional</a> dependence between a certain number (say, <i>n</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> can be reduced by the number (say, <i>k</i>) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_variable" title="Independent variable">independent</a> <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimensions</a> occurring in those variables to give a set of <i>p</i> = <i>n</i> − <i>k</i> independent, dimensionless <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a>. For the purposes of the experimenter, different systems that share the same description by dimensionless <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a> are equivalent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To demonstrate the application of the <span class="texhtml">π</span> theorem, consider the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">power</a> consumption of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stirrer" title="Stirrer">stirrer</a> with a given shape. The power, <i>P</i>, in dimensions [M · L<sup>2</sup>/T<sup>3</sup>], is a function of the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <i>ρ</i> [M/L<sup>3</sup>], and the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the fluid to be stirred, <i>μ</i> [M/(L · T)], as well as the size of the stirrer given by its <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, <i>D</i> [L], and the <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular speed</a> of the stirrer, <i>n</i> [1/T]. Therefore, we have a total of <i>n</i> = 5 variables representing our example. Those <i>n</i> = 5 variables are built up from <i>k</i> = 3 fundamental dimensions, the length: L (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> units: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meters" title="Meters">m</a>), time: T (<a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">s</a>), and mass: M (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilograms" title="Kilograms">kg</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dimensionless_physical_constants">Dimensionless physical constants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dimensionless physical constants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_physical_constant" title="Dimensionless physical constant">Dimensionless physical constant</a></div>
<p>Certain fundamental physical constants, such as the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in a vacuum, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_gravitational_constant" title="Universal gravitational constant">universal gravitational constant</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck%27s_constant" title="Planck's constant">Planck's constant</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_constant" title="Boltzmann's constant">Boltzmann's constant</a> can be normalized to 1 if appropriate units for <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> are chosen. The resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_of_units" title="System of units">system of units</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Natural_units" title="Natural units">natural units</a>. However, not all <a href="/wiki/Physical_constant" title="Physical constant">physical constants</a> can be normalized in this fashion. For example, the values of the following constants are independent of the system of units and must be determined experimentally:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>α</i> ≈ 1/137, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_structure_constant" title="Fine structure constant">fine structure constant</a>, which characterizes the magnitude of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interaction" title="Electromagnetic interaction">electromagnetic interaction</a> between electrons.</li>
<li><i>β</i> (or <i>μ</i>) ≈ 1836, the <a href="/wiki/Proton-to-electron_mass_ratio" title="Proton-to-electron mass ratio">proton-to-electron mass ratio</a>. This ratio is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> divided by that of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. An analogous ratio can be defined for any <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particle</a>;</li>
<li><i>α</i><sub>s</sub>, a constant characterizing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_force" title="Strong force">strong force</a>;</li>
<li><i>α</i><sub>G</sub> ≈ 1.75 × 10<sup>−45</sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_coupling_constant" title="Gravitational coupling constant">gravitational coupling constant</a>, which characterizes the magnitude of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitational interaction</a> between electrons.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_quantities_produced_by_nondimensionalization">Other quantities produced by nondimensionalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other quantities produced by nondimensionalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_dimensionless_quantities" title="List of dimensionless quantities">List of dimensionless quantities</a></div>
<p>Physics often uses dimensionless <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a> to simplify the characterization of systems with multiple interacting physical phenomena. These may be found by applying the <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_%CF%80_theorem" title="Buckingham π theorem">Buckingham <span class="texhtml">π</span> theorem</a> or otherwise may emerge from making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equations" title="Partial differential equations">partial differential equations</a> unitless by the process of <a href="/wiki/Nondimensionalization" title="Nondimensionalization">nondimensionalization</a>. Engineering, economics, and other fields often extend these ideas in <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> and analysis of the relevant systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_and_engineering">Physics and engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Physics and engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fresnel_number" title="Fresnel number">Fresnel number</a> – wavenumber over distance</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a> is commonly used in fluid mechanics to characterize flow, incorporating both properties of the fluid and the flow. It is interpreted as the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces and can indicate flow regime as well as correlate to frictional heating in application to flow in pipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach number</a> – ratio of the speed of an object or flow relative to the speed of sound in the fluid.</li>
</ul>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_numbers_in_fluid_mechanics" title="Dimensionless numbers in fluid mechanics">Dimensionless numbers in fluid mechanics</a></div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beta_(plasma_physics)" title="Beta (plasma physics)">Beta (plasma physics)</a> - ratio of plasma pressure to magnetic pressure, used in magnetospheric physics as well as fusion plasma physics.</li>
</ul>
