<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pressure gradient</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In atmospheric science (<a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a> and related fields), the <b>pressure gradient</b> (typically of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>, more generally of any <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a>) is a physical quantity that describes which direction and at what rate the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> changes the most rapidly around a particular location. The pressure gradient is a dimensional quantity expressed in <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">units</a> of pressure per unit length, or <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">m</a></p>
<p>Mathematically, it is obtained by applying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Del_operator" title="Del operator">del operator</a> to a pressure function of position. The negative gradient of pressure is known as the <a href="/wiki/Force_density" title="Force density">force density</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_geology" title="Petroleum geology">Petroleum Geology</a> and Petrochemical sciences pertaining to <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil wells</a>, and more specifically within <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatics" title="Hydrostatics">Hydrostatic Pressure</a>, pressure gradients refer to the <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> of vertical pressure in a column of fluid within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wellbore" title="Wellbore">wellbore</a> generally expressed in psi/ft. this column of fluid is subject to the compound pressure gradient of the overlying fluids. The path and geometry of the column is totally irrelevant, only the vertical depth of the column has any relevance on the vertical pressure of any point within its column and the pressure gradient for any given <a href="/wiki/True_vertical_depth" title="True vertical depth">true vertical depth</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_interpretation">Physical interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_gradient&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Strictly speaking, the concept of pressure gradient is a <i>local</i> characterisation of the air (more generally of the fluid under investigation). The pressure gradient is defined only at those spatial scales at which pressure (more generally <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>) itself is defined.</p>
<p>Within <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planetary</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheres</a> (including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's</a>), the pressure gradient is a vector pointing roughly downwards, because the pressure changes most rapidly vertically, increasing downwards, see <a href="/wiki/Vertical_pressure_variation" title="Vertical pressure variation">vertical pressure variation</a>. The value of the strength (or <a href="/wiki/Norm_(mathematics)" title="Norm (mathematics)">norm</a>) of the pressure gradient in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> is typically of the order 9 Pa/m (or 90 hPa/km).</p>
<p>The pressure gradient often has a small but critical horizontal component, which is largely responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> circulation. The <b>horizontal pressure gradient</b> is a 2-dimensional vector resulting from the projection of the pressure gradient onto a local horizontal plane.</p>
<p>Near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_surface" title="Earth's surface">Earth's surface</a>, this horizontal pressure gradient force is directed from higher toward lower pressure. Its particular orientation at any one time and place depends strongly on the weather situation. At mid-<a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>, the typical horizontal pressure gradient may take on values of the order of 10<sup>−2</sup> Pa/m (or 10 Pa/km), although rather higher values occur within <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">meteorological fronts</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_and_climate_relevance">Weather and climate relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_gradient&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Weather and climate relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Differences in air pressure between different locations are critical in weather forecasting and climate. As indicated above, the pressure gradient constitutes one of the main forces acting on the air to make it move as wind. Note that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a> points from high towards low pressure zones, it is thus oriented in the opposite direction from the pressure gradient itself.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">Sound</a> waves and <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a> are events that can induce very large pressure gradients, but these are often transitory disturbances.</p>
