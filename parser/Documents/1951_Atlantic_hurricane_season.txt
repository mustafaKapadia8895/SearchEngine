<p>The <b>1951 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was the first hurricane season in which tropical cyclones were officially named by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NamesTCs_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NamesTCs-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">season</a> officially started on June 15, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">United States Weather Bureau</a> began its daily monitoring for tropical cyclone activity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monitoring_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monitoring-2">[2]</a></sup> the season officially ended on November 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeasonStart_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SeasonStart-3">[3]</a></sup> It was the first year since <a href="/wiki/1937_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1937 Atlantic hurricane season">1937</a> in which no hurricanes made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the United States;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_hurricanes_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_hurricanes-4">[4]</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_How_(1951)" title="Hurricane How (1951)">Hurricane How</a> was the only tropical storm to hit the nation, the season had the least tropical cyclone damage in the United States since the <a href="/wiki/1939_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1939 Atlantic hurricane season">1939 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> As in the <a href="/wiki/1950_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1950 Atlantic hurricane season">1950 season</a>, names from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Army/Navy_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet">Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet</a> were used to name storms this season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LandseaPhonetic_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LandseaPhonetic-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first hurricane of the season, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Able_(1951)" title="Hurricane Able (1951)">Able</a>, formed prior to the official start of the season; before <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis_project" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis project">reanalysis</a> in 2015, it was once listed as the earliest major hurricane on record in the Atlantic basin. It formed on May 16 and executed a counterclockwise loop over the Bahamas; later it brushed the North Carolina coastline. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charlie_(1951)" title="Hurricane Charlie (1951)">Hurricane Charlie</a> was a powerful Category 4 hurricane that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">struck</a> Jamaica as a major hurricane, killing hundreds and becoming the worst disaster in over 50 years. The hurricane later struck Mexico twice as a major hurricane, producing deadly flooding outside of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a>. The strongest hurricane, Easy, spent its duration over the open Atlantic Ocean, briefly threatening Bermuda, and was formerly listed as one of a relatively few <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> hurricanes on record over the Atlantic Ocean. It briefly neared Category 5 status and <a href="/wiki/Fujiwhara_effect" title="Fujiwhara effect">interacted</a> with Hurricane Fox, marking the first known instance of a hurricane affecting another's path.</p>

<p>As the calendar entered the <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">new year</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a> occurred with a non-tropical <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone#Formation" title="Extratropical cyclone">frontal wave</a> over the western North Atlantic Ocean due to a closed <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low</a> forming in a mid-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>, which eventually produced a low-pressure center at the <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface</a> by January 2. Ships recorded moderate gales up to 60 <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">miles per hour</a> (97 <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a>) in connection with the new surface low, which formed several hundred miles east-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-7">[7]</a></sup> While initially lacking tropical attributes, the cyclone headed southeast for two days before curving southwestward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> As it did so, the temperature of the system warmed in its lower levels, causing the cyclone to evolve into a more <a href="/wiki/Barotropic_fluid" title="Barotropic fluid">barotropic</a> system. Late on January 4, the system shrunk in size and began developing an inner core; reanalysis determined that the system became a tropical storm at this time, though it would have likely been considered <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical</a> beginning in the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Able_(1951)" title="Hurricane Able (1951)">Hurricane Able (1951)</a></div>
<p>The origins of the first hurricane of the season were from a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> that exited the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> on May 12. A <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> developed on May 14, and two days later it developed into a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> about 300 miles (480 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. It formed beneath an upper-level low, and initially was not fully tropical. The depression followed the low, initially toward the northwest and later the southwest. Moving over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>, the depression intensified into Tropical Storm Able on May 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-9">[9]</a></sup> The storm turned to the south, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> reported that Able strengthened to hurricane status on May 17 off the coast of Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The outer <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> of Able produced light rainfall and high seas along the Florida coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up518_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-up518-10">[10]</a></sup> It later moved through the northern Bahamas early on May 18, where it produced hurricane-force winds of 85 mph (137 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> The hurricane later turned to the north, gradually strengthening through May 21. Shortly thereafter, Able passed about 70 miles (110 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a> before turning east and reaching its peak of 90 mph (145 km/h) early on May 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Along the coast, the hurricane produced high tides but little damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCtides_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCtides-11">[11]</a></sup> Able maintained hurricane intensity for two more days before weakening to a tropical storm early on May 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Able rapidly dissipated that same day, though originally it was assessed as having transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> on May 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 2015, Able was listed as having peak winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) and was analyzed to have been the earliest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#North_Atlantic" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane</a> on record. Such a storm would be a Category 3 or greater on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>, a system developed and introduced in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams-1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-1-12">[12]</a></sup> Able was also the strongest hurricane outside of the current hurricane season (June 1 through November 30).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OffseasonDorst_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-OffseasonDorst-13">[13]</a></sup> However, reanalysis by scientists in 2015 determined that Able was in fact far weaker than originally listed in <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">HURDAT</a>, the official database containing information on storm tracks and intensities in the Atlantic and Eastern North Pacific regions. It also lost its distinction as the strongest preseason cyclone on record, the record being held by a <a href="/wiki/1908_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_One" title="1908 Atlantic hurricane season">Category 2 hurricane</a> in March 1908.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> The hurricane was one of four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic hurricanes</a> on record to exist during the month of May, the others occurring in <a href="/wiki/1889_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1889 Atlantic hurricane season">1889</a>, <a href="/wiki/1908_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1908 Atlantic hurricane season">1908</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1970_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Alma" title="1970 Atlantic hurricane season">1970</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>On August 2, an <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">easterly wave</a> spawned a tropical depression about 680 miles (1095 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbuda" title="Barbuda">Barbuda</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. It moved northwestward, quickly strengthening into Tropical Storm Baker. Early on August 3, the storm attained peak winds of 60 mph (97 km/h), and the next day passed about 275 mi (443 km) east of Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> At its peak intensity, the gale force winds extended 100 miles (160 km) to the north of the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BakerGales_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-BakerGales-14">[14]</a></sup> After attaining its peak, Baker quickly weakened on August 4 and turned to the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Early the next day, it regained some of its former strength, peaking again at 50 mph (80 km/h) before losing its identity. It never affected land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charlie_(1951)" title="Hurricane Charlie (1951)">Hurricane Charlie (1951)</a></div>
<p>The third tropical cyclone of the season developed on August 12 from a tropical wave, 930 miles (1,495 km) east-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>. After a few days without further development, the system intensified into Tropical Storm Charlie on August 14, and subsequently crossed through the Lesser Antilles a day later with winds of 70 mph (113 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Shortly after entering the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, the storm intensified to hurricane status early on August 16. Passing south of Puerto Rico and Hispaniola, Charlie then underwent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapid deepening</a> beginning late that day, its winds increasing 35 mph (56 km/h) in 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> As it neared the island of Jamaica early on August 18, Charlie became a major hurricane and shortly afterward struck just south of <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a> with winds of 125 mph (201 km/h)—equivalent to a strong Category 3 hurricane on the Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale, making Charlie, along with <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a> in <a href="/wiki/1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1988 Atlantic hurricane season">1988</a>, the strongest ever to hit the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> On the island, the hurricane dropped heavy rainfall up to 17 in (430 mm). The combination of strong winds and the rains left around $50 million (1951 USD, $380 million 2005 USD) in crop and property damage. Across the country there were 152 deaths, 2,000 injuries, and 25,000 people left homeless; as a result, it was considered the worst disaster in the country in the 20th century until Hurricane Gilbert produced even costlier damage, though with fewer reported fatalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GilbertDamage_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-GilbertDamage-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Storm_and_its_Effects_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Storm_and_its_Effects-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After making landfall, Charlie weakened in its passage over the mountainous center of Jamaica, and by the time it left the island, its winds had diminished to 85 mph (137 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Charlie later passed south of the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a> reporting peak wind gusts of 92 mph (148 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> As it did so, the storm began to undergo yet another period of rapid intensification beginning on August 19. It re-attained major hurricane status late that day, and early on August 20 Charlie peaked at 130 mph (209 km/h), equivalent to low-end Category 4 status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Maintaining its strength, the hurricane then made landfall on the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a> and hit the Mexican mainland near <a href="/wiki/Akumal" title="Akumal">Akumal</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>. The strong winds destroyed 70% of the crops along its path, although no deaths were reported in the Yucatán Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> Several homes were wrecked in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valley_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Valley-17">[17]</a></sup> As it moved inland, Charlie weakened rapidly over land, reaching the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> as a minimal hurricane early on August 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Once over water, it failed to re-intensify for a full day, but began doing so early on August 22. As it did so, it rapidly re-intensified for a third and final time, reaching peak winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) before striking near the city of <a href="/wiki/Miramar,_Tamaulipas" title="Miramar, Tamaulipas">Miramar</a>, just north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It dissipated on August 23. The hurricane dropped heavy rainfall in the region, flooding rivers and causing dams to burst. Hundreds of people were killed in the Mexican mainland, and across Charlie's entire path, damage was estimated at over $75 million (1951 USD, $692 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> The outer fringes of the storm increased surf along the Texas coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CharlieTexas_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-CharlieTexas-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical wave spawned a tropical depression on August 27 southwest of Cape Verde .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-14"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It moved westward, eventually intensifying into a tropical storm early on August 31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> The next day, the storm was first observed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a>, several hundred miles east of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, and it was named "Dog".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> By that time, its winds were around 60 mph (97 km/h), and the storm continued intensifying as it approached the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-16"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> On September 2, Dog attained hurricane status,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> reaching its peak of 90 mph (145 km/h) as it passed between the islands of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-17"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm, then quite small in diameter, produced strong wind gusts of up to 115 mph (185 km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Martinique_Aim%C3%A9_C%C3%A9saire_International_Airport" title="Martinique Aimé Césaire International Airport">the airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort-de-France" title="Fort-de-France">Fort-de-France</a> on Martinique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> However, this peak was short-lived, for upon entering the eastern Caribbean Sea Dog began a slow but steady weakening trend. On September 4, Dog weakened to tropical storm status to the south of Hispaniola, and the next day dissipated in the western Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-18"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In northern Saint Lucia, the combination of flooding and high winds destroyed 70% of the banana crop. Two sailing vessels were destroyed, and another one damaged. Across the island, Hurricane Dog killed two people from drownings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> Damage was heavier on Martinique, located on the north side of the storm. The hurricane's winds destroyed 1,000 homes and the roofs of several others. Downed trees blocked roads and disrupted power lines. The winds also destroyed 90% of the banana crop and 30% of the sugar cane. Throughout Martinique, Dog left $3 million in damage (1951 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $27.7 million 2017 USD) and killed five people from drownings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> It was considered the "most violent storm" in Martinique in 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap93_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap93-19">[19]</a></sup> Initially the hurricane was expected to strike Jamaica, prompting hurricane warnings for the country, as well as along the southern coast of Hispaniola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap93_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap93-19">[19]</a></sup> Jamaica was earlier struck by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charlie_(1951)" title="Hurricane Charlie (1951)">Hurricane Charlie</a> a few weeks prior, and the threat from Dog prompted coastal evacuations and the closure of an airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DejaVuDog_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-DejaVuDog-20">[20]</a></sup> Ultimately, Dog dissipated and produced only light rainfall on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DogDissipates_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-DogDissipates-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hurricane Easy, the strongest tropical cyclone of the season, was a powerful and long-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> that originated as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#tropical_depression" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical depression</a> on September 1 between the Lesser Antilles and Cape Verde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-19"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Moving generally west-northwestward, the depression deepened into a tropical storm late that day, and further to hurricane status by September 3. On September 5, the cyclone reached its first peak of 110 mph (177 km/h), but failed to continue strengthening. Its winds fluctuated through the early morning on September 6, but then resumed strengthening, reaching major hurricane status by that evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-20"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> During this period, Hurricane Hunters flew into the hurricane to monitor its progress, recording a minimum pressure of 957 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">millibars</a> (28.26 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) on September 6 to the north of the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> The next day, as the storm continued strengthening, an aircraft was unable to penetrate the center, estimating winds of 160 mph (257 km/h) south of the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>. On this basis, Easy was once classified as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5 hurricane</a> on the Saffir-Simpson scale;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-22">[22]</a></sup> early estimates of the winds by reconnaissance aircraft were often based on visual cues, such as the state of the sea surface, and were thus subjective and often unreliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hagen2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hagen2012-23">[23]</a></sup> Subsequent reanalysis in 2015 lowered its peak winds to 150 mph (240 km/h) on September 8. This was based on the Hurricane Hunters reporting a pressure of 937 mb (27.67 inHg) on the previous day, and a ship reporting winds of 140 mph (220 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-meta-22">[22]</a></sup> By the time Easy attained peak intensity, it had turned to the north and northeast while beginning a steady weakening trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-21"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It <a href="/wiki/Fujiwhara_effect" title="Fujiwhara effect">interacted</a> with the small Hurricane Fox to the east; this was the first observed instance of a hurricane affecting another's path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s910_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-s910-24">[24]</a></sup> Easy then turned to the northeast, passing a short distance southeast of Bermuda on September 9 with winds of 110 mph (177 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-22"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Easy transitioned into an extratropical cyclone late on September 11, while still maintaining hurricane-force winds. The remnants lost their hurricane-force winds on September 12, only to briefly regain them two days later. On September 14, Easy lost its identity over the northern Atlantic Ocean after it was absorbed by another extratropical storm to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Weather Bureau advised the island to take precautionary measures in advance of the storm;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> tourists and residents "worked feverishly" to complete preparations, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> issued "a formal warning at noon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waterloo_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waterloo-26">[26]</a></sup> Numerous hotels and homes were shuttered. Heavy traffic snarled evacuations, and 100 tourists were stranded on the island without "roundtrip reservations." Air Force aircraft returned to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and personnel secured various facilities at the island's base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waterloo_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Waterloo-26">[26]</a></sup> On Bermuda, the hurricane produced winds of only 50 mph (80 km/h), which downed a few banana trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s910_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-s910-24">[24]</a></sup> In addition to affecting Bermuda, the strong winds of the hurricane damaged a few ships along its path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Around the same time as Easy was forming, a new tropical depression developed in the far eastern Atlantic Ocean. Moving generally westward, it passed south of the Cape Verde islands, quickly strengthening into Tropical Storm Fox early on September 3; by that time, its motion turned to the west-northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-23"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> On September 5, Fox attained hurricane status, around the same time as it was first observed by ships. Two days later, Hurricane Hunters reported peak winds of 115 mph (185 km/h), making it a major hurricane, albeit one of very small extent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-21"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> Around that time, Fox interacted with Hurricane Easy to its northwest. After maintaining peak winds for 12 hours, Fox began a steady weakening trend, accelerating to the north and northeast ahead of Easy and passing to the east of Bermuda. On September 10, Fox, while still of hurricane force, became extratropical between the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> in the far north Atlantic. It turned toward the north and dissipated on September 11 off the southwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. Although a few ships were affected by the hurricane's winds, there were no reports of any damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-22"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-24"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical Storm George developed in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on September 19. Moving west-northwestward, it quickly attained peak winds of 60 mph (97 km/h) late on the next day, as reported by the Hurricane Hunters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-23"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-25"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> George later made landfall on September 21 in Mexico about 55 mi (90 km) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico</a> as a moderate tropical storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-24"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-26"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Before it moved ashore, the storm spread rainfall along the coast and increased waves, causing one drowning death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> George quickly dissipated upon making landfall, and there were no reports of damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-25"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easterly_wave" title="Easterly wave">easterly wave</a> spawned a tropical depression in the western Caribbean Sea on September 29. It moved north-northwestward for a few days before turning eastward in the central Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-27"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Based on Hurricane Hunter reports, the depression intensified into Tropical Storm How late on September 30, and it continued to strengthen as it approached <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Florida" title="Southwest Florida">Southwest Florida</a>. On October 2, How attained its first peak of 65 mph (105 km/h) just before making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Boca_Grande,_Florida" title="Boca Grande, Florida">Boca Grande</a>, and within the day it crossed southern Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-28"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> At the time, the storm was not well organized, and its strongest winds were confined to squalls in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">southeast coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-26"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> Wind damage was minor, although heavy rainfall was reported,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-27"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> peaking at 15.7 inches (40 cm) near where it moved ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_Rain_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Florida_Rain-28">[28]</a></sup> The precipitation caused significant street flooding, while about 7,000 acres (28 km<sup>2</sup>) of tomato and bean fields were deluged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-florida_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-florida-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QPFRoth_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-QPFRoth-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm emerged into the Atlantic Ocean between <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pierce,_Florida" title="Fort Pierce, Florida">Fort Pierce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vero_Beach,_Florida" title="Vero Beach, Florida">Vero Beach</a>, quickly intensifying to hurricane strength by October 3. Turning northeastward, How reached its second and strongest peak of 100 mph (161 km/h) on October 4 as it passed near the Outer Banks of North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-28"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-29"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Along the coast, the hurricane produced high tides and minor damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap-31">[31]</a></sup> Subsequently, the hurricane briefly weakened, only to recover its peak of 100 mph (161 km/h) on October 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-30"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> It passed southeast of Cape Cod before turning more to the east-northeast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-29"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> causing road closures due to high tides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap-31">[31]</a></sup> Offshore, the hurricane sank a ship, killing 17 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mil_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mil-32">[32]</a></sup> While still of hurricane force, How became an extratropical storm on October 6, and a few days later it curved to the northeast. The extratropical cyclone later struck <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> near <a href="/wiki/Hvolsv%C3%B6llur" title="Hvolsvöllur">Hvolsvöllur</a> with winds of 75 mph (121 km/h) on October 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-31"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> A couple of days later, the remnants of How dissipated in the far northern Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-30"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup> Overall, Hurricane How caused about $2 million (1951 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $18.5 million 2017 USD) in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-31"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical depression formed southwest of Jamaica on October 12. A small system, it moved northwestward and intensified into Tropical Storm Item on October 13. It turned toward the north, and the next day attained peak winds of 65 mph (105 km/h) after moving through the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>. Concurrently, the Hurricane Hunters observed peak winds of 80 mph (129 km/h), and on this basis, Item was operationally upgraded to a minimal hurricane. Although it retained this status in HURDAT for years, subsequent reanalysis in 2015 found the reconnaissance estimates to have been too high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-32"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-32"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> Item maintained its peak for more than a day before weakening. It lost tropical storm status on October 16 as it drifted to the northwest. Continuing a slow weakening trend, it passed just east of the <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isla de la Juventud</a> before striking western Cuba as a tropical depression on October 17. Later that day it dissipated in the southeastern Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-33"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-33"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The threat of the hurricane prompted precautions to be made in portions of Cuba. Additionally, storm warnings were posted in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>, southern mainland Florida, as well as the Bahamas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, no damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-34"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Jig">Hurricane Jig</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hurricane Jig">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>One of the last tropical cyclones of the season formed on October 15 just northeast of the Bahamas. Although listed as a tropical storm, it would have likely been classified as a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> beginning in the 1970s, but was unable to be classified as such given the lack of satellite imagery to prove its status. Given the name "Jig", it moved northeastward, quickly attaining hurricane status with winds of 75 mph (121 km/h), which it maintained for a full day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-34"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> On October 16, Jig began a slow weakening trend, weakening below hurricane force and turning sharply northeastward. During this time, the storm made its closest approach to the southeastern United States while passing well southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-35"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-35"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup> While offshore, the storm increased surf along the North Carolina and Virginia coastlines, prompting storm warnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Early October 18, Jig became extratropical with winds of 70 mph (113 km/h) and began a counterclockwise loop over the western Atlantic. The next day it turned to the southeast before dissipating about 230 mi (370 km) south of Bermuda on October 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-36"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-36"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Twelve">Hurricane Twelve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hurricane Twelve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">
<tr>
<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold; background: #ffffcc">Category 1 hurricane (SSHWS)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center">
<div style="vertical-align: middle"><span class="nowrap"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png"><img alt="1951 Atlantic hurricane 12 track.png" data-file-height="1669" data-file-width="2700" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png/150px-1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png/225px-1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png/300px-1951_Atlantic_hurricane_12_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a><span class="nowrap"> </span></span></div>
</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th scope="row">Duration</th>
<td>December 7 – December 11</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th scope="row">Peak intensity</th>
<td>80 mph (130 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small> ≤ 995 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>In early December, a cold front passed north of Bermuda. A disturbance along the front began rotating on December 2, developing into a small but powerful extratropical storm on the next day. By late on December 3, the storm attained hurricane-force winds, and it increasingly became the dominant system within the broad frontal region. A ridge to the east turned this storm to the southwest. The winds diminished below hurricane-intensity on December 5, and concurrently the inner structure became more tropical as the frontal features dissipated. During this time, ships in the region reported strong winds, mostly to the north. Increasing water temperatures fueled atmospheric instability, likely causing an increase in convection, and the system was potentially a subtropical cyclone on December 6, while located about 1,015 mi (1,635 km) east-northeast of Bermuda. A nearby ship recorded a minimum pressure of 987 mbar (29.1 inHg) around that time. After the storm turned to the southeast, a ship in the region reported winds of 75 mph (120 km/h) near the center and a pressure of 995 mbar (29.4 inHg), while a weather station indicated that the system had a warm core. The data suggested that the system became a fully tropical hurricane by 12:00 UTC on December 7, and that it likely had transitioned into a tropical storm six hours earlier. By 18:00 UTC that night, the hurricane attained peak winds of 80 mph (130 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 8, the hurricane turned to the east and weakened into a tropical storm, steered by an approaching trough. Over the next day, the storm accelerated to the east-northeast toward the Azores. Late on December 10, the storm moved through the Azores as a tropical storm, although it was transitioning back into an extratropical storm at the time. By 06:00 UTC on December 11, the system was extratropical again after it rejoined with a nearby cold front. It likely merged with another nontropical storm to its east on December 12, although it is possible the former hurricane remained a distinct system. A building ridge near <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> forced the extratropical system to the southeast, eventually dissipating after moving ashore <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> on December 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
