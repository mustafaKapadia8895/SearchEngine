<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Supercontinent cycle</h1>

<p>The <b>supercontinent cycle</b> is the quasi-periodic aggregation and dispersal of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>. There are varying opinions as to whether the amount of continental crust is increasing, decreasing, or staying about the same, but it is agreed that the Earth's crust is constantly being reconfigured. One complete <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> cycle is said to take 300 to 500 million years. <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">Continental collision</a> makes fewer and larger continents while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rifting" title="Rifting">rifting</a> makes more and smaller continents.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercontinent_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Platetechsimple.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="2000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Platetechsimple.png/220px-Platetechsimple.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Platetechsimple.png/330px-Platetechsimple.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Platetechsimple.png/440px-Platetechsimple.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_supercontinents" title="List of supercontinents">List of supercontinents</a></div>
<p>The most recent supercontinent, <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, formed about 300 million years ago. There are two different views on the history of earlier supercontinents. The first proposes a series of supercontinents: <a href="/wiki/Vaalbara" title="Vaalbara">Vaalbara</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 3.6 to c. 2.8 billion years ago); <a href="/wiki/Ur_(continent)" title="Ur (continent)">Ur</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 3 billion years ago); <a href="/wiki/Kenorland" title="Kenorland">Kenorland</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 2.7 to 2.1 billion years ago); <a href="/wiki/Columbia_(supercontinent)" title="Columbia (supercontinent)">Columbia</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1.8 to 1.5 billion years ago); <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1.25 billion to 750 million years ago); and <a href="/wiki/Pannotia" title="Pannotia">Pannotia</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 600 million years ago), whose dispersal produced the fragments that ultimately collided to form Pangaea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhao1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhao1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhao2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhao2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second view (Protopangea-Paleopangea), based on both <a href="/wiki/Paleomagnetism" title="Paleomagnetism">palaeomagnetic</a> and geological evidence, is that supercontinent cycles did not occur before about 0.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigayear#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Gigayear">Ga</a> (during the <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a> Period). Instead, the continental crust comprised a single supercontinent from about 2.7 Ga (Gigaanum, or "billion years ago") until it broke up for the first time, somewhere around 0.6 Ga. This reconstruction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-piper_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-piper-3">[3]</a></sup> is based on the observation that if only small peripheral modifications are made to the primary reconstruction, the data show that the palaeomagnetic poles converged to quasi-static positions for long intervals between about 2.7–2.2, 1.5–1.25 and 0.75–0.6 Ga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-piper2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-piper2-4">[4]</a></sup> During the intervening periods, the poles appear to have conformed to a unified <a href="/wiki/Apparent_polar_wander" title="Apparent polar wander">apparent polar wander</a> path. Thus the paleomagnetic data are adequately explained by the existence of a single Protopangea–Paleopangea supercontinent with prolonged quasi-integrity. The prolonged duration of this supercontinent could be explained by the operation of <i>lid tectonics</i> (comparable to the tectonics operating on Mars and Venus) during <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a> times, as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> seen on the contemporary Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-piper_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-piper-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The kinds of minerals found inside ancient diamonds suggest that the cycle of supercontinental formation and breakup began roughly 3.0 billion years ago. Before 3.2 billion years ago only diamonds with <a href="/wiki/Peridot" title="Peridot">peridotitic</a> compositions (commonly found in the Earth's mantle) formed, whereas after 3.0 billion years ago eclogitic diamonds (rocks from the Earth's surface crust) became prevalent. This change is thought to have come about because the process of subduction and continental collision introduced <a href="/wiki/Eclogite" title="Eclogite">eclogite</a> into subcontinental diamond-forming fluids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hypothesized supercontinent cycle is complemented by the Wilson cycle named after <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> pioneer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._Tuzo_Wilson" title="J. Tuzo Wilson">J. Tuzo Wilson</a>, which describes the periodic opening and closing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">ocean basins</a>. Because the oldest seafloor material found today dates to only 170 million years old, whereas the oldest continental crust material found today dates to at least 4 billion years old, it makes sense to emphasize the much longer record of the planetary pulse that is recorded in the continents.</p>

<p>It is known that <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> is generally low when the continents are together and high when they are apart. For example, sea level was low at the time of formation of Pangaea (<a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a>) and Pannotia (latest <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a>), and rose rapidly to maxima during <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> times, when the continents were dispersed. This is because the age of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_lithosphere" title="Oceanic lithosphere">oceanic lithosphere</a> provides a major control on the depth of the ocean basins, and therefore on global sea level. Oceanic lithosphere forms at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridges" title="Mid-ocean ridges">mid-ocean ridges</a> and moves outwards. As this happens, it <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">conductively cools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">shrinks</a>. This cooling and shrinking decreases the thickness and increases the density of the oceanic lithosphere, and the result is the general lowering in elevation of the seafloor away from mid-ocean ridges. For oceanic lithosphere that is less than about 75 million years old, a simple cooling <a href="/wiki/Half-space_(geometry)" title="Half-space (geometry)">half-space</a> model of conductive cooling works, in which the depth of the ocean basins <i>d</i> in areas in which there is no nearby <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> is a function of the age of the oceanic lithosphere <i>t</i>. <p>where <i>d</i> is in meters and <i>t</i> is in millions of years, so that just-formed crust at the mid-ocean ridges lies at about 2,500 m depth, whereas 50-million-year-old seafloor lies at a depth of about 5,000 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-analysis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-analysis-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the mean level of the sea floor decreases, the volume of the ocean basins increases, and if other factors that can control sea level remain constant, sea level falls. The converse is also true: younger oceanic lithosphere leads to shallower oceans and higher sea levels if other factors remain constant.</p>
<p>Area <i>A</i>, of the oceans, can change when continents <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rift</a> (stretching the continents decreases A and raises sea level) or as a result of <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">continental collision</a> (compressing the continents leads to an increase <i>A</i> and lowers sea level). Increasing sea level will flood the continents, while decreasing sea level will expose continental shelves.</p>
<p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> has a very low slope, a small increase in sea level will result in a large change in the percent of continents flooded.</p>
<p>If the world ocean on average is young, the seafloor will be relatively shallow, and sea level will be high: more of the continents are flooded. If the world ocean is on average old, seafloor will be relatively deep, and sea level will be low: more of the continents will be exposed.</p>
<p>There is thus a relatively simple relationship between the supercontinent cycle and the mean age of the seafloor.</p>
<ul>
<li>Supercontinent = lots of old seafloor = low sea level</li>
<li>Dispersed continents = lots of young seafloor = high sea level</li>
</ul>
<p>There will also be a climatic effect of the supercontinent cycle that will amplify this further:</p>
<ul>
<li>Supercontinent = continental climate dominant = continental glaciation likely = still lower sea level</li>
<li>Dispersed continents = maritime climate dominant = continental glaciation unlikely = sea level is not lowered by this mechanism</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_global_tectonics">Relation to global tectonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercontinent_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to global tectonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a progression of tectonic regimes that accompany the supercontinent cycle:</p>
<p>During break-up of the supercontinent, rifting environments dominate. This is followed by passive margin environments, while seafloor spreading continues and the oceans grow. This in turn is followed by the development of collisional environments that become increasingly important with time. First collisions are between continents and island arcs, but lead ultimately to continent-continent collisions. This is the situation that was observed during the Paleozoic supercontinent cycle and is being observed for the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a>–<a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> supercontinent cycle, still in progress.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth" title="Greenhouse and icehouse Earth">Greenhouse and icehouse Earth</a></div>
<p>There are two types of global earth climates: icehouse and greenhouse. Icehouse is characterized by frequent continental glaciations and severe desert environments. Greenhouse is characterized by warm climates. Both reflect the supercontinent cycle. It is now a short greenhouse phase of an icehouse world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Read.2C_PhD_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Read.2C_PhD-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Icehouse climate</b>
<ul>
<li>Continents moving together</li>
<li>Sea level low due to lack of seafloor production</li>
<li>Climate cooler, arid</li>
<li>Associated with <a href="/wiki/Aragonite_sea" title="Aragonite sea">aragonite seas</a></li>
<li>Formation of <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinents</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Greenhouse climate</b>
<ul>
<li>Continents dispersed</li>
<li>Sea level high</li>
<li>High level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor_spreading" title="Sea floor spreading">sea floor spreading</a></li>
<li>Relatively large amounts of CO<sub>2</sub> production at oceanic rifting zones</li>
<li>Climate warm and humid</li>
<li>Associated with <a href="/wiki/Calcite_sea" title="Calcite sea">calcite seas</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Periods of icehouse climate: much of <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a>, late <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a>, late <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>.</p>
<p>Periods of greenhouse climate: Early <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a>–early <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_evolution">Relation to evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercontinent_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation to evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principal mechanism for evolution is natural selection among diverse populations. As <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a> occurs more frequently in small populations, diversity is an observed consequence of isolation. Less isolation, and thus less diversification, occurs when the continents are all together, producing both one continent and one ocean with one coast. In Latest Neoproterozoic to Early Paleozoic times, when the tremendous proliferation of diverse <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">metazoa</a> occurred, isolation of marine environments resulted from the breakup of Pannotia.</p>
<p>A north–south arrangement of continents and oceans leads to much more diversity and isolation than east–west arrangements. North-to-south arrangements give climatically different zones along the communication routes to the north and south, which are separated by water or land from other continental or oceanic zones of similar climate. Formation of similar tracts of continents and ocean basins oriented east–west would lead to much less isolation, diversification, and slower evolution, since each continent or ocean is in fewer climatic zones. Through the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>, isolation has been maximized by a north–south arrangement.</p>
<p>Diversity, as measured by the number of families, follows the supercontinent cycle very well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
