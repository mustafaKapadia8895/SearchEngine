<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Central Atlantic magmatic province</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_Magmatic_Province&amp;redirect=no" title="Central Atlantic Magmatic Province">Central Atlantic Magmatic Province</a>)</span></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Central Atlantic magmatic province</b> (<b>CAMP</b>) is the Earth's largest continental <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous province</a>, covering an area of roughly 11 million km<sup>2</sup>. It is composed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> that formed prior to the breakup of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> Era, near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> periods. The subsequent breakup of Pangaea created the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and provided a legacy of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dikes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lavas</a> over a vast area around the present central North Atlantic Ocean, including large deposits in northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, southwest <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, as well as northeast <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South</a> and southeast <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (found as continental <a href="/wiki/Tholeiitic_magma_series" title="Tholeiitic magma series">thoeliitic basalts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Subaerial_eruption" title="Subaerial eruption">subaerial flows</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion" title="Intrusion">intrusive bodies</a>). The name and CAMP acronym were proposed by Andrea Marzoli (Marzoli et al. 1999) and adopted at a symposium held at the 1999 Spring Meeting of the American Geophysical Union.</p>
<p>The CAMP volcanic eruptions occurred about 201 million years ago and split into four pulses lasting for over ~600,000 years. The resulting large igneous province is, in area covered, the most extensive on earth. The volume of magma flow of ~2–3 × 10<sup>6</sup> km<sup>3</sup> makes it one of the most voluminous as well.</p>
<p>This geologic event is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic–Jurassic extinction event">Triassic–Jurassic extinction event</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connected_magma_flows">Connected magma flows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Connected magma flows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="553" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg/250px-CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg/375px-CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg/500px-CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CAMP_Magmatism_in_the_context_of_Pangea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Location of large residual elements of the Central Atlantic magmatic province</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although some connections among these basalts had long been recognized, in 1988 they were linked as constituting a single major <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalt</a> <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">province</a> (<a href="#CITEREFRampinoStothers1988">Rampino &amp; Stothers 1988</a>). The basaltic sills of similar age (near 200 Ma, or earliest Jurassic) and composition (intermediate-Ti quartz tholeiite) which occur across the vast <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> basin of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> were linked to the province in 1999 (<a href="#CITEREFMarzoliRennePiccirilloErnesto1999">Marzoli et al. 1999</a>). Remnants of CAMP have been identified on four continents (Africa, Europe, North America and South America) and consist of thoeliitic basalts formed during the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> basin during the breakup of the Pangean supercontinent (<a href="#CITEREFBlackburnOlsenBowringMcLean2013">Blackburn et al. 2013</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_extent">Geographical extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographical extent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The province has been described as extending within Pangaea from present-day central Brazil northeastward about 5000 km across western <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a>, and northwestern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and from the interior of western Africa westward for 2500 km through eastern and southern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (<a href="#CITEREFMcHone2000">McHone 2000</a>). If not the largest province by volume, the CAMP certainly encompasses the greatest area known, roughly 11 million km², of any continental <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous province</a>.</p>
<p>Nearly all CAMP rocks are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tholeiite" title="Tholeiite">tholeiitic</a> in composition, with widely separated areas where basalt flows are preserved, as well as large groups of <a href="/wiki/Diabase" title="Diabase">diabase</a> (dolerite) sills or sheets, small <a href="/wiki/Lopolith" title="Lopolith">lopoliths</a>, and dikes throughout the province. Dikes occur in very large individual swarms with particular compositions and orientations. CAMP activity is apparently related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rift_(geology)" title="Rift (geology)">rifting</a> and breakup of Pangaea during the Late <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> through Early Jurassic periods, and the enormous province size, varieties of basalt, and brief time span of CAMP magmatism invite speculation about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> processes that could produce such a magmatic event as well as rift a <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> (<a href="#CITEREFWilson1997">Wilson 1997</a>), (<a href="#CITEREFMcHone2000">McHone 2000</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_with_the_Triassic-Jurassic_boundary_and_the_associated_mass-extinction_event">Connection with the Triassic-Jurassic boundary and the associated mass-extinction event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Connection with the Triassic-Jurassic boundary and the associated mass-extinction event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013 the CAMP's connection to the end-Triassic extinction, with major extinctions that enabled dinosaur domination of land, became more firmly established. Up until 2013, the uncertainties in the geochronologic dates had been too coarse to confirm the volcanic eruptions were correlated with major climate changes. The work by Blackburn et al. demonstrated a tight synchroneity between the earliest volcanism and extinction of large populations using zircon uranium-lead (U-Pb) dating. They further demonstrated that the magmatic eruptions as well as the accompanying atmospheric changes were split into four pulses lasting for over ~600,000 years (<a href="#CITEREFBlackburnOlsenBowringMcLean2013">Blackburn et al. 2013</a>).</p>
<p>Prior to that integration, two hypotheses were in debate. One hypothesis was based especially on studies on Triassic-Jurassic basins from <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> where CAMP <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava flows</a> are outcropping (e.g., <a href="#CITEREFMarzoliBertrandKnightCirilli2004">Marzoli et al. 2004</a>), whereas the other was based on end-Triassic extinction data from eastern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> basins and lava flows showing an extremely large turnover in fossil pollen, spores (sporomorphs), and vertebrates (<a href="#CITEREFWhitesideOlsenKentFowell2007">Whiteside et al. 2007</a>), respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morocco">Morocco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Morocco">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="780" data-file-width="985" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG/350px-Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG/525px-Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG/700px-Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Affioramento_CAMP2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A basaltic lava flow section from the Middle Atlas, Morocco</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The thickest lava flow sequences of the African CAMP are situated in Morocco, where there are basaltic lava piles more than 300 metres thick. The most-studied area is <a href="/wiki/High_Atlas" title="High Atlas">Central High Atlas</a>, where the best preserved and most complete basaltic lava piles are exposed. According to geochemical, petrographic and isotopic data four distinct tholeiitic basaltic units were recognized and can be placed throughout the Central High Atlas: Lower, Intermediate, Upper and Recurrent basalts.</p>
<p>The Lower and Intermediate units are constituted by <a href="/wiki/Basaltic_andesite" title="Basaltic andesite">basaltic andesites</a>, whereas the Upper and Recurrent units have <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> composition. From Lower to Recurrent unit, we observe:</p>
<ul>
<li>a progressive decrease of eruption rate (the Lower and the Intermediate units represent over 80% of preserved lava volume);</li>
<li>a trend going from intersertal to <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(geology)" title="Porphyry (geology)">porphyritic</a> texture;</li>
<li>a progressive depletion of incompatible element contents in the basalts, possibly linked to a progressive depletion of their mantle source.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopic_analyses">Isotopic analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopic analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Ages were determined by <a href="/wiki/Argon%E2%80%93argon_dating" title="Argon–argon dating"><sup>40</sup>Ar/<sup>39</sup>Ar</a> analysis on <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">plagioclase</a> (<a href="#CITEREFKnightNomadeRenneMarzoli2004">Knight et al. 2004</a>), (<a href="#CITEREFVeratiRapailleF.C3.A9raudMarzoli2007">Verati et al. 2007</a>), (<a href="#CITEREFMarzoliBertrandKnightCirilli2004">Marzoli et al. 2004</a>). These data show indistinguishable ages (199.5±0.5 Ma) from Lower to Upper lava flows, from central to northern Morocco. Therefore, CAMP is an intense, short magmatic event. Basalts of the Recurrent unit are slightly younger (mean age: 197±1 Ma) and represent a late event. Consistently, the Upper and Recurrent basalts are separated by a sedimentary layer that locally reaches a thickness of circa 80 m.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetostratigraphy">Magnetostratigraphy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Magnetostratigraphy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Magnetostratigraphy" title="Magnetostratigraphy">magnetostratigraphic</a> data, the Moroccan CAMP events were divided into five groups, differing in paleomagnetic orientations (declination and inclination) (<a href="#CITEREFKnightNomadeRenneMarzoli2004">Knight et al. 2004</a>). Each group is composed by a smaller number of lava flows (i.e., a lower volume) than the preceding one. These data suggest that they were created by five short magma pulses and eruption events, each one possibly &lt;400 (?) years long. All lava flow sequences are characterized by normal polarity, except for a brief <a href="/wiki/Paleomagnetism" title="Paleomagnetism">paleomagnetic</a> reversal yielded by one lava flow and by a localized interlayered limestone in two distinct section of the High Atlas CAMP.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palynological_analyses">Palynological analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Palynological analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Palynological</a> data from sedimentary layers samples at the base of four lava flow sequences constrain the onset of the CAMP, since there is no evidence of depositional hiatus or tectonic deformation at the bottom of the lava flow piles (<a href="#CITEREFMarzoliBertrandKnightCirilli2004">Marzoli et al. 2004</a>). The palynological assemblage observed in these basal layers is typical of Late <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> age, similar to that of the uppermost Triassic sedimentary rocks of eastern North America . Samples from interlayered limestone in lava flows provided unreliable palynological data. One limestone bed from the top to the central High Atlas upper basalts yielded a Late Triassic palynological assemblage. However, the observed sporomorphs in this sample are rare and poorly preserved.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusions">Conclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>All of these data indicate that the basaltic lava flows of the Central Atlantic magmatic province in Morocco were erupted at c. 200 Ma and spanned the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Tr</a>-<a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">J</a> boundary. Thus, it is very possible that there is a connection between this magmatic event and the Tr-J boundary climatic and biotic crisis that led to the <a href="/wiki/Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic–Jurassic extinction event">mass-extinction</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_North_America">Eastern North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Eastern North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The North American portion of the CAMP lava flows crop out in various sections in the basins of Newark, Culpeper, Hartford, Deerfield, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Newark_Supergroup" title="Newark Supergroup">Newark Supergroup</a> in New England (USA), and in the <a href="/wiki/Fundy_Basin" title="Fundy Basin">Fundy Basin</a> in Nova Scotia (Canada). The CAMP is here constituted by rare <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>- and common quartz-normative basalts showing a great lateral extension and a maximum thickness up to 1 km. The basaltic flows occur on top of continental fluvial and lacustrine sedimentary units of Triassic age. <sup>40</sup>Ar/<sup>39</sup>Ar data (on plagioclase) indicate for these basaltic units an absolute age of 198-200 Ma (<a href="#CITEREFHamesMcHoneRenneRuppel2003">Hames et al. 2003</a>) bringing this magmatic event undoubtedly close to the Triassic-Jurassic (Tr-J) boundary. Thus it is necessary to determine whether it straddles the boundary or not: if not, then the CAMP could not be a cause of the <a href="/wiki/Triassic#Late_Triassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic">Late Triassic extinction event</a>. For example, according to <a href="#CITEREFWhitesideOlsenKentFowell2007">Whiteside et al. 2007</a> there are palynological, geochemical, and magnetostratigraphic evidences that the CAMP postdates the Tr-J boundary.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetostratigraphy_2">Magnetostratigraphy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Magnetostratigraphy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the Newark basin a magnetic reversal (E23r) is observed just below the oldest basalts and more or less in the same position as a palynologic turnover, interpreted as the Tr-J boundary. In Morocco, two reversal have been detected in two lava flow sequences. Two distinct correlations between the Moroccan and the Newark magnetostratigraphy have been proposed. <a href="#CITEREFMarzoliBertrandKnightCirilli2004">Marzoli et al. 2004</a> suggest that the Tr-J boundary is located above the lower reverse polarity level which is positioned more or less at the base of the Intermediate basalt unit of Morocco. These two levels can be correlated with chron E23r of the Newark Basin, therefore the North American CAMP Basalts postdate the Tr–J boundary whereas part of the Moroccan CAMP was erupted within the Triassic. Contrarily, <a href="#CITEREFWhitesideOlsenKentFowell2007">Whiteside et al. 2007</a> propose that these two levels could be earliest Jurassic intervals of reverse polarity not sampled in the Newark Basin Sequence (many more lava flows are present in the Moroccan Succession than in the Newark Basin), but observed in Early Jurassic sedimentary sequences of the Paris Basin of France. Reverse polarity intervals in America could be present within <a href="/wiki/North_Mountain_(Nova_Scotia)" title="North Mountain (Nova Scotia)">North Mountain</a> (Fundy basin, Nova Scotia) which are poorly sampled even if previous magnetostratigraphy analysis in this sequence showed only normal polarity, or in the Scots Bay Member of the Fundy basin which have never been sampled. There is only one outcrop in the CAMP of America where reverse polarity is observable: a CAMP–related (about 200 Ma) dike in North Carolina. <a href="#CITEREFWhitesideOlsenKentFowell2007">Whiteside et al. 2007</a> suggest that reverse polarity intervals in this dike could be of post Triassic age and correlated with the same events in Morocco.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palynological_analyses_2">Palynological analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Palynological analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Tr-J boundary is not officially defined, but most workers recognise it in continental strata by the last appearance of index taxa such as <i>Ovalipollis ovalis</i>, <i>Vallasporites ignatii</i> and <i>Patinasporites densus</i> or, in marine sections, by the first appearance of the ammonite <i><a href="/wiki/Psiloceras" title="Psiloceras">Psiloceras</a> planorbis</i>. In the Newark basin the palynological turnover event (hence the Tr-J boundary mass extinction) occurs below the oldest CAMP lava flows. The same can be said for the Fundy, Hartford and Deerfield Basins. In the investigated Moroccan CAMP sections (Central High Atlas Basin), sedimentary layers sampled immediately below the oldest basaltic lava flows, apparently contain Triassic taxa (e.g., <i>P. densus</i>), and were thus defined as Triassic in age as at least the lowest lava flows (<a href="#CITEREFMarzoliBertrandKnightCirilli2004">Marzoli et al. 2004</a>). Still, a different interpretation is suggested by <a href="#CITEREFWhitesideOlsenKentFowell2007">Whiteside et al. 2007</a>: the sampled sedimentary strata are quite deformed and this can mean that some sedimentary units could be lacking (eroded or structurally omitted). With respect to the Triassic pollens found in some sedimentary units above the Upper Unit basalts, they could have been reworked, so they don’t represent a completely reliable constraint.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geochemical_analyses">Geochemical analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Atlantic_magmatic_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Geochemical analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>CAMP lava flows of North America can be geochemically separated in three units: the older ones are classified as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_and_low_titanium_basalts" title="High and low titanium basalts">high titanium</a> <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> <a href="/wiki/Normative_mineralogy" title="Normative mineralogy">normative</a></i> (HTQ) basalts (TiO<sub>2</sub> = 1.0-1.3 wt%); these are followed by lava flows classified as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_and_low_titanium_basalts" title="High and low titanium basalts">low titanium</a> quartz normative</i> (LTQ) basalts (TiO<sub>2</sub> = ca. 0.8-1.3 wt%); and then by the youngest lava flow unit classified as <i>high titanium iron quartz normative</i> (HTIQ) basalts (TiO<sub>2</sub> = 1.4-1.6 wt%). According to <a href="#CITEREFWhitesideOlsenKentFowell2007">Whiteside et al. 2007</a>, geochemical analyses based upon titanium, magnesium and silicon contents show a certain correlation between the lower North American lava flows and the Lower Unit of the Moroccan CAMP, thus reinforcing the conclusion that the Moroccan basalts postdate the Tr-J boundary.</p>
<p>Therefore, according to these data, CAMP basalts shouldn’t be included among the direct causes of the Tr-J mass extinction.</p>
