<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Wavelength</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Wavelength_(disambiguation)" title="Wavelength (disambiguation)">Wavelength (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

Wavelength of a <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine wave</a>, λ, can be measured between any two points with the same <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a>, such as between crests ( on top), or troughs (on bottom), or corresponding <a href="/wiki/Zero_crossing" title="Zero crossing">zero crossings</a> as shown.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, the <b>wavelength</b> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinusoidal_wave" title="Sinusoidal wave">sinusoidal wave</a> is the <b>spatial period</b> of the wave—the distance over which the wave's shape repeats,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hecht-1">[1]</a></sup> and thus the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">inverse</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Spatial_frequency" title="Spatial frequency">spatial frequency</a>. It is usually determined by considering the distance between consecutive corresponding points of the same <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a>, such as crests, troughs, or <a href="/wiki/Zero_crossing" title="Zero crossing">zero crossings</a> and is a characteristic of both traveling waves and <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing waves</a>, as well as other spatial wave patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seaway_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seaway-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Wavelength is commonly designated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_letter" title="Greek letter">Greek letter</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Lambda" title="Lambda">lambda</a></i> (λ). The concept can also be applied to periodic waves of non-sinusoidal shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-hecht-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flowers_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flowers-4">[4]</a></sup> The term <i>wavelength</i> is also sometimes applied to <a href="/wiki/Modulation" title="Modulation">modulated</a> waves, and to the sinusoidal <a href="/wiki/Envelope_(mathematics)" title="Envelope (mathematics)">envelopes</a> of modulated waves or waves formed by <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">interference</a> of several sinusoids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Assuming a sinusoidal wave moving at a fixed wave speed, wavelength is inversely proportional to <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of the wave: waves with higher frequencies have shorter wavelengths, and lower frequencies have longer wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wavelength depends on the medium (for example, vacuum, air, or water) that a wave travels through.</p>
<p>Examples of wave-like phenomena are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_wave" title="Water wave">water waves</a> and periodic electrical signals in a <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">conductor</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> wave is a variation in air <a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure" title="Sound pressure">pressure</a>, while in <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> vary. Water waves are variations in the height of a body of water. In a crystal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_vibration" title="Lattice vibration">lattice vibration</a>, atomic positions vary.</p>
<p>Wavelength is a measure of the distance between repetitions of a shape feature such as peaks, valleys, or zero-crossings, not a measure of how far any given particle moves. For example, in sinusoidal waves over deep water a particle near the water's surface moves in a circle of the same diameter as the wave height, unrelated to wavelength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinet_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pinet-7">[7]</a></sup> The range of wavelengths or frequencies for wave phenomena is called a <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a>. The name originated with the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible light spectrum</a> but now can be applied to the entire <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> as well as to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_spectrum" title="Sound spectrum">sound spectrum</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibration_spectrum" title="Vibration spectrum">vibration spectrum</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinusoidal_waves">Sinusoidal waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sinusoidal waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">linear</a> media, any wave pattern can be described in terms of the independent propagation of sinusoidal components. The wavelength <i>λ</i> of a sinusoidal waveform traveling at constant speed <i>v</i> is given by<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassidy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cassidy-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

</math></span><img alt="\lambda ={\frac {v}{f}}\,\,," aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56881f866662f801e8de88c2102848b01224697a" style="vertical-align: -2.505ex; width:8.031ex; height:5.343ex;"/></span></dd>

<p>where <i>v</i> is called the phase speed (magnitude of the <a href="/wiki/Phase_velocity" title="Phase velocity">phase velocity</a>) of the wave and <i>f</i> is the wave's <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>. In a <i>dispersive</i> medium, the phase speed itself depends upon the frequency of the wave, making the <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_relation" title="Dispersion relation">relationship between wavelength and frequency</a> nonlinear.</p>
<p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>—such as light—in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space" title="Free space">free space</a>, the phase speed is the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, about 3×10<sup>8</sup> m/s. Thus the wavelength of a 100 MHz electromagnetic (radio) wave is about: 3×10<sup>8</sup> m/s divided by 10<sup>8</sup> Hz = 3 metres. The wavelength of visible light ranges from deep <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>, roughly 700 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Violet_(color)" title="Violet (color)">violet</a>, roughly 400 nm (for other examples, see <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>).</p>
<p>For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound waves</a> in air, the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> is 343 m/s (at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">room temperature and atmospheric pressure</a>). The wavelengths of sound frequencies audible to the human ear (20 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a>–20 kHz) are thus between approximately 17 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a> and 17 <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">mm</a>, respectively. Note that the wavelengths in audible sound are much longer than those in visible light.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standing_waves">Standing waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standing waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing wave</a> is an undulatory motion that stays in one place. A sinusoidal standing wave includes stationary points of no motion, called <a href="/wiki/Node_(physics)" title="Node (physics)">nodes</a>, and the wavelength is twice the distance between nodes.</p>
<p>The upper figure shows three standing waves in a box. The walls of the box are considered to require the wave to have nodes at the walls of the box (an example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_conditions" title="Boundary conditions">boundary conditions</a>) determining which wavelengths are allowed. For example, for an electromagnetic wave, if the box has ideal metal walls, the condition for nodes at the walls results because the metal walls cannot support a tangential electric field, forcing the wave to have zero amplitude at the wall.</p>
<p>The stationary wave can be viewed as the sum of two traveling sinusoidal waves of oppositely directed velocities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Consequently, wavelength, period, and wave velocity are related just as for a traveling wave. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light#Cavity_resonance" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> can be determined from observation of standing waves in a metal box containing an ideal vacuum.</p>

<p>The speed of a wave depends upon the medium in which it propagates. In particular, the speed of light in a medium is less than in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum#In_electromagnetism" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, which means that the same frequency will correspond to a shorter wavelength in the medium than in vacuum, as shown in the figure at right.</p>
<p>This change in speed upon entering a medium causes <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a>, or a change in direction of waves that encounter the interface between media at an angle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mud_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mud-10">[10]</a></sup> For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_waves" title="Electromagnetic waves">electromagnetic waves</a>, this change in the angle of propagation is governed by <a href="/wiki/Snell%27s_law" title="Snell's law">Snell's law</a>.</p>
<p>The wave velocity in one medium not only may differ from that in another, but the velocity typically varies with wavelength. As a result, the change in direction upon entering a different medium changes with the wavelength of the wave.</p>

<p>When wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation are quoted, the wavelength in vacuum usually is intended unless the wavelength is specifically identified as the wavelength in some other medium. In acoustics, where a medium is essential for the waves to exist, the wavelength value is given for a specified medium.</p>
<p>The variation in speed of light with vacuum wavelength is known as <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">dispersion</a>, and is also responsible for the familiar phenomenon in which light is separated into component colors by a <a href="/wiki/Dispersive_prism" title="Dispersive prism">prism</a>. Separation occurs when the refractive index inside the prism varies with wavelength, so different wavelengths propagate at different speeds inside the prism, causing them to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refract" title="Refract">refract</a> at different angles. The mathematical relationship that describes how the speed of light within a medium varies with wavelength is known as a <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_relation" title="Dispersion relation">dispersion relation</a>.</p>

<p>Wavelength can be a useful concept even if the wave is not <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">periodic</a> in space. For example, in an ocean wave approaching shore, shown in the figure, the incoming wave undulates with a varying <i>local</i> wavelength that depends in part on the depth of the sea floor compared to the wave height. The analysis of the wave can be based upon comparison of the local wavelength with the local water depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinet2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pinet2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cochlea_wave_animated.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="143" data-file-width="200" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Cochlea_wave_animated.gif" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cochlea_wave_animated.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sinusoidal wave travelling in a nonuniform medium, with loss</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Waves that are sinusoidal in time but propagate through a medium whose properties vary with position (an <i>inhomogeneous</i> medium) may propagate at a velocity that varies with position, and as a result may not be sinusoidal in space. The figure at right shows an example. As the wave slows down, the wavelength gets shorter and the amplitude increases; after a place of maximum response, the short wavelength is associated with a high loss and the wave dies out.</p>
<p>The analysis of <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> of such systems is often done approximately, using the <i><a href="/wiki/WKB_approximation" title="WKB approximation">WKB method</a></i> (also known as the <i>Liouville–Green method</i>). The method integrates phase through space using a local <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a>, which can be interpreted as indicating a "local wavelength" of the solution as a function of time and space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This method treats the system locally as if it were uniform with the local properties; in particular, the local wave velocity associated with a frequency is the only thing needed to estimate the corresponding local wavenumber or wavelength. In addition, the method computes a slowly changing amplitude to satisfy other constraints of the equations or of the physical system, such as for <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> in the wave.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystals">Crystals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crystals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="744" data-file-width="1077" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG/220px-Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG/330px-Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG/440px-Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wavelength_indeterminacy.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A wave on a line of atoms can be interpreted according to a variety of wavelengths.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Waves in crystalline solids are not continuous, because they are composed of vibrations of discrete particles arranged in a regular lattice. This produces <a href="/wiki/Aliasing" title="Aliasing">aliasing</a> because the same vibration can be considered to have a variety of different wavelengths, as shown in the figure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putnis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Putnis-14">[14]</a></sup> Descriptions using more than one of these wavelengths are redundant; it is conventional to choose the longest wavelength that fits the phenomenon. The range of wavelengths sufficient to provide a description of all possible waves in a crystalline medium corresponds to the wave vectors confined to the <a href="/wiki/Brillouin_zone" title="Brillouin zone">Brillouin zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Razeghi_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Razeghi-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>This indeterminacy in wavelength in solids is important in the analysis of wave phenomena such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_bands" title="Energy bands">energy bands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonons" title="Phonons">lattice vibrations</a>. It is mathematically equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Aliasing" title="Aliasing">aliasing</a> of a signal that is <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(signal_processing)" title="Sampling (signal processing)">sampled</a> at discrete intervals.</p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The concept of wavelength is most often applied to sinusoidal, or nearly sinusoidal, waves, because in a linear system the sinusoid is the unique shape that propagates with no shape change – just a phase change and potentially an amplitude change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayleigh_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rayleigh-16">[16]</a></sup> The wavelength (or alternatively <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wave_vector" title="Wave vector">wave vector</a>) is a characterization of the wave in space, that is functionally related to its frequency, as constrained by the physics of the system. Sinusoids are the simplest <a href="/wiki/Wave#Travelling_waves" title="Wave">traveling wave</a> solutions, and more complex solutions can be built up by <a href="/wiki/Superposition_principle" title="Superposition principle">superposition</a>.</p>
<p>In the special case of dispersion-free and uniform media, waves other than sinusoids propagate with unchanging shape and constant velocity. In certain circumstances, waves of unchanging shape also can occur in nonlinear media; for example, the figure shows ocean waves in shallow water that have sharper crests and flatter troughs than those of a sinusoid, typical of a <a href="/wiki/Cnoidal_wave" title="Cnoidal wave">cnoidal wave</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pilipchuk_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pilipchuk-17">[17]</a></sup> a traveling wave so named because it is described by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobi_elliptic_function" title="Jacobi elliptic function">Jacobi elliptic function</a> of <i>m</i>-th order, usually denoted as <span class="nowrap"><i>cn</i>(<i>x</i>; <i>m</i>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludu_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludu-18">[18]</a></sup> Large-amplitude <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_wave" title="Ocean wave">ocean waves</a> with certain shapes can propagate unchanged, because of properties of the nonlinear surface-wave medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>If a traveling wave has a fixed shape that repeats in space or in time, it is a <i>periodic wave</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McPherson_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-McPherson-20">[20]</a></sup> Such waves are sometimes regarded as having a wavelength even though they are not sinusoidal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-may_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-may-21">[21]</a></sup> As shown in the figure, wavelength is measured between consecutive corresponding points on the waveform.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_packets">Wave packets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wave packets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wave_packet_(dispersion).gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="115" data-file-width="200" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Wave_packet_%28dispersion%29.gif" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wave_packet_(dispersion).gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A propagating wave packet</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wave_packet" title="Wave packet">Wave packet</a></div>
<p>Localized <a href="/wiki/Wave_packet" title="Wave packet">wave packets</a>, "bursts" of wave action where each wave packet travels as a unit, find application in many fields of physics. A wave packet has an <i>envelope</i> that describes the overall amplitude of the wave; within the envelope, the distance between adjacent peaks or troughs is sometimes called a <i>local wavelength</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper-23">[23]</a></sup> An example is shown in the figure. In general, the <i>envelope</i> of the wave packet moves at a different speed than the constituent waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fromhold_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fromhold-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Using <a href="/wiki/Fourier_analysis" title="Fourier analysis">Fourier analysis</a>, wave packets can be analyzed into infinite sums (or integrals) of sinusoidal waves of different <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumbers</a> or wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manners_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manners-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie" title="Louis de Broglie">Louis de Broglie</a> postulated that all particles with a specific value of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> <i>p</i> have a wavelength <i>λ = h/p</i>, where <i>h</i> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck%27s_constant" title="Planck's constant">Planck's constant</a>. This hypothesis was at the basis of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>. Nowadays, this wavelength is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength" title="De Broglie wavelength">de Broglie wavelength</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">CRT</a> display have a De Broglie wavelength of about 10<sup>−13</sup> m. To prevent the <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> for such a particle being spread over all space, de Broglie proposed using wave packets to represent particles that are localized in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marton_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marton-26">[26]</a></sup> The spatial spread of the wave packet, and the spread of the <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumbers</a> of sinusoids that make up the packet, correspond to the uncertainties in the particle's position and momentum, the product of which is bounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg uncertainty principle">Heisenberg uncertainty principle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manners_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manners-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p>The notion of path difference and constructive or destructive interference used above for the double-slit experiment applies as well to the display of a single slit of light intercepted on a screen. The main result of this interference is to spread out the light from the narrow slit into a broader image on the screen. This distribution of wave energy is called <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a>.</p>
<p>Two types of diffraction are distinguished, depending upon the separation between the source and the screen: <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_diffraction" title="Fraunhofer diffraction">Fraunhofer diffraction</a> or far-field diffraction at large separations and <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_diffraction" title="Fresnel diffraction">Fresnel diffraction</a> or near-field diffraction at close separations.</p>
<p>In the analysis of the single slit, the non-zero width of the slit is taken into account, and each point in the aperture is taken as the source of one contribution to the beam of light (<i>Huygen's wavelets</i>). On the screen, the light arriving from each position within the slit has a different path length, albeit possibly a very small difference. Consequently, interference occurs.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diffraction-limited_resolution">Diffraction-limited resolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Diffraction-limited resolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">Angular resolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffraction-limited_system" title="Diffraction-limited system">Diffraction-limited system</a></div>
<p>Diffraction is the fundamental limitation on the <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">resolving power</a> of optical instruments, such as <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotelescope" title="Radiotelescope">radiotelescopes</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopes" title="Microscopes">microscopes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saxby_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saxby-32">[32]</a></sup> For a circular aperture, the diffraction-limited image spot is known as an <a href="/wiki/Airy_disk" title="Airy disk">Airy disk</a>; the distance <i>x</i> in the single-slit diffraction formula is replaced by radial distance <i>r</i> and the sine is replaced by 2<i>J</i><sub>1</sub>, where <i>J</i><sub>1</sub> is a first order <a href="/wiki/Bessel_function" title="Bessel function">Bessel function</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The resolvable <i>spatial</i> size of objects viewed through a microscope is limited according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rayleigh_criterion" title="Rayleigh criterion">Rayleigh criterion</a>, the radius to the first null of the Airy disk, to a size proportional to the wavelength of the light used, and depending on the <a href="/wiki/Numerical_aperture" title="Numerical aperture">numerical aperture</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subwavelength">Subwavelength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Subwavelength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>subwavelength</i> is used to describe an object having one or more dimensions smaller than the length of the wave with which the object interacts. For example, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Subwavelength-diameter_optical_fibre" title="Subwavelength-diameter optical fibre">subwavelength-diameter optical fibre</a></i> means an <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fibre</a> whose diameter is less than the wavelength of light propagating through it.</p>
<p>A subwavelength particle is a particle smaller than the wavelength of light with which it interacts (see <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>). Subwavelength <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">apertures</a> are holes smaller than the wavelength of light propagating through them. Such structures have applications in <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_optical_transmission" title="Extraordinary optical transmission">extraordinary optical transmission</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zero-mode_waveguide" title="Zero-mode waveguide">zero-mode waveguides</a>, among other areas of <a href="/wiki/Photonics" title="Photonics">photonics</a>.</p>
<p><i>Subwavelength</i> may also refer to a phenomenon involving subwavelength objects; for example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subwavelength_imaging" title="Subwavelength imaging">subwavelength imaging</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Angular_wavelength">Angular wavelength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wavelength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Angular wavelength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A quantity related to the wavelength is the <b>angular wavelength</b> (also known as <b>reduced wavelength</b>), usually symbolized by <i>ƛ</i> (lambda-bar). It is equal to the "regular" wavelength "reduced" by a factor of 2π (<i>ƛ</i> = <i>λ</i>/2π). It is usually encountered in quantum mechanics, where it is used in combination with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant" title="Reduced Planck constant">reduced Planck constant</a> (symbol <i>ħ</i>, h-bar) and the <a href="/wiki/Angular_frequency" title="Angular frequency">angular frequency</a> (symbol <i>ω</i>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_wavenumber" title="Angular wavenumber">angular wavenumber</a> (symbol <i>k</i>).</p>
