<p><b>Bouvet Island</b> (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <span lang="no" xml:lang="no"><i>Bouvetøya</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Norwegian" title="Help:IPA/Norwegian">[bu.ˈʋɛ.øj.ɑ]</a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOR19711217_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOR19711217-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOV19300227_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-LOV19300227-2">[2]</a></sup>) is an uninhabited <a href="/wiki/Subantarctic" title="Subantarctic">subantarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/High_island" title="High island">high island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dependencies_of_Norway" title="Dependencies of Norway">dependency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> located in the South <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bouvet_Island&amp;params=54_25.8_S_3_22.8_E_type:isle_region:BV"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">54°25.8′S</span> <span class="longitude">3°22.8′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">54.4300°S 3.3800°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-54.4300; 3.3800</span></span></span></a></span><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bouvet_Island&amp;params=54_25.8_S_3_22.8_E_type:isle_region:BV"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">54°25.8′S</span> <span class="longitude">3°22.8′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">54.4300°S 3.3800°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-54.4300; 3.3800</span></span></span></a></span></span></span>, thus putting it north of and outside the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty_System" title="Antarctic Treaty System">Antarctic Treaty System</a>. It lies at the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_Earth#Remoteness" title="Extreme points of Earth">the most remote island</a> in the world, approximately 2,600 kilometres (1,600 mi) south-southwest of the coast of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and approximately 1,700 kilometres (1,100 mi) north of the <a href="/wiki/Princess_Astrid_Coast" title="Princess Astrid Coast">Princess Astrid Coast</a> of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Maud_Land" title="Queen Maud Land">Queen Maud Land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>
<p>The island has an area of 49 square kilometres (19 sq mi), of which 93 percent is covered by a <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a>. The centre of the island is an ice-filled crater of an inactive <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Skerry" title="Skerry">skerries</a> and one smaller island, <a href="/wiki/Lars%C3%B8ya" title="Larsøya">Larsøya</a>, lie along the coast. Nyrøysa, created by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_slide" title="Rock slide">rock slide</a> in the late 1950s, is the only easy place to land and is the location of a <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather station</a>.</p>
<p>The island was first spotted on 1 January 1739 by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Charles_Bouvet_de_Lozier" title="Jean-Baptiste Charles Bouvet de Lozier">Jean-Baptiste Charles Bouvet de Lozier</a>, after whom it was later named. He recorded inaccurate coordinates and the island was not sighted again until 1808, when the <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Whaler" title="Whaler">whaler</a> captain James Lindsay named it <b>Lindsay Island</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-google-3">[3]</a></sup> The first claim of landing, although disputed, was by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Morrell" title="Benjamin Morrell">Benjamin Morrell</a>. In 1825, the island was claimed for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Crown" title="British Crown">British Crown</a> by George Norris, who named it <b>Liverpool Island</b>. He also reported <a href="/wiki/Thompson_Island_(South_Atlantic)" title="Thompson Island (South Atlantic)">Thompson Island</a> as nearby, although this was later shown to be a <a href="/wiki/Phantom_island" title="Phantom island">phantom island</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Riiser-Larsen#The_Norvegia_expeditions" title="Hjalmar Riiser-Larsen">first <i>Norvegia</i> expedition</a> landed on the island in 1927 and claimed it for Norway. At this time the island was named <i>Bouvet Island</i>, or "Bouvetøya" in Norwegian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wordpress_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wordpress-4">[4]</a></sup> After a dispute with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, it was declared a Norwegian dependency in 1930. It became a <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a> in 1971.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_early_sightings">Discovery and early sightings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery and early sightings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_island_0.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1070" data-file-width="1608" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Bouvet_island_0.jpg/220px-Bouvet_island_0.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Bouvet_island_0.jpg/330px-Bouvet_island_0.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Bouvet_island_0.jpg/440px-Bouvet_island_0.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_island_0.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Southeast coast of Bouvet Island in 1898</div>

<p>The island was discovered on 1 January 1739 by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Charles_Bouvet_de_Lozier" title="Jean-Baptiste Charles Bouvet de Lozier">Jean-Baptiste Charles Bouvet de Lozier</a>, commander of the French ships <i>Aigle</i> and <i>Marie</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Bouvet, who was searching for a presumed large southern continent, spotted the island through the fog and named the cape he saw <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_de_la_Circoncision" title="Cap de la Circoncision">Cap de la Circoncision</a>. He was not able to land and did not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumnavigate" title="Circumnavigate">circumnavigate</a> his discovery, thus not clarifying if it was an island or part of <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">a continent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b62_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-b62-6">[6]</a></sup> His plotting of its position was inaccurate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> leading several expeditions to fail to find the island again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b58_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-b58-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Second_voyage_of_James_Cook" title="Second voyage of James Cook">second voyage</a> set off from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> on 22 November 1772 to find Cape Circoncision, but was unable to find the cape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next expedition to spot the island was in 1808 by James Lindsay, captain of the <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Enderby_%26_Sons" title="Samuel Enderby &amp; Sons">Samuel Enderby &amp; Sons</a>' (SE&amp;S) <a href="/wiki/Whaler" title="Whaler">whaler</a> <i>Snow Swan</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> They reached the island and recorded its position, though they were unable to land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WJMills_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WJMills-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Lindsay could confirm that the "cape" was indeed an island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b62_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-b62-6">[6]</a></sup> The next expedition to arrive at the island was American <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Morrell" title="Benjamin Morrell">Benjamin Morrell</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">seal hunting</a> ship <i>Wasp</i>. Morrell, by his own account, found the island without difficulty (with "improbable ease", in the words of historian William Mills)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WJMills_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-WJMills-11">[11]</a></sup> before landing and hunting 196 seals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b62_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-b62-6">[6]</a></sup> In his subsequent lengthy description, Morrell does not mention the island's most obvious physical feature, its permanent ice cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This has caused some commentators to doubt whether he actually visited the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WJMills_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-WJMills-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 10 December 1825, SE&amp;S's George Norris, master of the <i>Sprightly</i>, landed on the island,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b62_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-b62-6">[6]</a></sup> named it Liverpool Island and claimed it for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Crown" title="British Crown">British Crown</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_IV" title="George IV">George IV</a> on 16 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b63_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-b63-15">[15]</a></sup> The next expedition to spot the island was Joseph Fuller and his ship <i>Francis Allyn</i> in 1893, but he was not able to land on the island. German <a href="/wiki/Carl_Chun" title="Carl Chun">Carl Chun</a>'s <i>Valdivia</i> expedition arrived at the island in 1898. They were not able to land, but dredged the seabed for geological samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> They were also the first to accurately fix the island's position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b63_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-b63-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norris also spotted a second island in 1825, which he named <a href="/wiki/Thompson_Island_(South_Atlantic)" title="Thompson Island (South Atlantic)">Thompson Island</a>, which he placed 72 kilometres (45 mi) north-northeast of Liverpool Island. Thompson Island was also reported in 1893 by Fuller, but in 1898 Chun did not report seeing such an island, nor has anyone since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> However, Thompson Island continued to appear on maps as late as 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A 1967 paper suggested that the island might have disappeared in an undetected volcanic eruption, but in 1997 it was discovered that the ocean is more than 2,400 metres (7,900 ft) deep in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norwegian_annexation">Norwegian annexation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Norwegian annexation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="800" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg/220px-Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg/330px-Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg/440px-Bouvet_Island_1927.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In 1927, the First <i>Norvegia</i> Expedition – led by Harald Horntvedt and financed by <a href="/wiki/Lars_Christensen" title="Lars Christensen">Lars Christensen</a> – was the first to make an extended stay on the island. Observations and surveying were conducted on the islands and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanographic" title="Oceanographic">oceanographic</a> measurements performed in the sea around it. At Ny Sandefjord, a small hut was erected and, on 1 December, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Norway" title="Flag of Norway">Norwegian flag</a> was hoisted and the island claimed for Norway. The annexation was established by a royal decree on 23 January 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b63_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-b63-15">[15]</a></sup> The claim was initially protested by the United Kingdom, on the basis of Norris's landing and annexation. However, the British position was weakened by Norris's sighting of two islands and the uncertainty as to whether he had been on Thompson or Liverpool (i.e. Bouvet) Island. Norris's positioning deviating from the correct location combined with the island's lack of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_harbour" title="Natural harbour">natural harbour</a> and small size made the UK accept the Norwegian claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This resulted in diplomatic negotiations between the two countries, and in November 1929, Britain renounced its claim to the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b63_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-b63-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Riiser-Larsen#The_Norvegia_expeditions" title="Hjalmar Riiser-Larsen">Second <i>Norvegia</i> Expedition</a> arrived in 1928 with the intent of establishing a manned meteorological radio station, but a suitable location could not be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b63_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-b63-15">[15]</a></sup> By then both the flagpole and hut from the previous year had been washed away. The <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Riiser-Larsen#The_Norvegia_expeditions" title="Hjalmar Riiser-Larsen">Third <i>Norvegia</i> Expedition</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Riiser-Larsen" title="Hjalmar Riiser-Larsen">Hjalmar Riiser-Larsen</a>, arrived the following year and built a new hut at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kapp_Circoncision" title="Kapp Circoncision">Kapp Circoncision</a> and on Larsøya. The expedition carried out <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photography</a> of the island and was the first Antarctic expedition to use aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b64_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-b64-20">[20]</a></sup> The <i>Dependency Act</i>, passed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Norway" title="Parliament of Norway">Parliament of Norway</a> on 27 February 1930, established Bouvet Island as a dependency, along with <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_Island" title="Peter I Island">Peter I Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Maud_Land" title="Queen Maud Land">Queen Maud Land</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOV19300227_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-LOV19300227-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Eared_seal" title="Eared seal">eared seal</a> was protected on and around the island in 1929 and in 1935 all seals around the island were protected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-name-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1955, the South African frigate <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loch_class_frigate#South_African_Navy" title="Loch class frigate">Transvaal</a></i> visited the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Nyrøysa, a rock-strewn ice-free area, the largest such on Bouvet, was created sometime between 1955 and 1958, probably by a landslide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1964 the island was visited by the British naval ship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Protector_(A146)" title="HMS Protector (A146)">HMS <i>Protector</i></a>. On December 17, 1971, the entire island and its territorial waters were protected as a <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOR19711217_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOR19711217-1">[1]</a></sup> A scientific landing was made in 1978, during which the underground temperature was measured to be 25 °C (77 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-24">[24]</a></sup> In addition to scientific surveys,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(shipboard)" title="Lifeboat (shipboard)">lifeboat</a> was recovered at Nyrøysa, although no people were found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-24">[24]</a></sup> The lifeboat belonged to a scientific reconnaissance vessel ("The scientific reconnaissance vessel “Slava-9” began his regular 13th cruise with the “Slava” Antarctic whaling fleet on 22 October 1958 … On 27 November she got to Bouvet Island. A group of sailors landed which couldn’t leave the island in time because of worsened weather and stayed on it about 3 days. The people were withdrawn only by helicopter on 29 November"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vela_Incident" title="Vela Incident">Vela Incident</a> took place on 22 September 1979, on or above the sea between Bouvetøya and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Islands" title="Prince Edward Islands">Prince Edward Islands</a>, when the American <a href="/wiki/Vela_(satellite)" title="Vela (satellite)">Vela Hotel</a> satellite 6911 registered an unexplained <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_nuclear_explosions" title="Effects of nuclear explosions">double flash</a>. This observation has been variously interpreted as a nuclear test, meteor, or instrumentation glitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1970s, the island has been frequently visited by Norwegian Antarctic expeditions. In 1977, an automated <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather station</a> was constructed, and for two months in 1978 and 1979 a manned weather station was operated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b64_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-b64-20">[20]</a></sup> In March 1985, a Norwegian expedition experienced sufficiently clear weather to allow the entire island to be photographed from the air, resulting in the first accurate map of the whole island, 247 years after its discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Polar_Institute" title="Norwegian Polar Institute">Norwegian Polar Institute</a> established a 36-square-metre (390 sq ft) research station, made of <a href="/wiki/Shipping_container" title="Shipping container">shipping containers</a>, at Nyrøysa in 1996. On 23 February 2006, the island experienced a magnitude 6.2 earthquake whose epicentre was about 100 km (62 mi) away,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> weakening the station's foundation and causing it to be blown to sea during a winter storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2014, a new research station was sent from <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8" title="Tromsø">Tromsø</a> in Norway, via <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, to Bouvet. The new station is designed to house six people for periods of two to four months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Hanse Explorer</i> expedition ship visited Bouvet Island on 20 and 21 February 2012 as part of "Expédition pour le Futur". The expedition's goal was to land and summit the highest point on the island. The first four climbers (Aaron Halstead, Will Allen, Bruno Rodi and Jason Rodi) were the first humans to climb the highest peak. A <a href="/wiki/Time_capsule" title="Time capsule">time capsule</a> containing the top visions of the future for 2062 was left behind. The next morning, Aaron Halstead led five other climbers (Sarto Blouin, Seth Sherman, Chakib Bouayed, <a href="/wiki/Cindy_Sampson" title="Cindy Sampson">Cindy Sampson</a>, and Akos Hivekovics) to the top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several amateur radio <a href="/wiki/DX-pedition" title="DX-pedition">DX-peditions</a> have been conducted to the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> An international group is currently planning a DX-pedition to the island for mid-January 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, Bouvetøya, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> were considered as locations for the new <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_International_Ship_Register" title="Norwegian International Ship Register">Norwegian International Ship Register</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_convenience" title="Flag of convenience">flag of convenience</a> registry was ultimately established in <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a>, Norway in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2007, the island was added to Norway's tentative list of nominations as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> as part of the transnational nomination of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Krill_fishery" title="Krill fishery">Krill fishing</a> in the Southern Ocean is subject to the <a href="/wiki/Convention_for_the_Conservation_of_Antarctic_Marine_Living_Resources" title="Convention for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources">Convention for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources</a>, which defines maximum catch quotas for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_fisheries" title="Sustainable fisheries">sustainable exploitation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_krill" title="Antarctic krill">Antarctic krill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Surveys conducted in 2000 showed high concentration of krill around Bouvetøya. In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Aker_BioMarine" title="Aker BioMarine">Aker BioMarine</a> was awarded a concession to fish krill, and additional quotas were awarded from 2008 for a total catch of 620,000 tonnes (610,000 long tons; 680,000 short tons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> There is a controversy as to whether the fisheries are sustainable, particularly in relation to krill being important food for whales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2009, Norway filed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Commission_on_the_Limits_of_the_Continental_Shelf" title="UN Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf">UN Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf</a> to extend the outer limit of the continental shelf past 200 nautical miles (230 mi; 370 km) surrounding the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bouvetøya is a volcanic island constituting the top of a volcano located at the southern end of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge in the South Atlantic Ocean. The island measures 9.5 by 7 kilometres (5.9 by 4.3 mi) and covers an area of 49 square kilometres (19 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-name-21">[21]</a></sup> including a number of small rocks and skerries and one sizable island, <a href="/wiki/Lars%C3%B8ya" title="Larsøya">Larsøya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larsoya_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-larsoya-43">[43]</a></sup> It is located in the Subantarctic, south of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Convergence" title="Antarctic Convergence">Antarctic Convergence</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> which, by some definitions, would place the island in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Bouvet Island is the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_Earth" title="Extreme points of Earth">most remote island</a> in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The closest land is <a href="/wiki/Queen_Maud_Land" title="Queen Maud Land">Queen Maud Land</a> of Antarctica, which is 1,700 kilometres (1,100 mi) to the south,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b58_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-b58-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gough_Island" title="Gough Island">Gough Island</a>, 1,600 kilometres (990 mi) to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npolar_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-npolar-47">[47]</a></sup> The closest inhabited location is <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a> island, 2,250 kilometres (1,400 mi) to the northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-name-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nyrøysa is a 2-by-0.5-kilometre (1.2 by 0.3 mi) terrace located on the north-west coast of the island. Created by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_slide" title="Rock slide">rock slide</a> sometime between 1955 and 1957, it is the island's easiest access point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> It is the site of the automatic weather station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birdlife_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-48">[48]</a></sup> The north-west corner is the peninsula of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kapp_Circoncision" title="Kapp Circoncision">Kapp Circoncision</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> From there, east to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kapp_Valdivia" title="Kapp Valdivia">Kapp Valdivia</a>, the coast is known as Morgenstiernekysten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Store_Kari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Store Kari (page does not exist)">Store Kari</a> is an islet located 1.2 kilometres (0.75 mi) east of the cape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> From Kapp Valdivia, southeast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kapp_Lollo" title="Kapp Lollo">Kapp Lollo</a>, on the east side of the island, the coast is known as <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Terrasse" title="Victoria Terrasse">Victoria Terrasse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> From there to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kapp_Fie" title="Kapp Fie">Kapp Fie</a> at the southeastern corner, the coast is known as <a href="/wiki/Mowinckelkysten" title="Mowinckelkysten">Mowinckelkysten</a>. Svartstranda is a section of <a href="/wiki/Black_sand" title="Black sand">black sand</a> which runs 1.8 kilometres (1.1 mi) along the section from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kapp_Meteor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kapp Meteor (page does not exist)">Kapp Meteor</a>, south to Kapp Fie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> After rounding Kapp Fie, the coast along the south side is known as Vogtkysten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The westernmost part of it is the 300 metres (980 ft) long shore of Sjøelefantstranda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Off <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Catoodden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Catoodden (page does not exist)">Catoodden</a>, on the south-western corner, lies <a href="/wiki/Lars%C3%B8ya" title="Larsøya">Larsøya</a>, the only island of any size off Bouvetøya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larsoya_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-larsoya-43">[43]</a></sup> The western coast from Catoodden north to Nyrøysa, is known as Esmarchkysten. Midway up the coast lies <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Norvegiaodden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Norvegiaodden (page does not exist)">Norvegiaodden</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kapp_Norvegia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kapp Norvegia (page does not exist)">Kapp Norvegia</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and 0.5 kilometres (0.31 mi) off it the skerries of Bennskjæra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>93 percent of the island is covered by <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, giving it a domed shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> The summit region of the island is Wilhelmplatået, slightly to the west of the island's center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> is 3.5 kilometres (2.2 mi) across<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volcano_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-volcano-58">[58]</a></sup> and surrounded by several peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> The tallest is <a href="/wiki/Olavtoppen" title="Olavtoppen">Olavtoppen</a>, 780 metres (2,560 ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a> (AMSL),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> followed by <a href="/wiki/Lykketoppen" title="Lykketoppen">Lykketoppen</a> (766 metres or 2,513 feet AMSL)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mosbytoppane" title="Mosbytoppane">Mosbytoppane</a> (670 metres or 2,200 feet AMSL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Below Wilhelmplatået is the main <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> responsible for creating the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> The last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eruption" title="Eruption">eruption</a> took place 2000 BC, producing a <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> flow at Kapp Meteor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volcano_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-volcano-58">[58]</a></sup> The volcano is presumed to be in a declining state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baker_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-baker-16">[16]</a></sup> The temperature 30 centimetres (12 in) below the surface is 25 °C (77 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island's total coastline is 29.6 kilometres (18.4 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factbook_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-factbook-61">[61]</a></sup> Landing on the island is very difficult, as it normally experiences high seas and features a <a href="/wiki/Steep_coast" title="Steep coast">steep coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-5"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> During the winter, it is surrounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pack_ice" title="Pack ice">pack ice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-name-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bouvet_Triple_Junction" title="Bouvet Triple Junction">Bouvet Triple Junction</a> is located 275 kilometres (171 mi) west of Bouvet Island. It is a <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">triple junction</a> between the <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Plate" title="Antarctic Plate">Antarctic Plate</a>, and of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Indian_Ridge" title="Southwest Indian Ridge">Southwest Indian Ridge</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American%E2%80%93Antarctic_Ridge" title="American–Antarctic Ridge">American–Antarctic Ridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The island is located south of the Antarctic Convergence, giving it a marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_climate" title="Antarctic climate">Antarctic climate</a> dominated by heavy clouds and fog. It experiences a mean temperature of −1 °C (30 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-6"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> with January average of 1 °C (34 °F) and September average of −3 °C (27 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npolar_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-npolar-47">[47]</a></sup> The monthly high mean temperatures fluctuate little through the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate-63">[63]</a></sup> The peak temperature of 14 °C (57 °F) was recorded in March 1980, caused by intense sun radiation. Spot temperatures as high as 20 °C (68 °F) have been recorded in sunny weather on rock faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b59_29-7"><a href="#cite_note-b59-29">[29]</a></sup> The island predominantly experiences a weak <a href="/wiki/West_wind" title="West wind">west wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npolar_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-npolar-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature">Nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg/220px-Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg/330px-Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg/440px-Bouvet_aerial_photo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> image of Bouvet Island from space.</div>
</div>

<p>The harsh climate and ice-bound terrain limits vegetation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascomycetes" title="Ascomycetes">ascomycetes</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lichens" title="Lichens">lichens</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Non-vascular_plant" title="Non-vascular plant">non-vascular plants</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosses" title="Mosses">mosses</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverworts" title="Liverworts">liverworts</a>). The flora are representative for the maritime Antarctic and are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytogeographically" title="Phytogeographically">phytogeographically</a> similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Sandwich Islands">South Sandwich Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a>. Vegetation is limited because of the ice cover, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_algae" title="Snow algae">snow algae</a> are recorded. The remaining vegetation is located in snow-free areas such as <a href="/wiki/Nunatak" title="Nunatak">nunatak</a> ridges and other parts of the summit plateau, the coastal cliffs, capes and beaches. At Nyrøysa, five species of moss, six ascomycetes (including five lichens), and twenty algae have been recorded. Most snow-free areas are so steep and subject to frequent <a href="/wiki/Avalanche" title="Avalanche">avalanches</a> that only <a href="/wiki/Crustose" title="Crustose">crustose</a> lichens and algal formations are sustainable. There are six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> ascomycetes, three of which are lichenized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birdlife_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="521" data-file-width="800" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bouvet_island.jpg/220px-Bouvet_island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bouvet_island.jpg/330px-Bouvet_island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bouvet_island.jpg/440px-Bouvet_island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cape Valdivia, Bouvet Island, 2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The island has been designated as an <a href="/wiki/Important_Bird_Area" title="Important Bird Area">Important Bird Area</a> by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> because of its importance as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breeding_ground" title="Breeding ground">breeding ground</a> for <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>. In 1978–79 there were an estimated 117,000 breeding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penguins" title="Penguins">penguins</a> on the island, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Macaroni_penguin" title="Macaroni penguin">macaroni penguin</a> and, to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Chinstrap_penguin" title="Chinstrap penguin">chinstrap penguin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ad%C3%A9lie_penguin" title="Adélie penguin">Adélie penguin</a>, although these were only estimated to be 62,000 in 1989–90. Nyrøysa is the most important <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colony</a> for penguins, supplemented by Posadowskybreen, Kapp Circoncision, Norvegiaodden and across from Larsøya. <a href="/wiki/Southern_fulmar" title="Southern fulmar">Southern fulmar</a> is by far the most common non-penguin bird with 100,000 individuals. Other breeding seabirds consist of <a href="/wiki/Cape_petrel" title="Cape petrel">Cape petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_prion" title="Antarctic prion">Antarctic prion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_storm_petrel" title="Wilson's storm petrel">Wilson's storm petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-bellied_storm_petrel" title="Black-bellied storm petrel">black-bellied storm petrel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subantarctic_skua" title="Subantarctic skua">subantarctic skua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_giant_petrel" title="Southern giant petrel">southern giant petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow_petrel" title="Snow petrel">snow petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slender-billed_prion" title="Slender-billed prion">slender-billed prion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_tern" title="Antarctic tern">Antarctic tern</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kelp_gull" title="Kelp gull">Kelp gull</a> is thought to have bred on the island earlier. Non-breeding birds which can be found on the island include the <a href="/wiki/King_penguin" title="King penguin">king penguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wandering_albatross" title="Wandering albatross">wandering albatross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-browed_albatross" title="Black-browed albatross">black-browed albatross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Campbell_albatross" title="Campbell albatross">Campbell albatross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_yellow-nosed_albatross" title="Atlantic yellow-nosed albatross">Atlantic yellow-nosed albatross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sooty_albatross" title="Sooty albatross">sooty albatross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light-mantled_albatross" title="Light-mantled albatross">light-mantled albatross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_giant_petrel" title="Northern giant petrel">northern giant petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_petrel" title="Antarctic petrel">Antarctic petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_petrel" title="Blue petrel">blue petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft-plumaged_petrel" title="Soft-plumaged petrel">soft-plumaged petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_petrel" title="Kerguelen petrel">Kerguelen petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/White-headed_petrel" title="White-headed petrel">white-headed petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairy_prion" title="Fairy prion">fairy prion</a>, <a href="/wiki/White-chinned_petrel" title="White-chinned petrel">white-chinned petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_shearwater" title="Great shearwater">great shearwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_diving_petrel" title="Common diving petrel">common diving petrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_polar_skua" title="South polar skua">south polar skua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_jaeger" title="Parasitic jaeger">parasitic jaeger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birdlife_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The only non-bird <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> on the island are <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Southern_elephant_seal" title="Southern elephant seal">southern elephant seal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_fur_seal" title="Antarctic fur seal">Antarctic fur seal</a>, which both breed on the island. In 1998–99, there were 88 elephant seal pups and 13,000 fur seal pups at Nyrøysa. <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">Humpback whale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">killer whale</a> are seen in the surrounding waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birdlife_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_government">Politics and government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics and government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2098" data-file-width="3072" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG/220px-Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG/330px-Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG/440px-Bouvet_Island_ISS017-E-16161_no_text.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Bouvetøya is one of three dependencies of Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gisle_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-gisle-64">[64]</a></sup> Unlike <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_Island" title="Peter I Island">Peter I Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Maud_Land" title="Queen Maud Land">Queen Maud Land</a>, which are subject to the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty_System" title="Antarctic Treaty System">Antarctic Treaty System</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b65_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-b65-65">[65]</a></sup> Bouvetøya is not disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factbook_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-factbook-61">[61]</a></sup> The dependency status entails that the island is not part of the Kingdom of Norway, but is still under Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>. Specifically, this implies that the island can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cede" title="Cede">ceded</a> without violating the first article of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Norway" title="Constitution of Norway">Constitution of Norway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gisle_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-gisle-64">[64]</a></sup> Norwegian administration of the island is handled by the Polar Affairs Department of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_and_the_Police_(Norway)" title="Ministry of Justice and the Police (Norway)">Ministry of Justice and the Police</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The annexation of the island is regulated by the Dependency Act of 24 March 1933. It establishes that Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Private_law" title="Private law">private law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Procedural_law" title="Procedural law">procedural law</a> apply to the island, in addition to other laws that explicitly state they are valid on the island. It further establishes that all land belongs to the state, and prohibits the storage and detonation of nuclear products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOV19300227_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-LOV19300227-2">[2]</a></sup> Bouvet Island has been designated with the <a href="/wiki/ISO_3166-2" title="ISO 3166-2">ISO 3166-2</a> code <a href="/wiki/ISO_3166-2:BV" title="ISO 3166-2:BV">BV</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and was subsequently awarded the <a href="/wiki/Country_code" title="Country code">country code</a> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> <tt><a href="/wiki/.bv" title=".bv">.bv</a></tt> on 21 August 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The domain is managed by <a href="/wiki/Norid" title="Norid">Norid</a> but is not in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> surrounding the island covers an area of 441,163 square kilometres (170,334 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The island figures prominently in the book <i>A Grue of Ice</i> (1962, published in the US as <i>The Disappearing Island</i>), an adventure novel based on <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a>, Bouvet, and the mythical Thompson Island, by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Jenkins" title="Geoffrey Jenkins">Geoffrey Jenkins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Bouvet is the setting of the 2004 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_vs._Predator_(film)" title="Alien vs. Predator (film)">Alien vs. Predator</a></i>, in which it is referred to using its Norwegian name "Bouvetøya"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imdb_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-imdb-72">[72]</a></sup> even though in the unrated edition of the film, a satellite focuses in on the island which is geographically situated in the approximate location of <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_Island" title="Peter I Island">Peter I Island</a>.</li>
<li>Bouvet features in the novel <i>Warhead</i> (2005) by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Remic" title="Andy Remic">Andy Remic</a>.</li>
<li>Bouvet features in the novel <i>Batmans Schönheit</i> (2010) by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Steinfest" title="Heinrich Steinfest">Heinrich Steinfest</a>.</li>
</ul>
