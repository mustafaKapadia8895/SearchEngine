<p>The <b>1938 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was a period of annual <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> formation in the Atlantic, lasting from June 16 through October 31. These dates conventionally delimit the period of each year when most tropical cyclones form in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a>. This hurricane season is noteworthy for having the earliest documented hurricane in the month of January.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1938_January_hurricane" title="1938 January hurricane">1938 January hurricane</a></div>
<p>On January 1, a weak <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> formed about 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a> (3,200 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>) southwest of the island of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Miguel_Island" title="São Miguel Island">São Miguel</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. Moving eastward initially, it later began a turn to the south on January 3. On that day, the cyclone transitioned into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm-core_system" title="Warm-core system">warm-core system</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Hurricane_or_typhoon" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storm</a> with winds of 60 <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">miles per hour</a> (97 <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a>). Henceforth, it gradually curved to the southwest and on January 4 became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">hurricane</a>, equal to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_1_hurricane" title="Category 1 hurricane">Category 1</a> on the modern Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale. It peaked at 80 mph (130 km/h) that same day but weakened into a tropical storm on January 5. Turning to the south-southwest, it dissipated on January 6 while centered well to the east of the Lesser Antilles. The cyclone became the first documented Atlantic tropical cyclone to attain hurricane intensity in the month of January and is one of just six Atlantic storms known to have occurred in that month, the others being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_One_(1951)" title="Tropical Storm One (1951)">tropical storm in 1951</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">1955</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_Storm_One_(1978)" title="Subtropical Storm One (1978)">subtropical storm in 1978</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> in 2006 and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Hurricane Alex</a> in <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The second storm of the season formed in the area of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a> on August 8 and moved generally westward, passing just north of <a href="/wiki/Tortola" title="Tortola">Tortola</a> with winds of 70 mph (110 km/h). After nearly attaining hurricane strength, the rather small cyclone weakened and passed north of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>. On August 9, it made <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfall</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> with winds of 50 mph (80 km/h) before dissipating over the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. Some of the smaller islands sustained damage but the only other effects were squally weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<td>100 mph (155 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  995 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The second hurricane of the season formed south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> on August 9 and moved west-northwest at a high rate of speed, strengthening into a hurricane as it passed just north of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. Becoming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_2_hurricane" title="Category 2 hurricane">Category 2</a> storm while near <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a>, Storm Three bypassed the northern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> but caused little effect, perhaps due to its small size. The hurricane curved more to the northwest as it entered the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, weakening shortly before it reached the coast. Storm Two made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Cameron,_Louisiana" title="Cameron, Louisiana">Cameron, Louisiana</a>, as a minimal hurricane with winds of 75 mph (121 km/h), causing moderate flooding of lowlands and $250,000 in damage (1938 dollars, $3.4 million in 2006 dollars). The storm dissipated over east <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The fourth storm of the season was discovered as a strengthening tropical storm in the central Caribbean Sea on August 23. The storm became a hurricane the next day and maintained a northwest track, steadily strengthening. Storm Three became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">major hurricane</a>, with winds equivalent to Category 3 intensity, a day later and maintained that intensity until landfall on August 26 near present-day <a href="/wiki/Tulum,_Quintana_Roo" title="Tulum, Quintana Roo">Tulum</a>, located on the Yucatán Peninsula. Peak winds at landfall were near 120 mph (190 km/h) as <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a> reported 964 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">millibars</a> (28.47 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) and 91 mph (146 km/h) an hour before the worst conditions arrived. The cyclone weakened to a Category 1 as it passed inland and began to curve more to the west as it crossed the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. Hurricane Four made landfall on August 27 north of <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a> with winds of 85 mph (137 km/h), though it may have been stronger than this as it was compact in size at landfall and few observations were available near the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>. This storm caused tremendous flooding on the Yucatán peninsula and killed nine people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that originated near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> eventually became a tropical depression on September 9 while centered about 2,000 mi (3,200 km) east of Barbados. <p><b>Great New England Hurricane of 1938</b> or <b>The Long Island Express Hurricane</b><br/></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1938_New_England_hurricane" title="1938 New England hurricane">1938 New England hurricane</a></div>
<p>By far the most significant hurricane of the 1938 season, Hurricane Six, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> that caused great destruction across the northeastern United States.</p>
<p>The storm formed from an African wave in early September and tracked westward across the Atlantic. At first, the storm was slow to strengthen, remaining a tropical storm for five days during its travel westward. It finally became a hurricane before crossing 45°W while located several hundred miles east of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>. The storm continued to steadily strengthen and began to take a more northwesterly course. The <i>Long Island Express</i>, as it was nicknamed, peaked as a Category 5 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a> while just east of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> with winds reaching a sustained 160 mph (260 km/h). Following its peak, on September 20, the storm turned due north and accelerated, reaching an unusually fast forward speed of 70 mph (110 km/h), making it the fastest-moving Atlantic tropical cyclone on record. It made landfall on September 21 near <a href="/wiki/Bellport,_New_York" title="Bellport, New York">Bellport, New York</a>, with sustained winds of 120 mph (190 km/h) and an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 941 mbar (27.79 inHg) in the eye. It then made a second landfall near <a href="/wiki/Bridgeport,_Connecticut" title="Bridgeport, Connecticut">Bridgeport, Connecticut</a>, with winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) and a slightly higher pressure of 946 mbar (27.94 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup> Nearly 700 people died in the storm (682) and damages came to over $300 million, $4.5 billion in 2007 dollars.</p>

<p>This storm formed from a disturbance off the north coast of <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> on October 10 and almost immediately crossed into <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, heading north up the Yucatán Peninsula and into the Gulf of Mexico, where it began to move erratically. In the central Gulf on October 12, it made a rather sudden turn to the east, toward <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. However, a couple of days later, Storm Seven slowed down and began to turn back toward the west northwest, at which time it began to accelerate, weakening along the way. On October 17, the system finally made landfall along the north <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> coast near <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston</a> with winds of only 45 mph (72 km/h). No significant impact was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical Storm Eight, possibly a subtropical storm, was a weak storm that took a somewhat unusual southwesterly course. It formed near <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on October 16, brushing by the island on its southwesterly path, on which it remained for the two days. <p>The final storm of the 1938 season formed off the north coast of <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> on November 6 and quickly moved northwest into the Bahamas, during which time it reached its peak intensity of 70 mph (110 km/h); near-hurricane force, before curving back to the southwest while crossing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andros_Island" title="Andros Island">Andros Island</a>. Now in a weakening trend, the storm crossed central <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> on November 9 and dissipated in the Western Caribbean the following day. No one is reported to have died in the storm and damage to the Caribbean islands is unknown, but the Florida coast suffered $100,000 worth of beach erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
