<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Photogrammetry</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Photogrammetry</b> is the science of making measurements from photographs, especially for recovering the exact positions of surface points. Photogrammetry is as old as modern <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>, dating to the mid-19th century and in the simplest example, the distance between two points that lie on a plane parallel to the photographic <a href="/wiki/Image_plane" title="Image plane">image plane</a>, can be determined by measuring their distance on the image, if the <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">scale</a> (<i>s</i>) of the image is known.</p>
<p>Photogrammetric analysis may be applied to one photograph, or may use <a href="/wiki/High-speed_photography" title="High-speed photography">high-speed photography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> to detect, measure and record complex 2-D and 3-D motion fields by feeding measurements and <a href="/wiki/Imagery_analysis" title="Imagery analysis">imagery analysis</a> into <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computational models</a> in an attempt to successively estimate, with increasing accuracy, the actual, 3-D relative motions.</p>
<p>From its beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Stereoplotter" title="Stereoplotter">stereoplotters</a> used to plot <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">contour lines</a> on <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">topographic maps</a>, it now has a very wide range of uses.</p>
<p>(See also <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">lidar</a>, etc.).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photogrammetric_methods">Photogrammetric methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photogrammetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Photogrammetric methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="390" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg/220px-Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg/330px-Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg/440px-Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Photogrammetry_Wiora_EN.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A data model of photogrammetry<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Photogrammetry has been defined by the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Photogrammetry_and_Remote_Sensing" title="American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing">American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing</a> (ASPRS) as the art, science, and technology of obtaining reliable information about physical objects and the environment through processes of recording, measuring and interpreting photographic images and patterns of recorded radiant electromagnetic energy and other phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Photogrammetry uses methods from many disciplines, including <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Projective_geometry" title="Projective geometry">projective geometry</a>. Digital image capturing and photogrammetric processing includes several well defined stages, which allow to generate 2D or 3D digital models of the object as an end product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The data model on the right shows what type of information can go into and come out of photogrammetric methods.</p>
<p>The <i>3-D co-ordinates</i> define the locations of object points in the <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">3-D</a> space. The <i>image co-ordinates</i> define the locations of the object points' images on the film or an electronic imaging device. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrinsic_parameters" title="Extrinsic parameters">exterior orientation</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> of a camera defines its location in space and its view direction. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrinsic_parameters" title="Intrinsic parameters">inner orientation</a></i> defines the geometric parameters of the imaging process. This is primarily the focal length of the lens, but can also include the description of lens distortions. Further <i>additional observations</i> play an important role: With <i>scale bars</i>, basically a known distance of two points in space, or known <i>fix points</i>, the connection to the basic measuring units is created.</p>
<p>Each of the four main variables can be an <i>input</i> or an <i>output</i> of a photogrammetric method.</p>
<p>Algorithms for photogrammetry typically attempt to minimize the sum of the <a href="/wiki/Least_squares" title="Least squares">squares of errors</a> over the coordinates and relative displacements of the reference points. This minimization is known as <a href="/wiki/Bundle_adjustment" title="Bundle adjustment">bundle adjustment</a> and is often performed using the <a href="/wiki/Levenberg%E2%80%93Marquardt_algorithm" title="Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm">Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stereophotogrammetry">Stereophotogrammetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photogrammetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stereophotogrammetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Stereophotogrammetry" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Roentgen_stereophotogrammetry" title="Roentgen stereophotogrammetry">Roentgen stereophotogrammetry</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main category: <a href="/wiki/Category:Stereophotogrammetry" title="Category:Stereophotogrammetry">Stereophotogrammetry</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Computer_stereo_vision" title="Computer stereo vision">Computer stereo vision</a></div>
<p>A special case, called <b>stereophotogrammetry</b>, involves estimating the three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinates</a> of points on an object employing measurements made in two or more photographic images taken from different positions (see <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">stereoscopy</a>). Common points are identified on each image. A line of sight (or ray) can be constructed from the camera location to the point on the object. It is the intersection of these rays (triangulation) that determines the three-dimensional location of the point. More sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> can exploit other information about the scene that is known <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i>, for example <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetries</a>, in some cases allowing reconstructions of 3-D coordinates from only one camera position. Stereophotogrammetry is emerging as a robust non-contacting measurement technique to determine dynamic characteristics and mode shapes of non-rotating<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spie-turbine_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-spie-turbine-6">[6]</a></sup> and rotating structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Integration">Integration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photogrammetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Integration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Photogrammetric data with a dense range data in which scanners complement each other. Photogrammetry is more accurate in the x and y direction while range data are generally more accurate in the z direction. This range data can be supplied by techniques like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LiDAR" title="LiDAR">LiDAR</a>, laser scanners (using time of flight, triangulation or interferometry), white-light digitizers and any other technique that scans an area and returns x, y, z coordinates for multiple discrete points (commonly called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_clouds" title="Point clouds">point clouds</a>"). Photos can clearly define the edges of buildings when the point cloud footprint can not. It is beneficial to incorporate the advantages of both systems and integrate them to create a better product.</p>
<p>A 3-D visualization can be created by georeferencing the aerial photos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and LiDAR data in the same reference frame, orthorectifying the aerial photos, and then draping the orthorectified images on top of the LiDAR grid. It is also possible to create digital terrain models and thus 3-D visualisations using pairs (or multiples) of aerial photographs or satellite (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPOT_satellite" title="SPOT satellite">SPOT satellite</a> imagery). Techniques such as adaptive least squares stereo matching are then used to produce a dense array of correspondences which are transformed through a camera model to produce a dense array of x, y, z data which can be used to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_terrain_model" title="Digital terrain model">digital terrain model</a> and orthoimage products. Systems which use these techniques, e.g. the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ITG_digital_terrain_model_generation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ITG digital terrain model generation system (page does not exist)">ITG</a> system, were developed in the 1980s and 1990s but have since been supplanted by LiDAR and radar-based approaches, although these techniques may still be useful in deriving elevation models from old aerial photographs or satellite images.</p>

<p>Photogrammetry is used in fields such as <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">topographic mapping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> investigation, and <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Aerial_archaeology" title="Aerial archaeology">Archaeologists</a> use it to quickly produce plans of large or complex sites, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a> use it to determine the wind speed of <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornados</a> when objective weather data cannot be obtained.</p>
<p>It is also used to combine <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> with <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated imagery</a> in movies <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">post-production</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i> is a good example of the use of photogrammetry in film (details are given in the DVD extras). Photogrammetry was used extensively to create photorealistic environmental assets for video games including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vanishing_of_Ethan_Carter" title="The Vanishing of Ethan Carter">The Vanishing of Ethan Carter</a></i> as well as <a href="/wiki/EA_DICE" title="EA DICE">EA DICE</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_Battlefront_(2015_video_game)" title="Star Wars Battlefront (2015 video game)">Star Wars Battlefront</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A somewhat similar application is the scanning of objects to automatically make 3D models of them. Some programs like <a href="/wiki/3DF_Zephyr" title="3DF Zephyr">3DF Zephyr</a>, <a href="/wiki/RealityCapture" title="RealityCapture">RealityCapture</a>, Acute3D's Smart3DCapture, now part of Bentley Systems and renamed ContextCapture, Pix4Dmapper, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoscan" title="Photoscan">Photoscan</a>, 123D Catch, Bundler toolkit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> PIXDIM, and Photosketch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> have been made to allow people to quickly make 3D models using this photogrammetry method. It should be noted though that the produced model often still contains gaps, so additional cleanup with software like MeshLab, netfabb or MeshMixer is often still necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Photogrammetry is also commonly employed in collision engineering, especially with automobiles. When litigation for accidents occurs and engineers need to determine the exact deformation present in the vehicle, it is common for several years to have passed and the only evidence that remains is accident scene photographs taken by the police. Photogrammetry is used to determine how much the car in question was deformed, which relates to the amount of energy required to produce that deformation. The energy can then be used to determine important information about the crash (such as the velocity at time of impact).</p>
