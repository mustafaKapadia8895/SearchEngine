<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Snowmobile</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>snowmobile</b>, also known as a <b>snowmachine</b>, is a vehicle designed for winter travel and recreation on snow. It is designed to be operated on snow and ice and does not require a road or trail but most are driven on open terrain or trails. Snowmobiling is a sport that many people have taken on as a serious hobby.</p>
<p>Older snowmobiles could generally accommodate two people; however, most snowmobiles manufactured in the last twenty-five years have been designed for a single rider. Snowmobiles built with the ability to carry two riders are referred to as "2-up" snowmobiles or 'touring' models and make up an extremely small share of the market. Snowmobiles do not have any enclosures, except for a windshield, and their engines normally drive a <a href="/wiki/Continuous_track" title="Continuous track">continuous track</a> at the rear. <a href="/wiki/Ski" title="Ski">Skis</a> at the front provide directional control.</p>
<p>Early snowmobiles used rubber tracks, but modern snowmobiles' tracks are typically made of a <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a> <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite</a>. Originally, snowmobiles were powered by <a href="/wiki/Two-stroke_engine" title="Two-stroke engine">two-stroke</a> <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> and since the mid-2000s <a href="/wiki/Four-stroke_engine" title="Four-stroke engine">four-stroke engines</a> have also entered the market.</p>
<p>The second half of the 20th century saw the rise of recreational snowmobiling, whose riders are called <i>snowmobilers</i> or <i>sledders</i>. Recreational riding is known as snowcross/racing, trail riding, freestyle, mountain climbing, boondocking, carving, ditchbanging and grass drags. In the summertime snowmobilers can <a href="/wiki/Drag_racing" title="Drag racing">drag race</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> strips, or even across water (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Snowmobile_skipping" title="Snowmobile skipping">Snowmobile skipping</a></i>). Snowmobiles are sometimes modified to compete in long-distance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Off-road" title="Off-road">off-road</a> races such as Trevor Erickson's #901 entry in the 2014 Vegas to Reno race <a class="external text" href="http://nismostuff.blogspot.com/2014/10/snowmobile-to-reno.html" rel="nofollow">(see "Snowmobile to Reno")</a></p>

<p>In 1911 a 24 year old, Harold J. Kalenze (pronounced Collins) patent the Vehicle Propeller in Brandon, Manitoba, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1915 Ray H. Muscott of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waters,_Michigan" title="Waters, Michigan">Waters</a>, Michigan, received the Canadian patent for his motor sleigh, or "traineau automobile", and on June 27, 1916, he received the first United States <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> for a snow-vehicle using the now recognized format of rear track(s) and front skis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Many individuals later modified <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Ford Model Ts</a> with the undercarriage replaced by tracks and skis following this design. They were popular for rural mail delivery for a time. The common name for these conversion of cars and small trucks was <b>Snowflyers</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The challenges of cross-country transportation in the winter led to the invention of the snowmobile, an all-terrain vehicle specifically designed for travel across deep snow where other vehicles floundered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> During the 20th century, rapidly evolving designs produced machines that were two-person tracked vehicles powered by gas engines that enabled them to tow a sled or travel, initially at low-to-moderate speeds, depending on snow conditions, terrain and obstacles protruding above the snow like brush and trees. Where early designs had 10 horsepower (7.5 kW) two-stroke engines, there has been a move toward newer style 2-stroke and 4-stroke gasoline engines, some with over 200 hp (150 kW).</p>

<p>The origin of the snowmobile, or <i>sled</i>, as they are often referred to,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is not the work of any one inventor but more a process of advances in engines for the propulsion of vehicles and supporting devices over snow. It parallels the development of the automobile and later aviation, often inventors using the same components for a different use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsinites</a> experimented with over-snow vehicles before 1900, experimenting with bicycles equipped with runners and gripping fins; steam-propelled sleighs; and (later) Model T Fords converted with rear tractor treads and skis in front. A patent (554.482) for the Sled-Propeller design, without a model, was submitted on Sept. 5, 1895 by inventors William J. Culman and William B. Follis of <a href="/wiki/Brule,_Wisconsin" title="Brule, Wisconsin">Brule, Wisconsin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the first races held near <a href="/wiki/Three_Lakes,_Wisconsin" title="Three Lakes, Wisconsin">Three Lakes</a> in 1926, 104 of these "snowbuggies" started. Carl Eliason of <a href="/wiki/Sayner,_Wisconsin" title="Sayner, Wisconsin">Sayner</a> developed the prototype of the modern snowmobile in the 1920s when he mounted a two-cylinder motorcycle engine on a long sled, steered it with skis under the front, and propelled it with single, endless track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Eliason made 40 snowmobiles, patented in 1927.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Upon receiving an order for 200 from <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, he sold his patent to the <a href="/wiki/Four_Wheel_Drive" title="Four Wheel Drive">FWD Company</a> of <a href="/wiki/Clintonville,_Wisconsin" title="Clintonville, Wisconsin">Clintonville</a>. They made 300 for military use, then transferred the patent to a Canadian subsidiary.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Motor_Sleigh" title="American Motor Sleigh">American Motor Sleigh</a> was a short-lived novelty vehicle produced in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> in 1905. Designed for travel on snow, it consisted of a sleigh body mounted on a framework that held an engine, a drive-shaft system, and runners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Although considered an interesting novelty, sales were low and production ceased in 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Aerosani, propeller-driven and running on skis, was built in 1909–1910 by Russian inventor <a href="/wiki/Igor_Sikorsky" title="Igor Sikorsky">Igor Sikorsky</a> of helicopter fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Aerosanis were used by the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Winter War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> There is some dispute over whether Aerosanis count as snowmobiles because they were not propelled by tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adolphe_K%C3%A9gresse" title="Adolphe Kégresse">Adolphe Kégresse</a> designed an original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caterpillar_track" title="Caterpillar track">caterpillar tracks</a> system, called the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A9gresse_track" title="Kégresse track">Kégresse track</a>, while working for Tsar <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II of Russia</a> between 1906 and 1916. These used a flexible belt rather than interlocking metal segments and could be fitted to a conventional car or truck to turn it into a <a href="/wiki/Half-track" title="Half-track">half-track</a>, suitable for use over soft ground, including snow. Conventional front wheels and steering were used but the wheel could be fitted with skis as seen in the upper right image. He applied it to several cars in the Royal garage including <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Limited" title="Rolls-Royce Limited">Rolls-Royce</a> cars and <a href="/wiki/Packard" title="Packard">Packard</a> trucks. Although this was not a snowmobile, it is an ancestor of the modern concept.</p>

<p>The relatively dry snow conditions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Midwest" title="United States Midwest">United States Midwest</a> suited the converted Ford Model Ts and other like vehicles, but they were not suitable for humid snow areas such as southern <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. This led <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Armand_Bombardier" title="Joseph-Armand Bombardier">Joseph-Armand Bombardier</a> from the small town of <a href="/wiki/Valcourt_(city)" title="Valcourt (city)">Valcourt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, to invent a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caterpillar_track" title="Caterpillar track">caterpillar track</a> system suitable for all kinds of snow conditions. Bombardier had already made some "metal" tracked vehicles since 1928, but his new revolutionary track traction system (a toothed wheel covered in rubber, and a rubber-and-cotton track that wraps around the back wheels) was his first major invention. He started production of the B-7, an enclosed, seven-passenger snowmobile, in 1937, and introduced the B-12, a twelve-passenger model, in 1942. The B-7 had a V-8 <a href="/wiki/Flathead_engine" title="Flathead engine">flathead engine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>. The B-12 had a flathead in line six-cylinder engine from <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> industrial, and 2,817 units were produced until 1951. It was used in many applications, such as ambulances, <a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a> vehicles, winter "school buses", forestry machines, and even army vehicles in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Bombardier had always dreamed of a smaller version, more like the size of a motor scooter.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_models">Early models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerous people had ideas for a smaller personal snowmobile. In 1914, O. M. Erickson and Art Olsen of the P.N. Bushnell company in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_South_Dakota" title="Aberdeen, South Dakota">Aberdeen</a>, South Dakota, built an open two-seater "motor-bob" out of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_(motorcycle)" title="Indian (motorcycle)">Indian motorcycle</a> modified with a cowl-cover, side-by-side seating, and a set of sled-runners fore and aft. While it did not have the tracks of a true snowmobile, its appearance was otherwise similar to the modern version and is one of the earliest examples of a personal motorized snow-vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1951 Dr. Fritz Riemerschmid devised what he called a snow scooter. The machine had a track mounted beneath a snowboard like base, on top of which were an enclosed engine with motorcycle like seat and fuel tank. the vehicle was steered via a steering wheel and cables linked to two small skis on outriggers either side of the vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1950s, a United States firm built a "snowmobile the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Alaska" title="Arctic Alaska">arctic area</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> that had the drive train reversed of today's snowmobiles with two front wheels—the larger one behind the smaller one—with tires driving an endless loop track". Little is known about this "snowmobile" meant to haul cargo and trade goods to isolated settlements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polaris">Polaris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Polaris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Edgar and Allen Hetteen and David Johnson of <a href="/wiki/Roseau,_Minnesota" title="Roseau, Minnesota">Roseau</a>, Minnesota, were among the first to build a practical snowmobile in 1955–1956, but the early machines were heavy (1,000 lb or 450 kg) and slow (20 mph or 32 km/h). Their company, Hetteen Hoist &amp; Derrick Co., became <a href="/wiki/Polaris_Industries" title="Polaris Industries">Polaris Industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BRP">BRP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: BRP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was only in 1960, when engines became lighter and smaller than before, that <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Armand_Bombardier" title="Joseph-Armand Bombardier">Joseph-Armand Bombardier</a> invented what we now know as the modern snowmobile in its open-cockpit one- or two-person form, and started selling it under the brand name <a href="/wiki/Ski-Doo" title="Ski-Doo">Ski-Doo</a> through his company <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Inc." title="Bombardier Inc.">Bombardier Inc.</a> (now manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Recreational_Products" title="Bombardier Recreational Products">Bombardier Recreational Products</a>).</p>
<p>Competitors copied and improved his design; in the 1970s there were over a hundred snowmobile manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mus.C3.A9e-stat_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mus.C3.A9e-stat-22">[22]</a></sup> From 1970 to 1973, two million machines were sold, peaking at 500,000 sold in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mus.C3.A9e-stat_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mus.C3.A9e-stat-22">[22]</a></sup> Many of the snowmobile companies were small and the biggest manufacturers were often attempts by <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycle</a> makers and <a href="/wiki/Outboard_motor" title="Outboard motor">outboard motor</a> makers to branch off in a new market. Most of these companies went <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankrupt</a> or were acquired by larger companies during the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a> and succeeding recessions. Sales rebounded to 260,000 in 1997 but gradually decreased afterwards, influenced by warmer winters and the use during all four seasons of small one- or two-person <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">ATVs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alpina">Alpina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alpina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alpina Snowmobiles are manufactured in <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, by Alpina s.r.l., a manufacturer of various on-snow implements that had been building dual-track snowmobiles since 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two manufacturers of dual-track snowmobiles. One is Alpina and the other is a Russian sled called Buran (Bombardier discontinued manufacturing its dual-track model, the Elite, in 2005).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Models</dt>
</dl>
<p>Alpina manufactures one basic dual-track snowmobile design. In 2002 the <i>Sherpa</i> was introduced and is the model name for the four-stroke machine. Prior to introducing the Sherpa, Alpina offered a 2-stroke series designated the <i>Superclass</i>. The four-stroke Sherpa is currently the top machine in production. A new version of the Superclass has been released in 2017, with a lot of innovations and a new 4-stroke engine.</p>
<p>The <i>Sherpa</i> and <i>Superclass</i> series shared the same basic dual-track platform, twin 20 in × 156 in (510 mm × 3,960 mm) tracks with dual skis up front.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg/220px-Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg/330px-Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg/440px-Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dual-track_snowmobile.jpg" title="Ingrandisci"></a></div>
Alpina Sherpa, a dual track snowmobile</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Power for the Sherpa is supplied by a 1.6L in-line four-cylinder gasoline automotive engine. The new Superclass power is provided by a 1.2L 3-cylinder 4-stroke gasoline engine.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Features</dt>
</dl>
<p>The <i>Sherpa</i> and <i>Superclass</i> are designed as working snowmobiles for carrying supplies, pulling cargo sleds, pulling trail grooming implements, carrying several passengers, and negotiating deep snow.</p>
<p>Engine and transmission combination are designed to deliver optimum power to pull or carry large loads while top-end speeds are kept below 52 mph (84 km/h), depending on the model. The large footprint of the dual tracks and dual skis allows the <i>Sherpa</i> and <i>Superclass</i> to "float" on top of deep snow and not sink in and get stuck.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_markets">Current markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2003<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the snowmobile market has been shared between the four large North American makers (<a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Recreational_Products" title="Bombardier Recreational Products">Bombardier Recreational Products (BRP)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Cat" title="Arctic Cat">Arctic Cat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Corporation" title="Yamaha Motor Corporation">Yamaha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polaris_Industries" title="Polaris Industries">Polaris</a>) and some specialized makers like the Quebec-based AD Boivin, manufacturer of the Snow Hawk<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boivin_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boivin-25">[25]</a></sup> and the European Alpina snowmobile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mus.C3.A9e-stat_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mus.C3.A9e-stat-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISMA-stat_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISMA-stat-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Higher-powered modern snowmobiles can achieve speeds in excess of 150 mph (240 km/h). Drag racing snowmobiles can reach speeds in excess of 200 mph (320 km/h).</p>
<p>Snowmobiles are widely used in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> territories for travel. However, the small Arctic population means a correspondingly small market. Most snowmobiles are sold for recreational purposes, in places where snow cover is stable during winter. The number of snowmobiles in Europe and other parts of the world is low, but growing.</p>
<p>Snowmobiles designed to perform various work tasks have been available for many years with dual tracks from such manufacturers as Aktiv (Sweden), who made <a href="/wiki/Aktiv_Grizzly" title="Aktiv Grizzly">the Grizzly</a>, Ockelbo (Sweden), who made the 8000, and Bombardier who made the Alpine and later the Alpine II. Currently there are two manufacturers of dual-track snowmobiles; Russia's Buran<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the Italian Alpina snowmobiles (under the name <i>Sherpa</i> and <i>Superclass</i>).</p>
<p>An odd version of snowmobile is the Swedish <i><a href="/wiki/Larven" title="Larven">Larven</a></i>, made by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lenko_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lenko Company (page does not exist)">Lenko Company</a> of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96stersund" title="Östersund">Östersund</a>, from the 1960s until the end of the 1980s. It was a very small and basic design, with just an engine in the rear and a track. The driver sat on it and steered using skis on his feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larven_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larven-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propulsion">Propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All modern snowmobiles are powered by either a four- or two-stroke <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>. Historically, snowmobiles have always used two-stroke engines because of their reduced complexity, weight and cost, compared to a similarly powered four-stroke. However, four-stroke powered snowmobiles have been gaining popularity steadily in the last fifteen or so years, with manufacturer Yamaha producing four-stroke snowmobiles only. The <a href="/wiki/Whistler_Blackcomb" title="Whistler Blackcomb">Whistler Blackcomb</a> ski resort is testing <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric</a> snowmobiles with lower noise and shorter range,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and similar vehicles exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first snowmobiles made do with as little as 5 horsepower (3.7 kW) engines, but engine sizes and efficiency have improved drastically. In the early 1990s, the biggest engines available (typically 600-650cc range) produced around 115 hp (86 kW). As of 2010, several snowmobiles are available with engines sizes up to 1,200 cc, producing 150+ hp, as well as several models with up to 1,000 cc engines producing closer to 180 hp (130 kW). Recently, some models are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbo_charged" title="Turbo charged">turbo-charged</a>, resulting in dramatic increase of engine horsepower. Snowmobiles are capable of moving across steep hillsides without sliding down-slope if the rider transfers their weight towards the uphill side, a process called <i>side-hilling</i>.</p>
<p>Mountain sleds permit access in remote areas with deep snow, which was nearly impossible a few decades ago. This is mainly due to alterations, enhancements, and additions of original trail model designs such as weight, weight distribution, track length, paddle depth, and power. Technology and design advances in mountain snowmobiles have improved since 2003 with Ski-Doo's introduction of the "REV" framework platform. Most 2-stroke mountain snowmobiles have a top engine size of 800 cc, producing around 150 hp (110 kW), although some 1,000 cc factory machines have been produced. These may not be as popular as many 800 cc models outperform them because of weight and an increase of unneeded power.</p>
<p>Cornices and other kinds of jumps are sought after for aerial maneuvers. Riders often search for non-tracked, virgin terrain and are known to "trailblaze" or "boondock" deep into remote territory where there is absolutely no visible path to follow. However, this type of trailblazing is dangerous as contact with buried rocks, logs, and frozen ground can cause extensive damage and injuries. Riders look for large open fields of fresh snow where they can <a href="/wiki/Carve_turn" title="Carve turn">carve</a>. Some riders use extensively modified snowmobiles, customized with <a href="/wiki/Aftermarket_(automotive)" title="Aftermarket (automotive)">aftermarket</a> accessories like handle-bar risers, handguards, custom/lightweight hoods, windshields, and seats, running board supports, <a href="/wiki/Snowmobile_stud" title="Snowmobile stud">studs</a>, and numerous other modifications that increase power and maneuverability. Many of these customizations can now be purchased straight off the showroom floor on stock models.</p>
<p>Trail snowmobiles improved in the past 15 years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> as well (many of them borrowed from endeavors to produce winning mountain sleds). Heavy "muscle sleds" can produce speeds in excess of 100 mph (160 km/h) due to powerful engines (up to 1,200 cc stock, and custom engines exceeding 1,200 cc), short tracks, and good traction on groomed trails. Sno-cross oriented snowmobiles often have an engine size cap of 440 or 600 cc, but lighter machines with redesigned stances, formats, and weight control have produced extremely fast and quickly accelerating race sleds.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> impact of snowmobiles has been the subject of much debate. Governments have been reacting slowly to <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, partly because of <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> from manufacturers and snowmobilers. For instance, in 1999, the Canadian government adopted the <i><a href="/wiki/Canadian_Environmental_Protection_Act,_1999" title="Canadian Environmental Protection Act, 1999">Canadian Environmental Protection Act, 1999</a></i>, but the set of rules governing pollution emissions for off-road vehicles was only released in January 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In another example of regulation, only four-stroke snowmobiles are allowed in <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a> since a bylaw was recently passed to minimize CO<sub>2</sub> emissions and noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellowstone" title="Yellowstone">Yellowstone</a>, snowmobiles account for 80% of total hydrocarbon emissions and 50% of carbon monoxide emissions in the winter. This is just less than 2% and 1% respectively of the overall annual pollution within the park. Snowmobiles are only allowed to be ridden on the unplowed roads used in the summer, and riding off the roads is prohibited. This accounts for less than 1% (0.002%) of the park area.</p>

<p>Most snowmobiles are still powered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-stroke" title="Two-stroke">two-stroke</a> engines, although Alpina and <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">Yamaha</a> have been using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four-stroke" title="Four-stroke">four-strokes</a> since 2002 and 2003, respectively. However, in the last decade several manufacturers have been successful in designing less polluting motors, and putting most of them in production. Yamaha and Arctic-Cat were the first to mass-produce four-stroke models, which are significantly less polluting than the early two-stroke machines. Alpina offers only 4-stroke EFI engines equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> and dual oxygen-probe. Bombardier's E-Tec two-stroke motors emit 85% less pollutants than previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbureted" title="Carbureted">carbureted</a> 2-strokes. Polaris has developed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel-injection" title="Fuel-injection">fuel-injection</a> technology called "Cleanfire Injection" on their 2 strokes. The industry is also working on a direct-injected "clean two strokes" that is better in terms of <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>X</sub></a> emissions.</p>
<p>Independent researchers, undergraduates and graduate students participate in contests to lessen the impact of emissions from snowmobiles. The <i>Clean Snow Mobile Challenge</i> is held yearly at <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Technological_University" title="Michigan Technological University">Michigan Technological University</a> regrouping the entries from universities from across United States and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Some of the participants in recent years have been the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_polytechnique_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="École polytechnique de Montréal">École polytechnique de Montréal</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Quasiturbine" title="Quasiturbine">Quasiturbine</a> engine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UM_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-UM-33">[33]</a></sup> and students from <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_de_technologie_sup%C3%A9rieure" title="École de technologie supérieure">École de technologie supérieure</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_du_Qu%C3%A9bec_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Université du Québec à Montréal">UQAM</a> with a less polluting two-stroke engine using <a href="/wiki/E85" title="E85">E85</a> and direct injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ETS_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ETS-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noise">Noise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Noise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maximum noise restrictions have been enacted by law for both production of snowmobiles and aftermarket components. For instance, in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> (Canada) noise levels must be 78 <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">decibels</a> or less at 20 meters from a snowmobile path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SRC2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-SRC2-35">[35]</a></sup> As of 2009, snowmobiles produce 90% less noise than in the 1960s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISMA-stat_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-ISMA-stat-26">[26]</a></sup> but there are still numerous complaints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Efforts to reduce noise focus on suppressing mechanical noise of the suspension components and tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SRC2_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-SRC2-35">[35]</a></sup> Arctic Cat in 2005 introduced "Silent Track technology" on touring models such as the T660 Turbo, Bearcat, and some M-Series sleds. Ski-Doo has since then also used comparative "silent track technology" on some models.</p>
<p>The use of aftermarket exhaust systems ("cans" or "silencers") is controversial. These replace the stock muffler with a less restrictive system that is usually claimed to increase power output of the engine. However, these aftermarket exhausts are often much louder than those from the factory, with only some being slightly quieter than a completely open, unbaffled system. Most, if not all, local snowmobile clubs (that maintain and groom trail systems) do not recommend them because of noise. Local and state authorities have set up checkpoints on high-traffic trails, checking for excessively loud systems and issuing citations. Typically these systems are installed on 2-stroke powered machines (giving the distinctive "braap" sound); however, in recent years aftermarket companies have released silencers for 4-stroke models as well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_in_isolated_communities">Importance in isolated communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Importance in isolated communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the invention of snowmobiles, isolated communities of northern North America have always had a demand for them. However, the early snowmobiles designs were not economical or functional enough for the harsh environment of northern North America. Joseph-Armand Bombardier started producing the Ski-Doo in 1959 at the request of a priest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-37">[37]</a></sup> The priest had asked Bombardier to make an economical and reliable means of winter travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The Ski-Doo greatly changed life in northern North America's isolated communities, where Ski-Doo replaced sled dogs by the end of the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jozic_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jozic-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Ski-Doo also greatly improved communication between isolated communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_1999_485_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich_1999_485-41">[41]</a></sup> Snowmobiles are also called "Snow Machines” in some areas of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In northern North America, historically, isolated communities depended on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dog_sledding" title="Dog sledding">dog sledding</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowshoeing" title="Snowshoeing">snowshoeing</a> as their primary method of transportation for hunting during the winter months. The Ski-Doo allowed trappers to travel greater distances faster, allowing them to expand their hunting grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_1999_485_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reich_1999_485-41">[41]</a></sup> Prospectors, mining companies, foresters, backcountry cabin owners, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">Canadian Army</a> also found snowmobiles very effective because they were the most economical method of transportation of small loads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jozic_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jozic-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_1999_489_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich_1999_489-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to the International Snowmobile Manufacturers Association, snowmobilers in Canada and the United States spend over $28 billion on snowmobiling each year. This includes expenditures on equipment, clothing, accessories, snowmobiling vacations (lodging, fuel, and food), maintenance and others. Often this is the only source of income for some smaller towns, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bralorne,_British_Columbia" title="Bralorne, British Columbia">Bralorne</a>, British Columbia, that rely solely on <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> during the summer and winter months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Once a booming gold mining town, Bralorne is now a very small town with a population of 60,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and it is relatively inaccessible by car in the winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The economy relies on visits from snowmobilers, who contribute to the economy by spending money on gas, food, and hotels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_safety">Accidents and safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowmobile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Accidents and safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a result of their inherent maneuverability, acceleration, and high-speed abilities, both skill and physical strength are required to operate a snowmobile.</p>
<p>Snowmobile injuries and fatalities are high compared to those caused by on road motor vehicle traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Losing control of a snowmobile could easily cause extensive damage, injury, or death. One such cause of snowmobile accidents is loss of control from a loose grip. If the rider falls off, the loss of control can easily result in the snowmobile colliding with a nearby object, such as a rock or tree. Most snowmobiles are fitted with a cord connected to a kill switch, which would stop the snowmobile if the rider falls off; however, not all riders use this device every time they operate a snowmobile.</p>
<p>Swerving off of the path may result in rolling the snowmobile or crashing into an obstacle. In unfamiliar areas, riders may crash into suspended barbed wire or haywire fences at high speeds. Each year a number of serious/fatal accidents have been caused by these factors.</p>
<p>Each year, riders are killed by hitting other snowmobiles, automobiles, pedestrians, rocks, trees, or fences, or falling through thin ice. On average, 10 people a year have died in such crashes in <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> alone, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcohol</a> a contributing factor in many cases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, 16 out of 21 deaths in snowmobile collisions between 1996 and 2000 were caused by the effects of alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Wrestler <a href="/wiki/Lindsey_Durlacher" title="Lindsey Durlacher">Lindsey Durlacher</a> died in 2011 following surgery for a broken <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sternum</a> he sustained in a snowmobile accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fatal collisions with trains can also occur when a snowmobile operator engages in the illegal practice of "rail riding", riding between railroad track rails over snow-covered <a href="/wiki/Railroad_tie" title="Railroad tie">sleepers</a>. Inability to hear the sound of an oncoming train over the engine noise of a snowmobile makes this activity extremely dangerous. Collision with large animals such as moose and deer, which may venture onto a snowmobile trail, is another major cause of snowmobile accidents. Most often such encounters occur at night or in low-visibility conditions when the animal could not be seen in time to prevent a collision. Also even when successful, a sudden maneuver to miss hitting the animal could still result in the operator losing control of the snowmobile.</p>
<p>A large number of snowmobile deaths in Alaska are caused by drowning. Because of the cold weather in many parts of Alaska, the rivers and lakes are generally frozen over during certain times of the year in winter. People who ride early or late in the season run the risk of falling through weak ice, and heavy winter clothing can make it extremely difficult to escape the frozen water. While a snowmobile is heavy, it also distributes its weight at a larger area than a standing person, so a driver who has stopped his vehicle out on the ice of a frozen lake can go through the ice just by stepping off the snowmobile.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The next leading cause of injury and death is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalanches" title="Avalanches">Avalanches</a>, which can result from the practice of <a href="/wiki/Highmarking" title="Highmarking">Highmarking</a>, or driving a snowmobile as far up a hill as it can go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Risks can be reduced through education, proper training, appropriate gear and attention to published avalanche warnings. In some areas of Western U.S., organizations provide avalanche training, some of which is free. It is recommended that snowmobile riders wear a helmet and a <a href="/wiki/Snowmobile_suit" title="Snowmobile suit">snowmobile suit</a>.</p>

Snowmobile race</div>

<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/International_500" title="International 500">International 500</a> is a large racing event held annually in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sault_Sainte_Marie,_Michigan" title="Sault Sainte Marie, Michigan">Sault Sainte Marie</a>, Michigan. It is a 500-mile (800 km) race on a track, with the current purse being in excess of $40,000. It has been running since February 1969.</li>
<li>Drag racing is common with snowmobiles year-round, with summer and fall often with grass or closed-course (asphalt or concrete) drag strips. The largest event is Hay Days in <a href="/wiki/North_Branch,_Minnesota" title="North Branch, Minnesota">North Branch</a>, Minnesota, on the first weekend following <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Championship" title="World Championship">World Championship</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercross" title="Watercross">Watercross</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snowmobile_skipping" title="Snowmobile skipping">snowmobile skipping</a> races are held in <a href="/wiki/Grantsburg,_Wisconsin" title="Grantsburg, Wisconsin">Grantsburg</a>, Wisconsin, in July. The snowmobiles are raced on a marked course, similar to <a href="/wiki/Motocross" title="Motocross">motocross</a> courses, without the ramps and on water.</li>
<li>The Snocross racing series are snowmobile races on a motocross-like course. The races are held during the winter season in Northern United States and Canada. One of the largest in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> is the Northeast SnoX Challenge in early January in <a href="/wiki/Malone_(village),_New_York" title="Malone (village), New York">Malone</a>, New York, and run by Rock Maple Racing and sponsored by the Malone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Commerce" title="Chamber of Commerce">Chamber of Commerce</a>.</li>
<li>Snowmobiles are used for <a href="/wiki/Ice_racing" title="Ice racing">ice racing</a>. The racing is held on an "Ice <a href="/wiki/Oval_track_racing" title="Oval track racing">Oval</a>" track. The <a href="/wiki/World_Championship_Snowmobile_Derby" title="World Championship Snowmobile Derby">World Championship Snowmobile Derby</a> is held each winter in <a href="/wiki/Eagle_River,_Wisconsin" title="Eagle River, Wisconsin">Eagle River</a>, Wisconsin.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Iron_Dog" title="Iron Dog">Iron Dog</a>" is the longest snowmachine race in the world. It is 2,031 miles (3,269 km) long and runs from <a href="/wiki/Big_Lake,_Alaska" title="Big Lake, Alaska">Big Lake</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nome,_Alaska" title="Nome, Alaska">Nome</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairbanks" title="Fairbanks">Fairbanks</a>. The name refers to dog <a href="/wiki/Mushing" title="Mushing">mushing</a>, long popular in Alaska.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_Snowmobile_Racing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vintage Snowmobile Racing (page does not exist)">Vintage snowmobiling</a> is the racing of vintage snowmobiles and has grown in popularity as a sporting event on the Canadian prairie and in America.</li>
<li>The World Championship Hill Climb competition is held in <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Wyoming" title="Jackson, Wyoming">Jackson</a>, Wyoming, at the Snow King ski resort each year in March.</li>
</ul>
