<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_seas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a> - the single connected salty <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">body of water</a> that covers the majority of Earth's surface.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Ocean</a> - the four to seven largest named bodies of water in the World Ocean, all of which have "Ocean" in the name. See <a href="/wiki/Borders_of_the_oceans" title="Borders of the oceans">borders of the oceans</a> for details.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">Sea</a> has several definitions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[a]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marginal_sea" title="Marginal sea">marginal sea</a> - a <b>marginal sea</b> is a division of an ocean, partially enclosed by <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsulas</a>, adjacent to or widely open to the open ocean at the surface, and/or bounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submarine_ridge" title="Submarine ridge">submarine ridges</a> on the sea floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mapping1994_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mapping1994-5">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>A division of an ocean, delineated by landforms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oceanservice.noaa_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-oceanservice.noaa-6">[5]</a></sup> currents (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a>), or specific latitude or longitude boundaries. This includes but is not limited to marginal seas, and this is the definition used for inclusion in this list.</li>
<li>The World Ocean. For example, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">Law of the Sea</a> states that all of the World Ocean is "sea",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geography.about_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-geography.about-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[b]</a></sup> and this is also common usage for "the sea".</li>
<li>Any large body of water with "Sea" in the name, including lakes.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">Strait</a> - a narrow area of water connecting two wider areas of water</li>
</ul>
<p>There are several terms used for bulges of ocean that result from indentations of land, which overlap in definition:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">Bay</a> - generic term; though most features with "Bay" in the name are small, some are very large</li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gulf" title="wikt:gulf">Gulf</a> - a very large bay, often a top-level division of an ocean or sea</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">Fjord</a> - a long bay with steep sides, typically formed by a <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bight_(geography)" title="Bight (geography)">Bight</a> - a bay that is typically shallower than a sound</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sound_(geography)" title="Sound (geography)">Sound</a> - a large, wide bay which is typically deeper than a bight, or a strait</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cove" title="Cove">Cove</a> - a very small, typically sheltered bay</li>
</ul>
<p>Many features could be considered to be more than one of these, and all of these terms are used in <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">place names</a> inconsistently; especially bays, gulfs, and bights which can be very large or very small. This list includes large areas of water no matter the term used in the name.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marginal_seas">Marginal seas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_seas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marginal seas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sources differ over which seas are considered marginal seas as well as which ocean a given sea is considered a marginal part of. There is no single ultimate authority on the matter.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_seas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There is no accepted technical definition of <i>sea</i> amongst oceanographers. A rather weak definition is that a sea is a sub-division of an ocean, which means that it must have <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_basin" title="Oceanic basin">oceanic basin</a> crust on its floor. This definition for example accepts the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian</a>, which was once part of an ancient ocean, as a sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <i>Introduction to Marine Biology</i> defines a sea as a "land-locked" body of water, adding that the term "sea" is only one of convenience, but the book is written by marine biologists, not oceanographers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>The Glossary of Mapping Sciences</i> similarly states that the boundaries between seas and other bodies of water are arbitrary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-11">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to this definition, the Caspian would be excluded as it is legally an "international lake".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-proposed-19"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-proposed_19-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-proposed_19-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-proposed_19-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-proposed_19-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-proposed_19-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-proposed_19-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Proposed names to the IHO 2002 draft. This draft was never approved by the IHO (or any other organization), and the 1953 IHO document (which does not contain these names which mostly originated from 1962 onward) remains currently in force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IHO_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-IHO-16">[14]</a></sup> Leading geographic authorities and atlases do not use these names, including the 2014 10th edition World Atlas from the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> and the 2014 12th edition of the <a href="/wiki/Times_Atlas_of_the_World" title="Times Atlas of the World">Times Atlas of the World</a>. But Soviet and Russian-issued state maps do include them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup></span></li>
</ol>
