<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bow echo</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <b>bow echo</b> is the characteristic radar return from a <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_system" title="Mesoscale convective system">mesoscale convective system</a> that is shaped like an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_(weapon)" title="Bow (weapon)">archer’s bow</a>. These systems can produce severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_winds" title="Straight-line winds">straight-line winds</a> and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, causing major damage. They can also become <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_echo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "bow echo" was first used by <a href="/wiki/Ted_Fujita" title="Ted Fujita">Dr. Theodore Fujita</a> in his May 1978 paper "Manual of Downburst Identification for Project NIMROD."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2004, research was done to better anticipate the formation of bow echoes, specifically the formation of bow echoes from weakly organized squall lines and <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a>. Researchers determined that bow echoes were most likely to occur in weakly organized cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A Midwest Bow Echo Workshop was held in 2007, at which meteorologists gathered to share their research to better understand bow echoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_echo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A bow echo is associated with <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> or lines of convective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a>. These echoes can range in size from 20 to 200 km, and have a life span of 3 to 6 hours. Bow echoes tend to develop when moderate to strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> exists in the lower 2 to 3 km of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. While similar to squall lines, bow echoes are smaller in scale and are moved by the wind inside them. They tend to push outward and after time die out. A bow echo also lowers the chance of a tornado being formed in the storm itself. The "bow shaped" echo is a result of focusing of the strong flow at the rear of the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetEd_Registration_Page_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MetEd_Registration_Page-4">[4]</a></sup> Especially strong bow echoes that cause devastating damage all along the width of the storm are often called <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rear_inflow_jet">Rear inflow jet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_echo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rear inflow jet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The formation of a bow echo requires a strong elevated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_inflow_jet" title="Rear inflow jet">rear inflow jet</a> at mid-levels. The strength of the cold pool and mesohigh at the surface as well as warmer temperatures aloft due to convection works to create a mesolow at mid-levels which strengthens the jet. Upon reaching the edge of the convection the jet descends and spreads along the surface, generating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_winds" title="Straight-line winds">straight-line winds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetEd_Registration_Page_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MetEd_Registration_Page-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_end_vortices">Book end vortices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_echo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Book end vortices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the rear inflow jet has bowed the storm system, book end or line end vortices develop on either side of the jet. These vortices are similar in strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_Glossary_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS_Glossary-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to the small size of the bow echo, the vortices help enhance the mid-level flow between them. This strengthens the rear inflow jet. The surface winds increase from the descending jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetEd_Registration_Page_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-MetEd_Registration_Page-4">[4]</a></sup> As the life of the storm increases, the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> acts to intensify the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclonic" title="Cyclonic">cyclonic</a> vortex and weaken the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticyclonic" title="Anticyclonic">anticyclonic</a> vortex. The system then develops an asymmetric comma-shaped echo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetEd_Registration_Page_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-MetEd_Registration_Page-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_Glossary_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMS_Glossary-5">[5]</a></sup> Some embedded <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gustnado" title="Gustnado">gustnadoes</a> develop within these vortices.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strongest_winds">Strongest winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_echo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Strongest winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bow_echo_chicago.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="838" data-file-width="786" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Bow_echo_chicago.jpg/220px-Bow_echo_chicago.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Bow_echo_chicago.jpg/330px-Bow_echo_chicago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Bow_echo_chicago.jpg/440px-Bow_echo_chicago.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bow_echo_chicago.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A bow echo west of the Chicago area</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Damaging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_winds" title="Straight-line winds">straight-line winds</a> often occur near the center of a bow echo. Damage from all severe thunderstorm winds account for half of all severe reports in the lower 48 states of the US and is more common than damage from tornadoes. In a type of long-lived and powerful bow echo known as a <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a>, wind speeds can reach up to or exceeding 100 mph (160 km/h) and can produce a damage path extending for hundreds of miles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Bow echoes are capable of producing straight-line winds that are just as strong as many tornadoes. A strong bow echo will produce more widespread and intense damage than the majority of tornadoes. Also, bow echoes in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">line echo wave pattern</a> create a favorable environment for tornadoes to form.</p>
<p>The semiarid climate and rugged terrain in the interior west of the United States do not favour the development of bow echoes. However, on 21 April 2011, a bow echo associated with a fast-moving mid-tropospheric perturbation formed across the Great Salt Lake (GSL) in Utah, producing damaging winds along its path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_bow_echo_storms">Notable bow echo storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_echo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable bow echo storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1674, the city of <a href="/wiki/Utrecht" title="Utrecht">Utrecht</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> was devastated by a storm now thought to have been a bow echo storm. Some of the damage to the city is still visible, and severe storm activity was recorded across other areas of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
