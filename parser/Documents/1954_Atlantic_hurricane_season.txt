<p>The <b>1954 Atlantic hurricane season</b> resulted in over $750 million in damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[nb 1]</a></sup> the most of any <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">season</a> at the time. The season officially began on June 15, and nine days later the first <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> developed. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(June_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (June 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> developed in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and moved inland along the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a>, producing significant precipitation and record flooding that killed 55 people. Activity was slow until late August; only Barbara, a minimal tropical storm, developed in July. In the span of two weeks, hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol" title="Hurricane Carol">Carol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edna" title="Hurricane Edna">Edna</a> followed similar paths before both striking <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricanes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 2]</a></sup> The latter became the costliest hurricane in Maine's history.</p>
<p>In late September, Tropical Storm Gilda killed 29 people after drenching northern Honduras. A tropical depression in early October was captured by a high-altitude photograph on a rocket, thus producing the first large-scale image of a tropical cyclone. The strongest and deadliest hurricane of the season was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hurricane Hazel</a>, which killed thousands in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> before striking near the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>/<a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> border in October. It caused heavy damage in the United States before becoming <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> and affecting <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. Intense rainfall affected <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> with severe flooding, leaving significant damage. The season officially ended on November 15, although another hurricane named <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Alice</a> developed on December 30 to the northeast of the Lesser Antilles; it lasted until January 6 of the following year. In total, there were 16 tropical storms, 7 hurricanes, and 3 major hurricanes.</p>

<p>The hurricane season officially began on June 15, when the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">United States Weather Bureau</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> began 24‑hour operations. The agency, under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Grady_Norton" title="Grady Norton">Grady Norton</a> had access to 43 other weather stations across the Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdn_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mdn-4">[2]</a></sup> Norton died during the season from a stroke while tracking Hurricane Hazel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-5">[3]</a></sup> Additionally, a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> planes were put on standby for the season, able to be flown out into storms and collect data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdn_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mdn-4">[2]</a></sup> During the season, there were eight <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storms</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-5">[3]</a></sup> as well as an unnamed hurricane and a tropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> The season officially ended on November 15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-5">[3]</a></sup> although an unnamed tropical storm formed a day later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Tropical Storm Alice</a> developed on December 31, the latest a tropical cyclone has developed in the calendar year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cumulatively, tropical cyclones left 193 deaths and $756 million in damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup> becoming the costliest hurricane season at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-5">[3]</a></sup> The season was one of six to have three major hurricanes strike the country, along with <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a> (which had four), <a href="/wiki/1893_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1893 Atlantic hurricane season">1893</a>, <a href="/wiki/1909_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1909 Atlantic hurricane season">1909</a>, <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup> Two of the major hurricanes – Carol and Edna – struck New England; this occurred despite an average of only 5–10 New England hurricanes per century. Carol struck <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> as a hurricane and left widespread heavy damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Only ten days later, Edna became the costliest hurricane in Maine's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ednamaine_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ednamaine-9">[7]</a></sup> The third major hurricane, Hazel, was the strongest hurricane of the season, attaining winds of 150 mph (240 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Unusually, no tropical cyclones affected Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season's activity was reflected with a cumulative <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 113,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> which is categorized as being "above normal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-averageACE_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-averageACE-11">[9]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. ACE is only calculated for full advisories on tropical systems at or exceeding 34 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (39 mph, 63 km/h) or tropical storm strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>A broad <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> developed over the eastern Gulf of Mexico on May 26. A frontal disturbance formed over Florida along the trough, spawning an area of broad cyclonic turning. After emerging into the western Atlantic Ocean, a closed circulation developed within the system, and it is estimated that a tropical depression formed on May 28 about 100 mi (160 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>. Ship observations in the region suggested that the depression intensified into a tropical storm by late on May 28. The system was small, and was not discovered to have been a tropical cyclone until a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">reanalysis</a> of data in 2015. The storm moved to the northeast ahead of an approaching trough, bypassing the Carolinas to the east; rainfall brushed the coast of North Carolina. On May 29, ship observations suggested peak winds of 50 mph (85 km/h). On the next day, the storm became associated with a warm front, indicating that it became extratropical to the southeast of New England. Continuing to the northeast, the former storm crossed over the southeastern coast of Newfoundland before being absorbed by another nontropical low to the northwest on May 31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-13">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>A trough formed over southern Florida on June 17. Early the next day, a circulation formed, signaling that a tropical depression also developed over the <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a>. The system moved slowly north-northeastward, emerging into the western Atlantic Ocean at 06:00 UTC on June 20. Shortly thereafter, the depression intensified into a tropical storm, although the structure was broad and asymmetric due to association with a nearby <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">upper-level low</a>, signaling that the system was possibly a subtropical cyclone. Ship reports in the region indicate that the storm continued to intensify. On June 22, the structure became much more symmetrical while passing just off the Outer Banks, bringing winds of 33 mph (54 km/h) to <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>. That day, the Hurricane Hunters estimated winds of 80 mph (130 km/h), and is possible that the storm briefly attained hurricane status. The peak winds were estimated at 70 mph (110 km/h), due to a pressure reading of 994 mbar (29.4 inHg). The storm accelerated northeastward due to an approaching cold front. On June 24, the storm became extratropical, and shortly thereafter made landfall along southwestern <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. The system dissipated the next day over the northern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Saint_Lawrence" title="Gulf of Saint Lawrence">Gulf of Saint Lawrence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-meta-13">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>On June 24, a tropical storm and later hurricane rapidly developed in the western Gulf of Mexico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> giving the citizens of southern Texas and northeastern Mexico little time to prepare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vic_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-vic-14">[12]</a></sup> Hurricane Alice rapidly intensified to at least a strong Category 2 storm (similar to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2010)" title="Hurricane Alex (2010)">2010's Hurricane Alex</a>, moved ashore in northeastern Mexico and quickly weakened,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> producing minor damage and killing one person due to a fallen power line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vic_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-vic-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 26, Alice dissipated, although the system dropped torrential rainfall across southern Texas and northeastern Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> The maximum rainfall officially peaked at 24.07 in (611 mm) near <a href="/wiki/Pandale,_Texas" title="Pandale, Texas">Pandale, Texas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-15">[13]</a></sup> concentrated in an area around the <a href="/wiki/Pecos_River" title="Pecos River">Pecos River</a>. High precipitation accumulations occurred in areas that had seen little rains in three years. This led to significant flooding along the Pecos River that produced "probably the greatest rate of runoff for a watershed of [that] size in the United States", as reported by the <a href="/wiki/International_Boundary_and_Water_Commission" title="International Boundary and Water Commission">International Boundary and Water Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flash_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-flash-16">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ozona,_Texas" title="Ozona, Texas">Ozona, Texas</a> sustained the most impact, estimated at $2 million in damage. Downstream, the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a> rose to the highest level since 1865,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-15">[13]</a></sup> which flooded seven towns on either side of the border;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sht_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sht-17">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Pass,_Texas" title="Eagle Pass, Texas">Eagle Pass, Texas</a> was flooded with 8 ft (2.4 m) of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pub_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pub-18">[16]</a></sup> Overall there were at least 55 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>A dissipating cold front stalled over the southeastern United States from Arkansas to North Carolina on July 9. On the next day, a low pressure area developed along the coast of Georgia. Soon after, the front dissipated, and by 12:00 UTC on July 10, the system developed into a tropical storm. Observations from a coastal lighthouse and a nearby ship indicated that the storm attained peak winds of 50 mph (85 km/h) while moving slowly east-northeastward, just offshore the Carolinas. The storm began weakening on July 13, and dissipated the next day when it was absorbed by a larger extratropical storm that was developing to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-13">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>On July 27, a tropical depression developed in the northern Gulf of Mexico, about 100 mi (160 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Isle,_Louisiana" title="Grand Isle, Louisiana">Grand Isle, Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> A map of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure areas</a> forming in July 1954 indicated that the system originated near the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Delta" title="Mississippi Delta">Mississippi Delta</a> and tracked southward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> After forming, the depression tracked northwestward, intensifying into Tropical Storm Barbara early on July 28. <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">Maximum sustained winds</a> reached 45 mph (75 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> although a tanker offshore named the <i>Henry M. Dawes</i> reported wind gusts of 60 mph (97 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Officials at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> posted a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">storm warning</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cameron,_Louisiana" title="Cameron, Louisiana">Cameron</a> to <a href="/wiki/Burrwood,_Louisiana" title="Burrwood, Louisiana">Burrwood, Louisiana</a>, and also advised small craft to remain at port along the entire northern Gulf Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> Without intensifying further, Barbara made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Vermilion_Bay_(Louisiana)" title="Vermilion Bay (Louisiana)">Vermilion Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> on July 29,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> dissipating the next day over Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> The storm dropped heavy rainfall along its path,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> including over 2 in (51 mm) in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> The rains caused flooding as well as some damage to the rice crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Monthly_Weather_Review" title="Monthly Weather Review">Monthly Weather Review</a> yearly summary described the precipitation as "far more beneficial than damaging". There was no wind damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol" title="Hurricane Carol">Hurricane Carol</a></div>
<p>Carol developed from a tropical wave near the Bahamas on August 25. It quickly intensified as it tracked generally to the north, becoming a hurricane on August 27. Moving parallel to the coastline of the southeastern United States, Carol passed just east of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a> with winds estimated at 110 mph (175 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> It intensified further as it accelerated, striking eastern <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3 hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-aoml-23">[21]</a></sup> Carol made its final landfall on <a href="/wiki/Old_Saybrook,_Connecticut" title="Old Saybrook, Connecticut">Old Saybrook, Connecticut</a> late on August 31. Within a few hours, the hurricane became extratropical over <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, which later dissipated over <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In North Carolina, hurricane-force winds left minor damage to houses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdam_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdam-24">[22]</a></sup> estimated around $228,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Rains from Carol alleviated drought conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-virhis_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-virhis-25">[23]</a></sup> On Long Island, the <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> flooded the <a href="/wiki/Montauk_Highway" title="Montauk Highway">Montauk Highway</a> with 4 ft (1.2 m) of water about a mile across. High winds left 275,000 homes without power, and damage totaled $3 million on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hurricane moved ashore in Connecticut shortly after high tide, producing a storm surge of 10–15 ft (3–4.5 m) from <a href="/wiki/New_London,_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">New London</a> eastward. The surge in <a href="/wiki/Narragansett_Bay" title="Narragansett Bay">Narragansett Bay</a> reached 14.4 ft (4.4 m), which surpassed that of the <a href="/wiki/1938_New_England_hurricane" title="1938 New England hurricane">1938 New England hurricane</a>, which flooded downtown <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence</a> with 12 feet (3.7 m) of water. In New London, rainfall peaked around 6 in (150 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-nws-27">[25]</a></sup> The hurricane produced winds of over 115 mph (185 km/h) in Connecticut and Rhode Island,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup> including a record-high gust of 135 mph (215 km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Block_Island" title="Block Island">Block Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-nws-27">[25]</a></sup> Widespread areas were left without power from eastern Connecticut to southern Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-nws-27">[25]</a></sup> Further north in Maine, Hurricane Carol downed hundreds of trees, as well as destroying widespread apple groves and corn fields. It became the costliest natural disaster in the state's history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carolmaine_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-carolmaine-28">[26]</a></sup> only to be surpassed by Hurricane Edna ten days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ednamaine_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ednamaine-9">[7]</a></sup> Across New England, the hurricane destroyed about 4,000 homes, 3,500 cars, and 3,000 boats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-nws-27">[25]</a></sup> Damage totaled $460 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> and there were 60 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup> In neighboring Canada, high rains caused flooding while strong winds downed trees and power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> spawned a tropical depression on August 31 to the northwest of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. It moved rapidly north-northwestward, intensifying into Tropical Storm Dolly later that day and into a hurricane early on September 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> Early in the storm's duration, the U.S. Weather Bureau noted the potential for Dolly to affect the same areas of New England that Hurricane Carol struck just days prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> However, the hurricane turned to the north away from land. The Hurricane Hunters estimated maximum sustained winds of 85 mph (140 km/h) on September 2 after the storm passed about halfway between the Outer Banks and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. Dolly turned to the northeast on September 2 and became extratropical later that day to the south of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. It was tracked until September 4, until it was last located to the north of the Azores. There was no reported damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edna" title="Hurricane Edna">Hurricane Edna</a></div>
<p>As Dolly became extratropical, the tropical depression that later became Hurricane Edna developed east of the Lesser Antilles on September 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> It moved northwestward, dropping heavy rainfall on Puerto Rico as it passed north of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EdnaMWR_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-EdnaMWR-31">[29]</a></sup> On September 7 Edna became a hurricane, and the next day reached peak winds of 120 mph (195 km/h). It turned to the north and northeast, bypassing the Outer Banks and skirting <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> On September 11, Edna struck Massachusetts as a strong Category 2 hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup> The hurricane later moved ashore near the border between Maine and New Brunswick around the time it became extratropical. The remnants persisted a few more days before dissipating south of Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early in its duration, Edna produced high seas and gale-force winds in the Bahamas, but there was no damage there. Hurricane-force winds occurred in the Outer Banks, although damage was minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> As it passed New England, Edna produced a 6 ft (1.8 m) storm surge during a high tide, which caused severe flooding in <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha's Vineyard">Martha's Vineyard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a>, and Cape Cod. Wind gusts peaked at 120 mph (190 km/h) on Martha's Vineyard, and the strong winds across the region left widespread power outages. The hurricane dropped additional heavy rainfall to areas affected by Carol, resulting in flooding in Massachusetts and Rhode Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> In Long Island and New England, the hurricane left over $42 million in damage and 20 deaths, only 11 days after Carol affected the same area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> About a third of the damage occurred in Maine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ednamaine_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ednamaine-9">[7]</a></sup> becoming the costliest hurricane on record in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carolmaine_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-carolmaine-28">[26]</a></sup> Hurricane-force winds extended into Canada, causing $6 million in damage (1954 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a>), mostly from crop damage. There was one death in Nova Scotia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>A surface trough persisted across the central Atlantic Ocean on September 2. Over the next few days, the system moved slowly northeastward, independent of any frontal systems. On September 6, nearby ships indicated that the a closed circulation had developed, marking the genesis of the system as a tropical depression. Accelerating to the northeast, the storm reached peak winds of 45 mph (75 km/h) on September 7. By the next day, the system became extratropical, and soon after was absorbed by a larger extratropical storm to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-13">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical storm formed in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on September 11. Given the name Florence, the storm moved generally west-southwestward with a small <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">radius of maximum winds</a>. Hurricane Hunters reported winds of 65 mph (105 km/h), although winds were initially (before reanalysis confirmed it was a tropical storm) estimated to have reached minimal hurricane force, or 75 mph (120 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> Before the storm moved ashore, officials in Veracruz evacuated residents from low-lying areas, and overall about 90,000 people left their homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up912_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-up912-34">[32]</a></sup> On September 12, Florence moved ashore between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuxpan,_Veracruz" title="Tuxpan, Veracruz">Tuxpan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nautla,_Veracruz" title="Nautla, Veracruz">Nautla, Veracruz</a> and quickly dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> The hurricane flooded coastal cities with up to 3 ft (0.91 m) of water, cutting off communications throughout the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up912_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-up912-34">[32]</a></sup> Damage was heaviest around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poza_Rica,_Veracruz" title="Poza Rica, Veracruz">Poza Rica</a>, primarily from the destruction of banana plantations. Monetary damage was estimated around $1.5 million, and there were five reported deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>A low pressure area was present north of the Yucatán Peninsula on September 14. Nearby ship observations indicated that a tropical depression developed the next day in the central Gulf of Mexico, although the system's large structure meant it could have been a subtropical cyclone. The system moved slowly westward, bringing winds of 30 mph (45 km/h) to the coast of Louisiana. It is estimated that the storm reached peak winds of 45 mph (75 km/h) on September 16, based on reports from ships and the Hurricane Hunters. On the next day, the system began weakening over water, dissipating on September 18 without moving ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-13">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>On September 24, Tropical Storm Gilda developed in the central <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> about halfway between Jamaica and Colombia. A small storm, it moved westward throughout its duration. On September 26, Gilda attained peak winds of 70 mph (110 km/h) while nearing northeastern <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. The storm paralleled the coastline a short distance offshore, maintaining its intensity before hitting and later weakening over <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> on September 27. It re-emerged in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> before making another landfall near Tampico as a tropical depression on September 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-21"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> While moving just offshore the coast of Honduras, Gilda dropped intense rainfall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-22"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> which flooding about 680 mi<sup>2</sup> (1760 km<sup>2</sup>) of land along the <a href="/wiki/Chamelec%C3%B3n_River" title="Chamelecón River">Chamelecón</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ul%C3%BAa_River" title="Ulúa River">Ulúa</a> rivers. The waters caused the widespread destruction of banana plantations and houses, leaving about 3,000 people homeless. The United Fruit Company reacted to this destruction by firing 10,000 of its about 100,000 workers within Honduras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> The Honduras 39th President <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_G%C3%A1lvez" title="Juan Manuel Gálvez">Juan Manuel Gálvez</a> appealed for aid from the United States. In response, U.S. Air Force planes flew food, medicine, rafts, and water purification tablets to the most affected regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mil_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-mil-36">[34]</a></sup> A total of 29 people were killed in Honduras. When Gilda made its landfall in Belize, it left little property damage and no deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-23"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical depression formed in the Central Atlantic on September 25. It drifted east-northeastward, eventually becoming a tropical storm on September 29. The storm curved to the west and west-southwest, becoming a hurricane on October 2. It turned to the northeast on October 3, and reached a peak of 100 mph (155 km/h) winds about halfway between Bermuda and the Azores. By that point it began accelerating, and late on October 6 the hurricane became extratropical over the Northeastern Atlantic. Early the next day it was last observed to the south of Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> The hurricane was not named due to its presence in the eastern Atlantic and not being a threat to land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>On October 2, a tropical depression moved westward from the coast of Cuba into the Gulf of Mexico. It moved westward without intensifying beyond winds of 35 mph (55 km/h), moving ashore about 40 mi (65 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville, Texas</a>. The depression weakened as it moved across southern Texas and dropped heavy rainfall. Brownsville recorded over 3 in (75 mm) in a 45-minute period, as well as about 6 in (150 mm) in six hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dep_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-dep-38">[36]</a></sup> The rainfall rates of about an inch per hour was the heaviest since June 1950. The depression also moved ashore with high waves and tides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the system reached the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bend_(Texas)" title="Big Bend (Texas)">Big Bend</a> region on October 5 it reintensified slightly. Around that time, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> conducted a rocket test from <a href="/wiki/White_Sands,_New_Mexico" title="White Sands, New Mexico">White Sands, New Mexico</a>; this was despite that such tests were rarely performed when the skies were cloudy. The rocket took an unintended photograph of the depression from an altitude of about 100 miles (160 km), which became the first such large-scale image of a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dep_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-dep-38">[36]</a></sup> The depression later moved into New Mexico and dropped additional rainfall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dep_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-dep-38">[36]</a></sup> interacting with an approaching <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tdrain_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-tdrain-40">[38]</a></sup> Flooding was reported around <a href="/wiki/Roswell,_New_Mexico" title="Roswell, New Mexico">Roswell</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dep_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-dep-38">[36]</a></sup> as well as along the <a href="/wiki/Pecos_River" title="Pecos River">Pecos River</a> system. Rainfall in the state peaked at 9.8 in (250 mm) in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canton,_New_Mexico&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canton, New Mexico (page does not exist)">Canton, New Mexico</a>. The system dissipated on October 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tdrain_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-tdrain-40">[38]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hurricane Hazel</a></div>
<p>Hurricane Hazel was the deadliest and costliest hurricane of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-24"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> The storm killed as many as 1,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> before striking the United States near the border between <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_4" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4 hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami13_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami13-41">[39]</a></sup> After causing 95 fatalities in the U.S.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-25"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Hazel struck Canada as an extratropical storm, raising the death toll by 81 people, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-env_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-env-42">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hazel formed on October 5 just east of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> and intensified into a Category 1 hurricane while crossing the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. After making a hard turn northward (similar to <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012's</a> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>), it rapidly intensified as it approached Haiti, reaching Category 3 intensity before landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-26"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> In Haiti, Hazel destroyed 40% of the coffee trees and 50% of the cacao crop, affecting the economy for several years to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[42]</a></sup> It fluctuated in intensity before making landfall as a Category 4 hurricane in the Carolinas, which destroyed most waterfront dwellings near its point of impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> It affected several more states, including <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, bringing gusts near 100 mph (160 km/h) and causing $308 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-27"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami16_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami16-46">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Hazel arrived in Ontario, rivers and streams passing through the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Toronto_Area" title="Greater Toronto Area">Greater Toronto Area</a> overflowed their banks, causing severe flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbec_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-lbec-47">[45]</a></sup> As a result, many residential areas located in floodplains, such as the <a href="/wiki/Raymore_Drive" title="Raymore Drive">Raymore Drive</a> area, were subsequently converted to parkland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrec_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrec-48">[46]</a></sup> In Canada alone, over <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">C$</a>135 million (2009: $1.1 billion) of damage was incurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evec_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-evec-49">[47]</a></sup> Hazel was particularly destructive in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, as a result of a combination of a lack of experience in dealing with tropical storms and the storm's unexpected retention of power. Hazel had traveled 680 mi (1,090 km) over land, but while approaching Canada, it had merged with an existing powerful cold front. The storm stalled over the Greater Toronto Area, and although it was now extratropical, it remained as powerful as a category 1 hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trans_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-trans-50">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impec_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-impec-51">[49]</a></sup> To help with the cleanup, 800 members of the military were summoned, and a Hurricane Relief Fund was established that distributed $5.1 million (2009: $41.7 million) in aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evec_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-evec-49">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>On November 16, a tropical depression developed about halfway between the Lesser Antilles and the Azores. It moved to the west, intensifying into a tropical storm and reaching peak winds of 40 mph (65 km/h) (however, due to limited data, it is possible that the storm remained a tropical depression). <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice (December 1954)</a></div>
<p>The final storm of the season, Alice, developed on December 30 from a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> of low pressure in the central Atlantic Ocean in an area of unusually favorable conditions. The storm moved southwestward and gradually strengthened to reach hurricane status. It persisted into the following calendar year, passing through the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> on January 2. Alice reached peak winds of 90 mph (150 km/h) before encountering cold air and turning to the southeast. It dissipated on January 6 over the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alice_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-alice-52">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alice produced heavy rainfall and moderately strong winds across several islands along its path. <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">Saba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a> were affected the most, with total damage amounting to $623,500 (1955 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alice_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-alice-52">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1955mwr_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-1955mwr-53">[51]</a></sup> Operationally, lack of definitive data prevented the U.S. Weather Bureau from declaring the system a hurricane until January 2. It received the name <i>Alice</i> in early 1955, though re-analysis of the data supported extending its track to the previous year, resulting in two tropical cyclones of the same name in one season. It was one of only two storms to span two calendar years, along with <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> in 2005-06.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alice_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-alice-52">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-54">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonus_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-bonus-55">[53]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
