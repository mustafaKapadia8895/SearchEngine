<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mid-atlantic_ridge_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="200" height="415" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/Mid-atlantic_ridge_map.png" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mid-atlantic_ridge_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge</div>
</div>

<p>The <b>Mid-Atlantic Ridge (MAR)</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridge</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">divergent</a> <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic plate</a> or constructive plate boundary located along the floor of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, and part of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountain_ranges#By_length" title="List of mountain ranges">longest mountain range in the world</a>. In the North Atlantic it separates the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plates</a>, and in the South Atlantic it separates the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plates</a>. The ridge extends from a junction with the <a href="/wiki/Gakkel_Ridge" title="Gakkel Ridge">Gakkel Ridge</a> (Mid-Arctic Ridge) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> southward to the <a href="/wiki/Bouvet_Triple_Junction" title="Bouvet Triple Junction">Bouvet Triple Junction</a> in the South Atlantic. Although the Mid-Atlantic Ridge is mostly an underwater feature, portions of it have enough elevation to extend above sea level. The section of the ridge that includes the island of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> is also known as the <b>Reykjanes Ridge</b>. The ridge has an average spreading rate of about 2.5 centimetres (0.98 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_understanding_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_understanding-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mid-Atlantic_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pangea_animation_03.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="200" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/Pangea_animation_03.gif" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pangea_animation_03.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pangaea's separation animated</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A ridge under the Atlantic Ocean was first inferred by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Fontaine_Maury" title="Matthew Fontaine Maury">Matthew Fontaine Maury</a> in 1850. The existence of the ridge was discovered during the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_expedition" title="Challenger expedition">expedition of HMS <i>Challenger</i></a> in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A team of scientists on board, led by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wyville_Thomson" title="Charles Wyville Thomson">Charles Wyville Thomson</a>, discovered a large rise in the middle of the Atlantic while investigating the future location for a <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">transatlantic telegraph cable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The existence of such a ridge was confirmed by sonar in 1925<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and was found to extend around the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> by the <a href="/wiki/German_Meteor_expedition" title="German Meteor expedition">German Meteor expedition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stein_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stein-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1950s, mapping of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s ocean floors by <a href="/wiki/Bruce_C._Heezen" title="Bruce C. Heezen">Bruce Heezen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ewing" title="Maurice Ewing">Maurice Ewing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marie_Tharp" title="Marie Tharp">Marie Tharp</a> and others revealed that the Mid-Atlantic Ridge had a strange <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetry</a> of valleys and ridges,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with its central valley being <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismologically</a> active and the <a href="/wiki/Epicenter" title="Epicenter">epicenter</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Ewing, Heezen and Tharp discovered that the ridge is part of a 40,000-km-long essentially continuous system of <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridges</a> on the floors of all the Earth’s oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The discovery of this worldwide ridge system led to the <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a> and general acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">Wegener</a>'s theory of <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a> and expansion in the modified form of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>. The ridge is central to the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothetical</a> <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> that began some 180 million years ago.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_features">Notable features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mid-Atlantic_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Mid-Atlantic Ridge includes a deep <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> that runs along the axis of the ridge along nearly its entire length. This rift marks the actual boundary between adjacent tectonic plates, where <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> reaches the seafloor, erupting as <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> and producing new <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">crustal</a> material for the plates.</p>
<p>Near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, the Mid-Atlantic Ridge is divided into the North Atlantic Ridge and the South Atlantic Ridge by the <a href="/wiki/Romanche_Trench" title="Romanche Trench">Romanche Trench</a>, a narrow submarine trench with a maximum depth of 7,758 m (25,453 ft), one of the deepest locations of the Atlantic Ocean. This trench, however, is not regarded as the boundary between the North and South American Plates, nor the Eurasian and African Plates.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mid-Atlantic_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg/220px-Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg/330px-Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg/440px-Expl2286_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<dl>
<dd><i>For a general explanation of mid-oceanic ridges, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-oceanic_ridge" title="Mid-oceanic ridge">mid-oceanic ridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a></i></dd>
</dl>
<p>The ridge sits atop a geologic feature known as the <b>Mid-Atlantic Rise</b>, which is a progressive bulge that runs the length of the Atlantic Ocean, with the ridge resting on the highest point of this linear bulge. This bulge is thought to be caused by upward convective forces in the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> pushing the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>. This divergent boundary first formed in the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> period, when a series of <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">three-armed</a> <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">grabens</a> coalesced on the supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> to form the ridge. Usually, only two arms of any given three-armed graben become part of a divergent plate boundary. The failed arms are called <i><a href="/wiki/Aulacogen" title="Aulacogen">aulacogens</a></i>, and the aulacogens of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge eventually became many of the large river valleys seen along the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">Niger River</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Fundy_Basin" title="Fundy Basin">Fundy Basin</a> on the Atlantic coast of North America between <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> is evidence of the ancestral Mid-Atlantic Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
