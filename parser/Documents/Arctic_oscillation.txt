<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Arctic oscillation</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="630" data-file-width="300" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg/110px-Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg/165px-Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg/220px-Arctic_Oscillation_II.svg.png 2x" width="110"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Positive and negative phases of the Arctic Oscillation</div>
</div>

<p>The <b>Arctic oscillation</b> (<b>AO</b>) or <b>Northern Annular Mode</b>/<b>Northern Hemisphere Annular Mode</b> (<b>NAM</b>) is an index (which varies over time with no particular periodicity) of the dominant pattern of non-seasonal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea-level_pressure" title="Sea-level pressure">sea-level pressure</a> variations north of 20N latitude, and it is characterized by pressure anomalies of one sign in the Arctic with the opposite anomalies centered about 37–45N.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">North Atlantic oscillation</a> (NAO) is a close relative of the AO and there exist arguments about whether one or the other is more fundamentally representative of the atmosphere's dynamics; Ambaum et al. argue that the NAO can be identified in a more physically meaningful way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AO is believed by climatologists to be causally related to, and thus partially predictive of, weather patterns in locations many thousands of miles away, including many of the major population centers of Europe and North America. NASA climatologist Dr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">James E. Hansen</a> explained the mechanism by which the AO affects weather at points so distant from the Arctic, as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The degree to which Arctic air penetrates into middle latitudes is related to the AO index, which is defined by surface atmospheric pressure patterns. When the AO index is positive, surface pressure is low in the polar region. This helps the middle latitude jet stream to blow strongly and consistently from west to east, thus keeping cold Arctic air locked in the polar region. When the AO index is negative, there tends to be high pressure in the polar region, weaker zonal winds, and greater movement of frigid polar air into middle latitudes." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_2009-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Periodicity">Periodicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Periodicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Oscillation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="1000" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Arctic_Oscillation.svg/220px-Arctic_Oscillation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Arctic_Oscillation.svg/330px-Arctic_Oscillation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Arctic_Oscillation.svg/440px-Arctic_Oscillation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Oscillation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Arctic Oscillation time series for the extended (DJFM) winter season 1899–2011.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Over most of the past century, the Arctic Oscillation alternated between its positive and negative phases. Starting in the 1970s the oscillation has trended to more of a positive phase when averaged using a 60-day running mean, though it has trended to a more neutral state in the last decade. The oscillation still fluctuates <a href="/wiki/Stochastic" title="Stochastic">stochastically</a> between negative and positive values on daily, monthly, seasonal and annual time scales, although, despite its stochastic nature, meteorologists have attained high levels of predictive accuracy in recent times, at least for the shorter term forecasts. (The correlation between actual observations and the 7-day mean <a href="/wiki/Global_Forecast_System" title="Global Forecast System">GFS</a> <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_forecasting" title="Ensemble forecasting">ensemble</a> AO forecasts is approximately 0.9, a figure at the high end for that statistic.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlCPC-DailyAOIndex_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlCPC-DailyAOIndex-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>This zonally symmetric seesaw between sea level pressures in polar and temperate latitudes was first identified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Lorenz" title="Edward Lorenz">Edward Lorenz</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lorenz_1951_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lorenz_1951-5">[5]</a></sup> and named in 1998 by David W.J. Thompson and <a href="/wiki/John_Michael_Wallace" title="John Michael Wallace">John Michael Wallace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1998_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_1998-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Snow_and_Ice_Data_Center" title="National Snow and Ice Data Center">National Snow and Ice Data Center</a> describes the effects of the AO in some detail: In the positive phase, higher pressure at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midlatitudes" title="Midlatitudes">midlatitudes</a> drives ocean storms farther north, and changes in the circulation pattern bring wetter weather to <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, as well as drier conditions to the western <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. In the positive phase, frigid winter air does not extend as far into the middle of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> as it would during the negative phase of the oscillation. This keeps much of the United States east of the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> warmer than normal, but leaves <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> colder than usual. Weather patterns in the negative phase are in general "opposite" to those of the positive phase.</p>
<p>Climatologists are now routinely invoking the Arctic Oscillation in their official public explanations for extremes of weather. The following statement from the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Climatic_Data_Center" title="National Climatic Data Center">National Climatic Data Center</a>: <i>State of the Climate December 2010</i> which uses the phrase "negative Arctic Oscillation" four times, is very representative of this increasing tendency:<br/></p>
<dl>
<dd>"Cold arctic air gripped western Europe in the first three weeks of December. Two major snowstorms, icy conditions, and frigid temperatures wreaked havoc across much of the region...The harsh winter weather was attributed to a negative Arctic Oscillation, which is a climate pattern that influences weather in the Northern Hemisphere. A very persistent, strong ridge of high pressure, or 'blocking system', near Greenland allowed cold Arctic air to slide south into Europe. Europe was not the only region in the Northern Hemisphere affected by the Arctic Oscillation. A large snow storm and frigid temperatures affected much of the Midwest United States on December 10–13...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlClimateDecember2010_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlClimateDecember2010-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>However, the correlation between sharply negative Arctic Oscillations and excessive winter cold and snow in regions vulnerable in that way to these negative AOs should not be overstated. It is by no means a simple, one-to-one equivalence. An extreme Arctic Oscillation does not necessarily mean extreme weather will occur. For example, since 1950, eight out of the 10 coldest Januarys in New York did not coincide with the 10 lowest January AO values. And the fourth warmest January there since 1950 coincided with one of those 10 most negative AOs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> So, although many climatologists believe that the Arctic Oscillation affects the probability of certain weather events occurring in certain places, the heightened chance of a phenomenon by no means assures it, nor does the lessened likelihood exclude it. Further, the precise value of the AO index only imperfectly reflects the severity of the weather associated with it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (January 2012)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A further, quite graphic illustration of the effects of the negative phase of the oscillation occurred in February 2010. In that month, the Arctic Oscillation reached its most negative monthly mean value, −4.266, in the entire post-1950 era (the period of accurate record-keeping).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> That month was characterized by three separate historic snowstorms that occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">mid-Atlantic region</a> of the United States. The <a href="/wiki/February_5%E2%80%936,_2010_North_American_blizzard" title="February 5–6, 2010 North American blizzard">first storm</a> dumped 25 inches (640 mm) on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore, Maryland</a>, on February 5–6, and then a <a href="/wiki/February_9%E2%80%9310,_2010_North_American_blizzard" title="February 9–10, 2010 North American blizzard">second storm</a> dumped 19.5 inches (500 mm) on February 9–10. In New York City, a <a href="/wiki/February_25%E2%80%9327,_2010_North_American_blizzard" title="February 25–27, 2010 North American blizzard">separate storm</a> deposited 20.9 inches (530 mm) on February 25–26. This kind of snowstorm activity is as anomalous and extreme as the negative AO value itself. Similarly, the greatest negative value for the AO since 1950 in January was −3.767 in 1977, which coincided with the coldest mean January temperature in New York City, Washington, D.C., Baltimore, and many other mid-Atlantic locations in that span of time. And though the January AO has been negative only 60.6% of the time between 1950 and 2010, 9 of the 10 coldest Januarys in New York City since 1950 have coincided with negative AOs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
