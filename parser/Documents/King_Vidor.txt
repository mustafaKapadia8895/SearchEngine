<p><b>King Wallis Vidor</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span></span>/</a></span></span>; February 8, 1894 – November 1, 1982) was an American film director, film producer, and screenwriter whose career spanned nearly seven decades. In 1979, he was awarded an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Academy_Award" title="Honorary Academy Award">Honorary Academy Award</a> for his "incomparable achievements as a cinematic creator and innovator."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was nominated five times for a <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director Oscar</a>, and won eight international film awards during his career. Vidor's best known films include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Parade" title="The Big Parade">The Big Parade</a></i> (1925), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crowd_(1928_film)" title="The Crowd (1928 film)">The Crowd</a></i> (1928), <i><a href="/wiki/Stella_Dallas_(1937_film)" title="Stella Dallas (1937 film)">Stella Dallas</a></i> (1937), and <i><a href="/wiki/Duel_in_the_Sun_(film)" title="Duel in the Sun (film)">Duel in the Sun</a></i> (1946). (He is not related to fellow director <a href="/wiki/Charles_Vidor" title="Charles Vidor">Charles Vidor</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Vidor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vidor (pronounced "vee-dor") was born in <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a>, where he survived the great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1900" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1900">Galveston Hurricane of 1900</a>. Based on that experience, he published a fictionalized account of that cyclone, titled "Southern Storm", for the May 1935 issue of <a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)"><i>Esquire</i> magazine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Erik_Larson_(author)" title="Erik Larson (author)">Erik Larson</a> excerpts a passage from that article in his 2005 book, <i><a href="/wiki/Isaac%27s_Storm" title="Isaac's Storm">Isaac's Storm</a></i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>I remember now that it seemed as if we were in a bowl looking up toward the level of the sea. As we stood there in the sandy street, my mother and I, I wanted to take my mother's hand and hurry her away. I felt as if the sea was going to break over the edge of the bowl and come pouring down upon us.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>His grandfather, Károly (Charles) Vidor, was a refugee of the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1848" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1848">Hungarian Revolution of 1848</a>, who settled in Galveston in the early 1850s.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1040" data-file-width="1317" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg/330px-King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg/495px-King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg/660px-King_Vidor_on_a_Hacker.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a>

King Vidor and his cameramen set out in his <a href="/wiki/Hacker-Craft" title="Hacker-Craft">Hacker-Craft</a> speedboat to film water sequences for his 1928 MGM picture, <i>The Patsy</i>.</div>

<p>A freelance <a href="/wiki/Newsreel" title="Newsreel">newsreel</a> cameraman and cinema <a href="/wiki/Projectionist" title="Projectionist">projectionist</a>, Vidor made his debut as a director in 1913 with <i>The Grand Military Parade</i>. In Hollywood from 1915, he worked as a screenwriter and as director of a series of six short juvenile-delinquency films for <a href="/wiki/Judge_Willis_Brown" title="Judge Willis Brown">Judge Willis Brown</a> before directing his first feature, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Turn_in_the_Road" title="The Turn in the Road">The Turn in the Road</a></i>, in 1919. A successful mounting of <i><a href="/wiki/Peg_o%27_My_Heart_(1922_film)" title="Peg o' My Heart (1922 film)">Peg o' My Heart</a></i> in 1922 won him a long-term contract with Goldwyn Studios (later to be absorbed into <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">MGM</a>). Three years later he made <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Parade" title="The Big Parade">The Big Parade</a></i>, among the most acclaimed war films of the silent era, and a tremendous commercial success. This success established him as one of MGM's top studio directors for the next decade. In 1928, Vidor received his first Oscar nomination, for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crowd_(1928_film)" title="The Crowd (1928 film)">The Crowd</a></i>, widely regarded as his masterpiece and one of the greatest American silent films. In the same year, he made the classic <i><a href="/wiki/Show_People" title="Show People">Show People</a></i>, a comedy about the film industry starring Marion Davies (in which Vidor had a cameo as himself), and his much-loved screwball comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Patsy_(1928_film)" title="The Patsy (1928 film)">The Patsy</a></i>, which also starred Davies and was his last silent film.</p>
<p>Vidor's first sound film was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallelujah!_(1929_film)" title="Hallelujah! (1929 film)">Hallelujah!</a></i>, a groundbreaking film featuring an African-American cast. He had no difficulty adjusting to sound and he continued making feature films until the late 1950s. Some of his better known sound films include <i><a href="/wiki/Stella_Dallas_(1937_film)" title="Stella Dallas (1937 film)">Stella Dallas</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Daily_Bread_(1934_film)" title="Our Daily Bread (1934 film)">Our Daily Bread</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Citadel_(film)" title="The Citadel (film)">The Citadel</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Duel_in_the_Sun_(film)" title="Duel in the Sun (film)">Duel in the Sun</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fountainhead_(film)" title="The Fountainhead (film)">The Fountainhead</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/War_and_Peace_(1956_film)" title="War and Peace (1956 film)">War and Peace</a></i>. He directed the Kansas sequences in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i> (including "<a href="/wiki/Over_the_Rainbow" title="Over the Rainbow">Over the Rainbow</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">twister</a>) when director <a href="/wiki/Victor_Fleming" title="Victor Fleming">Victor Fleming</a> had to replace <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">George Cukor</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">Gone with the Wind</a></i>, but did not receive screen credit.</p>
<p>In 1962 he was head of the jury at the <a href="/wiki/12th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="12th Berlin International Film Festival">12th Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale_1962_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale_1962-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1969 he was a member of the jury at the <a href="/wiki/6th_Moscow_International_Film_Festival" title="6th Moscow International Film Festival">6th Moscow International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscow1969_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscow1969-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vidor was entered in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a></i> for the longest career as a film director: beginning in 1913 with <i>Hurricane in Galveston</i> and ending in 1980 with <i>The Metaphor</i>, a 36-minute documentary featuring the painter Andrew Wyeth. He was nominated five times for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_award" title="Academy award">Oscar</a> but never won in direct competition; he received an honorary award in 1979.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Desmond_Taylor">William Desmond Taylor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Vidor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: William Desmond Taylor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967, Vidor researched the unsolved 1922 murder of fellow director <a href="/wiki/William_Desmond_Taylor" title="William Desmond Taylor">William Desmond Taylor</a> for a possible screenplay. Vidor never published or wrote of this research during his lifetime, but biographer <a href="/wiki/Sidney_D._Kirkpatrick" title="Sidney D. Kirkpatrick">Sidney D. Kirkpatrick</a> posthumously examined Vidor's notes. He alleged in his 1986 book <i>A Cast of Killers</i> that Vidor had solved the sensational crime but kept his conclusions private to protect individuals still living at the time. The widely cited newsletter <i><a href="/wiki/Taylorology" title="Taylorology">Taylorology</a></i> later noted over 100 factual errors in <i>Cast of Killers</i> and strongly disputes Kirkpatrick's conclusions, but credits the book with renewing popular interest in the crime.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Vidor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1944 Vidor, a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>, joined the <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-communist</a> <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Alliance_for_the_Preservation_of_American_Ideals" title="Motion Picture Alliance for the Preservation of American Ideals">Motion Picture Alliance for the Preservation of American Ideals</a>.</p>
<p>Vidor published his autobiography, <i>A Tree is a Tree</i>, in 1953. This book's title is inspired by an incident early in Vidor's Hollywood career. Vidor wanted to film a movie in the locations where its story was set, a decision which would have greatly added to the film's production budget. A budget-minded producer told him, "A rock is a rock. A tree is a tree. Shoot it in <a href="/wiki/Griffith_Park" title="Griffith Park">Griffith Park</a>" (a nearby public space which was frequently used for film exterior shots).</p>
<p>King Vidor was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Scientist" title="Christian Scientist">Christian Scientist</a> and wrote occasionally for church publications, such as "The Bible" and the hit, "Truth Be Told."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Vidor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vidor died at age 88 of a heart ailment at his ranch in Paso Robles, California on November 1, 1982. His remains were cremated and scattered on the ranch property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
