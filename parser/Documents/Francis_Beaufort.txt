<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral" title="Rear Admiral">Rear Admiral</a> <b>Sir Francis Beaufort</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Bath" title="Order of the Bath">KCB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Geographical_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Geographical Society">FRGS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society#Fellowship" title="Royal Astronomical Society">FRAS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Academy#Membership" title="Royal Irish Academy">MRIA</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 27 May 1774 – 17 December 1857) was an <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrographer" title="Hydrographer">hydrographer</a> and officer in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. Beaufort was the creator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Scale" title="Beaufort Scale">Beaufort Scale</a> for indicating wind force.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Francis Beaufort (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Proinsias Lios an Phúca</i></span>) was descended from French Protestant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a>, who fled the <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">French Wars of Religion</a> in the sixteenth century. His parents moved to Ireland from London. His father, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Augustus_Beaufort" title="Daniel Augustus Beaufort">Daniel Augustus Beaufort</a>, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> clergyman from <a href="/wiki/Navan" title="Navan">Navan</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Meath" title="County Meath">County Meath</a>, Ireland, and a member of the learned <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Academy" title="Royal Irish Academy">Royal Irish Academy</a>. His mother Mary was the daughter and co-heiress of William Waller, of <a href="/wiki/Allenstown_House" title="Allenstown House">Allenstown House</a>. Francis was born at Navan on 27 May 1774.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He had an older brother, William Louis Beaufort and two sisters, <a href="/wiki/Frances_Anne_Edgeworth" title="Frances Anne Edgeworth">Frances</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Henrietta_Beaufort" title="Harriet Henrietta Beaufort">Harriet</a>. His father created and published a new map of Ireland in 1792.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loc-1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loc-1-2">[2]</a></sup> Francis grew up in Wales and Ireland until age fourteen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friendly_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-friendly-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He left school and went to sea, but never stopped his education. By later in life, he had become sufficiently self-educated to associate with some of the greatest scientists and applied mathematicians of his time, including <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">John Herschel</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Biddell_Airy" title="George Biddell Airy">George Biddell Airy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">Charles Babbage</a>.</p>
<p>Francis Beaufort had a lifelong keen awareness of the value of accurate charts for those risking the seas, as he was shipwrecked at the age of fifteen due to a faulty chart. His most significant accomplishments were in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_charts" title="Nautical charts">nautical charting</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_naval_career">Early naval career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early naval career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning on a merchant ship of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_East_India_Company" title="British East India Company">British East India Company</a>, Beaufort rose (during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Midshipman" title="Midshipman">Midshipman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a> (on 10 May 1796) and <a href="/wiki/Commander" title="Commander">Commander</a> (on 13 November 1800). He served on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fifth_rate" title="Fifth rate">fifth rate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sailing_frigate" title="Sailing frigate">frigate</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Aquilon" title="HMS Aquilon">HMS <i>Aquilon</i></a> during the battle of the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_First_of_June" title="Glorious First of June">Glorious First of June</a>, when <i>Aquilon</i> rescued the dismasted <a href="/wiki/HMS_Defence_(1763)" title="HMS Defence (1763)">HMS <i>Defence</i></a> and exchanged broadsides with the French ship-of-the line, <i>Impetueux</i>.</p>
<p>When serving on <a href="/wiki/HMS_Phaeton_(1782)" title="HMS Phaeton (1782)">HMS <i>Phaeton</i></a>, Beaufort was badly wounded leading a cutting-out operation off <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a> in 1800; the action resulted in the capture of the 14-gun <a href="/wiki/Polacca" title="Polacca">polacca</a> <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Calpe_(1800)" title="HMS Calpe (1800)">Calpe</a></i>. While recovering, during which he received a "paltry" pension of £45 p.a., he helped his brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lovell_Edgeworth" title="Richard Lovell Edgeworth">Richard Lovell Edgeworth</a> to construct a <a href="/wiki/Semaphore_line" title="Semaphore line">semaphore line</a> from <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>. He spent two years at this activity, for which he would accept no remuneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Command">Command</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Command">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beaufort returned to active service and was appointed a Captain (on 30 May 1810) in the Royal Navy. Whereas other wartime officers sought leisurely pursuits, Beaufort spent his leisure time taking <a href="/wiki/Depth_sounding" title="Depth sounding">soundings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a>, making astronomical observations to determine <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, and measuring shorelines. His results were compiled in new charts.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> gave Beaufort his first ship command, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Woolwich_(1785)" title="HMS Woolwich (1785)">HMS <i>Woolwich</i></a>. He sailed her to the East Indies and escorted a convoy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Indiamen" title="East Indiamen">East Indiamen</a> back to Britain. Then the Admiralty tasked him with of conducting a <a href="/wiki/Hydrographic_survey" title="Hydrographic survey">hydrographic survey</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_de_la_Plata" title="Rio de la Plata">Rio de la Plata</a> estuary in South America. Experts were very impressed by the survey Beaufort brought back. Notably, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dalrymple" title="Alexander Dalrymple">Alexander Dalrymple</a> remarked in a note to the Admiralty in March 1808, that "we have few officers (indeed I do not know one) in our Service who have half his professional knowledge and ability, and in zeal and perseverance he cannot be excelled".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatolia">Anatolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anatolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <i>Woolwich</i>, Beaufort received his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post_Captain" title="Post Captain">post Captain</a> commission, commanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDMS_Friderichssteen_(1800)" title="HDMS Friderichssteen (1800)"><i>Frederickstein</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gf10-1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gf10-1-7">[7]</a></sup> Throughout 1811–1812, Beaufort charted and explored southern <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, locating many <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical</a> ruins. An attack on the crew of his boat (at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayas_(city)" title="Ayas (city)">Ayas</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Adana" title="Adana">Adana</a>), by <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a> interrupted his work and he received a serious bullet wound in the hip. He returned to England and drew up his <a href="/wiki/Chart" title="Chart">charts</a>. In 1817 he published his book <i><a href="/wiki/Caramania" title="Caramania">Karamania</a>; or a brief description of the South Coast of Asia Minor, and of the Remains of Antiquity</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrographer_of_the_Navy">Hydrographer of the Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hydrographer of the Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1829 Beaufort was elected as a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Examiner_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Examiner-8">[8]</a></sup> and at age 55 (retirement age for most administrative contemporaries), Beaufort was appointed as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Admiralty" title="British Admiralty">British Admiralty</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrographer_of_the_Navy" title="Hydrographer of the Navy">Hydrographer of the Navy</a>. He served in that post for 25 years. Beaufort converted what had been a minor chart repository into the finest surveying and charting institution in the world. Some of the excellent charts the Office produced are still in use today.</p>
<p>During his tenure, he took over the administration of the great astronomical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatories" title="Observatories">observatories</a> at <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a>, England, and the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, Africa. Beaufort directed some of the major maritime explorations and experiments of that period. For eight years, he directed the Arctic Council during its search for the explorer, <a href="/wiki/John_Franklin" title="John Franklin">Sir John Franklin</a>, who was lost during his last polar voyage to search for the legendary <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>. As a council member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">Royal Observatory</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Geographic_Society" title="Royal Geographic Society">Royal Geographic Society</a> (which he helped found), Beaufort used his position and prestige as a top administrator to act as a "middleman" for many scientists of his time. Beaufort represented the geographers, astronomers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanographer" title="Oceanographer">oceanographers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodesist" title="Geodesist">geodesists</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a> to that government agency, the Hydrographic Office, which could support their research.</p>
<p>Beaufort trained <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a>, who was put in temporary command of the survey ship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS <i>Beagle</i></a> after her previous captain committed suicide. When FitzRoy was reappointed as Commander for what became the famous second <a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle" title="The Voyage of the Beagle">voyage of the <i>Beagle</i></a>, he requested of Beaufort "that a well-educated and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gentleman_scientist" title="Gentleman scientist">scientific gentleman</a> be sought" as a companion on the voyage. Beaufort's enquiries led to an invitation to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, who later drew on his discoveries in formulating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_evolution" title="Theory of evolution">theory of evolution</a> he presented in his book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Origin_of_Species" title="The Origin of Species">The Origin of Species</a></i>.</p>
<p>Overcoming many objections, Beaufort obtained government support for the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> voyage of 1839–1843 by <a href="/wiki/James_Clark_Ross" title="James Clark Ross">James Clark Ross</a> for extensive measurements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_magnetism" title="Terrestrial magnetism">terrestrial magnetism</a>, coordinated with similar measurements in Europe and Asia. (This is comparable to the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a> of our time.)</p>
<p>Beaufort promoted the development of reliable tide tables around British shores, inspiring similar research for Europe and North America. Aiding his friend <a href="/wiki/William_Whewell" title="William Whewell">William Whewell</a>, Beaufort gained the support of the Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington</a>, in expanding record-keeping at 200 British <a href="/wiki/Coast_guard" title="Coast guard">Coast guard</a> stations. Beaufort gave enthusiastic support to his friend, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Airy" title="George Airy">Sir George Airy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">Astronomer Royal</a> and noted mathematician, in achieving a historic period of measurements by the Greenwich and Good Hope observatories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beaufort retired from the Royal Navy with the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral" title="Rear Admiral">Rear Admiral</a> on 1 October 1846, at the age of 72. He became "Sir Francis Beaufort" on being appointed <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Bath" title="Order of the Bath">KCB</a> (Knight Commander of the Bath) on 29 April 1848, a relatively belated honorific considering the eminence of his position from 1829 onward.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He married Alicia Magdalena Wilson. Their son, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Lestock_Beaufort" title="Francis Lestock Beaufort">Francis Lestock Beaufort</a> (1815–1879) later went out to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and served in the <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> civil service, from 1837 to 1876. He was for many years judge of the twenty-four Purgunnahs,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Calcutta. He was the author of the well-known <i>Digest of the Criminal Law Procedure in Bengal</i> (1850), and died in 1879. Beaufort's youngest daughter was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emily_Anne_Smythe" title="Emily Anne Smythe">Emily Anne Smythe</a> was a hero of Bulgaria, a writer, illustrator and advocate of change in the training of nurses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emilyodnb_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-emilyodnb-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alice Beaufort died on August 27, 1834<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1834-08-27</span>)</span>. Francis Beaufort married again in 1838, to Honora Edgeworth, the daughter of his brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lovell_Edgeworth" title="Richard Lovell Edgeworth">Richard Lovell Edgeworth</a> and his second wife. (Francis' sister Frances Beaufort had married Edgeworth as his fourth wife years in the 1810s.)</p>
<p>Beaufort's extant correspondence of 200+ letters and journals contained portions written in personal cipher. Beaufort altered the <a href="/wiki/Vigen%C3%A8re_cipher" title="Vigenère cipher">Vigenère cipher</a>, by reversing the cipher alphabet, and the resulting variant is called the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_cipher" title="Beaufort cipher">Beaufort cipher</a>. The deciphered writings have revealed family and personal problems, including some of a sexual nature. It appears that between 1835 and his marriage to Honora Edgeworth in November 1838, he had incestuous relations with his sister Harriet. His diary entries, in cypher, show that he was tortured by guilt over this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friendly_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-friendly-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2013)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>He died on 17 December 1857 at age 83 in <a href="/wiki/Hove" title="Hove">Hove</a>, Sussex, England. He is buried in the church gardens of St John at <a href="/wiki/Hackney_Central" title="Hackney Central">Hackney</a>, London, where his tomb may still be seen. His home in London, No. 51 Manchester Street, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">Westminster</a>, is marked by an historic <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> noting his residency and achievements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_force_scale">Wind force scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Wind force scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During these early years of command, Beaufort developed the first versions of his <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Wind Force Scale</a> and Weather Notation coding, which he was to use in his journals for the remainder of his life. From the circle representing a weather station, a staff (rather like the stem of a note in musical notation) extends, with one or more half or whole barbs. For example, a stave with 3½ barbs represents Beaufort seven on the scale, decoded as 32–38 mph, or a "moderate Gale".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_legacy">Geographical legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Beaufort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geographical legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beaufort, like other patrons of exploration, has had his name given to many geographical places. Among these:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Sea" title="Beaufort Sea">Beaufort Sea</a> (arm of Arctic Ocean)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Island" title="Beaufort Island">Beaufort Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a></li>
</ul>
