<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anticyclogenesis</h1>

<p><b>Anticyclogenesis</b> is the development or strengthening of <a href="/wiki/Anticyclonic_rotation" title="Anticyclonic rotation">anticyclonic circulation</a> in the atmosphere. It is the opposite of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anticyclolysis (page does not exist)">anticyclolysis</a>, and has a cyclonic equivalent—<a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnticyclogenesisDef_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnticyclogenesisDef-1">[1]</a></sup> Anticyclones are alternatively referred to as <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high pressure systems</a>. High pressure areas form due to downward motion through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> layer where <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> occurs. Preferred areas within a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> flow pattern in higher levels of the troposphere are beneath the western side of troughs. On weather maps, these areas show converging winds (isotachs), also known as <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a>, or converging height lines near or above the level of non-divergence, which is near the 500 hPa pressure surface about midway up through the troposphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Level_of_nondivergence_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Level_of_nondivergence-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Middle-Latitude_Cyclones_-_II_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Middle-Latitude_Cyclones_-_II-3">[3]</a></sup> On weather maps, high pressure centers are associated with the letter H.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandnet.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet.com-4">[4]</a></sup> On constant pressure upper level charts, it is located within the highest height line contour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>High-pressure systems form due to downward motion through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> layer where <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> occurs. Preferred areas within a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> flow pattern in higher levels of the troposphere are beneath the western side of troughs. On weather maps, these areas show converging winds (isotachs), also known as <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a>, or converging height lines near or above the level of non-divergence, which is near the 500 hPa pressure surface about midway up through the troposphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Level_of_nondivergence_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Level_of_nondivergence-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Middle-Latitude_Cyclones_-_II_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Middle-Latitude_Cyclones_-_II-3">[3]</a></sup> High-pressure systems are alternatively referred to as anticyclones. On weather maps, high-pressure centers are associated with the letter H<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandnet.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet.com-4">[4]</a></sup> placed within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">isobar</a> with the highest pressure value. On constant pressure upper level charts, it is located within the highest height line contour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
