<p><i><b>Esquire</b></i> is an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men%27s_magazine" title="Men's magazine">men's magazine</a>, published by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst Corporation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Founded in 1933, it flourished during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> under the guidance of founders <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Gingrich" title="Arnold Gingrich">Arnold Gingrich</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_A._Smart" title="David A. Smart">David A. Smart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_L._Jackson" title="Henry L. Jackson">Henry L. Jackson</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esquire_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Esquire</i> was first issued in October 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The magazine was first headquartered in Chicago and then, in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was founded and edited by <a href="/wiki/David_A._Smart" title="David A. Smart">David A. Smart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_L._Jackson" title="Henry L. Jackson">Henry L. Jackson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Gingrich" title="Arnold Gingrich">Arnold Gingrich</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Jackson died in the crash of <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_624" title="United Airlines Flight 624">United Airlines Flight 624</a> in 1948, while Gingrich led the magazine until his own death in 1976. Smart died in 1952, although he left <i>Esquire</i> in 1936 to found a different magazine, <i><a href="/wiki/Coronet_(magazine)" title="Coronet (magazine)">Coronet</a></i>. The founders all had different focuses; Gingrich specialized in publishing, Smart led the business side of the magazine while Jackson led and edited the fashion section, which made up most of the magazine in its first fifteen years of publishing. Additionally, Jackson's <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> political viewpoints contrasted with the <a href="/wiki/Liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Liberalism in the United States">liberal</a> <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> views of Smart, which allowed for the magazine to publish debates between the two. This grew particularly heated in 1943 when the Democratic <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postmaster_General" title="United States Postmaster General">United States Postmaster General</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Comerford_Walker" title="Frank Comerford Walker">Frank Comerford Walker</a> brought charges against the magazine on behalf of the administration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The administration alleged that <i>Esquire</i> had used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Postal_Service" title="US Postal Service">US Postal Service</a> to promote "lewd images". Republicans opposed the lawsuit and in 1946 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> found in <i>Esquire v. Walker</i> that Esquire's right to use the Postal Service was protected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Amendment_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment of the United States Constitution">First Amendment of the United States Constitution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Esquire</i> started in 1933 as a quarterly press run of a hundred thousand copies. It cost fifty cents per copy (equivalent to $9.25 today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It later transformed itself into a more refined periodical with an emphasis on men's fashion and contributions by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._Scott_Fitzgerald" title="F. Scott Fitzgerald">F. Scott Fitzgerald</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Moravia" title="Alberto Moravia">Alberto Moravia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gide" title="André Gide">André Gide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Julian_Huxley" title="Julian Huxley">Julian Huxley</a>. In the 1940s, the popularity of the <a href="/wiki/George_Petty" title="George Petty">Petty Girls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Vargas" title="Alberto Vargas">Vargas Girls</a> provided a circulation boost. In the 1960s, <i>Esquire</i> helped pioneer the trend of <a href="/wiki/New_Journalism" title="New Journalism">New Journalism</a> by publishing such writers as <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_O%27Brien_(author)" title="Tim O'Brien (author)">Tim O'Brien</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Sack" title="John Sack">John Sack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gay_Talese" title="Gay Talese">Gay Talese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Southern" title="Terry Southern">Terry Southern</a>. In August 1969, <i>Esquire</i> published <a href="/wiki/Normand_Poirier" title="Normand Poirier">Normand Poirier</a>'s piece, "An American Atrocity", one of the first reports of American atrocities committed against Vietnamese civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-9">[9]</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/Harold_Hayes" title="Harold Hayes">Harold Hayes</a>, who ran it from 1961 to 1973, it became as distinctive as its oversized pages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The magazine shrank to the conventional <a href="/wiki/Letter_(paper_size)" title="Letter (paper size)">8½×11</a> inches in 1971. The magazine was sold by the original owners to <a href="/wiki/Clay_Felker" title="Clay Felker">Clay Felker</a> in 1977, who reinvented the magazine as a fortnightly in 1978, under the title of <i>Esquire Fortnightly</i>. However, the fortnightly experiment proved to be a failure, and by the end of that year, the magazine lost <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>5 million. Felker sold <i>Esquire</i> in 1979 to the 13-30 Corporation, a Tennessee publisher, whose owners refocused the magazine into a monthly. During this time, <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Woman" title="New York Woman">New York Woman</a></i> magazine was launched as something of a spinoff version of <i>Esquire</i> aimed at female audience. 13-30 split up in 1986, and <i>Esquire</i> was sold to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst</a> at the end of the year, with <i>New York Woman</i> going its separate way to American Express Publishing.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_M._Granger" title="David M. Granger">David M. Granger</a> was named editor-in-chief of the magazine in June 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Since his arrival, the magazine has received numerous awards, including multiple National Magazine Awards—the industry's highest honor. Prior to becoming <a href="/wiki/Editor-in-chief" title="Editor-in-chief">editor-in-chief</a> at <i>Esquire</i>, <a href="/wiki/David_M._Granger" title="David M. Granger">Granger</a> was the executive editor at <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GQ_(magazine)" title="GQ (magazine)">GQ</a></i> for nearly six years. Its award-winning staff writers include <a href="/wiki/Tom_Chiarella" title="Tom Chiarella">Tom Chiarella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Raab" title="Scott Raab">Scott Raab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Sager" title="Mike Sager">Mike Sager</a>, Chris Jones, John H. Richardson, <a href="/wiki/Cal_Fussman" title="Cal Fussman">Cal Fussman</a>, Lisa Taddeo, and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Junod" title="Tom Junod">Tom Junod</a>. Famous photographers have also worked for the magazine, among which fashion photographer <a href="/wiki/Gleb_Derujinsky" title="Gleb Derujinsky">Gleb Derujinsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Avedon" title="Richard Avedon">Richard Avedon</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blog">Blog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esquire_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Blog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In January 2009 <i>Esquire</i> launched a new blog—the <i>Daily Endorsement Blog</i>. Each morning the editors of the magazine recommend one thing for readers' immediate enjoyment: "not a political candidate or position or party, but a breakthrough idea or product or Web site."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The concept for this blog probably emerged from the November 2008 "Endorsement Issue", in which, after 75 years, <i>Esquire</i> publicly endorsed a presidential candidate for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <i>Daily Endorsement Blog</i> was officially discontinued on April 2011.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esquire_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1969 to 1976, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Lish" title="Gordon Lish">Gordon Lish</a> served as fiction editor for <i>Esquire</i> and became known as "Captain Fiction" because of the authors whose careers he assisted. Lish helped establish the career of writer <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Carver" title="Raymond Carver">Raymond Carver</a> by publishing his short stories in <i>Esquire</i>, often over the objections of Hayes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Lish is noted for encouraging Carver's <a href="/wiki/Minimalism" title="Minimalism">minimalism</a> and publishing the short stories of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ford" title="Richard Ford">Richard Ford</a>. Using the influential publication as a vehicle to introduce new fiction by emerging authors, he promoted the work of such writers as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T._Coraghessan_Boyle" title="T. Coraghessan Boyle">T. Coraghessan Boyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hannah" title="Barry Hannah">Barry Hannah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Ozick" title="Cynthia Ozick">Cynthia Ozick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_Price" title="Reynolds Price">Reynolds Price</a>.</p>
<p>In February 1977, <i>Esquire</i> published "For Rupert – with no promises" as an unsigned work of fiction: this was the first time it had published a work without identifying the author. Readers speculated that it was the work of <a href="/wiki/J._D._Salinger" title="J. D. Salinger">J. D. Salinger</a>, the reclusive author best known for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></i>. Told in first-person, the story features events and Glass family names from the story "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/For_Esm%C3%A9_%E2%80%93_with_Love_and_Squalor" title="For Esmé – with Love and Squalor">For Esmé – with Love and Squalor</a>". <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Lish" title="Gordon Lish">Gordon Lish</a> is quoted as saying, "I tried to borrow Salinger's voice and the psychological circumstances of his life, as I imagine them to be now. And I tried to use those things to elaborate on certain circumstances and events in his fiction to deepen them and add complexity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other authors appearing in <i>Esquire</i> at that time included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_F._Buckley" title="William F. Buckley">William F. Buckley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murray_Kempton" title="Murray Kempton">Murray Kempton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Muggeridge" title="Malcolm Muggeridge">Malcolm Muggeridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rosenbaum" title="Ron Rosenbaum">Ron Rosenbaum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Vachss" title="Andrew Vachss">Andrew Vachss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Garry_Wills" title="Garry Wills">Garry Wills</a>.</p>
<p>The magazine's policy of nurturing young writing talent has continued with <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Gilbert" title="Elizabeth Gilbert">Elizabeth Gilbert</a>, who debuted in <i>Esquire</i> in 1993, and more recently, with the work of such writers as <a href="/wiki/Chris_Adrian" title="Chris Adrian">Chris Adrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Englander" title="Nathan Englander">Nathan Englander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Percy" title="Benjamin Percy">Benjamin Percy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Somerville" title="Patrick Somerville">Patrick Somerville</a>. Other writers who have recently appeared in the magazine and on Esquire.com include <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Lombreglia" title="Ralph Lombreglia">Ralph Lombreglia</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Lee_Burke" title="James Lee Burke">James Lee Burke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Napkin_Fiction_Project">The Napkin Fiction Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esquire_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Napkin Fiction Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007 <i>Esquire</i> launched the Napkin Fiction Project, in which 250 cocktail napkins were mailed to writers all over the country by the incoming fiction editor, in a playful attempt to revive short fiction—"some with a half dozen books to their name, others just finishing their first."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In return, the magazine received nearly a hundred stories. <a href="/wiki/Rick_Moody" title="Rick Moody">Rick Moody</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ames" title="Jonathan Ames">Jonathan Ames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bret_Anthony_Johnston" title="Bret Anthony Johnston">Bret Anthony Johnston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Ferris" title="Joshua Ferris">Joshua Ferris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yiyun_Li" title="Yiyun Li">Yiyun Li</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aimee_Bender" title="Aimee Bender">Aimee Bender</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ZZ_Packer" title="ZZ Packer">ZZ Packer</a> are among the notable writers included.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dubious_Achievement_Awards">Dubious Achievement Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esquire_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dubious Achievement Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For many years, <i>Esquire</i> has published its annual <i>Dubious Achievement Awards</i>, lampooning events of the preceding year. As a <a href="/wiki/Running_gag" title="Running gag">running gag</a>, the annual article almost always displayed an old photo of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> laughing, with the caption, "Why is this man laughing?" However, the February 2006 "Dubious Achievement Awards" used the caption under a photo of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W._Mark_Felt" title="W. Mark Felt">W. Mark Felt</a>, the former FBI official revealed in 2005 to be the "<a href="/wiki/Deep_Throat_(Watergate)" title="Deep Throat (Watergate)">Deep Throat</a>" <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate</a> source for <a href="/wiki/Bob_Woodward" title="Bob Woodward">Bob Woodward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bernstein" title="Carl Bernstein">Carl Bernstein</a>. The magazine did continue the Nixon photo in February 2007, referring to a poll stating that <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> had surpassed Nixon as the "worst president ever". Another running gag has been headlining one especially egregious achievement, "And then they went to <a href="/wiki/Elaine%27s" title="Elaine's">Elaine's</a>." (<a href="/wiki/Elaine%27s" title="Elaine's">Elaine's</a> was a popular restaurant in New York City. It closed May 2011.)</p>
<p><i>Esquire</i> did not publish "Dubious Achievement Awards" for 2001, but resumed them with the 2002 awards, published in the February 2003 issue.</p>
<p>"Dubious Achievement Awards" were discontinued in 2008, according to an editor's note in the January 2008 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, after a nine-year hiatus, the feature was revived in the January 2017 issue with a skewering of 2016 events.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexiest_Woman_Alive">Sexiest Woman Alive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esquire_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sexiest Woman Alive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/People_(magazine)#Sexiest_Man_Alive" title="People (magazine)">Sexiest Man Alive</a></div>
<p>The annual feature <b>Sexiest Woman Alive</b> designation by the magazine is billed as a benchmark of female attractiveness.</p>
<p>Originally, it was a part of the "Women We Love" issue that was released in November. To build interest, the magazine would do a tease, releasing partial images of the woman in the issues preceding the November issue. By 2007, it had become the dominating story of the issue and to create an element of surprise the hints were abandoned.</p>
