<p>The <b>Pillars of Hercules</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i>Columnae Herculis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: Ἡράκλειοι Στῆλαι, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: أعمدة هرقل / <i>Aʿmidat Hirqul</i>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i>Columnas de Hércules</i>) was the phrase that was applied in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Antiquity</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontories</a> that flank the entrance to the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a>. The northern Pillar, Calpe Mons, is the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Gibraltar" title="Rock of Gibraltar">Rock of Gibraltar</a> (part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_overseas_territory" title="British overseas territory">British overseas territory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>). A corresponding North African peak not being predominant, the identity of the southern Pillar, Abila Mons, has been disputed throughout history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> with the two most likely candidates being <a href="/wiki/Monte_Hacho" title="Monte Hacho">Monte Hacho</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Musa_(Morocco)" title="Jebel Musa (Morocco)">Jebel Musa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillars_of_Hercules&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="274" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg/120px-PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg/180px-PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg/240px-PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PillarsHerculesPeutingeriana.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Modern conjectural depiction of the lost western section of the <a href="/wiki/Tabula_Peutingeriana" title="Tabula Peutingeriana">Tabula Peutingeriana</a>, showing a representation of the Pillars of Hercules (<i>Columne Ercole</i>).</div>
</div>

<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> adopted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscans" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a> and Romans, when <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules</a> had to perform <a href="/wiki/Labours_of_Hercules" title="Labours of Hercules">twelve labours</a>, one of them (the tenth) was to fetch the Cattle of <a href="/wiki/Geryon" title="Geryon">Geryon</a> of the far West and bring them to <a href="/wiki/Eurystheus" title="Eurystheus">Eurystheus</a>; this marked the westward extent of his travels. A lost passage of <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a> quoted by <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> was the earliest traceable reference in this context: "the pillars which Pindar calls the 'gates of Gades' when he asserts that they are the farthermost limits reached by Heracles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since there has been a one-to-one association between Heracles and <a href="/wiki/Melqart" title="Melqart">Melqart</a> since <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, the "Pillars of Melqart" in the temple near Gades/Gádeira (modern <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">Cádiz</a>) have sometimes been considered to be the true <i>Pillars of Hercules</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burkert1985_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burkert1985-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s account, the lost realm of <a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantis</a> was situated beyond the Pillars of Hercules, in effect placing it in the realm of the Unknown. Renaissance tradition says the pillars bore the warning <i>Ne plus ultra</i> (also <i>Non plus ultra</i>, "nothing further beyond"), serving as a warning to sailors and navigators to go no further.</p>
<p>According to some Roman sources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> while on his way to the garden of the <a href="/wiki/Hesperides" title="Hesperides">Hesperides</a> on the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erytheia" title="Erytheia">Erytheia</a>, Hercules had to cross the mountain that was once <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">Atlas</a>. Instead of climbing the great mountain, Hercules used his superhuman strength to smash through it. By doing so, he connected the Atlantic Ocean to the Mediterranean Sea and formed the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a>. One part of the split mountain is <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> and the other is either <a href="/wiki/Monte_Hacho" title="Monte Hacho">Monte Hacho</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Musa_(Morocco)" title="Jebel Musa (Morocco)">Jebel Musa</a>. These two mountains taken together have since then been known as the Pillars of Hercules, though other natural features have been associated with the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> however, held that instead of smashing through an isthmus to create the Straits of Gibraltar, Hercules <i>narrowed</i> an already existing strait to prevent monsters from the Atlantic Ocean from entering the Mediterranean Sea.</p>

<p>The Pillars appear as supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Spain" title="Coat of arms of Spain">coat of arms of Spain</a>, originating in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_device" title="Personal device">impresa</a> of Spain's sixteenth century king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Spain" title="Charles I of Spain">Charles I</a>, who was also the Holy Roman Emperor as <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>. It bears the motto <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plus_Ultra_(motto)" title="Plus Ultra (motto)">Plus Ultra</a></i>, Latin for <i>further beyond</i>, implying that the pillars were a gateway.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phoenician_connection">Phoenician connection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillars_of_Hercules&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phoenician connection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beyond Gades, several important Mauritanian colonies (in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>) were founded by the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a> as the Phoenician merchant navy pushed through the Pillars of Hercules and began constructing a series of bases along the Atlantic coast starting with <a href="/wiki/Lixus_(ancient_city)" title="Lixus (ancient city)">Lixus</a> in the north, then <a href="/wiki/Chellah" title="Chellah">Chellah</a> and finally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mogador" title="Mogador">Mogador</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Near the eastern shore of the island of Gades/Gadeira (modern <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">Cádiz</a>, just beyond the strait) <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> describes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the westernmost temple of <a href="/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon" title="Tyre, Lebanon">Tyrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>, the god with whom Greeks associated the Phoenician and Punic <a href="/wiki/Melqart" title="Melqart">Melqart</a>, by <i><a href="/wiki/Interpretatio_graeca" title="Interpretatio graeca">interpretatio graeca</a></i>. Strabo notes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> that the two bronze pillars within the temple, each eight <a href="/wiki/Cubit" title="Cubit">cubits</a> high, were widely proclaimed to be the true Pillars of Hercules by many who had visited the place and had sacrificed to Heracles there. But Strabo believes the account to be fraudulent, in part noting that the inscriptions on those pillars mentioned nothing about Heracles, speaking only of the expenses incurred by the Phoenicians in their making. The <a href="/wiki/Melqart#Cult" title="Melqart">columns of the Melqart temple</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon" title="Tyre, Lebanon">Tyre</a> were also of religious significance.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pillars_in_Syriac_geography">The Pillars in Syriac geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillars_of_Hercules&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Pillars in Syriac geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Syriac_studies" title="Syriac studies">Syriac</a> scholars were aware of the Pillars through their efforts to translate Greek scientific works into their language as well as into Arabic. The Syriac compendium of knowledge known as <i>Ktaba d'ellat koll 'ellan</i>. "The Cause of all Causes", is unusual in asserting that there were three, not two, columns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Dante's_Inferno"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dante.27s_Inferno">Dante's <i>Inferno</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillars_of_Hercules&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dante's Inferno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Inferno_(Dante)" title="Inferno (Dante)">Inferno</a></i> XXVI <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> mentions <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Ulysses</a> in the pit of the Fraudulent Counsellors and his voyage past the Pillars of Hercules. Ulysses justifies endangering his sailors by the fact that his goal is to gain knowledge of the unknown. After five months of navigation in the ocean, Ulysses sights the mountain of <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">Purgatory</a> but encounters a <a href="/wiki/Whirlwind" title="Whirlwind">whirlwind</a> from it that sinks his ship and all on it for their <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">daring</a> to approach Purgatory while alive, by their strength and wits alone.</p>

<h2><span id="Sir_Francis_Bacon's_Novum_Organum"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sir_Francis_Bacon.27s_Novum_Organum">Sir Francis Bacon's <i>Novum Organum</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillars_of_Hercules&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sir Francis Bacon's Novum Organum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Pillars appear prominently on the engraved title page of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Francis_Bacon" title="Sir Francis Bacon">Sir Francis Bacon</a>'s <i>Instauratio Magna</i> ("Great Renewal"), 1620, an unfinished work of which the second part was his influential <i><a href="/wiki/Novum_Organum" title="Novum Organum">Novum Organum</a></i>. The motto along the base says <i>Multi pertransibunt et augebitur scientia</i> ("Many will pass through and knowledge will be the greater"). The image was based on the use of the pillars in Spanish and Habsburg propaganda.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_architecture">In architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillars_of_Hercules&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the Spanish coast at <a href="/wiki/Los_Barrios" title="Los Barrios">Los Barrios</a> are <a href="/wiki/Torres_de_Hercules" title="Torres de Hercules">Torres de Hercules</a> which are twin towers that were inspired by the <i>Pillars of Hercules</i>. These towers were the tallest in Andalusia until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cajasol_Tower" title="Cajasol Tower">Cajasol Tower</a> was completed in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> in 2015.</p>
