<p><b>Hurricane Ophelia</b> (known as <b>Storm Ophelia</b> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> while <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a>) was the easternmost <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" title="Major hurricane">major hurricane</a> on record. The tenth consecutive hurricane and the sixth major hurricane of the very active <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Ophelia had non-tropical origins from a decaying cold front on 6 October. Located within a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Factors" title="Tropical cyclone">favorable environment</a>, the storm steadily strengthened over the next two days, drifting north and then southeastwards before becoming a hurricane on 11 October. After becoming a Category 2 hurricane and fluctuating in intensity for a day, Ophelia intensified into a major hurricane on 14 October south of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, brushing the archipelago with high winds and heavy rainfall. Shortly after achieving peak intensity, Ophelia began weakening as it accelerated over progressively colder waters to its northeast towards <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. Completing an extratropical transition early on 16 October, Ophelia became the second storm of the <a href="/wiki/2017%E2%80%9318_UK_and_Ireland_windstorm_season" title="2017–18 UK and Ireland windstorm season">2017–18 UK and Ireland windstorm season</a>. Early on 17 October, the cyclone crossed the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and struck <a href="/wiki/Western_Norway" title="Western Norway">western Norway</a>, with wind gusts up to 70 kilometres per hour (43 mph) in <a href="/wiki/Rogaland" title="Rogaland">Rogaland county</a>, before weakening during the evening. The system made additional landfalls in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> before dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</p>
<p>Three deaths can be direcly attributed to Ophelia, all of which occurred in Ireland. The total losses from the storm are estimated at <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>1 billion (US$1.18 billion), mostly from economic losses; insured damage claims as of 24 October reached €6 million (US$7 million) in Ireland and £5–10 million (US$6.6–13.2 million) in the United Kingdom.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ophelia_2017_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2162" data-file-width="3000" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Ophelia_2017_track.png/220px-Ophelia_2017_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Ophelia_2017_track.png/330px-Ophelia_2017_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Ophelia_2017_track.png/440px-Ophelia_2017_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ophelia_2017_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 6 October, the United States' <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) began monitoring the tail end of a decaying <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> for possible subtropical or <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup> On the same day, a circulation developed on the periphery of this front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup> Soon afterwards, a non-tropical low developed within the circulation, and drifted to the southwest before becoming nearly stationary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The system began to acquire <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical characteristics</a> on the next day, and the chances of development were raised to a high percentage of <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After a brief loss of organization and diminished convection, due to moderate <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the system continued to steadily organize, and developed a well-defined circulation center early on 9 October, as deep convection began to persist near the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By 09:00 UTC that day, the convection had persisted long enough for the system to be classified as a tropical depression about 875 mi (1,410 km) west-southwest of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, and the storm was identified as Tropical Depression Seventeen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">curved banding feature</a> wrapped around the center as the <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellite presentation</a> improved, leading to the upgrade to Tropical Storm Ophelia six hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2200" data-file-width="2800" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg/220px-17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg/330px-17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg/440px-17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:17L_2017-10-09_1335Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical Depression Seventeen organizing in the Atlantic Ocean on 9 October</div>
</div>

<p>Despite marginally warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> of around 79 °F (26 °C), the effects of below-average temperatures aloft and low wind shear allowed Ophelia to gradually strengthen; with banding features becoming more prominent as it drifted northwards in an area of weak steering currents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, the large <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">temperature contrast</a> between the unusually-warm ocean surface and the extremely cold temperatures in the upper atmosphere provided instability for Ophelia's thunderstorms, which allowed the storm to continue strengthening, despite being over marginally-warm ocean temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremely_weird_storm_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-extremely_weird_storm-10">[10]</a></sup> A slight degradation of the structure of the storm resulted in some weakening early on 11 October,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> but this was short-lived as deep convection wrapped around the entire storm. It was upgraded to a hurricane at 21:00 UTC that day after a ragged <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> developed, becoming the record-tying tenth consecutive hurricane to form during the 2017 hurricane season; Ophelia was the fourth such occurrence since the <a href="/wiki/1878_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1878 Atlantic hurricane season">1878</a>, <a href="/wiki/1886_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1886 Atlantic hurricane season">1886</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1893_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1893 Atlantic hurricane season">1893 seasons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ophelia_discussion_11_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ophelia_discussion_11-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10_hurricanes_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-10_hurricanes-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-records_note_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-records_note-14">[nb 1]</a></sup> Afterwards, Ophelia steadily intensified as it became nearly stationary, intensifying into a Category 2 hurricane on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a> on 12 October, as the <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a> became more symmetric and the eye became better defined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hurricane then weakened slightly the on next day, as cloud tops warmed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> only to quickly strengthen as the eye became much more defined and cloud tops cooled significantly due to <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> from a strong <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> to the northeast. The NHC upgraded the storm to a major hurricane (Category 3 or higher) at 15:00 UTC on 14 October, the farthest east that a storm of such intensity had been observed in the satellite era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ophelia_discussion_12_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ophelia_discussion_12-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordEast_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-RecordEast-18">[17]</a></sup> At the same time, it attained its peak intensity with winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 960 millibars (28.35 inHg) while located approximately 235 miles (375 km) southeast of the Azores. After peaking in intensity, increasing shear and dry air from an encroaching frontal boundary began to disrupt the core of the storm, which caused Ophelia to begin weakening early on 15 October as it began its extratropical transition, with the eye collapsing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The process accelerated as Ophelia became further embedded in the trough, and ultimately, the storm became extratropical at 03:00 UTC on the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations_and_impact">Preparations and impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations and impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Azores">Azores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Azores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Portugu%C3%AAs_do_Mar_e_da_Atmosfera" title="Instituto Português do Mar e da Atmosfera">Portuguese Institute of the Sea and the Atmosphere</a> issued a red warning for heavy rainfall for the eastern group of the Azores—<a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Miguel_Island" title="São Miguel Island">São Miguel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Island" title="Santa Maria Island">Santa Maria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formigas" title="Formigas">Formigas</a>—on 14 October from 17:59 UTC to 23:59 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warning_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-warning-21">[20]</a></sup> An orange gale warning was issued for the eastern group for the afternoon through night of 14–15 October,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warning_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-warning-21">[20]</a></sup> as well as a yellow alert for high seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warning_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-warning-21">[20]</a></sup> Rainfall alerts were also issued for the central group—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terceira" title="Terceira">Terceira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graciosa" title="Graciosa">Graciosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Jorge_Island" title="São Jorge Island">São Jorge Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pico_Island" title="Pico Island">Pico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faial_Island" title="Faial Island">Faial</a>.</p>
<p>The President of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Autoridade_Nacional_de_Prote%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Civil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Autoridade Nacional de Proteção Civil (page does not exist)">Regional Service of Civil Protection</a> of the Azores, <a href="/wiki/Military_ranks_of_Portugal#Officers_2" title="Military ranks of Portugal">Lieutnant-Colonel</a> Carlos Neves, announced there was no serious damage. High winds downed four trees on São Miguel, three in the <a href="/wiki/Ponta_Delgada" title="Ponta Delgada">Ponta Delgada</a> district and one in <a href="/wiki/Povoa%C3%A7%C3%A3o,_Azores" title="Povoação, Azores">Povoação</a>. The island also experienced some minor flooding. In the central group of the Azores, there were a few instances of light damage, with one home suffering a roof leak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iberia">Iberia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Iberia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/October_2017_Iberian_wildfires" title="October 2017 Iberian wildfires">October 2017 Iberian wildfires</a></div>
<p>Starting on 15 October 2017, winds from Hurricane Ophelia fanned wildfires in both <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. The wildfires have claimed the lives of at least 49 individuals, including 45 in Portugal and four in Spain, and dozens more were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> In Portugal, more than 4,000 firefighters are battling around 150 fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_Ireland">Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Republic of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ophelia_16_October_2017.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3020" data-file-width="4040" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ophelia_16_October_2017.png/220px-Ophelia_16_October_2017.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ophelia_16_October_2017.png/330px-Ophelia_16_October_2017.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ophelia_16_October_2017.png/440px-Ophelia_16_October_2017.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ophelia_16_October_2017.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ophelia making landfall in Ireland as an extratropical cyclone on 16 October</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Met_%C3%89ireann" title="Met Éireann">Met Éireann</a>, Ireland's national meteorological service, reported on 12 October that the storm would reach Ireland. On 14 October, it issued a 'Status Red' warning, its highest storm category,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> for portions of Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Issuing such a warning more than 48 hours in advance was "unprecedented," as such warnings are normally issued within 24 hours of the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-is_a_hurricane_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-is_a_hurricane-32">[31]</a></sup> On 15 October, the National Emergency Coordination Centre and Met Éireann convened to advise the public in relation to the post-tropical storm reaching the Republic of Ireland. At 20:15 on the 15th, 'status red' was extended to all of Ireland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-is_a_hurricane_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-is_a_hurricane-32">[31]</a></sup> and all public education services were confirmed as cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Education_and_Skills_(Ireland)" title="Department of Education and Skills (Ireland)">Department of Education and Skills</a> confirmed that all Montessoris, creches, primary and post-primary schools would be closed on 16 and 17 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Other public services would be withdrawn such as Court and District Court services, third-level institutes such as UCC, CIT, University of Limerick, and Waterford Institute of Technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> confirmed a number of flights from Cork Airport and Shannon Airport would be cancelled, with the likelihood of 50 flights being cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> All public transport previously scheduled within the red alert zone were cancelled including bus, rail and ferry journeys. <a href="/wiki/Bus_%C3%89ireann" title="Bus Éireann">Bus Éireann</a> announced the cancellation of school bus services for the west of Ireland after <a href="/wiki/Met_%C3%89ireann" title="Met Éireann">Met Éireann</a> issued a rare Status Red warning affecting the south western and western counties of Wexford, Waterford, Cork, Kerry, Clare, Mayo and Galway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9CRTE_News.E2.80.9D_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9CRTE_News.E2.80.9D-38">[37]</a></sup> The Department of Housing, Planning and Local Government confirmed members of the public should not make any unnecessary journeys especially travelling within the red level warning areas and the department reiterated the storm is potential risk to life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16 October, gusts of up to 191 kilometres per hour (119 mph) were recorded at Fastnet Rock off the coast of County Cork, the highest wind speeds ever recorded in Ireland. 10-minute sustained wind speeds at Roches Point, Co. Cork, reached 111 kilometres per hour (69 mph), with gusts of 156 kilometres per hour (97 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/ESB_Group" title="ESB Group">ESB Group</a> confirmed that more than 360,000 customers were without power in the wake of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> Two people, a man in <a href="/wiki/Dundalk" title="Dundalk">Dundalk</a> and a woman in <a href="/wiki/Aglish" title="Aglish">Aglish</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Waterford" title="County Waterford">County Waterford</a>, were killed when trees fell on their cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_Times_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_Times-44">[43]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/County_Tipperary" title="County Tipperary">County Tipperary</a>, another fatality occurred when a man was clearing a fallen tree with a chainsaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> Two men died in separate incidents after suffering fatal injuries while carrying out repairs to damage caused by Ophelia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_Brian" title="Storm Brian">Storm Brian</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>, a man died after he fell while working on a shed roof, and in <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a> another man died after falling from a ladder while carrying out repairs to his farm shed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent.ie_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent.ie-46">[45]</a></sup> On 17 October, it was estimated that Ophelia had caused €1 billion ($1.18 billion USD) losses in Ireland, for the most part for the shutdown of economic activities the day of its passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-financial_storm_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-financial_storm-47">[46]</a></sup> As of 24 October, insurance claims across Ireland reached €6 million (US$7 million), primarily coming from residents in Cork and Tipperary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Insured damage is not expected to exceed that of <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tini" title="Cyclone Tini">Storm Darwin</a> in 2014, which resulted in €111 million (US$131 million) in payouts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Insured_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Insured-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3450" data-file-width="5176" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg/220px-Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg/330px-Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg/440px-Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orange_sun_in_Gloucestershire_16_October_2017.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saharan dust and smoke brought north by the former hurricane created hazy skies across parts of the United Kingdom on 16 October, resulting in an orange or red sun.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> in the United Kingdom issued the first severe weather warnings for Ophelia on 12 October, referring to the hurricane as "ex-Ophelia" in the context of the <a href="/wiki/2017%E2%80%9318_UK_and_Ireland_windstorm_season" title="2017–18 UK and Ireland windstorm season">2017–18 UK and Ireland windstorm season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9Cwarning.E2.80.9D_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9Cwarning.E2.80.9D-50">[49]</a></sup> The severe weather warning initially issued on 12 October was a yellow weather warning for wind, covering Northern Ireland, northern England, Wales, south west England and southern and western Scotland, valid between 12:00 and 23:55 BST on 15 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9Cwarning.E2.80.9D_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9Cwarning.E2.80.9D-50">[49]</a></sup> The weather warning impact matrix warned of relatively severe impacts anticipated, although with a low level of certainty so far in advance preventing the issuance of amber weather warnings initially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9Cwarning.E2.80.9D_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9Cwarning.E2.80.9D-50">[49]</a></sup> Subsequently, on 13 October, a yellow severe weather warning for wind was issued for Northern Ireland, southern Scotland and northern England, valid between 00:05 and 15:00 BST on 17 October. On 15 October, the weather warning for wind in Northern Ireland on 16 October was upgraded to an amber weather warning.</p>
<p>The arrival of Ophelia brought <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saharan_dust" title="Saharan dust">Saharan dust</a> to parts of the United Kingdom, giving the sky an orange or yellow-sepia appearance, and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> a red or orange appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> A strange 'burning' smell was also reported across <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, also attributed to the dust, and smoke from forest fires in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> Winds up to 115 km/h (71 mph) were observed in Orlock Head, <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">County Down</a>, at the height of the storm. Approximately 50,000 households lost power in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. Insurance claims from Northern Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> are estimated to reach £5–10 million (US$6.6–13.2 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estonia">Estonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Estonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, black rain fell because Ophelia brought smoke and the soot of fires to Estonia from Portugal, as well as dust from the Sahara Desert, Report informs citing the Estonian media. "We looked at photos from satellites and the Finnish weather service confirmed that the smoke and soot of the fires in Portugal and partly the dust from the Sahara reached us," meteorologist Taimi Paljak said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hurricane Ophelia was the easternmost major hurricane (at Category 3 intensity or higher) ever observed in the North Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ophelia_discussion_12_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ophelia_discussion_12-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordEast_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-RecordEast-18">[17]</a></sup> Hurricane Ophelia's intensification into a hurricane also made the 2017 Atlantic hurricane season the fourth such season to see 10 consecutive hurricanes, after the <a href="/wiki/1878_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1878 Atlantic hurricane season">1878</a>, <a href="/wiki/1886_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1886 Atlantic hurricane season">1886</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1893_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1893 Atlantic hurricane season">1893 seasons</a>, as far as records go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ophelia_discussion_11_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ophelia_discussion_11-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10_hurricanes_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-10_hurricanes-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-records_note_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-records_note-14">[nb 1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_climate_change">Relation to climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ophelia_(2017)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relation to climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate scientist Reindert Haarsma of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Meteorological_Institute" title="Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute">Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute</a> said that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a> is likely to cause Europe to see more hurricanes like Ophelia, as the oceans get warmer, although they were still comparing their model's results (previously reported in 2013) with those from other climate centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RTE2017-10-16a_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-RTE2017-10-16a-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
