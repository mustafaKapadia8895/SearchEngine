<p><b>Boscastle</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <span lang="kw" xml:lang="kw"><i>Kastel Boterel</i></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a village and <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> on the north coast of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England, UK, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Forrabury_and_Minster" title="Forrabury and Minster">Forrabury and Minster</a> (where the 2011 Census population was included) . It is 14 miles (23 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Bude" title="Bude">Bude</a> and 5 miles (8 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Tintagel" title="Tintagel">Tintagel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harbour" title="Harbour">harbour</a> is a natural inlet protected by two stone harbour walls built in 1584 by Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Grenville" title="Richard Grenville">Richard Grenville</a> and is the only significant harbour for 20 miles (32 km) along the coast. The village extends up the valleys of the <a href="/wiki/River_Valency" title="River Valency">River Valency</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=River_Jordan,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="River Jordan, Cornwall (page does not exist)">River Jordan</a>. Heavy rainfall on 16 August 2004 caused extensive damage to the village.</p>
<p>Boscastle lies within the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Cornwall Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Cornwall Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB). Almost a third of Cornwall has AONB designation, with the same status and protection as a National Park, and the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Coast_Path" title="South West Coast Path">South West Coast Path</a> passes through the village.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boscastle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the village comes from Botreaux Castle (pronounced "But'ry"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> a 12th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" title="Motte-and-bailey">motte-and-bailey</a> fortress, of which few remains survive. The castle was anciently in the possession of the de Botreaux family, which became under <a href="/wiki/William_de_Botreaux,_1st_Baron_Botreaux" title="William de Botreaux, 1st Baron Botreaux">William de Botereaux</a> (1337–91) the <a href="/wiki/Baron_Botreaux" title="Baron Botreaux">Barons Botreaux</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiquary" title="Antiquary">antiquary</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Leland_(antiquary)" title="John Leland (antiquary)">John Leland</a> in the mid 16th-century described the village ″<i>... it is a very filthy town and il kept.</i>″<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Boscastle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harbour" title="Harbour">harbour</a> is a natural inlet protected by two stone harbour walls built in 1584 by Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Grenville" title="Richard Grenville">Richard Grenville</a> (of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Revenge_(1577)" title="HMS Revenge (1577)">HMS <i>Revenge</i></a>). It is the only significant harbour for 20 miles (32 km) along the coast. Boscastle was once a small port (similar to many others on the north coast of Cornwall), importing limestone and coal, and exporting slate and other local produce.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boscastle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rector of Boscastle is responsible for seven churches in the district: <a href="/wiki/Forrabury_and_Minster" title="Forrabury and Minster">Forrabury (St Symphorian), Minster (St Merthiana)</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Juliot" title="St Juliot">St Juliot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesnewth" title="Lesnewth">Lesnewth</a> (St Michael and All Angels), <a href="/wiki/Trevalga" title="Trevalga">Trevalga</a> (St Petroc), <a href="/wiki/Otterham" title="Otterham">Otterham</a> (St Denis) and <a href="/wiki/Davidstow" title="Davidstow">Davidstow</a> (St David).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> St Juliot is of particular interest to devotees of the works of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> since he acted as the architect for the church's <a href="/wiki/Victorian_restoration" title="Victorian restoration">restoration</a> in March 1870 and this is where he met his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Emma_Gifford" title="Emma Gifford">Emma Gifford</a>, who was the Rector's sister-in-law. Their love affair was the inspiration for his novel <i><a href="/wiki/A_Pair_of_Blue_Eyes" title="A Pair of Blue Eyes">A Pair of Blue Eyes</a></i> and later in life, some of his poetry.</p>
<dl>
<dt><i>A Seaside Parish</i></dt>
</dl>
<p>In 2004 British television channel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_2" title="BBC 2">BBC 2</a> began broadcasting <i><a href="/wiki/A_Seaside_Parish" title="A Seaside Parish">A Seaside Parish</a></i>, a weekly series focusing on the life of the newly appointed Rector of Boscastle, Christine Musser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boscastle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The village, with its picturesque harbour, is a popular tourist destination. Among the attractions are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_Witchcraft" title="Museum of Witchcraft">Museum of Witchcraft</a>,Uncle Paul's Emporium, the Boscastle pottery shop, and access to the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Coast_Path" title="South West Coast Path">South West Coast Path</a>.</p>
<p>Much of the land in and around Boscastle is owned by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a>, including both sides of the harbour, Forrabury Stitches, high above the Boscastle and divided into ancient "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stitchmeal_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stitchmeal farming (page does not exist)">stitchmeal</a>" cultivation plots, and large areas of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valency_Valley" title="Valency Valley">Valency Valley</a>, known for its connections to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a>.</p>
<p>The former harbour stables (part of the National Trust estate) are now a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Youth_hostel" title="Youth hostel">youth hostel</a> run by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YHA_(England_and_Wales)" title="YHA (England and Wales)">YHA</a>, popular with walkers. The National Trust runs a shop at the harbour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visitor_centre" title="Visitor centre">visitor centre</a> in the Old Smithy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boscastle_floods">Boscastle floods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boscastle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Boscastle floods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boscastle_flood_of_2004" title="Boscastle flood of 2004">Boscastle flood of 2004</a></div>
<dl>
<dt>Flood of 2004</dt>
</dl>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boscastle_flood,_2004" title="Boscastle flood, 2004">flash flood</a> on 16 August 2004 caused extensive damage to the village. Residents were trapped in houses as the roads turned into rivers: people were trapped on roofs, in cars, in buildings and on the river's banks. and the village's visitor centre was washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Two <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> <a href="/wiki/Westland_Sea_King" title="Westland Sea King">Westland Sea King</a> rescue helicopters from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Marines_Base_Chivenor" title="Royal Marines Base Chivenor">Chivenor</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> Sea Kings from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culdrose" title="Culdrose">Culdrose</a>, one RAF Sea King from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAF_St._Mawgan" title="RAF St. Mawgan">St Mawgan</a> and one Coastguard S61 helicopter from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portland,_Dorset" title="Portland, Dorset">Portland</a> searched for and assisted casualties in and around the village.</p>
<p>The operation was coordinated by the Aeronautical Rescue Coordination Centre (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ARCC" title="ARCC">ARCC</a>) based at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Kinloss" title="RAF Kinloss">RAF Kinloss</a> in Scotland in the largest peacetime rescue operation ever launched in the UK. A total of 91 people were rescued and there were no fatalities, only one broken thumb. Around 50 cars were swept into the harbour and the bridge was washed away, roads were submerged under 2.75 metres (9 ft) of water, making communication effectively impossible until flood-waters subsided. The sewerage system burst, and for this range of health and safety reasons Boscastle was declared temporarily inaccessible.</p>
<p>The causes were: over 60 millimetres (2.4 in) of rainfall (typically a month's rainfall) fell in two hours; the ground was already saturated due to the previous two weeks of above average rainfall; the drainage basin has many steep slopes, and has areas of impermeable slate causing rapid surface run-off; Boscastle is at the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> (where tributaries meet) of three rivers - Valency, Jordan, and Paradise; a large quantity of water all arrived within a short space of time causing the rivers to overflow; the flooding coincided with a high tide, making the effect worse.</p>
<p>The effects were homes, businesses and cars belonging to more than 1,000 people were swept away; income from tourism was lost, which affected livelihoods and the local economy; there were vast numbers of subsequent insurance claims; no lives were lost, partly due to the rapid response of the emergency services.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Flood of 2007</dt>
</dl>
<p>Boscastle was flooded again on 21 June 2007 although the scale of destruction was not nearly as serious as in 2004.</p>
