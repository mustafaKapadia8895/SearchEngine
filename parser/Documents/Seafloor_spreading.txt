<p><b>Seafloor spreading</b> is a process that occurs at <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridges</a>, where new <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> is formed through <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a> and then gradually moves away from the ridge. Seafloor spreading helps explain <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a> in the theory of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>. When oceanic plates <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">diverge</a>, tensional stress causes fractures to occur in the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>.The motivating force for seafloor spreading ridges is tectonic plate pull rather than magma pressure, although there is typically significant magma activity at spreading ridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> At a spreading center <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic magma</a> rises up the fractures and cools on the ocean floor to form new <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">Hydrothermal vents</a> are common at spreading centers. Older rocks will be found farther away from the spreading zone while younger rocks will be found nearer to the spreading zone. Additionally spreading rates determine if the ridge is a fast, intermediate, or slow. As a general rule, fast ridges see spreading rate of more than 9 cm/year. Intermediate ridges have a spreading rate of 4–9 cm/year while slow spreading ridges have a rate less than 4 cm/year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Earlier theories (e.g. by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">Alfred Wegener</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_du_Toit" title="Alexander du Toit">Alexander du Toit</a>) of continental drift postulated that <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> "ploughed" through the sea. The idea that the seafloor itself moves (and also carries the continents with it) as it expands from a central axis was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Hammond_Hess" title="Harry Hammond Hess">Harry Hess</a> from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The theory is well accepted now, and the phenomenon is known to be caused by <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> currents in the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a>, which is ductile, or <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plastic</a>, and the brittle lithosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incipient_spreading">Incipient spreading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seafloor_spreading&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Incipient spreading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the general case, sea floor spreading starts as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rift_(geology)" title="Rift (geology)">rift</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_plate" title="Continental plate">continental land mass</a>, similar to the Red Sea-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Africa_Rift" title="East Africa Rift">East Africa Rift</a> System today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The process starts with heating at the base of the continental crust which causes it to become more plastic and less dense. Because less dense objects rise in relation to denser objects, the area being heated becomes a broad dome (see <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostasy</a>). As the crust bows upward, fractures occur that gradually grow into rifts. The typical rift system consists of three rift arms at approximately 120 degree angles. These areas are named <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">triple junctions</a> and can be found in several places across the world today. The separated margins of the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> evolve to form <a href="/wiki/Passive_margin" title="Passive margin">passive margins</a>. Hess' theory was that new seafloor is formed when magma is forced upward toward the surface at a mid-ocean ridge.</p>
<p>If spreading continues past the incipient stage described above, two of the rift arms will open while the third arm stops opening and becomes a 'failed rift'. As the two active rifts continue to open, eventually the continental crust is attenuated as far as it will stretch. At this point basaltic oceanic crust begins to form between the separating continental fragments. When one of the rifts opens into the existing ocean, the rift system is flooded with seawater and becomes a new sea. The <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> is an example of a new arm of the sea. The East African rift was thought to be a "failed" arm that was opening somewhat more slowly than the other two arms, but in 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopian</a> Afar Geophysical Lithospheric Experiment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> reported that in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afar_(region)" title="Afar (region)">Afar region</a> last September,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> a 60 km fissure opened as wide as eight meters. During this period of initial flooding the new sea is sensitive to changes in climate and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eustasy" title="Eustasy">eustasy</a>. As a result, the new sea will evaporate (partially or completely) several times before the elevation of the rift valley has been lowered to the point that the sea becomes stable. During this period of evaporation large evaporite deposits will be made in the rift valley. Later these deposits have the potential to become hydrocarbon seals and are of particular interest to <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> geologists.</p>
<p>Sea floor spreading can stop during the process, but if it continues to the point that the continent is completely severed, then a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">ocean basin</a> is created. The Red Sea has not yet completely split Arabia from Africa, but a similar feature can be found on the other side of Africa that has broken completely free. South America once fit into the area of the <a href="/wiki/Niger_Delta" title="Niger Delta">Niger Delta</a>. The Niger River has formed in the failed rift arm of the triple junction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Continued_spreading_and_subduction">Continued spreading and subduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seafloor_spreading&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Continued spreading and subduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ridge_render.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="179" data-file-width="350" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Ridge_render.jpg/300px-Ridge_render.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/Ridge_render.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ridge_render.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spreading at a mid-ocean ridge</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As new seafloor forms and spreads apart from the <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridge</a> it slowly cools over time. Older seafloor is therefore colder than new seafloor, and older oceanic basins deeper than new oceanic basins due to <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostasy</a>. If the diameter of the earth remains relatively constant despite the production of new crust, a mechanism must exist by which crust is also destroyed. The destruction of oceanic crust occurs at <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> zones where oceanic crust is forced under either continental crust or oceanic crust. Today, the Atlantic basin is actively spreading at the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. Only a small portion of the oceanic crust produced in the Atlantic is subducted. However, the plates making up the Pacific Ocean are experiencing subduction along many of their boundaries which causes the volcanic activity in what has been termed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Ring_of_Fire" title="Pacific Ring of Fire">Ring of Fire</a> of the Pacific Ocean. The Pacific is also home to one of the world's most active spreading centres (the <a href="/wiki/East_Pacific_Rise" title="East Pacific Rise">East Pacific Rise</a>) with spreading rates of up to 13 cm/yr. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is a "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/textbook" title="wiktionary:textbook">textbook</a>" slow-spreading centre, while the East Pacific Rise is used as an example of fast spreading. The differences in spreading rates affect not only the geometries of the ridges but also the geochemistry of the basalts that are produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the new oceanic basins are shallower than the old oceanic basins, the total capacity of the world's ocean basins decreases during times of active sea floor spreading. During the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, sea level was so high that a <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a> formed across <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debate_and_search_for_mechanism">Debate and search for mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seafloor_spreading&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Debate and search for mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> (and in other areas), material from the upper <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> rises through the faults between oceanic plates to form new <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> as the plates move away from each other, a phenomenon first observed as <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">Alfred Wegener</a> first presented a hypothesis of continental drift in 1912, he suggested that continents ploughed through the ocean crust. This was impossible: oceanic crust is both more dense and more rigid than continental crust. Accordingly, Wegener's theory wasn't taken very seriously, especially in the United States.</p>
<p>Since then, it has been shown that the motion of the continents is linked to seafloor spreading. In the 1960s, the past record of <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">geomagnetic reversals</a> was noticed by observing the magnetic stripe "anomalies" on the ocean floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This results in broadly evident "stripes" from which the past magnetic field polarity can be inferred by looking at the data gathered from simply towing a magnetometer on the sea surface or from an aircraft. The stripes on one side of the mid-ocean ridge were the mirror image of those on the other side. The seafloor must have originated on the Earth's great fiery welts, like the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> and the East Pacific Rise.</p>
<p>The driver for seafloor spreading in plates with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_margin" title="Active margin">active margins</a> is the weight of the cool, dense, subducting slabs that pull them along. The magmatism at the ridge is considered to be "passive upswelling", which is caused by the plates being pulled apart under the weight of their own slabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This can be thought of as analogous to a rug on a table with little friction: when part of the rug is off of the table, its weight pulls the rest of the rug down with it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_floor_global_topography:_half-space_model">Sea floor global topography: half-space model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seafloor_spreading&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sea floor global topography: half-space model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
