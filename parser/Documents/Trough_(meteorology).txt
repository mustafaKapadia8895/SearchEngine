<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Trough (meteorology)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

Representation of alternating troughs and ridges in upper-level <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, with regions of convergence and divergence labeled.</div>

<p>A <b>trough</b> is an elongated (extended) region of relatively low <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, often associated with <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">fronts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nov-12-2012-7am.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="460" data-file-width="565" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Nov-12-2012-7am.gif/350px-Nov-12-2012-7am.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Nov-12-2012-7am.gif/525px-Nov-12-2012-7am.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Nov-12-2012-7am.gif 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nov-12-2012-7am.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A very large trough (about 8000 km or more) crosses the North Atlantic Ocean from north east to south west. The elongated cloud is surrounded by two big areas of higher atmospheric pressure, clearly shown with no clouds at all.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unlike fronts, there is not a universal symbol for a trough on a weather chart. The weather charts in some countries or regions mark troughs by a line. In the United States, a trough may be marked as a dashed line. In the UK, Hong Kong<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Fiji,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it is represented by a bold line extended from a low pressure center<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or between two low pressure centers;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in Macau<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> it is a dashed line. If they are not marked, troughs may still be identified as an extension of isobars away from a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">low pressure center</a>.</p>
<p>Sometimes the region between two high pressure centers may assume the character of a trough when there is a detectable wind shift noted at the surface. In the absence of a wind shift, the region is designated a <b>col</b>, akin to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_saddle" title="Geographic saddle">geographic saddle</a> between two mountain peaks.</p>
<p>If a trough forms in the mid-latitudes, a temperature difference between two sides of the trough usually exists in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather front</a>. A weather front is usually less convective than a trough in the tropics or subtropics (such as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a>). Sometimes collapsed frontal systems will degenerate into troughs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Convection_cell#Within_the_Earth.27s_troposphere" title="Convection cell">Convective cells</a> may develop in the vicinity of troughs and give birth to a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>. Some tropical or subtropical regions such as the Philippines or south China are greatly affected by convection cells along a trough. In the mid-latitude <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a>, troughs and <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridges</a> often alternate, especially when upper-level winds are in a high-amplitude pattern. For a trough in the westerlies, the region just west of the trough axis is typically an area of convergent winds and descending air - and hence high pressure - while the region just east of the trough axis is an area of fast, divergent winds and low pressure. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">Tropical waves</a> are a type of trough in easterly currents, a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclonic</a> northward deflection of the trade winds.</p>
<p>Troughs may be at the surface, or aloft, or both under various conditions. Most troughs bring clouds, showers, and a wind shift, particularly following the passage of the trough. This results from convergence or "squeezing" which forces lifting of moist air behind the trough line.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_trough">Types of trough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trough_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of trough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to standard troughs, some may be described further with a qualifying term indicating a specific or a set of characteristics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inverted_trough">Inverted trough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trough_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inverted trough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An inverted trough is an atmospheric trough which is oriented opposite to most troughs of the mid-latitudes. Most (but not all) inverted troughs are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_waves" title="Tropical waves">tropical waves</a> (also commonly called easterly waves).</p>
<p>Most troughs of low pressure in the mid-latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere are characterized by decreasing atmospheric pressure from south to north while inverted troughs are characterized by decreasing pressure from north to south. The situation is opposite in the Southern Hemisphere. Inverted troughs in both hemispheres move to the west from the east, while mid-latitude troughs generally move with the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> toward the east.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lee_trough">Lee trough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trough_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lee trough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A lee trough, also known as a dynamic trough, is "A pressure trough formed on the lee side of a mountain range in situations where the wind is blowing with a substantial component across the mountain ridge; often seen on United States weather maps east of the Rocky Mountains, and sometimes east of the Appalachians, where it is less pronounced."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeTroughDef_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeeTroughDef-8">[8]</a></sup> It can be formed either as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> compression of sinking air on the lee side of a mountain range, or through <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a> resulting from "the horizontal convergence associated with vertical stretching of air columns passing over the ridge and descending the lee slope."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeTroughDef_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-LeeTroughDef-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeSideTroughLow_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeeSideTroughLow-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
