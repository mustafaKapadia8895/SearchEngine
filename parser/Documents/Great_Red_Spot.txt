<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Great Red Spot</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4637" data-file-width="5489" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg/320px-Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg/480px-Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg/640px-Jupiter_from_Voyager_1.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A <a href="/wiki/False_color" title="False color">false-color</a> image of the Great Red Spot of Jupiter from <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i>. <p>The <b>Great Red Spot</b> is a persistent zone of high pressure, producing an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclonic_storm" title="Anticyclonic storm">anticyclonic storm</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, 22° south of the equator. It has been continuously observed for 187 years, since 1830. Earlier observations from 1665 to 1713 are believed to be the same storm; if this is correct, it has existed for at least 350 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Storms such as this are not uncommon within the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmospheres</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_history">Observation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Red_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:211px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pioneer_10_jup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="552" data-file-width="489" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Pioneer_10_jup.jpg/209px-Pioneer_10_jup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Pioneer_10_jup.jpg/314px-Pioneer_10_jup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Pioneer_10_jup.jpg/418px-Pioneer_10_jup.jpg 2x" width="209"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pioneer_10_jup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Image of Jupiter by <i>Pioneer 10</i> in 1974, showing a more solid-looking spot than when shown by <i>Voyager 1</i> in 1979</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Great Red Spot may have existed since before 1665, but the present spot was first seen only after 1830 and well-studied only after a prominent apparition in 1879. A long gap separates its period of current study after 1830 from its seventeenth-century discovery; whether the original spot dissipated and reformed, whether it faded, or even if the observational record was simply poor, are all unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beebe1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beebe1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, its first sighting is often credited to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">Robert Hooke</a>, who described a spot on the planet in May 1664; however, it is likely that Hooke's spot was in the wrong belt altogether (the North Equatorial Belt, versus the current Great Red Spot's location in the South Equatorial Belt). Much more convincing is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Cassini" title="Giovanni Cassini">Giovanni Cassini</a>'s description of a "permanent spot" the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With fluctuations in visibility, Cassini's spot was observed from 1665 to 1713; however, the 118-year observational gap makes the identity of the two spots inconclusive, and the older spot's shorter observational history and slower motion than the modern spot make their identity unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A minor mystery concerns a Jovian spot depicted in a 1711 canvas by <a href="/wiki/Donato_Creti" title="Donato Creti">Donato Creti</a>, which is exhibited in the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hockey1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hockey1-7">[7]</a></sup> Part of a series of panels in which different (magnified) heavenly bodies serve as backdrops for various <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> scenes, and all overseen by the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Eustachio_Manfredi" title="Eustachio Manfredi">Eustachio Manfredi</a> for accuracy, Creti's painting is the first known to depict the Great Red Spot as red. No Jovian feature was explicitly described as red before the late 1800s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hockey1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hockey1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="895" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg/250px-Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg/375px-Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg/500px-Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A wider view of Jupiter and the Great Red Spot as seen from <i>Voyager 1</i> in 1979</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On February 25, 1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> when the <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i> spacecraft was 9.2 million kilometres (5.7 million miles) from Jupiter it transmitted the first detailed image of the Great Red Spot back to Earth. Cloud details as small as 160 kilometres (100 miles) across were visible. The colorful, wavy cloud pattern seen to the left (west) of the Red Spot is a region of extraordinarily complex and variable wave motion.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="390" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif/220px-790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif/330px-790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a3/790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:790106-0203_Voyager_58M_to_31M_reduced.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Time-lapse sequence from the approach of <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i> to Jupiter, showing the motion of atmospheric bands, and circulation of the Great Red Spot. <i>NASA image</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the start of 2004, the Great Red Spot had approximately half the longitudinal extent it had a century ago, when it reached a size of 40,000 kilometres. At the present rate of reduction it would become circular by 2040. It is not known how long the spot will last, or whether the change is a result of normal fluctuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A smaller spot, designated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oval_BA" title="Oval BA">Oval BA</a>, formed recently (March 2000) from the merger of three white ovals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> has turned reddish in color. Astronomers have named it the <i>Little Red Spot</i> or <i>Red, Jr.</i> As of June 5, 2006, the Great Red Spot and Oval BA appeared to be approaching convergence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The storms pass each other about every two years but the passings of 2002 and 2004 were of little significance. Amy Simon-Miller, of the <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a>, predicted the storms would have their closest passing on July 4, 2006. She worked with Imke de Pater and Phil Marcus of <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">UC Berkeley</a>, and a team of professional astronomers since April 2006 to study the storms using the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>; on July 20, the two storms were photographed passing each other by the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_Observatory" title="Gemini Observatory">Gemini Observatory</a> without converging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In May 2008 a third storm turned red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Red Spot should not be confused with the <i>Great Dark Spot</i>, a feature observed near the northern pole of Jupiter in 2000 with the <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a></i> spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Note that a feature in the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> was also called the <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot" title="Great Dark Spot">Great Dark Spot</a></i>. The latter feature was imaged by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> in 1989, and may have been an atmospheric hole rather than a storm and it was no longer present as of 1994 (although a similar spot had appeared farther to the north).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">Juno Spacecraft</a> flew over the Great Red Spot on July 11, 2017, taking several images of the Spot from about 5,000 miles (8,000 km) above the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Red_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter,_Earth_size_comparison.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2571" data-file-width="3000" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Jupiter%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg/250px-Jupiter%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Jupiter%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg/375px-Jupiter%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Jupiter%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg/500px-Jupiter%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter,_Earth_size_comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Approximate size comparison of Earth and the Great Red Spot.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The oval object rotates counter-clockwise, with a period of about six Earth days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> or fourteen Jovian days. Measuring in at 10,159 miles (16,350 kilometers) in width (as of April 3, 2017) Jupiter's Great Red Spot is 1.3 times as wide as Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a>-tops of this storm are about eight kilometres above the surrounding cloud-tops.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a> data have long indicated that the Great Red Spot is colder (and thus, higher in altitude) than most of the other clouds on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, recent infrared measurements of the upper atmosphere show far more heat above the Great Red Spot than the rest of the planet; "acoustic waves" rising from the storm have been proposed as an explanation for Jupiter's temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Careful tracking of atmospheric features revealed the spot's counter-clockwise circulation as far back as 1966, observations dramatically confirmed by the first time-lapse movies from the <i>Voyager</i> fly-bys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The spot is confined by a modest eastward <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> to its south and a very strong westward one to its north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Though winds around the edge of the spot peak at ~120 metres per second (432 kilometres per hour), currents inside it seem stagnant, with little inflow or outflow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The rotation period of the spot has decreased with time, perhaps as a direct result of its steady reduction in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1799" height="83" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif/250px-PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif/375px-PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif/500px-PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Color animation of Jupiter's cloud motion.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Great Red Spot's <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> has been stable for the duration of good observational records, typically varying by about a degree. Its <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, however, is subject to constant variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Because Jupiter does not rotate uniformly at all latitudes, astronomers have defined three different systems for defining the longitude. System II is used for latitudes of more than 10°, and was originally based on the average rotation rate of the Great Red Spot of 9h 55m 42s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Despite this, however, the spot has "lapped" the planet in System II at least 10 times since the early nineteenth century. Its drift rate has changed dramatically over the years and has been linked to the brightness of the South Equatorial Belt, and the presence or absence of a South Tropical Disturbance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>It is not known exactly what causes the Great Red Spot's reddish color. Theories supported by laboratory experiments suppose that the color may be caused by complex organic molecules, red phosphorus, or a compound containing sulphur, but a consensus has yet to be reached.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Great Red Spot varies greatly in hue, from almost brick-red to pale salmon, or even white. In fact, the spot occasionally "disappears", becoming evident only through the Red Spot Hollow, which is its niche in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloud_pattern_on_Jupiter" title="Cloud pattern on Jupiter">South Equatorial Belt</a> (SEB). Interestingly, its visibility is apparently coupled to the SEB; when the belt is bright white, the spot tends to be dark, and when it is dark, the spot is usually light. These periods when the spot is dark or light occur at irregular intervals; as of 1997, during the preceding 50 years, the spot was darkest in the periods 1961–1966, 1968–1975, 1989–1990, and 1992–1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beebe1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beebe1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_dynamics">Mechanical dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Red_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanical dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no definitive theory as to what is causing the formation or colour of the storm that is known as the Great Red Spot. Laboratory studies are examining the effects that <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_radiation" title="UV radiation">UV Light</a> from the Sun are having on the chemical composition of the clouds of Jupiter. In particular whether the Sun's radiation is reacting with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_hydrosulfide" title="Ammonium hydrosulfide">ammonium hydrosulfide</a> in the planet's outer atmosphere to create the deep red colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Research has shown that the storm produces extreme amounts of heat because it is simultaneously generating <a href="/wiki/Gravity_wave" title="Gravity wave">gravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_wave" title="Acoustic wave">acoustic waves</a>. When these turbulent energy waves collide 500 mi (800 km) above the red spot, they are creating extremely high temperatures in the planet's upper atmosphere. The effect is described as being like "crashing [..] ocean waves on a beach".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The reason the storm has continued to exist for centuries is because of the physical nature of Jupiter. As there is no planetary surface to provide friction (only a liquid core of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>), circulating gas eddies persist for a very long time in the atmosphere because there is nothing to oppose their angular momentum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
