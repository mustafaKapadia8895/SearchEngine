<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gibraltar Arc</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

Gibraltar Arc</div>

<p>The <b>Gibraltar Arc</b> is a geological region corresponding to an arcuate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orogen" title="Orogen">orogen</a> surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Alboran_Sea" title="Alboran Sea">Alboran Sea</a>, between the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It consists of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betic_Cordillera" title="Betic Cordillera">Betic Cordillera</a> (south Spain), and the <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">Rif</a> (North Morocco).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Gibraltar Arc is located at the western end of the Mediterranean <a href="/wiki/Alpine_orogeny" title="Alpine orogeny">Alpine</a> belt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and formed during the Neogene due to convergence of the Eurasian and African plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maximum altitudes of the region are reached at the <a href="/wiki/Mulhac%C3%A9n" title="Mulhacén">Mulhacén</a> peak (3482 m) at the Cordillera Betica. Precipitation is collected mainly by the <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">Guadalquivir</a> (Betics) and <a href="/wiki/Sebou_River" title="Sebou River">Sebou</a> (Rif) rivers, which have delivered most sedimentary infill of the homonym sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Foreland_basin" title="Foreland basin">foreland basins</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonic_evolution">Tectonic evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gibraltar_Arc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tectonic evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>North–south convergence of the Eurasian and African plates occurred during the middle <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> to the late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>, followed by northwest–southeast convergence from the late <a href="/wiki/Tortonian" title="Tortonian">Tortonian</a> to present. The Gibraltar Arc was formed during the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> due to a combination of western migration of the orogenic mountain front and late orogenic extension. The present convergence rate of the plates is estimated to be approximately 4.5 to 5.0 mm/year with an azimuth of 135–120°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eastward Gibraltar Arc oceanic <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> system was active during the Early and Middle Miocene and has likely been inactive since. At this time, the Alboran Sea acted as a <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">back-arc basin</a> during the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(geology)" title="Accretion (geology)">accretionary</a> units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedrera_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pedrera-4">[4]</a></sup> Since the Late Miocene, north–south to northwest–southeast <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">continental convergence</a> forced the subduction system along the arc that is oriented N20°E to N100°E. There is a lithospheric slab dipping east from the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a> down to 600 km depth beneath the Alboran Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedrera_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pedrera-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crustal structure of the Gibraltar Arc is characterized by an arcuate bulge parallel to the arc, with crustal thinning occurring uniformly from the margins of the mountain ranges towards the Alboran Sea. The <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithospheric</a> mantle also has an arcuate bulge below the arc with extreme mantle thinning in the Alboran Sea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-3">[3]</a></sup> which is the typical structure of a back-arc basin located on the concave side of an arcuate mountain belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A major left-lateral <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Strike-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">strike-slip</a> fault zone, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-Alboran_Shear_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trans-Alboran Shear Zone (page does not exist)">Trans-Alboran Shear Zone</a>, crosscuts the Gibraltar Arc with a NE trend from the eastern Betics to the western Rif. It was active during the Neogene, contributing to the westward advance of the Gibraltar Arc. Some of the previous fault segments are active, with left-lateral <a href="/wiki/Transpression" title="Transpression">transpressive</a> faulting and moderate to significant clockwise stress rotations. Oblique to this shear zone, there are two major right-lateral strike-slip fault systems, the Maro-Nerja and Yusuf systems. These trend NW and have <a href="/wiki/Transtension" title="Transtension">transtensive</a> deformation. The present-day stress pattern is probably the consequence of the interference between two stress sources: ongoing continent convergence and secondary stress sources from variations in crustal thickness, sedimentary accumulations causing loading, and the active strike-slip fault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gibraltar_Arc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Arc has two sections: the Internal and External Zones. The Internal Zone, which is located on the inner side of the arc, adjacent to the Alboran Sea, mostly consists of high-pressure, low-temperature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rocks" title="Metamorphic rocks">metamorphic rocks</a>. The External Zone, located on the outer side of the arc, is mostly made of sediments deposited on the <a href="/wiki/Passive_margin" title="Passive margin">passive margins</a> of Africa and Iberia. These rocks became highly deformed during the westward emplacement of the hinterland of the subduction system, with some units in the External Rif having undergone medium-pressure, low-temperature metamorphism during the Oligocene. <a href="/wiki/Flysch" title="Flysch">Flysch</a> units from the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> to Early Miocene are located between the External and Internal Zones. These were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folding_(geology)" title="Folding (geology)">folded</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrusted</a> during the westward migration of the Internal Zones and can be interpreted as the palaeo-<a href="/wiki/Accretionary_wedge" title="Accretionary wedge">accretionary wedge</a> of the subduction system that was active during the Lower and Middle Miocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedrera_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pedrera-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
