<p>The <b>capelin</b> or <b>caplin</b> (<i>Mallotus villosus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Smelt_(fish)" title="Smelt (fish)">smelt</a> family found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic</a> oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-1">[1]</a></sup> In summer, it grazes on dense swarms of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> at the edge of the ice shelf. Larger capelin also eat a great deal of <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>. Among others, <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_mackerel" title="Atlantic mackerel">Atlantic mackerel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> prey on capelin, in particular during the spawning season of the capelin while it migrates southwards. Capelin spawn on sand and gravel bottoms or sandy beaches at the age of 2–6 years, and have an extremely high mortality rate on the beaches after spawning, for males close to 100%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Males reach 20 cm (8 in) in length, while females are up to 25.2 cm (10 in) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-1">[1]</a></sup> They are olive-colored dorsally, shading to silver on sides. Males have a translucent ridge on both sides of their bodies. The ventral aspects of the males iridesce reddish at the time of spawn.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capelin_migration">Capelin migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capelin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Capelin migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Capelin in the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> are stocks that perform extensive seasonal <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migrations</a>. Barents Sea capelin migrate during winter and early spring to the coast of northern Norway (<a href="/wiki/Finnmark" title="Finnmark">Finnmark</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Kola_Peninsula" title="Kola Peninsula">Kola Peninsula</a> (Russia) for spawning. During summer and autumn capelin migrate north- and north-eastward for feeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Icelandic capelin move inshore in large <a href="/wiki/Shoaling_and_schooling" title="Shoaling and schooling">schools</a> to <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawn</a> and migrate in spring and summer to feed in the <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>-rich oceanic area between Iceland, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a>. Capelin distribution and migration is linked with <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> and water masses. Around Iceland, maturing capelin usually undertake extensive northward feeding migrations in spring and summer and the return migration takes place in September to November. The spawning migration starts from north of Iceland in December to January. In a paper published in 2009, researchers from Iceland recounted their application of an interacting particle model to the capelin stock around Iceland, successfully predicting the spawning migration route for 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capelin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As an <a href="/wiki/R/K_selection_theory" title="R/K selection theory">r-selected</a> species, capelin have a high reproductive potential and an intrinsic population growth rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICES2005_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICES2005-4">[4]</a></sup> They reproduce by <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> and their main spawning season occurs in spring, but can extend into the summer. The majority of capelin are three or four years old when they spawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter-2">[2]</a></sup> The males migrate directly to the shallow water of fjords, where spawning will take place, while the females remain in deeper water until they are completely mature. Once the females are mature, they will migrate to the spawning grounds and spawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friis-R.C3.B8del_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friis-R.C3.B8del-5">[5]</a></sup> This process usually takes place at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter-2">[2]</a></sup> In the North European Atlantic spawning typically occurs over sand or gravel at depths of 2 to 100 m (7–328 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but in the North Pacific and waters off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> most spawn on beaches, jumping as far up land as possible, with some managing to strand themselves in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICES2005_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ICES2005-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Although some other fish species leave their eggs in locations that dry out (a few, such as <a href="/wiki/Porichthys_notatus" title="Porichthys notatus">plainfin midshipman</a>, may even remain on land with the eggs during low tide) or on plants above the water (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Splash_tetra" title="Splash tetra">splash tetras</a>), jumping onto land <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/En_masse" title="En masse">en masse</a></i> to spawn is unique to the capelin, <a href="/wiki/Grunion" title="Grunion">grunions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grass_puffer" title="Grass puffer">grass puffer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After the female capelins have spawned, they immediately leave the spawning grounds and will potentially spawn again in the following years if they survive. The males do not leave the spawning grounds and will potentially spawn more than once throughout the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friis-R.C3.B8del_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friis-R.C3.B8del-5">[5]</a></sup> Male capelin are considered to be <a href="/wiki/Semelparity_and_iteroparity" title="Semelparity and iteroparity">semelparous</a> because they die soon after the spawning season is over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gj.C3.B8s.C3.A6ter-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capelin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Capelin is an important <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a>, and is essential as the key food of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a>. The northeast Atlantic cod and capelin fisheries therefore are managed by a multispecies approach developed by the main resource owners Norway and Russia.</p>
<p>In some years with large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_herring" title="Atlantic herring">Atlantic herring</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a>, capelin seem to be heavily affected. Probably both food competition and herring feeding on capelin larvae lead to collapses in the capelin stock. However, in some years there has been good recruitment of capelin despite a high herring biomass, suggesting that herring are only one factor influencing capelin dynamics.</p>
<p>In the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> (particularly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_peninsula" title="Gaspé peninsula">Gaspé peninsula</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a> in Canada, it is a regular summertime practice for locals to go to the <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a> and scoop the capelin up in nets or whatever is available, as the capelin "roll in" in the millions each year at the end of May or in early June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commercially, capelin is used for <a href="/wiki/Fish_meal" title="Fish meal">fish meal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_oil" title="Fish oil">oil</a> industry products, but is also appreciated as food. The flesh is agreeable in flavor, resembling herring. Capelin <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">roe</a> ("masago") is considered a high-value product. It is also sometimes mixed with <a href="/wiki/Wasabi" title="Wasabi">wasabi</a> or green food coloring and wasabi flavor and sold as "wasabi caviar". Often, masago is commercialized as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebiko" title="Ebiko">ebiko</a> and used as a substitute for <a href="/wiki/Tobiko" title="Tobiko">tobiko</a>, flying fish roe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> owing to its similar appearance and taste —although the <a href="/wiki/Mouthfeel" title="Mouthfeel">mouthfeel</a> is different due to the individual eggs being smaller and less crunchy than tobiko.</p>
