<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Doggerland</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doggerland.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="545" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Doggerland.svg/250px-Doggerland.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Doggerland.svg/375px-Doggerland.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Doggerland.svg/500px-Doggerland.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doggerland.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing hypothetical extent of Doggerland (c. 10,000 BC), which provided a land bridge between Great Britain and continental Europe</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Doggerland</b> was an area now beneath the southern <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> that connected <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> to <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a> during and after the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>. It was flooded by rising sea levels around 6,500–6,200 BC. Geological surveys have suggested that it stretched from Britain's east coast to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and the western coasts of Germany and the peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was probably a rich habitat with human habitation in the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usask_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-usask-2">[2]</a></sup> although rising sea levels gradually reduced it to low-lying islands before its final submergence, possibly following a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Storegga_Slide" title="Storegga Slide">Storegga Slide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc010514_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc010514-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> potential of the area had first been identified in the early 20th century, but interest intensified in 1931 when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_trawler" title="Commercial trawler">fishing trawler</a> operating east of <a href="/wiki/The_Wash" title="The Wash">the Wash</a> dragged up a barbed antler point that was subsequently dated to a time when the area was <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. Vessels have dragged up remains of <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a> and other animals, as well as a few prehistoric tools and weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Doggerland was named after the <a href="/wiki/Dogger_Bank" title="Dogger Bank">Dogger Bank</a>, which in turn was named after the 17th century Dutch fishing boats called <i><a href="/wiki/Dogger_(boat)" title="Dogger (boat)">doggers</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doggerland3er_en.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1212" data-file-width="2166" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Doggerland3er_en.png/300px-Doggerland3er_en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Doggerland3er_en.png/450px-Doggerland3er_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Doggerland3er_en.png/600px-Doggerland3er_en.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doggerland3er_en.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing hypothetical extent of Doggerland from <a href="/wiki/Weichselian_glaciation" title="Weichselian glaciation">Weichselian glaciation</a> until the current situation.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Until the middle <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, Britain was a peninsula of Europe, connected by the massive chalk <a href="/wiki/Weald%E2%80%93Artois_Anticline" title="Weald–Artois Anticline">Weald–Artois Anticline</a> across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Dover" title="Straits of Dover">Straits of Dover</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglian_glaciation" title="Anglian glaciation">Anglian glaciation</a>, approximately 450,000 years ago, an ice sheet filled much of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, with a large <a href="/wiki/Proglacial_lake" title="Proglacial lake">proglacial lake</a> in the southern part fed by the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">Thames</a> river systems. The catastrophic overflow of this lake carved a channel through the anticline, leading to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_River" title="Channel River">Channel River</a>, which carried the combined Scheldt and Thames to the Atlantic. This probably created the potential for Britain to become isolated from the continent during periods of high sea level, although some scientists argue that the final break did not occur until a second ice-dammed lake overflowed during the <a href="/wiki/Marine_isotope_stage" title="Marine isotope stage">MIS</a>8 or MIS6 glaciations, around 340,000 or 240,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the most recent glaciation of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, which ended around 18,000 years ago, the North Sea and much of the British Isles were covered with glacial ice and the sea level was about 120 m (390 ft) lower. Subsequently, the climate became warmer and during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Glacial_Maximum" title="Late Glacial Maximum">Late Glacial Maximum</a> around 12,000 BC Britain, as well as much of the North Sea and English Channel, was an expanse of low-lying <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sussex_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sussex-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence, including the contours of the present seabed, indicates that after the first main Ice Age, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_divide" title="Water divide">watershed</a> between the North Sea and <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> extended east from East Anglia then south-east to the <a href="/wiki/Hook_of_Holland" title="Hook of Holland">Hook of Holland</a>, rather than across the Strait of Dover. The <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">Seine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">Thames</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meuse_(river)" title="Meuse (river)">Meuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> rivers joined and flowed west along the English Channel as a wide slow river before eventually reaching the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usask_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-usask-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sussex_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-sussex-6">[6]</a></sup> At about 10,000 BC the north-facing coastal area of Doggerland had a coastline of <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltmarsh" title="Saltmarsh">saltmarshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mudflats</a> and beaches as well as inland streams, rivers, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and lakes. It may have been the richest hunting, fowling and fishing ground in Europe in the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usask_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-usask-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaffney_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaffney-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>One big river system found by 3D seismic survey was the "Shotton River", which drained the south-east part of the <a href="/wiki/Dogger_Bank" title="Dogger Bank">Dogger Bank</a> hill area into the east end of the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Silver_Pit" title="Outer Silver Pit">Outer Silver Pit</a> lake. It is named after Birmingham geologist <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_Shotton" title="Frederick William Shotton">Frederick William Shotton</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance">Disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doggerbank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1700" data-file-width="1300" height="327" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Doggerbank.jpg/250px-Doggerbank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Doggerbank.jpg/375px-Doggerbank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Doggerbank.jpg/500px-Doggerbank.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doggerbank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The red line marks <a href="/wiki/Dogger_Bank" title="Dogger Bank">Dogger Bank</a>, which is most likely a <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraine</a> formed in the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stride_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stride-8">[8]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As ice melted at the end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weichselian_Ice_Age" title="Weichselian Ice Age">the last glacial period of the current ice age</a>, sea levels rose and the land began to tilt in an <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">isostatic adjustment</a> as the huge weight of ice lessened. Doggerland eventually became submerged, cutting off what was previously the British peninsula from the European mainland by around 6500 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sussex_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-sussex-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanpast_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-humanpast-9">[9]</a></sup> The Dogger Bank, an upland area of Doggerland, remained an island until at least 5000 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sussex_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-sussex-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanpast_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-humanpast-9">[9]</a></sup> Key stages are now believed to have included the gradual evolution of a large tidal bay between eastern England and Dogger Bank by 9000 BCE and a rapid sea-level rise thereafter, leading to Dogger Bank becoming an island and Great Britain becoming physically disconnected from the continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weninger_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-weninger-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A recent hypothesis postulates that much of the remaining coastal land was flooded by a <a href="/wiki/Megatsunami" title="Megatsunami">megatsunami</a> around 6200 BCE, caused by a submarine landslide off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Storegga_Slide" title="Storegga Slide">Storegga Slide</a>. This suggests: "that the Storegga Slide tsunami would have had a catastrophic impact on the contemporary coastal Mesolithic population.... Britain finally became separated from the continent and in cultural terms, the Mesolithic there goes its own way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weninger_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-weninger-10">[10]</a></sup> A study published in 2014 suggested that the only remaining parts of Doggerland at the time of the Storegga Slide were low-lying islands, but supported the view that the area had been abandoned at about the same time as the tsunamis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc010514_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc010514-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another view speculates that the Storegga tsunami devastated Doggerland but then ebbed back into the sea, and that later <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agassiz" title="Lake Agassiz">Lake Agassiz</a> (in North America) burst releasing so much fresh water that sea levels over about two years rose to flood much of Doggerland and make Britain an island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_investigation_by_archaeologists">Discovery and investigation by archaeologists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery and investigation by archaeologists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1954" data-file-width="3120" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg/220px-200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg/330px-200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg/440px-200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:200805231215_Wollhaarmammut_Millie_Sch%C3%A4del.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">Woolly mammoth</a> skull discovered by fishermen in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, at Celtic and Prehistoric Museum, Ireland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The prehistoric existence of what is now known as Doggerland was established in the late 19th century. <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a> referred to the concept in his short story <i><a href="/wiki/A_Story_of_the_Stone_Age" title="A Story of the Stone Age">A Story of the Stone Age</a></i> of 1897, set in "a time when one might have walked dryshod from France (as we call it now) to England, and when a broad and sluggish Thames flowed through its marshes to meet its father Rhine, flowing through a wide and level country that is under water in these latter days, and which we know by the name of the North Sea...Fifty thousand years ago it was, fifty thousand years if the reckoning of geologists is correct", though most of the action seems to occur in what is now <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, but stretching out to Doggerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remains of plants brought to the surface from Dogger Bank were studied in 1913 by <a href="/wiki/Paleobiology" title="Paleobiology">paleobiologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Clement_Reid" title="Clement Reid">Clement Reid</a>, and the remains of animals and worked flints from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_period" title="Neolithic period">Neolithic period</a> had also been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keith_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keith-13">[13]</a></sup> In his book <i>The Antiquity of Man</i> of 1915, <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Keith" title="Sir Arthur Keith">Sir Arthur Keith</a> discussed the archaeological potential of the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keith_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keith-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1931, the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_trawler" title="Fishing trawler">trawler</a> <i>Colinda</i> hauled up a lump of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> whilst fishing near the Ower Bank, 40 kilometres (25 mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>. The peat was found to contain a barbed antler point, possibly used as a <a href="/wiki/Harpoon" title="Harpoon">harpoon</a> or fish spear, 220 millimetres (8.5 in) long, which dated from between 4,000 and 10,000 BC when the area was <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usask_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-usask-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaffney_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gaffney-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Interest was reinvigorated in the 1990s by Professor <a href="/wiki/Bryony_Coles" title="Bryony Coles">Bryony Coles</a>, who named the area "Doggerland" ("after the great banks in the southern North Sea")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaffney_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-gaffney-7">[7]</a></sup> and produced speculative maps of the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaffney_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-gaffney-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coles_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-coles-14">[14]</a></sup> Although she recognised that the current relief of the southern North Sea <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a> is not a sound guide to the topography of Doggerland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coles_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-coles-14">[14]</a></sup> this topography has more recently begun to be reconstructed more authoritatively using <a href="/wiki/Reflection_seismology" title="Reflection seismology">seismic survey</a> data obtained from oil exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archaeology_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-archaeology-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A skull fragment of a <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a>, dated at over 40,000 years old, was recovered from material dredged from the Middeldiep, some 16 kilometres (10 mi) off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a>, and exhibited in <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">Leiden</a> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In March 2010 it was reported that recognition of the potential archaeological importance of the area could affect the future development of offshore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_farms" title="Wind farms">wind farms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2012, the results of a fifteen-year study of Doggerland by the universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">St Andrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Dundee" title="University of Dundee">Dundee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, including artefacts survey results, were displayed at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc2012_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc2012-20">[20]</a></sup> Richard Bates of St Andrews University said:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc2012_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc2012-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We have speculated for years on the lost land's existence from bones dredged by fishermen all over the North Sea, but it's only since working with oil companies in the last few years that we have been able to re-create what this lost land looked like.... We have now been able to model its flora and fauna, build up a picture of the ancient people that lived there and begin to understand some of the dramatic events that subsequently changed the land, including the sea rising and a devastating tsunami.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In September 2015, archaeologists at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bradford" title="University of Bradford">University of Bradford</a> announced their project to chart Doggerland in 3D, and to study DNA from deep sea core samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sarah_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-sarah-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The "Mammoth Journey" episode of the BBC television programme <i><a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Beasts" title="Walking with Beasts">Walking with Beasts</a></i> is partly set on the dry bed of the southern North Sea.</li>
<li>The area featured in a 2007 episode of the Channel 4 <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Team_(specials)" title="Time Team (specials)">Time Team (specials)</a></i> documentary series called "Britain's Drowned World".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>The first chapter of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Rutherfurd" title="Edward Rutherfurd">Edward Rutherfurd</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Sarum_(novel)" title="Sarum (novel)">Sarum</a></i> describes the flooding of Doggerland.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">Science fiction</a> author <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Baxter_(author)" title="Stephen Baxter (author)">Stephen Baxter</a>'s <i>Northland</i> trilogy is set in an alternative timeline in which Doggerland (Northland in the books) is never inundated.</li>
<li>The opening song of <a href="/wiki/Ian_Anderson" title="Ian Anderson">Ian Anderson</a>'s 2014 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_Erraticus" title="Homo Erraticus">Homo Erraticus</a></i>, is titled "Doggerland," and provides a first person narrative from the point of view of the prehistoric people who might have lived there.</li>
</ul>
