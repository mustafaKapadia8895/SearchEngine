<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Pandalus borealis</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Pandalus borealis</b></i> is a species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caridean_shrimp" title="Caridean shrimp">caridean shrimp</a> found in cold parts of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Oceans</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> refers to them as the <b>northern prawn</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-1">[1]</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common names</a> include coldwater prawn, pink shrimp, deepwater prawn, deep-sea prawn, great northern prawn, <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">crevette nordique</span></i> and northern shrimp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandalus_borealis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>P. borealis</i> lives at depths of 20–1,330 m (66–4,364 ft), usually on soft muddy bottoms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-1">[1]</a></sup> in waters with a temperature of 0–8 °C (32–46 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The distribution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>P. b. borealis</i> in the Atlantic ranges from <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada's</a> eastern seaboard (off <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>), southern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> as far south as the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. In the Pacific, <i>P. b. eous</i> is found from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Okhotsk" title="Sea of Okhotsk">Sea of Okhotsk</a>, across the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>, and as far south in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a> state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandalus_borealis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In their 8-year lifespan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seafish_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seafish-3">[3]</a></sup> males can reach a length of 120 mm (4.7 in), while females can reach 165 mm (6.5 in) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shrimp are <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphroditic</a>. They start out male, but after a year or two, their testicles turn to ovaries and they complete their lives as females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seafish_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seafish-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_fishing">Commercial fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandalus_borealis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>P. borealis</i> is an important food resource, and has been widely <a href="/wiki/Shrimp_fishery" title="Shrimp fishery">fished</a> since the early 1900s in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and later in other countries following <a href="/wiki/Johan_Hjort" title="Johan Hjort">Johan Hjort's</a> practical discoveries of how to locate them. In Canada, these shrimp are sold peeled, cooked and frozen in bags in supermarkets, and are consumed as appetizers.</p>
<p>Northern shrimp have a short life, which contributes to a variable stock on a yearly basis. However, the species is not considered overfished due to a large amount reported and a large amount harvested.</p>
<p>In Canada, the annual harvest limit is set to 164,000 tonnes (2008).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seafish_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Seafish-3">[3]</a></sup> The Canadian fishery began in the 1980s and expanded in 1990s.</p>
<p>In 2013 the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission determined that stocks of P. borealis were too low and shut down the New England fishery. This was the first cancellation in 35 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandalus_borealis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beyond human consumption, shrimp <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_phosphatase" title="Alkaline phosphatase">alkaline phosphatase</a> (SAP), an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>, is obtained from <i>Pandalus borealis</i>, and the species' <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> is a source of <a href="/wiki/Chitosan" title="Chitosan">chitosan</a>, a versatile chemical used for such different applications as treating bleeding wounds, filtering wine or improving the soil in <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a>.</p>
