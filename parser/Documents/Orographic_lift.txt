<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Orographic lift</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wave_cloud.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1215" data-file-width="1919" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Wave_cloud.jpg/320px-Wave_cloud.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Wave_cloud.jpg/480px-Wave_cloud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Wave_cloud.jpg/640px-Wave_cloud.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wave_cloud.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Gravity_wave" title="Gravity wave">gravity wave</a> cloud pattern—analogous to a ship <a href="/wiki/Wake" title="Wake">wake</a>—in the downwind zone behind the <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_Amsterdam" title="Île Amsterdam">Île Amsterdam</a>, in the far southern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. The island generates wave motion in the wind passing over it, creating regularly spaced orographic clouds. The wave crests raise and cool the air to form clouds, while the troughs remain too low for cloud formation. Note that while the wave motion is generated by orographic lift, it is not required. In other words, one cloud often forms at the peak. See <a href="/wiki/Wave_cloud" title="Wave cloud">wave cloud</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Orographic lift</b> occurs when an <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air mass</a> is forced from a low <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> to a higher elevation as it moves over rising <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a>. As the air mass gains <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> it quickly cools down <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_cooling" title="Adiabatic cooling">adiabatically</a>, which can raise the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> to 100% and create <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> and, under the right conditions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_orographic_lifting">Effects of orographic lifting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orographic_lift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects of orographic lifting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation">Precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orographic_lift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Precipitation induced by orographic lift occurs in <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow#Regions_of_notable_rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">many places throughout the world</a>. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mogollon_Rim" title="Mogollon Rim">Mogollon Rim</a> in central Arizona</li>
<li>The western slope of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> range in California</li>
<li>The mountains near Baja California North – specifically La Bocana to Laguna Hanson.</li>
<li>The windward slopes of Khasi and Jayantia Hills (see <a href="/wiki/Mawsynram" title="Mawsynram">Mawsynram</a>) in the state of <a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">Meghalaya</a> in India.</li>
<li>The Western Highlands of <a href="/wiki/Yemen#Regions_and_climate" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>, which receive by far the most rain in Arabia.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western Ghats</a> that run along India's western coast.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Dividing_Range" title="Great Dividing Range">Great Dividing Range</a> of Eastern and South Eastern Australia which forces uplift of moist air originating from the ocean to the east.</li>
<li>The mountains of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, which faces a prevailing <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerly</a> flow off the <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Sea" title="Tasman Sea">Tasman Sea</a>.</li>
<li>The mountains of western <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> which also face a prevailing westerly flow.</li>
<li>The southern <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, which faces a prevailing westerly flow off the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_United_States" title="Northwestern United States">Northwestern United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, and Southern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>) see prevailing westerly flow off the northern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Places on the sea-facing side of coastal mountains see in excess of 140 inches (over 3.5 m) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> per year. These locales are on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> which are in the path of <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> systems, and therefore receive the moisture which is effectively squeezed from the clouds.</li>
</ul>

Windy evening <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> enhanced by the Sun's angle, can visually mimic a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> resulting from orographic lift</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ski_country" title="Ski country">ski country</a> region of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, particularly with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_effect_snow" title="Lake effect snow">lake effect snows</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transylvania_County,_North_Carolina" title="Transylvania County, North Carolina">Transylvania County, North Carolina</a>, which gets the most rainfall of anywhere in the Eastern U.S. (90 inches [2,300 mm]).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> (particularly the western facing slopes).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ecoregions_of_Madagascar" title="Ecoregions of Madagascar">Eastern seaboard of</a> <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. The cold Atlantic air mass flows up over the north western face to 3,500 feet (1,100 m) above sea level and is met by the warm Indian Ocean air mass from the south eastern back side of the mountain forming the famous "Table Cloth".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oppland" title="Oppland">Oppland</a> mountain area, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>..</li>
<li>The Front Range Foothills of Northern Colorado – west of Boulder to Golden as storms pass by. Winter storms can produce 5–6 feet (1.5–1.8 m) of snow.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rain_shadowing">Rain shadowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orographic_lift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rain shadowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">Rain shadow</a></div>
<p>The highest precipitation amounts are found slightly upwind from the prevailing winds at the crests of mountain ranges, where they relieve and therefore the upward lifting is greatest. As the air descends the lee side of the mountain, it warms and dries, creating a rain shadow. On the lee side of the mountains, sometimes as little as 15 miles (25 km) away from high precipitation zones, annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> can be as low as 8 inches (200 mm) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Areas where this effect is observed include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> block moisture from the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne_River" title="Rhône River">Rhone valley</a></li>
<li>Areas east of the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a> in the Pacific Northwest (<a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>)</li>
<li>Areas east of the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Mountains" title="Olympic Mountains">Olympic Mountains</a> in Washington state, (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Sequim,_Washington" title="Sequim, Washington">Sequim, Washington</a>)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> of the United States, east of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_States_Pacific_Mountain_System" title="Geography of the United States Pacific Mountain System">Geography of the United States Pacific Mountain System</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Cordillera" title="Pacific Cordillera">Pacific Cordillera</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Canadian Prairies</a></li>
<li>All of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>. The entire island of <a href="/wiki/Kahoolawe" title="Kahoolawe">Kaho'olawe</a> is in the rain shadow of <a href="/wiki/Maui" title="Maui">Maui</a></li>
<li>North East England is in the rain shadow of the Pennines, this combined with Britain's prevailing wind from the South West. This explains the significant differences between the rainfall in the North West and North East.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judean_Desert" title="Judean Desert">Judean Desert</a> in the Land of Israel and the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leeward_winds">Leeward winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orographic_lift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leeward winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:408px;max-width:408px">

<p>Downslope winds occur on the leeward side of mountain barriers when a stable air mass is carried over the mountain by strong winds that increase in strength with height. Moisture is removed and latent heat released as the air mass is orographically lifted. As the air mass descends, it is compression heated. The warm <a href="/wiki/Foehn_wind" title="Foehn wind">foehn wind</a>, locally known as the <a href="/wiki/Chinook_wind" title="Chinook wind">Chinook wind</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bergwind" title="Bergwind">Bergwind</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diablo_wind" title="Diablo wind">Diablo wind</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nor%27west_arch" title="Nor'west arch">Nor'wester</a> depending on the region, provide examples of this type of wind, and are driven in part by latent heat released by orographic-lifting-induced precipitation.</p>
<p>A similar class of winds, the <a href="/wiki/Sirocco" title="Sirocco">Sirocco</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bora_(wind)" title="Bora (wind)">Bora</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Ana_wind" title="Santa Ana wind">Santa Ana winds</a>, are examples where orographic lifting has limited effect since there is limited moisture to remove in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> or other air masses; the Sirocco, Bora and Santa Ana are driven primarily by (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic" title="Adiabatic">adiabatic</a>) compression heating.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_clouds">Associated clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orographic_lift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Associated clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As air flows over mountain barriers, orographic lift can create a variety of cloud effects.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Orographic fog</i> is formed as the air rises up the slope and will often envelope the summit. When the air is humid, some of the moisture will fall on the windward slope and on the summit of the mountain.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>When there is a high wind, a <i>banner cloud</i> is formed downwind of the upper slopes of isolated, steep-sided mountains. It is created by the low pressure areas in the downwind <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a> drawing in relatively humid air from the lower slopes of the mountain. This reduction in pressure compared to the surrounding air increases condensation, in the same manner as an aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Wingtip_vortices" title="Wingtip vortices">wingtip vortices</a>. The most famous such cloud forms routinely in the lee of the <a href="/wiki/Matterhorn" title="Matterhorn">Matterhorn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:408px;max-width:408px">
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Banner_clouds.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="1411" data-file-width="1992" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Banner_clouds.jpg/200px-Banner_clouds.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Banner_clouds.jpg/300px-Banner_clouds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Banner_clouds.jpg/400px-Banner_clouds.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a></div>
</div>
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg/200px-New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg/300px-New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg/400px-New-Mexico-Lenticular.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a></div>
</div>
<div style="clear:left"></div>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">Banner cloud formation on the <a href="/wiki/Matterhorn" title="Matterhorn">Matterhorn</a> (left) and a <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_cloud" title="Lenticular cloud">lenticular cloud</a> in New Mexico</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The leeward edge of an extensive mass of orographic clouds may be quite distinct. On the leeward side of the mountain, the air flowing downward is known as a <a href="/wiki/Foehn_wind" title="Foehn wind">foehn wind</a>. Because some of the moisture that has condensed on the top of the mountain has precipitated, the foehn (or föhn) is drier, and the lower moisture content causes the descending air mass to warm up more than it had cooled down during ascent. The distinct cut-off line which forms along and parallel to the ridge line is sometimes known as a <i>foehn wall</i> (or <i>föhn wall</i>). This is because the edge appears stationary and it often appears to have an abrupt wall-like edge. A foehn wall is a common feature along the <a href="/wiki/Front_Range" title="Front Range">Front Range</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rockies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <i>rotor cloud</i> is sometimes formed downwind and below the level of the ridge. It has the appearance of the ragged <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus</a> cloud type but it is caused by a turbulent horizontal vortex, i.e. the air is very rough.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lenticular_cloud" title="Lenticular cloud">Lenticular clouds</a></i> are stationary lens-shaped clouds that are formed downwind of mountains by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_waves" title="Lee waves">lee waves</a> if the air mass is close to the dew point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup> They are normally aligned at right-angles to the wind direction and are formed at altitudes up to 12,000 metres (39,370 ft).</li>
<li>A <i>cap cloud</i> is a special form of the lenticular cloud with a base low enough that it forms around and covers the peak, capping it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Chinook_wind#Chinook_arch" title="Chinook wind">chinook arch cloud</a></i> is an extensive wave cloud. It has this special name in North America where it is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Chinook_wind" title="Chinook wind">Chinook wind</a>. It forms above the mountain range, usually at the beginning of a chinook wind as a result of orographic lifting over the range. It appears when seen from downwind to form an arch over the mountain range. A layer of clear air separates it from the mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
