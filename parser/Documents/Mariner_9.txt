<p><b>Mariner 9</b> (<b>Mariner Mars '71</b> / <b>Mariner-I</b>) was an unmanned <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> space probe that contributed greatly to the exploration of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and was part of the <a href="/wiki/Mariner_program" title="Mariner program">Mariner program</a>. Mariner 9 was launched toward Mars on May 30, 1971<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M9_trajectory_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-M9_trajectory-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-final_report_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-final_report-2">[2]</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station</a> and reached the planet on November 14 of the same year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M9_trajectory_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-M9_trajectory-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-final_report_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-final_report-2">[2]</a></sup> becoming the first spacecraft to orbit another planet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M9_details_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-M9_details-3">[3]</a></sup> – only narrowly beating the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviets'</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars_2" title="Mars 2">Mars 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a>, which both arrived within a month. After months of dust storms it managed to send back clear pictures of the surface.</p>
<p>Mariner 9 returned 7329 images over the course of its mission, which concluded in October 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-log_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-log-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1420" data-file-width="1107" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg/220px-Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg/330px-Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg/440px-Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlas_I-CENTAUR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mariner 9 launch</div>
</div>

<p>Mariner 9 was designed to continue the atmospheric studies begun by <a href="/wiki/Mariner_6_and_7" title="Mariner 6 and 7">Mariner 6 and 7</a>, and to map over 70% of the Martian surface from the lowest altitude (1,500 kilometers (930 mi)) and at the highest resolutions (from 1 kilometer per pixel to 100 meters per pixel) of any Mars mission up to that point. An infrared <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometer</a> was included to detect heat sources in search of evidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_activity" title="Volcanic activity">volcanic activity</a>. It was to study temporal changes in the Martian atmosphere and surface. Mars' two moons were also to be analyzed. Mariner 9 more than met its objectives.</p>
<p>Under original plans, a dual mission was to be flown like Mariners 6–7, however the launch failure of <a href="/wiki/Mariner_8" title="Mariner 8">Mariner 8</a> ruined this scheme and forced NASA planners to fall back on a simpler one-probe mission. NASA still held out hope that another Mariner probe and <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Centaur" title="Atlas-Centaur">Atlas-Centaur</a> could be readied before the 1971 Mars <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch window</a> closed. A few logistical problems emerged, including the lack of an available Centaur payload shroud of the correct configuration for the Mariner probes, however there was a shroud in NASA's inventory which could be modified. <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a> also had an available Centaur stage on hand and could have an Atlas readied in time, but the idea was ultimately abandoned for lack of funding.</p>
<p>Mariner 9 was mated to Atlas-Centaur AC-23 on May 9 with investigation into Mariner 8's failure ongoing. The malfunction was traced to a problem in the Centaur's pitch control servoamplifier and because it was not clear if the spacecraft itself had been responsible, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interference" title="Electromagnetic interference">RFI</a> testing was conducted on Mariner 9 to ensure the probe was not releasing interference that could cause problems with the Centaur's electronics. All testing came back negative and on May 22, a tested and verified rate gyro package arrived from Convair and was installed in the Centaur.</p>
<p>Liftoff took place on May 30 at 5:23 PM EST. All launch vehicle systems performed normally and the Mariner separated from the Centaur at 13 minutes and 18 seconds after launch.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments">Experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol>
<li>Ultraviolet Spectrometer (UVS)</li>
<li>Infrared Interferometer Spectrometer (IRIS)</li>
<li>Celestial Mechanics (not a separate instrument; it relied upon tracking measurements including range, range rate, and Doppler)</li>
<li>S-Band <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">Occultation</a> (not a separate instrument; experiment observed the attenuation of the communication signal as the orbiting satellite passed out of view)</li>
<li>Infrared Radiometer (IRR)</li>
<li>Visual Imaging System – in a lower orbit, half that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mariner_6" title="Mariner 6">Mariner 6</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mariner_7" title="Mariner 7">Mariner 7</a> flyby missions, and with a vastly improved imaging system, Mariner 9 achieved a resolution of 320 feet (98 m) per <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixel</a>, whereas previous Martian probes had achieved only approximately 2,600 feet (790 m) per pixel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ol>

<p>Mariner 9 was the first <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> to <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> another <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>. It carried an instrument payload similar to Mariners 6 and 7, but because of the need for a larger propulsion system to control the spacecraft in Martian orbit, it weighed more than Mariners 6 and 7 combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Mariner 9 arrived at Mars on November 14, 1971, <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary scientists</a> were surprised to find the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body%27s_atmosphere" title="Celestial body's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> was thick with "a planet-wide robe of <a href="/wiki/Martian_soil#Atmospheric_dust" title="Martian soil">dust</a>, the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_of_mars#Dust_storms_on_Mars" title="Climate of mars">storm</a> ever observed." The surface was totally obscured. Mariner 9's computer was thus reprogrammed from Earth to delay imaging of the surface for a couple of months until the dust settled. The main surface imaging did not get underway until mid-January 1972. However, surface-obscured images did contribute to the collection of Mars science, including understanding of the existence of several huge high-altitude volcanoes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tharsis_Bulge" title="Tharsis Bulge">Tharsis Bulge</a> that gradually became visible as the dust storm abated. This unexpected situation made a strong case for the desirability of studying a planet from orbit rather than merely flying past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup> It also highlighted the importance of flexible mission software. The Soviet Union's <a href="/wiki/Mars_2" title="Mars 2">Mars 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a> probes, which arrived during the same dust storm, were unable to adapt to the unexpected conditions, which severely limited the amount of data that they were able to collect.</p>
<p>After 349 days in orbit, Mariner 9 had transmitted 7,329 images, covering 85% of Mars' surface, whereas previous flyby missions had returned less than one thousand images covering only a small portion of the planetary surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The images revealed <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> beds, <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">craters</a>, massive extinct <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Olympus_Mons" title="Olympus Mons">Olympus Mons</a>, the largest known volcano in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>; Mariner 9 led directly to its reclassification from Nix Olympica), <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">canyons</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Valles_Marineris" title="Valles Marineris">Valles Marineris</a>, a system of canyons over about 2,500 miles (4,020 km) long), evidence of wind and water <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and deposition, weather fronts, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fogs</a>, and more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Mars' small <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">Deimos</a>, were also photographed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The findings from the Mariner 9 mission underpinned the later <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The enormous <a href="/wiki/Valles_Marineris" title="Valles Marineris">Valles Marineris</a> canyon system is named after Mariner 9 in honor of its achievements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After depleting its supply of attitude control gas, the spacecraft was turned off on October 27, 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a> aboard Mariner 9 was constructed by the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Atmospheric_and_Space_Physics" title="Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics">Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">University of Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder, Colorado</a>. The ultraviolet spectrometer team was led by Professor Charles Barth.</p>
<p>The Infrared Interferometer Spectrometer (IRIS) team was led by Dr. Rudolf A. Hanel from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Goddard_Spaceflight_Center" title="NASA Goddard Spaceflight Center">NASA Goddard Spaceflight Center</a> (GSFC). The IRIS instrument was built by <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas, Texas</a>.</p>
<p>The Infrared Radiometer (IRR) team was led by Professor Gerald Neugebauer from the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> (Caltech).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Error-Correction_Codes_achievements">Error-Correction Codes achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Error-Correction Codes achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To control for errors in the reception of the <a href="/wiki/Grayscale" title="Grayscale">grayscale</a> image data sent by Mariner 9 (caused by a low <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">signal-to-noise ratio</a>), the data had to be encoded before transmission using a so-called <a href="/wiki/Forward_error_correction" title="Forward error correction">error-correcting code</a> (ECC). Without ECC, noise would have made up roughly a quarter of a received image, while the ECC encoded the data in a redundant way which allowed for the reconstruction of the sent image data at reception.</p>
<p>As the flown hardware was constrained with regards to weight, power consumption, storage and computing power, some considerations had to be put into choosing an error-correcting code, and it was decided to use a <a href="/wiki/Hadamard_code" title="Hadamard code">Hadamard code</a> for Mariner 9. Each image pixel was represented as a 6-bit binary value, which had 64 possible <a href="/wiki/Grayscale" title="Grayscale">grayscale</a> levels. Because of limitations of the transmitter, the maximum useful data length was about 30 bits. Instead of using a <a href="/wiki/Repetition_code" title="Repetition code">repetition code</a>, a [32, 6, 16] punctured Hadamard code was used, which is also a 1st-order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reed-Muller_code" title="Reed-Muller code">Reed-Muller code</a>. Errors of up to seven bits per each 32-bit word could be corrected using this scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Compared to a five-repetition code, the error correcting properties of this Hadamard code were much better, yet its rate was comparable. The efficient decoding <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a> was an important factor in the decision to use this code. The circuitry used was called the "Green Machine", which employed the <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">fast Fourier transform</a>, increasing the decoding speed by a factor of three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_location">Present location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Present location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mariner 9 remains a derelict satellite in Mars orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-5">[5]</a></sup> It is expected to remain in orbit until approximately 2022, when the spacecraft is projected to enter the Martian atmosphere and either burn up or crash into the planet's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
