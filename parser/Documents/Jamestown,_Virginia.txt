<p>The <b>Jamestown</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> settlement in the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a> was the first permanent <a href="/wiki/British_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="British colonization of the Americas">English settlement</a> in the Americas. <a href="/wiki/William_Kelso" title="William Kelso">William Kelso</a> writes that Jamestown "is where the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> began".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was established by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Company_of_London" title="Virginia Company of London">Virginia Company of London</a> as "James Fort" on May 4, 1607 (<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a>; May 14, 1607 <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">N.S.</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and was considered permanent after brief abandonment in 1610. It followed several failed attempts, including the <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">Lost Colony of Roanoke</a>. Jamestown served as the capital of the colony of Virginia for 83 years, from 1616 until 1699.</p>
<p>The settlement was located within the country of <a href="/wiki/Tsenacommacah" title="Tsenacommacah">Tsenacommacah</a>, which was ruled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powhatan_Confederacy" title="Powhatan Confederacy">Powhatan Confederacy</a>, and specifically in that of the <a href="/wiki/Paspahegh" title="Paspahegh">Paspahegh</a> tribe. The natives initially welcomed and provided crucial provisions and support for the colonists, who were not agriculturally inclined. Relations soured fairly early on, however, leading to the total annihilation of the Paspahegh in warfare within three years. Mortality was very high at Jamestown itself due to disease and starvation, with over 80-percent of the colonists perishing in 1609–10 in what became known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starving_Time_(Jamestown)" title="Starving Time (Jamestown)">Starving Time</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Company" title="Virginia Company">Virginia Company</a> brought eight <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jamestown_Pioneers_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jamestown_Pioneers-5">[5]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JamestownColony_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-JamestownColony-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> colonists in 1608 in the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_supply_missions" title="Jamestown supply missions">Second Supply</a>, some of whom built a small glass factory—although the Germans and a few others soon defected to the Powhatans with weapons and supplies from the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timeline_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billings35_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billings35-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horn123_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horn123-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horn127_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horn127-10">[10]</a></sup> The Second Supply also brought the first two European women to the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timeline_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billings35_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Billings35-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1619, the first documented Africans came to Jamestown—about 50 men, women, and children aboard a Portuguese slave ship that had been captured in the West Indies and brought to the Jamestown region. They most likely worked in the tobacco fields as indentured servants, but they became slaves as time went on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The modern conception of <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery in the United States</a> was formalized in 1640 (the <a href="/wiki/John_Punch_(slave)" title="John Punch (slave)">John Punch</a> hearing) and was fully entrenched in Virginia by 1660.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The London Company's second settlement in <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> claims to be the site of the oldest <i>town</i> in the English <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, as <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s,_Bermuda" title="St. George's, Bermuda">St. George's, Bermuda</a> was officially established in 1612 as New London, whereas James Fort in Virginia was not converted into James Towne until 1619, and further did not survive to the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1676, Jamestown was deliberately burned during <a href="/wiki/Bacon%27s_Rebellion" title="Bacon's Rebellion">Bacon's Rebellion</a>, though it was quickly rebuilt. In 1699, the capital was relocated from Jamestown to what is today <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Virginia" title="Williamsburg, Virginia">Williamsburg, Virginia</a>, after which Jamestown ceased to exist as a settlement, existing today only as an <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> site.</p>
<p>Today, Jamestown is one of three locations comprising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_Triangle_of_Colonial_Virginia" title="Historic Triangle of Colonial Virginia">Historic Triangle of Colonial Virginia</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Williamsburg" title="Colonial Williamsburg">Williamsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorktown,_Virginia" title="Yorktown, Virginia">Yorktown</a>, with two primary heritage sites. <a href="/wiki/Historic_Jamestowne" title="Historic Jamestowne">Historic Jamestowne</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> is the archaeological site on Jamestown Island and is a cooperative effort by Jamestown National Historic Site (part of <a href="/wiki/Colonial_National_Historical_Park" title="Colonial National Historical Park">Colonial National Historical Park</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Preservation_Virginia" title="Preservation Virginia">Preservation Virginia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Settlement" title="Jamestown Settlement">Jamestown Settlement</a>, a living history interpretive site, is operated by the Jamestown Yorktown Foundation, a state agency of the Commonwealth of Virginia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement">Settlement</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Jamestown,_Virginia_(1607%E2%80%9399)" title="History of Jamestown, Virginia (1607–99)">History of Jamestown, Virginia (1607–99)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">European colonization of the Americas</a></div>
<p>Spain, Portugal, and France moved quickly to establish a presence in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, while other European countries moved more slowly. The English did not attempt to found colonies until many decades after the explorations of <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a>, and early efforts were failures—most notably the <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">Roanoke Colony</a> which vanished about 1590.</p>
<h3><span id="Arrival_and_beginning_(1607–1609)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_and_beginning_.281607.E2.80.931609.29">Arrival and beginning (1607–1609)</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jamestown_Island_(1958_base_map).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2457" data-file-width="3189" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Jamestown_Island_%281958_base_map%29.png/220px-Jamestown_Island_%281958_base_map%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Jamestown_Island_%281958_base_map%29.png/330px-Jamestown_Island_%281958_base_map%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Jamestown_Island_%281958_base_map%29.png/440px-Jamestown_Island_%281958_base_map%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jamestown_Island_(1958_base_map).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Island" title="Jamestown Island">Jamestown Island</a>, showing the terrain and location of the original 1607 fort. (Modern roads, causeway, and buildings not shown)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Late in 1606, English <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_supply_missions#Original_mission" title="Jamestown supply missions">entrepreneurs set sail</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_1606" title="Charter of 1606">charter</a> from the <a href="/wiki/London_Company" title="London Company">London Company</a> to establish a colony in the New World. The fleet consisted of the ships <i><a href="/wiki/Susan_Constant" title="Susan Constant">Susan Constant</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Discovery_(1602_ship)" title="Discovery (1602 ship)">Discovery</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Godspeed_(ship)" title="Godspeed (ship)">Godspeed</a></i>, all under the leadership of Captain <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Newport" title="Christopher Newport">Christopher Newport</a>. They made a particularly long voyage of four months, including a stop in <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and finally departed for the American mainland on April 10, 1607. The expedition made landfall on April 26, 1607 at a place which they named <a href="/wiki/Cape_Henry" title="Cape Henry">Cape Henry</a>. Under orders to select a more secure location, they set about exploring what is now <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> and an outlet to the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> which they named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">James River</a> in honor of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I of England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Maria_Wingfield" title="Edward Maria Wingfield">Captain Edward Maria Wingfield</a> was elected president of the governing council on April 25, 1607. On May 14, he selected a piece of land on a large peninsula some 40 miles (64 km) inland from the Atlantic Ocean as a prime location for a fortified settlement. The river channel was a defensible strategic point due to a curve in the river, and it was close to the land, making it navigable and offering enough land for piers or wharves to be built in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPS_excavation_1958_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPS_excavation_1958-18">[18]</a></sup> Perhaps the most favorable fact about the location was that it was not inhabited by nearby <a href="/wiki/Native_American_tribes_in_Virginia" title="Native American tribes in Virginia">Virginia Indian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> tribes, who regarded the site as too poor and remote for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The island was swampy and isolated, and it offered limited space, was plagued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosquitoes" title="Mosquitoes">mosquitoes</a>, and afforded only brackish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal" title="Tidal">tidal</a> river water unsuitable for drinking.</p>
<p>The Jamestown settlers arrived in Virginia during a severe drought, according to a research study conducted by the Jamestown Archaeological Assessment (JAA) team in the 1990s. The JAA analyzed information from a study conducted in 1985 by David Stahle and others, who obtained borings of 800 year-old baldcypress trees along the Nottoway and Blackwater rivers. The lifespan of these trees is up to 1,000 years and their rings offer a good indication of an area’s annual amount of rainfall. The borings revealed that the worst drought in 700 years occurred between 1606 and 1612. This severe drought affected the Jamestown colonist and Powhatan tribe’s ability to produce food and obtain a safe supply of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_(Robin_Baranowski,_NPS_Photo)_(8426448355).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_%28Robin_Baranowski%2C_NPS_Photo%29_%288426448355%29.jpg/220px-Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_%28Robin_Baranowski%2C_NPS_Photo%29_%288426448355%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_%28Robin_Baranowski%2C_NPS_Photo%29_%288426448355%29.jpg/330px-Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_%28Robin_Baranowski%2C_NPS_Photo%29_%288426448355%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_%28Robin_Baranowski%2C_NPS_Photo%29_%288426448355%29.jpg/440px-Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_%28Robin_Baranowski%2C_NPS_Photo%29_%288426448355%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marsh_-_Colonial_National_Historical_Park_(Robin_Baranowski,_NPS_Photo)_(8426448355).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Salt <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> along Jamestown Island. The ample wetlands on the island proved to be a breeding ground for mosquitoes.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The settlers also arrived too late in the year to get crops planted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-22">[22]</a></sup> Many in the group were either gentlemen unused to work or their manservants, both equally unaccustomed to the hard labor demanded by the harsh task of carving out a viable colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-22">[22]</a></sup> One of these was <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hunt_(chaplain)" title="Robert Hunt (chaplain)">Robert Hunt</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Vicar" title="Vicar">vicar</a> of <a href="/wiki/Reculver" title="Reculver">Reculver</a>, England who celebrated the first known <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a> in the territory of the future United States on June 21, 1607.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a few months, 80% of the party were dead; some of the survivors were deserting to the Indians whose land they had colonized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-22">[22]</a></sup> Virginia Native Americans had established settlements long before the English settlers arrived, and there were an estimated 14,000 in the region who were politically known as <a href="/wiki/Tsenacommacah" title="Tsenacommacah">Tsenacommacah</a> and who spoke an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian language</a>. They were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powhatan_Confederacy" title="Powhatan Confederacy">Powhatan Confederacy</a>, ruled by their paramount chief known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wahunsenacawh" title="Wahunsenacawh">Wahunsenacawh</a> or "Chief Powhatan". Wahunsenacawh initially sought to resettle the English colonists from Jamestown, considered part of <a href="/wiki/Paspahegh" title="Paspahegh">Paspahegh</a> territory, to another location known as <i><a href="/wiki/Capahosic,_Virginia" title="Capahosic, Virginia">Capahosick</a></i> where they would make metal tools for him as members of his Confederacy, but this never transpired.</p>
<p>The first explorers had been welcomed by the Indians with dancing, feasting, and tobacco ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Despite the hospitality of Wahunsenacawh, the presence of the English settlers and perhaps a further expedition up the James River by Captain <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Newport" title="Christopher Newport">Christopher Newport</a> provoked the Paspahegh, Weyanock, and other groups to mount a series of attacks on the fort during period of violence lasting from May 27 to July 14, 1607.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jamestownzuniga.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="537" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Jamestownzuniga.jpg/220px-Jamestownzuniga.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Jamestownzuniga.jpg/330px-Jamestownzuniga.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Jamestownzuniga.jpg/440px-Jamestownzuniga.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jamestownzuniga.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail of the map made by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Z%C3%BA%C3%B1iga_y_de_la_Cueva&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedro de Zúñiga y de la Cueva (page does not exist)">Pedro de Zúñiga</a>, depicting the fort in about 1608</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Two-thirds of the settlers died before ships arrived in 1608 with supplies and <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slovaks" title="Slovaks">Slovak</a> <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">craftsmen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR-JanBogdan-1975_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CR-JanBogdan-1975-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR-JanBogdan-1976_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-CR-JanBogdan-1976-27">[27]</a></sup> who helped to establish the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufactories" title="Manufactories">manufactories</a> in the colony. As a result, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glassware" title="Glassware">glassware</a> became the foremost American products to be exported to Europe at the time. <a href="/wiki/Clapboard_(architecture)" title="Clapboard (architecture)">Clapboard</a> had already been sent back to England beginning with the first returning ship.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Page_445.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="677" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Page_445.JPG/220px-Page_445.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Page_445.JPG/330px-Page_445.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Page_445.JPG/440px-Page_445.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Page_445.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Names of those on the Second Supply - Page 445 (or Page 72)"The Generall Historie of Virginia, New-England, and the Summer Isles", by Capt. John Smith - <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The delivery of supplies in 1608 on the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_supply_missions" title="Jamestown supply missions">First and Second Supply</a> missions of Captain Newport had also added to the number of hungry settlers. It seemed certain at that time that the colony at Jamestown would meet the same fate as earlier English attempts to settle in North America, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">Roanoke Colony</a> (Lost Colony) and the <a href="/wiki/Popham_Colony" title="Popham Colony">Popham Colony</a>, unless there was a major relief effort. The Germans who arrived with the Second Supply and a few others defected to the Powhatans, with weapons and equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horn123_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horn123-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horn127_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horn127-10">[10]</a></sup> The Germans even planned to join a rumored Spanish attack on the colony and urged the Powhatans to join it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Spanish were driven off by the timely arrival in July 1609 of Captain <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Argall" title="Samuel Argall">Samuel Argall</a> in <i>Mary and John</i>, a larger ship than the Spanish reconnaissance ship <i>La Asunción de Cristo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The investors of the Virginia Company of London expected to reap rewards from their speculative investments. With the Second Supply, they expressed their frustrations and made demands upon the leaders of Jamestown in written form. They specifically demanded that the colonists send commodities sufficient to pay the cost of the voyage, a lump of gold, assurance that they had found the South Sea, and one member of the lost <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">Roanoke Colony</a>. It fell to the third president of the Council <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(explorer)" title="John Smith (explorer)">Captain John Smith</a> to deliver a bold and much-needed wake-up call in response to the investors in London, demanding practical laborers and craftsmen who could help make the colony more self-sufficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Starving_Time_and_Third_Supply_(1609–1610)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Starving_Time_and_Third_Supply_.281609.E2.80.931610.29">Starving Time and Third Supply (1609–1610)</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Starving_Time" title="Starving Time">Starving Time</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Supply" title="Third Supply">Third Supply</a></div>
<p>After Smith was forced to return to England due to an explosion which gave him deep burn wounds during a trading expedition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the colony was led by <a href="/wiki/George_Percy" title="George Percy">George Percy</a>, who proved incompetent in negotiating with the native tribes.</p>
<p>There are indications that those in London comprehended and embraced Smith's message. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Supply" title="Third Supply">Third Supply</a> mission of 1609 was to be by far the largest and best equipped. They also had a new purpose-built flagship, <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Venture" title="Sea Venture">Sea Venture</a></i>, constructed, and placed in the most experienced of hands, Christopher Newport.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg/220px-Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg/330px-Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg/440px-Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graveyard_at_Jamestowne_Historic_National_Park_-_Stierch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mass grave at Jamestown discovered by archaeologists, beneath the foundations of one of the later capitol buildings</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On June 2, 1609, <i>Sea Venture</i> set sail from <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> as the flagship of a seven-ship fleet (towing two additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_rigged_pinnace" title="Full rigged pinnace">pinnaces</a>) destined for Jamestown, Virginia as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Supply" title="Third Supply">Third Supply</a>, carrying 214 settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apva.org_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-apva.org-33">[33]</a></sup> On July 24, the fleet ran into a strong storm, likely a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a>, and the ships were separated. Although some of the ships did make it to Jamestown, the leaders, and most of the supplies had been aboard <i>Sea Venture</i>, which fought the storm for three days before Admiral of the Company, Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Somers" title="George Somers">George Somers</a>, deliberately drove her onto the reefs of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> to prevent her foundering. This allowed all aboard to be landed safely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The survivors (including Lieutenant-General <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gates_(governor)" title="Thomas Gates (governor)">Sir Thomas Gates</a>, Captain <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Newport" title="Christopher Newport">Christopher Newport</a>, Sylvester Jordain, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_Hopkins_(settler)" title="Stephen Hopkins (settler)">Stephen Hopkins</a>, later of <i><a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a></i>, and secretary <a href="/wiki/William_Strachey" title="William Strachey">William Strachey</a>) were stranded on Bermuda for approximately nine months. During that time, they built two new ships, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_rigged_pinnace" title="Full rigged pinnace">pinnaces</a> <i>Deliverance</i> and <i>Patience</i>. The original plan was to build only one vessel, <i>Deliverance</i>, but it soon became evident that she would not be large enough to carry the settlers and all of the food (salted pork) that was being sourced on the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the Third Supply was stranded in Bermuda, the colony at Jamestown was in even worse shape. In the "<a href="/wiki/Starving_Time" title="Starving Time">Starving Time</a>" of 1609–1610, the Jamestown settlers faced rampant starvation for want of additional provisions. During this time, lack of food drove people to eat snakes and even boil the leather from shoes for sustenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Only 60 of the original 214 settlers at Jamestown survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apva.org_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-apva.org-33">[33]</a></sup> There is scientific evidence that the settlers at Jamestown had turned to <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism" title="Cannibalism">cannibalism</a> during the starving time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ships from Bermuda arrived in Jamestown on 23 May 1610.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Many of the surviving colonists were near death, and Jamestown was judged to be unviable. Everyone was boarded onto <i>Deliverance</i> and <i>Patience</i>, which set sail for England.</p>
<p>However, on June 10, 1610, the timely <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_supply_missions#.22Fourth.22_supply_mission" title="Jamestown supply missions">arrival of another relief fleet</a>, bearing Governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_West,_3rd_Baron_De_La_Warr" title="Thomas West, 3rd Baron De La Warr">Baron De La Warr</a> (who would eventually give his name to the colony of <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>), which met the two ships as they descended the James River, granted Jamestown a reprieve. The Colonists called this <i>The Day of Providence</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_supply_missions" title="Jamestown supply missions">fleet brought not only supplies</a>, but also additional settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> All the settlers returned to the colony, though there was still a critical shortage of food.</p>
<p>Relations between the colonists and the Powhatans quickly deteriorated after De La Warr's arrival, eventually leading to conflict. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Powhatan_War" title="Anglo-Powhatan War">Anglo-Powhatan War</a> lasted until <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Argall" title="Samuel Argall">Samuel Argall</a> captured Wahunsenacawh's daughter Matoaka, better known by her nickname <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas" title="Pocahontas">Pocahontas</a>, after which the chief accepted a treaty of peace.</p>
<h3><span id="Rising_fortunes_(1610–1624)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rising_fortunes_.281610.E2.80.931624.29">Rising fortunes (1610–1624)</span></h3>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocratic</a> backgrounds of many of the new colonists, a historic drought and the communal nature of their work load, progress through the first few years was inconsistent at best. By 1613, six years after Jamestown's founding, the organizers and shareholders of the Virginia Company were desperate to increase the efficiency and profitability of the struggling colony. Without stockholder consent the Governor, Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dale" title="Thomas Dale">Thomas Dale</a>, assigned 3-acre (12,000 m<sup>2</sup>) plots to its "<a href="/wiki/Ancient_planter" title="Ancient planter">ancient planters</a>" and smaller plots to the "settlement's" later arrivals. Measurable economic progress was made, and the settlers began expanding their planting to land belonging to local native tribes. That this turnaround coincided with the end of a drought that had begun the year before the English settlers' arrival probably indicates multiple factors were involved besides the colonists' ineptitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahle_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stahle-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the colonists who survived the Third Supply was <a href="/wiki/John_Rolfe" title="John Rolfe">John Rolfe</a>, who carried with him a cache of untested new <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> seeds from Bermuda, which had grown wild there after being planted by shipwrecked Spaniards years before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1614, Rolfe began to successfully harvest tobacco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Prosperous and wealthy, he married <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas" title="Pocahontas">Pocahontas</a>, daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Powhatan" title="Chief Powhatan">Chief Powhatan</a>, bringing several years of peace between the English and natives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> However, at the end of a public relations trip to England, Pocahontas became sick and died on March 21, 1617.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The following year, her father also died. Powhatan's brother, a fierce warrior named <a href="/wiki/Opchanacanough" title="Opchanacanough">Opchanacanough</a>, became head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powhatan_Confederacy" title="Powhatan Confederacy">Powhatan Confederacy</a>. As the English continued to appropriate more land for tobacco farming, relations with the natives worsened.</p>
<p>Due to the high cost of the trans-atlantic voyage at this time, many English settlers came to Jamestown as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_servant" title="Indentured servant">indentured servants</a>: in exchange for the passage, room, board, and the promise of land or money, these immigrants would agree to work for three to seven years. Immigrants from continental Europe, mainly Germans, were usually <a href="/wiki/Redemptioner" title="Redemptioner">redemptioners</a>—they purchased some portion of their voyage on credit and, upon arrival, borrowed or entered into a work contract to pay the remainder of their voyage costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Along with European indentured servants, around 20 African slaves arrived in Jamestown in 1619. These slaves were captives taken from a ship headed for Mexico. Though these Africans started in Jamestown as slaves, some were able to obtain the status of indentured servant later in life.</p>
<p>In 1619, the first representative assembly in America convened in the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Church" title="Jamestown Church">Jamestown Church</a>, "to establish one equal and uniform government over all Virginia" which would provide "just laws for the happy guiding and governing of the people there inhabiting." This became known as the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Burgesses" title="House of Burgesses">House of Burgesses</a> (forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a>). Initially, only men of English origin were permitted to vote. On June 30, 1619, in what was the <a href="/wiki/1619_Jamestown_Polish_craftsmen_strike" title="1619 Jamestown Polish craftsmen strike">first recorded strike in Colonial America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Polish_craftsmen" title="Jamestown Polish craftsmen">Polish artisans</a> protested and refused to work if not allowed to vote ("<a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">No Vote, No Work</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSO-2007_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSO-2007-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DB-2002_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-DB-2002-52">[52]</a></sup> On July 21, 1619, the court granted the Poles and Slovaks equal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voting_rights" title="Voting rights">voting rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JamestownMarker_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-JamestownMarker-53">[53]</a></sup> Afterwards, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_strike" title="Labor strike">labor strike</a> (the first "in American history"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JamestownColony_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-JamestownColony-6">[6]</a></sup>) was ended and the <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a> resumed their work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DB-2002_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-DB-2002-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JamestownStrike_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-JamestownStrike-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSmith-1624_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSmith-1624-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTS-1911_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTS-1911-56">[56]</a></sup> Individual land ownership was also instituted, and the colony was divided into four large "boroughs" or "incorporations" called "citties" by the colonists. Jamestown was located in <a href="/wiki/James_City_(Virginia_Company)" title="James City (Virginia Company)">James Cittie</a>.</p>
<p>After several years of strained coexistence, Chief Opchanacanough and his Powhatan Confederacy attempted to eliminate the English colony once and for all. On the morning of March 22, 1622, they attacked outlying plantations and communities up and down the James River in what became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Massacre_of_1622" title="Indian Massacre of 1622">Indian Massacre of 1622</a>. More than 300 settlers were killed in the attack, about a third of the colony's English-speaking population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahle_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stahle-44">[44]</a></sup> Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dale" title="Thomas Dale">Thomas Dale</a>'s development at <a href="/wiki/Henricus" title="Henricus">Henricus</a>, which was to feature a <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> to educate the natives, and <a href="/wiki/Wolstenholme_Towne" title="Wolstenholme Towne">Wolstenholme Towne</a> at <a href="/wiki/Martin%27s_Hundred" title="Martin's Hundred">Martin's Hundred</a>, were both essentially wiped out. Jamestown was spared only through a timely warning by a Virginia Indian employee. There was not enough time to spread the word to the outposts.</p>
<p>Of the 6,000 people who came to the settlement between 1608 and 1624, only 3,400 survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahle_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stahle-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Later_years_(1624–1699)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years_.281624.E2.80.931699.29">Later years (1624–1699)</span></h3>
<p>In 1624, King James revoked the Virginia Company's charter, and Virginia became a royal colony. Despite the setbacks, the colony continued to grow. Ten years later, in 1634, by order of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a>, the colony was divided into the original eight <a href="/wiki/Shires_of_Virginia" title="Shires of Virginia">shires of Virginia</a> (or <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a>), in a fashion similar to that practiced in England. Jamestown was now located in <a href="/wiki/James_City_Shire" title="James City Shire">James City Shire</a>, soon renamed the "County of James City", better known in modern times as <a href="/wiki/James_City_County,_Virginia" title="James City County, Virginia">James City County, Virginia</a>, the nation's oldest county.</p>
<p>Another large-scale "Indian attack" occurred in 1644. In 1646, Opchanacanough was captured and while in custody an English guard shot him in the back—against orders—and killed him. Subsequently, the Powhatan Confederacy began to decline. Opechancanough's successor signed the first peace treaties between the Powhatan Indians and the English. The treaties required the Powhatan to pay yearly tribute payment to the English and confined them to reservations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>A generation later, during <a href="/wiki/Bacon%27s_Rebellion" title="Bacon's Rebellion">Bacon's Rebellion</a> in 1676, Jamestown was burned, eventually to be rebuilt. During its recovery, the Virginia legislature met first at Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Berkeley_(governor)" title="William Berkeley (governor)">William Berkeley</a>'s nearby <a href="/wiki/Green_Spring_Plantation" title="Green Spring Plantation">Green Spring Plantation</a>, and later at <a href="/wiki/Middle_Plantation_(Virginia)" title="Middle Plantation (Virginia)">Middle Plantation</a>, which had been started in 1632 as a fortified community inland on the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Peninsula" title="Virginia Peninsula">Virginia Peninsula</a>, about 8 miles (13 km) distant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the statehouse burned again in 1698, this time accidentally, the legislature again temporarily relocated to Middle Plantation, and was able to meet in the new facilities of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/College_of_William_and_Mary" title="College of William and Mary">College of William and Mary</a>, which had been established after receiving a royal charter in 1693. Rather than rebuilding at Jamestown again, the capital of the colony was moved permanently to Middle Plantation in 1699. The town was soon renamed <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Virginia" title="Williamsburg, Virginia">Williamsburg</a>, to honor the reigning monarch, <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">King William III</a>. A new Capitol building and "Governor's Palace" were erected there in the following years. This was a revolutionary change.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_and_preservation">Aftermath and preservation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="712" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG/220px-Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG/330px-Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG/440px-Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jamestown_Virginia_ruin.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1854 image of the ruins of Jamestown showing the tower of the old <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Church" title="Jamestown Church">Jamestown Church</a> built in the 17th century</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to the movement of the capital to Williamsburg, the old town of Jamestown began to slowly disappear from view. Those who lived in the general area attended services at Jamestown's church until the 1750s, when it was abandoned. By the mid-18th century, the land was heavily cultivated, primarily by the Travis and Ambler families.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, although the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Green_Spring" title="Battle of Green Spring">Battle of Green Spring</a> was fought nearby at the site of former Governor Berkeley's plantation, Jamestown was apparently inconsequential. In 1831, David Bullock purchased Jamestown from Travis and Ambler families.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Civil_War">American Civil War</span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, in 1861, <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_Army" title="Confederate States Army">Confederate</a> <a href="/wiki/William_(Orgain)_Allen" title="William (Orgain) Allen">William Allen</a>, who owned the Jamestown Island, occupied Jamestown with troops he raised at his own expense with the intention of blockading the James River and <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a> from the Union Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> He was soon joined by Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Catesby_ap_Roger_Jones" title="Catesby ap Roger Jones">Catesby ap Roger Jones</a>, who directed the building of batteries and conducted ordnance and armor tests for the first Confederate <a href="/wiki/Ironclad_warship" title="Ironclad warship">ironclad warship</a>, <a href="/wiki/CSS_Virginia" title="CSS Virginia">CSS <i>Virginia</i></a>, which was under construction at the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Naval_Shipyard" title="Norfolk Naval Shipyard">Gosport Naval Shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">Portsmouth</a> in late 1861 and early 1862.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> Jamestown had a peak force of 1,200 men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Peninsula_Campaign" title="Peninsula Campaign">Peninsula Campaign</a>, which began later that spring, Union forces under General <a href="/wiki/George_B._McClellan" title="George B. McClellan">George B. McClellan</a> moved up the Peninsula from Fort Monroe in an attempt to capture the Confederate capital of Richmond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> The Union forces captured <a href="/wiki/Yorktown,_Virginia" title="Yorktown, Virginia">Yorktown</a> in April 1862, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Williamsburg" title="Battle of Williamsburg">Battle of Williamsburg</a> was fought the following month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> With these developments, Jamestown and the lower James River were abandoned by the Confederates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-5"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> Some of the forces from Jamestown, and the crew of <i>Virginia</i>, relocated to <a href="/wiki/Drewry%27s_Bluff" title="Drewry's Bluff">Drewry's Bluff</a>, a fortified and strategic position high above the river about 8 miles (13 km) below Richmond. There, they successfully blocked the Union Navy from reaching the Confederate capital.</p>
<p>Once in Federal hands, Jamestown became a meeting place for runaway slaves, who burned the Ambler house, an eighteenth-century plantation house, which along with the old church was one of the few remaining signs of old Jamestown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-6"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> When Allen sent men to assess the damage in late 1862, they were killed by the former slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-7"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup> Following the Confederate surrender at <a href="/wiki/Appomattox_Court_House_National_Historical_Park" title="Appomattox Court House National Historical Park">Appomattox Courthouse</a>, the oath of allegiance was administered to former Confederate soldiers at Jamestown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Civil_War_59-8"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Civil_War-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation_and_early_archaeology">Preservation and early archaeology</span></h3>

<p>In the years after the Civil War, Jamestown became quiet and peaceful once again. In 1892, Jamestown was purchased by Mr. and Mrs. Edward Barney. The following year, the Barneys donated 22½ acres of land, including the ruined church tower, to the Association for the Preservation of Virginia Antiquities (now known as <a href="/wiki/Preservation_Virginia" title="Preservation Virginia">Preservation Virginia</a>).</p>
<p>By this time, erosion from the river had eaten away the island's western shore. Visitors began to conclude that the site of James Fort lay completely underwater. With federal assistance, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_wall" title="Sea wall">sea wall</a> was constructed in 1900 to protect the area from further erosion. The archaeological remains of the original 1607 fort, which had been protected by the sea wall, were discovered in 1994.</p>
<p>In 1932, <a href="/wiki/George_C._Gregory" title="George C. Gregory">George Craghead Gregory</a> of Richmond was credited with discovering the foundation of the first brick statehouse (capitol) building, circa 1646, at Jamestown on the land owned by Preservation Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Around 1936, Gregory, who was active with the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Historical_Society" title="Virginia Historical Society">Virginia Historical Society</a>, founded the <a href="/wiki/Jamestowne_Society" title="Jamestowne Society">Jamestowne Society</a> for descendants of stockholders in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Company_of_London" title="Virginia Company of London">Virginia Company of London</a> and the descendants of those who owned land or who had domiciles in Jamestown or on Jamestown Island prior to 1700.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colonial National Monument was authorized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> on July 3, 1930 and established on December 30, 1930. In 1934, the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> obtained the remaining 1,500 acre (6.1 km²) portion of Jamestown Island which had been under private ownership by the Vermillion family. The National Park Service partnered with Preservation Virginia to preserve the area and present it to visitors in an educational manner. On June 5, 1936, the national monument was re-designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_historical_park" title="National historical park">national historical park</a>, and became known as <a href="/wiki/Colonial_National_Historical_Park" title="Colonial National Historical Park">Colonial National Historical Park</a>.</p>
<p>From 1936 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.C._Harrington" title="J.C. Harrington">J.C. “Pinky” Harrington</a> worked on the NPS's excavations at Jamestown. In 1954 <a href="/wiki/John_L._Cotter" title="John L. Cotter">John L. Cotter</a> took charge of field projects at Jamestown, conducted with the site's 350th anniversary (1957) in mind. Cotter worked with <a href="/wiki/Edward_B._Jelks" title="Edward B. Jelks">Edward B. Jelks</a> and Harrington to survey the area's colonial sites. In 1957 Cotter and J. Paul Hudson co-authored <a class="external text" href="http://www.gutenberg.org/files/16277/16277-h/16277-h.htm" rel="nofollow"><i>New Discoveries at Jamestown</i></a>. Cotter contributed, along with Jelks, Georg Neumann, and Johnny Hack, to the 1958 report <i>Archaeological Excavations at Jamestown</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span></h2>
<p>In the present time, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_National_Historical_Park" title="Colonial National Historical Park">Colonial National Historical Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Island" title="Jamestown Island">Jamestown Island</a> area is home to two heritage tourism sites related to the original fort and town. Nearby, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamestown-Scotland_Ferry" title="Jamestown-Scotland Ferry">Jamestown-Scotland Ferry</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> service provides a link across the navigable portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">James River</a> for vehicles and affords passengers a view of <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Island" title="Jamestown Island">Jamestown Island</a> from the river.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_Jamestowne">Historic Jamestowne</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Historic_Jamestowne" title="Historic Jamestowne">Historic Jamestowne</a></div>
<p>Historic Jamestowne, located at the original site of Jamestown, is administered by Preservation Virginia and the National Park Service. The central 22½ acres of land, where the archaeological remains of the original James Fort were found, are owned by <a href="/wiki/Preservation_Virginia" title="Preservation Virginia">Preservation Virginia</a> (formerly known as the Association for the Preservation of Virginia Antiquities); the remaining 1,500 acres (6.1 km<sup>2</sup>) are held by the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_National_Historical_Park" title="Colonial National Historical Park">Colonial National Historical Park</a>.</p>
<p>The site gained renewed importance when in 1996 the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Rediscovery" title="Jamestown Rediscovery">Jamestown Rediscovery</a> project began excavations in search of the original James Fort site, originally in preparation for the quadricentennial of Jamestown's founding. The primary goal of the archaeological campaign was to locate archaeological remains of "the first years of settlement at Jamestown, especially of the earliest fortified town; [and the] the subsequent growth and development of the town".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, visitors to Historic Jamestowne can view the site of the original 1607 James Fort, the 17th-century church tower and the site of the 17th-century town, as well as tour an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeology" title="Archeology">archaeological</a> museum called the Archaearium and view many of the close to two million artifacts found by Jamestown Rediscovery. They also may participate in <a href="/wiki/Living_history" title="Living history">living history</a> ranger tours and Archaeological tours given by the Jamestown Rediscovery staff. Visitors can also often observe archaeologists from the Jamestown Rediscovery Project at work, as archaeological work at the site continues. As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jamestown,_Virginia&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the archaeological work and studies are ongoing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In addition to their newsletter and <a class="external text" href="http://www.historicjamestowne.org" rel="nofollow">website</a>, new discoveries are frequently reported in the local newspaper, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Gazette" title="Virginia Gazette">Virginia Gazette</a></i> based in nearby Williamsburg, and by other news media, often worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamestown_Settlement">Jamestown Settlement</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Settlement" title="Jamestown Settlement">Jamestown Settlement</a></div>
<p>Jamestown Settlement is a living-history park and museum located 1.25 miles (2.01 km) from the original location of the colony and adjacent to Jamestown Island. Initially created for the celebration of the 350th anniversary in 1957, Jamestown Settlement is operated by the Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation, and largely sponsored by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Virginia" title="Commonwealth of Virginia">Commonwealth of Virginia</a>. The museum complex features a reconstruction of a Powhatan village, the James Fort as it was c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1610</span>–1614, and replicas of the three ships that brought the first settlers, <i><a href="/wiki/Susan_Constant" title="Susan Constant">Susan Constant</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Godspeed_(ship)" title="Godspeed (ship)">Godspeed</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Discovery_(1602_ship)" title="Discovery (1602 ship)">Discovery</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemorations">Commemorations</span></h2>
<p>With the national independence of the United States established by the end of the 18th century, Jamestown came to be looked at as a starting point. Its founding in 1607 has been regularly commemorated, with the most notable events being held every fifty years.</p>
<h3><span id="200th_anniversary_(1807)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="200th_anniversary_.281807.29">200th anniversary (1807)</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bicentennial" title="wikt:bicentennial">bicentennial</a> of Jamestown on May 13–14, 1807, was called the Grand National Jubilee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> Over 3,000 people attended the event, many arriving on vessels which anchored in the river off the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>May 13 was the opening day of the festival, which began with a procession which marched to the graveyard of the old church, where the attending bishop delivered the prayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> The procession then moved to the Travis mansion, where the celebrants dined and danced in the mansion that evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-3"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> Also during the festivities, students of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/College_of_William_and_Mary" title="College of William and Mary">College of William and Mary</a> gave orations. An old barn on the island was used as a temporary theater, where a company of players from Norfolk performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-4"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> Attending were many dignitaries, politicians, and historians. The celebration concluded on May 14 with a dinner and toast at the <a href="/wiki/Raleigh_Tavern" title="Raleigh Tavern">Raleigh Tavern</a> in Williamsburg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-5"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="250th_anniversary_(1857)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="250th_anniversary_.281857.29">250th anniversary (1857)</span></h3>
<p>In 1857, the Jamestown society organized a celebration marking the 250th anniversary of Jamestown's founding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-6"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> According to the Richmond <i>Enquirer</i>, the site for the celebration was on 10 acres (40,000 m<sup>2</sup>) on the spot where some of the colonists' houses were originally built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-7"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> However, it is also speculated that the celebration was moved further east on the island closer to the Travis grave site, in order to avoid damaging Major <a href="/wiki/William_(Orgain)_Allen" title="William (Orgain) Allen">William Allen</a>'s corn fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-8"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The attendance was estimated at between 6,000 and 8,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-9"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> Sixteen large steam ships anchored offshore in the James River and were gaily decorated with streamers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-10"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> Former US President <a href="/wiki/John_Tyler" title="John Tyler">John Tyler</a> of nearby <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Forest_Plantation" title="Sherwood Forest Plantation">Sherwood Forest Plantation</a> gave a 2½ hour speech, and there were military displays, a grand ball and fireworks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-11"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="300th_anniversary_(1907):_Jamestown_Exposition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="300th_anniversary_.281907.29:_Jamestown_Exposition">300th anniversary (1907): Jamestown Exposition</span></h3>

<p>The 100th anniversary of the Surrender at Yorktown in 1781 had generated a new interest in the historical significance of the colonial sites of the Peninsula. Williamsburg, a sleepy but populated town of shops and homes, was still celebrating Civil War events. However, as the new century dawned, thoughts turned to the upcoming 300th anniversary of the founding of Jamestown. The Association for the Preservation of Virginia Antiquities (now known as <a href="/wiki/Preservation_Virginia" title="Preservation Virginia">Preservation Virginia</a>) started the movement in 1900 by calling for a celebration honoring the establishment of the first permanent English colony in the New World at Jamestown to be held on the 300th anniversary in 1907.</p>
<p>As a celebration was planned, virtually no one thought that the actual isolated and long-abandoned original site of Jamestown would be suitable for a major event because Jamestown Island had no facilities for large crowds. The original fort housing the Jamestown settlers was believed to have been long ago swallowed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">James River</a>. The general area in James City County near Jamestown was also considered unsuitable, as it was not very accessible in the day of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">rail travel</a> before automobiles were common.</p>
<p>As the tricentennial of the 1607 Founding of the Jamestown neared, around 1904, despite an assumption in some quarters that Richmond would be a logical location, leaders in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a> began a campaign to have a celebration held there. The decision was made to locate the international exposition on a mile-long frontage at <a href="/wiki/Sewell%27s_Point" title="Sewell's Point">Sewell's Point</a> near the mouth of <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a>. This was about 30 miles (48 km) downstream from Jamestown in a rural section of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_County,_Virginia" title="Norfolk County, Virginia">Norfolk County</a>. It was a site which could become accessible by both long-distance passenger railroads and local streetcar service, with considerable frontage on the harbor of Hampton Roads. This latter feature proved ideal for the naval delegations which came from points all around the world.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Exposition" title="Jamestown Exposition">Jamestown Exposition</a> of 1907 was one of the many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_fairs" title="World's fairs">world's fairs</a> and expositions that were popular in the early part of the 20th century. Held from April 26, 1907 to December 1, 1907, attendees included US President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_II" title="Kaiser Wilhelm II">Kaiser Wilhelm II</a> of Germany, the Prince of Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_H._Rogers" title="Henry H. Rogers">Henry H. Rogers</a>, and dozens of other dignitaries and famous persons. A major naval review featuring the United States' <a href="/wiki/Great_White_Fleet" title="Great White Fleet">Great White Fleet</a> was a key feature. U.S. Military officials and leaders were impressed by the location, and the Exposition site later formed the first portion of the large U.S. <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Station Norfolk</a> in 1918 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="350th_Anniversary_(1957):_Jamestown_Festival"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="350th_Anniversary_.281957.29:_Jamestown_Festival">350th Anniversary (1957): Jamestown Festival</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:123px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Queen_elizabeth_and_prince_phillip.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="121" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/63/Queen_elizabeth_and_prince_phillip.gif" width="121"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Queen_elizabeth_and_prince_phillip.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> of the United Kingdom and her consort <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Philip" title="Prince Philip">Prince Philip</a> inspect the replica of <i>Susan Constant</i> at Jamestown Festival Park in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> on October 16, 1957</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With America's increased access to automobiles, and with improved roads and transportation, it was feasible for the 350th anniversary celebration to be held at Jamestown itself in 1957. Although erosion had cut off the land bridge between Jamestown Island and the mainland, the isthmus was restored and new access provided by the completion of the National Park Service's <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Parkway" title="Colonial Parkway">Colonial Parkway</a> which led to Williamsburg and <a href="/wiki/Yorktown,_Virginia" title="Yorktown, Virginia">Yorktown</a>, the other two portions of Colonial Virginia's <a href="/wiki/Historic_Triangle" title="Historic Triangle">Historic Triangle</a>. There were also improvements of state highways. The north landing for the popular <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Ferry" title="Jamestown Ferry">Jamestown Ferry</a> and a portion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Route_31_(Virginia)" title="State Route 31 (Virginia)">State Route 31</a> were relocated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-12"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Major projects such were developed by non-profit, state and federal agencies. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamestown_Festival_Park" title="Jamestown Festival Park">Jamestown Festival Park</a> was established by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Virginia" title="Commonwealth of Virginia">Commonwealth of Virginia</a> adjacent to the entrance to Jamestown Island. Full-sized replicas of the three ships that brought the colonists, <i><a href="/wiki/Susan_Constant" title="Susan Constant">Susan Constant</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Godspeed_(ship)" title="Godspeed (ship)">Godspeed</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Discovery_(1602_ship)" title="Discovery (1602 ship)">Discovery</a></i> were constructed at a shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and placed on display at a new dock at Jamestown, where the largest, <i>Susan Constant</i>, could be boarded by visitors. On Jamestown Island, the reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Glasshouse" title="Jamestown Glasshouse">Jamestown Glasshouse</a>, the Memorial Cross and the visitors center were completed and dedicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-13"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> A loop road was built around the island.</p>
<p>Special events included army and navy reviews, air force fly-overs, ship and aircraft christenings and even an outdoor drama at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Henry,_Virginia" title="Cape Henry, Virginia">Cape Henry</a>, site of the first landing of the settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-14"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> This celebration continued from April 1 to November 30 with over a million participants, including dignitaries and politicians such as the British Ambassador and U.S. Vice President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-15"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> The highlight for many of the nearly 25,000 at the Festival Park on October 16, 1957 was the visit and speech of Queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom</a> and her consort, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Philip" title="Prince Philip">Prince Philip</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology_67-16"><a href="#cite_note-COLO_NPS_Commemorations_Chronology-67">[67]</a></sup> Queen Elizabeth II loaned a copy of the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> for the exhibition. It was her first visit to the United States since assuming the throne.</p>
<p>The 1957 Jamestown Festival was so successful that tourists still kept coming long after the official event was completed. Jamestown became a permanent attraction of the <a href="/wiki/Historic_Triangle" title="Historic Triangle">Historic Triangle</a>, and has been visited by families, school groups, tours, and thousands of other people continuously ever since.</p>

<p>Early in the 21st century, new accommodations, transportation facilities and attractions were planned in preparation for the quadricentennial of the founding of Jamestown. Numerous events were promoted under the banner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/America%27s_400th_Anniversary" title="America's 400th Anniversary">America's 400th Anniversary</a> and promoted by the Jamestown 2007 Commission. The commemoration included 18 months of statewide, national and international festivities and events, which began in April 2006 with a tour of the new replica <i>Godspeed</i>.</p>
<p>In January 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a> held a session at Jamestown. On May 4, 2007, Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> of the United Kingdom and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip</a> attended a ceremony commemorating the 400th anniversary of the settlement's arrivals, reprising the honor they paid in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the Virginia State Quarter, Jamestown was also the subject of two <a href="/wiki/United_States_commemorative_coin" title="United States commemorative coin">United States commemorative coins</a> celebrating the 400th anniversary of its settlement. A <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_400th_Anniversary_silver_dollar" title="Jamestown 400th Anniversary silver dollar">silver dollar</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_400th_Anniversary_gold_five_dollar_coin" title="Jamestown 400th Anniversary gold five dollar coin">gold five dollar coin</a> were issued in 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2019_Commemoration">2019 Commemoration</span></h3>
<p>In 2019 Jamestown, in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Virginia" title="Williamsburg, Virginia">Williamsburg</a>, will hold a commemoration that marks the 400th anniversary of three landmark events in American history: the first meeting of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Burgesses" title="House of Burgesses">Virginia House of Burgesses</a>, the arrival of the first Africans to English North America, and the first Thanksgiving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamestown_in_fiction">Jamestown in fiction</span></h2>
<p>A fictional romantic adventure set at Jamestown, <i><a href="/wiki/To_Have_and_to_Hold" title="To Have and to Hold">To Have and to Hold</a></i>, was the <a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly_list_of_bestselling_novels_in_the_United_States_in_the_1900s" title="Publishers Weekly list of bestselling novels in the United States in the 1900s">bestselling novel in the United States in 1900</a>. The novel was later adapted into two feature films, in <a href="/wiki/To_Have_and_to_Hold_(1916_film)" title="To Have and to Hold (1916 film)">1916</a> and <a href="/wiki/To_Have_and_to_Hold_(1922_film)" title="To Have and to Hold (1922 film)">1922</a>.</p>
<p>A feature-length film, <a href="/wiki/The_New_World_(2005_film)" title="The New World (2005 film)"><i>The New World</i></a> (2005), directed by <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Malick" title="Terrence Malick">Terrence Malick</a>, covers the story of Jamestown's colonization. Although the historical details are accurate in most ways, the plot focuses on a dramatized relationship between John Smith, played by <a href="/wiki/Colin_Farrell" title="Colin Farrell">Colin Farrell</a>, and Pocahontas, played by <a href="/wiki/Q%27orianka_Kilcher" title="Q'orianka Kilcher">Q'orianka Kilcher</a>. It also features John Rolfe, played by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">Christian Bale</a>. Many scenes were filmed on-location along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">James</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chickahominy_River" title="Chickahominy River">Chickahominy Rivers</a> and at Henricus Historical Park in <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield_County,_Virginia" title="Chesterfield County, Virginia">Chesterfield County</a>, Virginia.</p>
<p>Another feature-length film, <i>First Landing: The Voyage from England to Jamestown</i> (2007), documents the 1607 landing of English colonists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>A highly fictionalized version of the Jamestown settlement is depicted in the animated <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(1995_film)" title="Pocahontas (1995 film)">Pocahontas</a></i> (1995) as well as its direct-to-video sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_II:_Journey_to_a_New_World" title="Pocahontas II: Journey to a New World">Pocahontas II: Journey to a New World</a></i> (1998). Among other inaccuracies it is shown as being near mountains, when it was actually located on the <a href="/wiki/Tidewater_region" title="Tidewater region">Tidewater region</a>.</p>
<p>In 2017, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_1" title="Sky 1">Sky 1</a> launched a new series based in Jamestown. <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_(TV_series)" title="Jamestown (TV series)">The series</a>, named after its eponymous setting, revolves around the societal change triggered by the arrival of women to the settlement to marry the male citizens of the area, and is made by the producers of <a href="/wiki/Downton_Abbey" title="Downton Abbey">Downton Abbey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
