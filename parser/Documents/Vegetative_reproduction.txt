<p><b>Vegetative reproduction</b> (<b>vegetative propagation</b>, <b>vegetative multiplication</b>, <b>vegetative cloning</b>) is any form of asexual reproduction occurring in plants in which a new plant grows from a fragment of the parent plant or grows from a specialized reproductive structure (such as a stolon, rhizome, tuber, corm, or bulb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It can occur naturally or be induced by <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulturists</a>.</p>
<p>Although most plants normally reproduce sexually, many have the ability for vegetative propagation, or can be vegetatively propagated if small pieces are subjected to chemical (hormonal) treatments. This is because <a href="/wiki/Meristem" title="Meristem">meristematic cells</a> capable of <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">cellular differentiation</a> are present in many plant tissues. Horticulturalists are interested in understanding how meristematic cells can be induced to reproduce an entire plant.</p>
<p>Success rates and difficulty of propagation vary greatly. For example, <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coleus" title="Coleus">coleus</a> can be propagated merely by inserting a stem in water or moist soil. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Monocotyledon" title="Monocotyledon">monocotyledons</a>, unlike <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicotyledons</a>, typically lack a <a href="/wiki/Vascular_cambium" title="Vascular cambium">vascular cambium</a> and therefore are harder to propagate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vegetative_reproduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a wide sense, methods of vegetative propagation include cutting, vegetative apomixis, layering, division, budding, grafting and tissue culture. Cutting is the most common artificial vegetative propagation method, where pieces of the "parent" plant are removed and placed in a suitable environment so that they can grow into a whole new plant, the "clone", which is genetically identical to the parent. Cutting exploits the ability of plants to grow adventitious roots (i.e. root material that can generate from a location other than the existing or primary root system, as in from a leaf or cut stem) under certain conditions. Vegetative propagation is usually considered a cloning method. However, there are several cases where vegetatively propagated plants are not genetically identical. Root cuttings of thornless blackberries will revert to thorny type because the adventitious shoot develops from a cell that is genetically thorny. Thornless blackberry is a chimera, with the epidermal layers genetically thornless but the tissue beneath it genetically thorny.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Similarly, leaf cutting propagation of certain chimeral variegated plants, such as snake plant (Sansevieria trifasciata), will produce mainly nonvariegated plants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Grafting is often not a complete cloning method because seedlings are used as rootstocks. In that case only the top of the plant is clonal. In some crops, particularly apples, the rootstocks are vegetatively propagated so the entire graft can be clonal if the scion and rootstock are both clones. <a href="/wiki/Apomixis" title="Apomixis">Apomixis</a> (including apospory and diplospory) is a type of reproduction that does not involve fertilisation. In flowering plants, unfertilized seeds are involved, or plantlets that grow instead of flowers. Hawkweed (<i><a href="/wiki/Hieracium" title="Hieracium">Hieracium</a></i>), dandelion (<i><a href="/wiki/Taraxacum" title="Taraxacum">Taraxacum</a></i>), some citrus (<i><a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">Citrus</a></i>) and many grasses such as Kentucky blue grass (<i><a href="/wiki/Poa_pratensis" title="Poa pratensis">Poa pratensis</a></i>) all use this form of asexual reproduction. Bulbils are sometimes formed instead of the flowers of garlic.budding ,fragmentation ,vegetative propagation ,spore formation and budding are all types of asexual reproduction we have learnt vegitative propagation types in this topic</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetative_structures">Vegetative structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vegetative_reproduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vegetative structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Virtually all types of <a href="/wiki/Shoot" title="Shoot">shoots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> are capable of vegetative propagation, including <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basal_shoot" title="Basal shoot">basal shoots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">stolons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corm" title="Corm">corms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulb" title="Bulb">bulbs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bud" title="Bud">buds</a>. In a few species (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Kalanchoe" title="Kalanchoe">Kalanchoë</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are involved in vegetative reproduction.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizome</a></b> is a modified underground stem serving as an organ of vegetative reproduction, e. g. <i><a href="/wiki/Polypodium" title="Polypodium">Polypodium</a></i> (polypody), <a href="/wiki/Iris_(plant)" title="Iris (plant)">iris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Couch_grass" title="Couch grass">couch grass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urtica" title="Urtica">nettles</a>.</li>
<li>Prostrate aerial stems, called <b>runners</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">stolons</a></b> are important vegetative reproduction organs in some species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberry</a>, numerous <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grasses</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adventitious" title="Adventitious">Adventitious</a></b> buds form on roots near the ground surface, on damaged stems (as on the stumps of cut trees), or on old roots. These develop into above-ground stems and leaves.</li>
<li>A form of budding called <b><a href="/wiki/Basal_shoot" title="Basal shoot">suckering</a></b> is the reproduction or <a href="/wiki/Regeneration_(biology)" title="Regeneration (biology)">regeneration</a> of a plant by shoots that arise from an existing <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root system</a>. Species that characteristically produce suckers include <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">Elm</a> (<i>Ulmus</i>) and members of the <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">Rose</a> Family (<i>Rosa</i>).</li>
<li>Another type of a vegetative reproduction is the production of <a href="/wiki/Bulb" title="Bulb">bulbs</a>. Plants like <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onion</a> (<i>Allium cepa</i>), <a href="/wiki/Hyacinth_(plant)" title="Hyacinth (plant)">hyacinth</a> (<i>Hyacinth</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narcissus_(genus)" title="Narcissus (genus)">narcissus</a> (<i>Narcissus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Tulip" title="Tulip">tulips</a> (<i>Tulipa</i>) reproduce by forming bulbs.</li>
<li>Other plants like <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> (<i>Solanum tuberosum</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Dahlia" title="Dahlia">dahlia</a> (<i>Dahlia</i>) reproduce by a method similar to bulbs: they produce <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gladiolus" title="Gladiolus">Gladioli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crocus" title="Crocus">crocuses</a> (<i>Crocus</i>) reproduce by forming a bulb-like structure called a <a href="/wiki/Corm" title="Corm">corm</a>.</li>
<li>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchids" title="Orchids">orchids</a> reproduce by the growth of <a href="/wiki/Keiki" title="Keiki">keikis</a> from the stem or cane of the parent plant.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_vegetative_propagation">Natural vegetative propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vegetative_reproduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural vegetative propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural vegetative propagation is mostly a <a href="/wiki/Biological_process" title="Biological process">process</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woody_plant" title="Woody plant">woody</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> plants, and typically involves structural modifications of the <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stem</a>, although any horizontal, underground part of a plant (whether stem, leaf, or <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root</a>) can contribute to vegetative reproduction of a plant. Most plant species that survive and significantly expand by vegetative reproduction would be perennial almost by definition, since specialized organs of vegetative reproduction, like seeds of annuals, serve to survive <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasonally</a> harsh conditions. A plant that persists in a location through vegetative reproduction of individuals over a long period of time constitutes a <a href="/wiki/Clonal_colony" title="Clonal colony">clonal colony</a>.</p>
<p>In a sense, this process is not one of reproduction but one of survival and expansion of biomass of the individual. When an individual <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> increases in size via cell multiplication and remains intact, the process is called "vegetative growth". However, in vegetative reproduction, the new plants that result are new individuals in almost every respect except genetic. Of considerable interest is how this process appears to reset the <a href="/wiki/Plant_senescence" title="Plant senescence">aging clock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_vegetative_propagation">Artificial vegetative propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vegetative_reproduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Artificial vegetative propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;max-width:204px">
<div style="clear:both;font-weight:bold;text-align:center;background-color:transparent">Mass propagation of eucalyptus seedlings</div>

<p>Vegetative propagation of particular <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> that have desirable characteristics is very common practice. Reasons for preferring vegetative rather than sexual means of reproduction vary, but commonly include greater ease and speed of propagation of certain plants, such as many perennial root crops and vines. Another major attraction is that the resulting plant amounts to a clone of the parent plant and accordingly is of a more predictable quality than most seedlings. However, as can be seen in many <a href="/wiki/Variegation" title="Variegation">variegated</a> plants, this does not always apply, because many plants actually are <a href="/wiki/Chimera_(genetics)" title="Chimera (genetics)">chimeras</a> and cuttings might reflect the attributes of only one or some of the parent cell lines. Man-made methods of vegetative reproduction are usually enhancements of natural processes, but they range from rooting cuttings to <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">grafting</a> and artificial propagation by laboratory <a href="/wiki/Plant_tissue_culture" title="Plant tissue culture">tissue culture</a>.</p>
<p>In horticulture, a "cutting" is a piece that has been cut off from a mother <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and then caused to grow into a whole plant. Often this involves a piece of stem that is treated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rooting_powder" title="Rooting powder">rooting liquid or powder</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Plant_hormone" title="Plant hormone">hormones</a>. In some species root cuttings can produce shoot growth. When the cutting has become a self-sufficient plant, it is genetically identical to the mother plant except when chimeric tissues or similar complications affect the outcome.</p>
<p>A related form of regeneration is that of <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">grafting</a>. A stem piece or a single bud (the scion) is joined onto the stem of a plant that has roots (the <a href="/wiki/Rootstock" title="Rootstock">rootstock</a>), or a stem piece can be joined to a root piece. A popular use of grafting is <a href="/wiki/Fruit_tree_propagation" title="Fruit tree propagation">to produce fruit trees</a>, sometimes with more than one variety of the same fruit species growing from the same stem. <a href="/wiki/Rootstock" title="Rootstock">Rootstocks</a> for fruit trees are either seedlings or propagated by <a href="/wiki/Layering" title="Layering">layering</a>.</p>
