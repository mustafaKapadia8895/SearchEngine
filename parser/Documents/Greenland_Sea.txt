<p>The <b>Greenland Sea</b> is a body of water that borders <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> to the west, the <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Fram_Strait" title="Fram Strait">Fram Strait</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> to the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> to the south. The Greenland Sea is often defined as part of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> sometimes as part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reddy2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reddy2001-4">[4]</a></sup> However, definitions of the Arctic Ocean and its seas tend to be imprecise or arbitrary. In general usage the term "Arctic Ocean" would exclude the Greenland Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SerrezeBarry2005_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SerrezeBarry2005-5">[5]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanographic studies</a> the Greenland Sea is considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Seas" title="Nordic Seas">Nordic Seas</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a>. The Nordic Seas are the main connection between the Arctic and Atlantic oceans and, as such, could be of great significance in a possible <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a>. In oceanography the Arctic Ocean and Nordic Seas are often referred to collectively as the "Arctic Mediterranean Sea", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marginal_sea" title="Marginal sea">marginal sea</a> of the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drange2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drange2005-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessment_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Assessment-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meincke_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-meincke-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sea has <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_the_Arctic" title="Climate of the Arctic">Arctic climate</a> with regular northern winds and temperatures rarely rising above 0 °C. It previously contained the <b>Odden ice tongue</b> (or <b>Odden</b>) area, which extended eastward from the main East Greenland <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> edge in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/72nd_parallel_north" title="72nd parallel north">72</a>–<a href="/wiki/74th_parallel_north" title="74th parallel north">74°N</a> during the winter and acted as a key winter ice formation area in the Arctic. The <a href="/wiki/West_Ice" title="West Ice">West Ice</a> forms in winter in the Greenland Sea, north of Iceland, between Greenland and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> island. It is a major breeding ground of <a href="/wiki/Harp_seal" title="Harp seal">harp seal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hooded_seal" title="Hooded seal">hooded seal</a> that has been used for <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">seal hunting</a> for more than 200 years.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent">Extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> defines the limits of the Greenland Sea as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>On the North.</i> A line joining the Northernmost point of Spitzbergen  [<i>sic</i>] [<a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>] to the Northernmost point of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.</p>
<p><i>On the East.</i> The West coast of West Spitzbergen  [<i>sic</i>] [island of <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a>].</p>
<p><i>On the Southeast.</i> A line joining the Southernmost point of West Spitzbergen  [<i>sic</i>] to the Northern point of <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> Island, down the West coast of that island to its Southern extreme, thence a Line to the Eastern extreme of Gerpir (67°05'N, 13°30'W) [NB Actually at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Greenland_Sea&amp;params=65_05_N_13_30_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">65°05′N</span> <span class="longitude">13°30′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">65.083°N 13.500°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">65.083; -13.500</span></span></span></a></span>] in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.</p>
<p><i>On the Southwest.</i> A line joining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straumnes,_Iceland" title="Straumnes, Iceland">Straumnes</a> (NW extreme of Iceland) to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Nansen" title="Cape Nansen">Cape Nansen</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Greenland_Sea&amp;params=68_15_N_29_30_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">68°15′N</span> <span class="longitude">29°30′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">68.250°N 29.500°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">68.250; -29.500</span></span></span></a></span>) in Greenland.</p>
<p><i>On the West.</i> The East and Northeast coast of Greenland between Cape Nansen and the northernmost point.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the sea is known for millennia, its first scientific investigations were carried out in 1876–1878 within the Norwegian North-Atlantic Expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Since then, many countries, mostly Norway, Iceland and Russia have sent scientific expeditions to the area. The complex water current system was detailed in 1909 by the <a href="/wiki/Fridtjof_Nansen" title="Fridtjof Nansen">Fridtjof Nansen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Greenland Sea was a popular hunting ground for the <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a> industry for 300 years, until 1911, primarily based in <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a>. At that point, the formerly rich whale population here, was so depleted that the industry was no longer profitable. The remaining whales of the Greenland Sea has been protected ever since, but the populations had not shown any proof of significant regeneration. In the last 20 years, polar biologists reports an increase in the local <a href="/wiki/Bowhead_whale" title="Bowhead whale">bowhead whale</a> population and in 2015, arctic scientists discovered a surprising abundance of them in a small area. These results may be interpreted as an early sign of a beginning recovery for this particular species, that once formed the largest bowhead population in the world, at an estimated 52,000 whales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowheads_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowheads-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> hunted whales on a non-industrial scale in the Greenland Sea since the 15th century, as evidenced by archaeology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowheads_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bowheads-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_geology">Geography and geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography and geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg/220px-Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg/330px-Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg/440px-Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grenland_Sea_Dalvik.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Eyjafj%C3%B6r%C3%B0ur" title="Eyjafjörður">Eyjafjörður</a>, the longest fjord in Northern Iceland, belongs to the Greenland Sea.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Greenland Sea is bounded to the west by the island of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and to the south by the <a href="/wiki/Denmark_Strait" title="Denmark Strait">Denmark Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. To the southeast, behind the <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> island (Norway) lies the vast expanse of the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a>, of which Greenland Sea may be considered an extension. Across the <a href="/wiki/Fram_Strait" title="Fram Strait">Fram Strait</a> to the northeast, the sea is delimited by the <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> (Norway).</p>
<p>The bottom of the Greenland Sea is a <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">depression</a> bounded to the south by the underwater <a href="/wiki/File:N-Atlantic-topo.png" title="File:N-Atlantic-topo.png">Greenland-Iceland ridge</a> and to the east by the Mohns Ridge and Knipovich Ridge (parts of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>). To the west, the bottom rises first slowly, but then rapidly toward the wide Greenland coastal strip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bse-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">Silts</a> fill the submarine hollows and gorges; silty sands, gravel, boulders, and other products of erosion coat the shelves and ridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the deepest point inside of the sea is 4846 meters, depths down to 5570 meters have been measured in the Molloy Deep of the <a href="/wiki/Fram_Strait" title="Fram Strait">Fram Strait</a> which connects the sea to the Arctic Ocean on the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Major islands of the Greenland Sea include the <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> archipelago, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> as well as coastal islands off the NE Greenland shores, such as <a href="/wiki/Hovgaard_Island_(Greenland)" title="Hovgaard Island (Greenland)">Hovgaard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ella_Island" title="Ella Island">Ella</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godfred_Hansens_Island" title="Godfred Hansens Island">Godfred Hansens</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France_(Greenland)" title="Île-de-France (Greenland)">Île-de-France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Island" title="Lynn Island">Lynn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norske_Island" title="Norske Island">Norske</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schnauders_Island" title="Schnauders Island">Schnauders Island</a>. Of those, only the Svalbard islands are inhabited,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and Jan Mayen has only temporal military staff. After the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> gave Norway jurisdiction over the island, in 1921 Norway opened the first meteorological station there, which was a subject of contention between Germany and United Kingdom during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rigge_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rigge-14">[14]</a></sup> Several radio and meteorological stations operate on the island nowadays.</p>
<h2><span id="Hydrology,_climate_and_ice"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrology.2C_climate_and_ice">Hydrology, climate and ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hydrology, climate and ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg/220px-Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg/330px-Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg/440px-Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jan_mayen_egg-oeja_hg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A beach on Jan Mayen island</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The climate is <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_the_Arctic" title="Climate of the Arctic">Arctic</a> and varies significantly across the vast sea area. Air temperatures fluctuate between −49 °C (−57 °F), near <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a> in winter, and 25 °C (77 °F) off Greenland in summer. Averages are −10 °C (14 °F) in the south and −26 °C (−15 °F) in the north in February, which is the coldest month. The corresponding values for the warmest month, August, are 5 °C (41 °F) in the south and 0 °C (32 °F) in the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup> The summer is very short, as the number of days per year when the temperature rises above 0 °C varies between 225 in the north to 334 in the south. The annual precipitation is 250 mm (10 in) in the north, but is twice higher in the south.</p>
<p>Northern winds continue through the whole year, cooling the surface water and bringing ice to the south. The average surface water temperature is about −1 °С (30 °F) or lower in the north and 1–2 °C in the south; the corresponding summer temperatures are about 0 °C and 6 °C (43 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup> The bottom water temperatures are below −1 °С. The surface water salinity is 33.0–34.5 parts per thousand (‰) in the eastern and below 32.0‰ in the western parts, increasing to 34.9‰ toward the bottom. The water color is green. <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">Tides</a> are semi-diurnal with the average height of 4.4 m. Together with the water currents, they break up the floating ice sheets and mix various water layers both laterally and along the depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-bse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The progressively colder waters of <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> sink in the Arctic Ocean, returning south in the form of cold <a href="/wiki/East_Greenland_Current" title="East Greenland Current">East Greenland Current</a>, an important part of the Atlantic conveyor belt, which flows along the western part of the sea. Along the eastern part flows the warm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spitsbergen_Current" title="Spitsbergen Current">Spitsbergen Current</a>, a part of <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>. Mixtures of cold, fresh water ice melt and the warm, salty Spitsbergen Current may experience <a href="/wiki/Cabbeling" title="Cabbeling">cabbeling</a>, and might contribute to thermohaline circulation. The combination of those currents creates a counter-clockwise water flow in the central part of the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-bse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-whirl2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-whirl-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of frequent fogs and winds and currents, which continuously transport ice and icebergs through the Greenland Sea to the south, the Greenland Sea has a narrow window for commercial navigation – the ice season starts in October and ends in August. Three types of floating ice are distinguished: Arctic pack ice (several meters thick), sea ice (about a meter thick) and freshwater icebergs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Ice">West Ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: West Ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1467" data-file-width="2200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg/220px-Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg/330px-Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg/440px-Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nilas_Sea_Ice1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Frazil ice</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In winter, a large area north of Iceland, between <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a>, which is called <a href="/wiki/West_Ice" title="West Ice">West Ice</a> is covered by continuous ice. It is a major breeding ground for seals, including <a href="/wiki/Harp_seal" title="Harp seal">harp seal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hooded_seal" title="Hooded seal">hooded seal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray_seal" title="Gray seal">gray seal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It was discovered in the early 18th century by British whalers and since late 1750s was used for seal hunting. The hunting was especially intensive in the 19th century, but declined in the 20th century due to hunting restrictions and then declined market demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-19">[19]</a></sup> Around 5 April 1952, a major storm resulted in disappearance of ships with 79 Norwegian seal hunters on board. Seven other Norwegian seal hunting vessels shipwrecked the same month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Odden_ice_tongue">Odden ice tongue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Odden ice tongue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1296" data-file-width="1944" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg/220px-Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg/330px-Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg/440px-Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pancakeice_ross_sea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pancake ice</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Odden ice tongue or simply the Odden (Odden is <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> word for <i>headland</i>) was a key winter ice formation area in the Arctic. It was known for a long time and was encountered by <a href="/wiki/Fridtjof_Nansen" title="Fridtjof Nansen">Fridtjof Nansen</a> but was only fully understood with the advent of satellite imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Odden had a length of about 1,300 km and covered an area of up to 330,000 km² in most years. It extended eastward from the main East Greenland ice edge in the vicinity of 72–74°N during the winter because of the presence of very cold polar surface water in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Mayen_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jan Mayen Current (page does not exist)">Jan Mayen Current</a>, which diverts some water eastward from the <a href="/wiki/East_Greenland_Current" title="East Greenland Current">East Greenland Current</a> at that latitude. Most of the already formed ice continued floating south, driven by the wind, so a cold open water surface was exposed on which new ice formed as <a href="/wiki/Frazil_ice" title="Frazil ice">frazil ice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pancake_ice" title="Pancake ice">pancake ice</a> in the rough seas, producing a giant tongue shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The salt rejected back into the ocean from this ice formation caused the surface water to become denser and sink, sometimes to great depths (2500 m or more), making this one of the few regions of the ocean where winter convection occurred, which helped drive the entire worldwide system of surface and deep currents known as the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-whirl2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-whirl-16">[16]</a></sup> Since the 1990s, the Odden Ice Tongue rarely develops.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Greenland Sea is densely inhabited by the organisms that form the base of the oceanic <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. Large <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redfish" title="Redfish">redfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halibut" title="Halibut">halibut</a>, and <a href="/wiki/European_plaice" title="European plaice">plaice</a>), birds and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> (including various species of <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>) all feed on the smaller invertebrates and small <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>. Mosses, lichens, and scanty bushes around the coasts serve as food to the deer and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musk_ox" title="Musk ox">musk oxen</a>, which in turn are hunted by the <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-brit-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Greenland sea was formerly home to a large population of various whale species, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowhead_whales" title="Bowhead whales">bowhead whales</a>, but the whaling industry decimated them greatly from the beginning of the 1600s till 1911. In the last few decades there have been a few signs indicating a beginning recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowheads_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bowheads-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_and_gas">Oil and gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Oil and gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_exploration_in_the_Arctic" title="Petroleum exploration in the Arctic">Petroleum exploration in the Arctic</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Geological_Survey" title="US Geological Survey">US Geological Survey</a> has estimated that at least 13% of the worlds undiscovered oil deposits and 30% of the worlds undiscovered gas pockets are located in the Arctic, with the Greenland Sea potentially holding large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> and lesser amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_liquids" title="Natural gas liquids">natural gas liquids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This has led the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Greenland" title="Parliament of Greenland">Greenland's minister and provincial council</a> to offer a large number of off-shore concessions to potential hydrocarbon (oil and gas) extraction. The majority of the concessions are located in seas west of Greenland (primarily the <a href="/wiki/Davis_Strait" title="Davis Strait">Davis Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Bay" title="Baffin Bay">Baffin Bay</a>), but with 19 concessions in the Greenland Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAI_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-TAI-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2013, a total of three <a href="/wiki/Consortium" title="Consortium">consortia</a> obtained hydrocarbon extraction rights to four large areas of the Greenland Sea from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Mineral_and_Petroleum_(Greenland)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bureau of Mineral and Petroleum (Greenland) (page does not exist)">Greenland Bureau of Mineral and Petroleum</a>. The consortia are led by the oil companies of <a href="/wiki/Statoil" title="Statoil">Statoil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eni" title="Eni">Eni</a>, but includes several other smaller companies such as <a href="/wiki/Shell_Oil_Company" title="Shell Oil Company">Shell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Petroleum" title="British Petroleum">British Petroleum</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dong_Energy" title="Dong Energy">Dong Energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nunaoil" title="Nunaoil">Nunaoil</a>. Since then, a fifth hydrocarbon concession has been sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exxon_Mobil" title="Exxon Mobil">Exxon Mobil</a>, the largest oil company in the world and with a lot of experience in the Arctic, was also applying for oil extraction rights in the Greenland Sea initially, but pulled out in December 2013 for unexplained reasons, concentrating efforts on <a href="/wiki/Shale_gas" title="Shale gas">shale gas</a> and the American market instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAJ_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-TAJ-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Drilling for oil on deep waters in an ice-filled Arctic environment is a potentially new undertaking for the oil industry and poses many risks and dangers. Because of these difficulties the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Greenland" title="Parliament of Greenland">Greenland Minister Council</a> expects the first <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Exploratory_drill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Exploratory drill (page does not exist)">exploratory drills</a> to take place no sooner than the mid 2020s. They estimates that a full preliminary program with seismic surveys, exploratory drills and proper safety measures will take about 16 years and an investment of about 500 million dollars in each concession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAI_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-TAI-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAJ_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-TAJ-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
