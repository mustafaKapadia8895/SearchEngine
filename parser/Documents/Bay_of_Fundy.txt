<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bay of Fundy</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>Bay of Fundy</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Baie de Fundy</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">bay</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, on the northeast end of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Maine" title="Gulf of Maine">Gulf of Maine</a> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_province" title="Canadian province">Canadian provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, with a small portion touching the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>. Some sources believe the name "Fundy" is a corruption of the French word <i>Fendu</i>, meaning "split",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while others believe it comes from the Portuguese <i>funda</i>, meaning "deep".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The bay was also named <b>Baie Française</b> (French Bay) by explorer-cartographer <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a> during a 1604 expedition led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre_Dugua,_Sieur_de_Monts" title="Pierre Dugua, Sieur de Monts">Pierre Dugua, Sieur de Monts</a> which resulted in a failed settlement attempt on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Croix_Island_(Maine)" title="Saint Croix Island (Maine)">St. Croix Island</a>.</p>
<p>The Bay of Fundy is known for having the highest <a href="/wiki/Tidal_range" title="Tidal range">tidal range</a> in the world. Rivaled by <a href="/wiki/Ungava_Bay" title="Ungava Bay">Ungava Bay</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_Sound" title="King Sound">King Sound</a> in Western Australia, <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Khambhat" title="Gulf of Khambhat">Gulf of Khambhat</a> in India, and the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Estuary" title="Severn Estuary">Severn Estuary</a> in the UK, it has one of the highest vertical tidal ranges in the world. The <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a> (1975) declared that <a href="/wiki/Burntcoat_Head,_Nova_Scotia" title="Burntcoat Head, Nova Scotia">Burntcoat Head, Nova Scotia</a> has the highest tides in the world:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“The Natural World, Greatest Tides: The greatest tides in the world occur in the Bay of Fundy.... Burntcoat Head in the Minas Basin, Nova Scotia, has the greatest mean spring range with 14.5 metres (47.5 feet) and an extreme range of 16.3 metres (53.5 feet).”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Portions of the Bay of Fundy, <a href="/wiki/Shepody_Bay" title="Shepody Bay">Shepody Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minas_Basin" title="Minas Basin">Minas Basin</a>, form one of six Canadian sites in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere_Shorebird_Reserve_Network" title="Western Hemisphere Shorebird Reserve Network">Western Hemisphere Shorebird Reserve Network</a>, and are classified as a <i>Hemispheric</i> site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHSRN_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHSRN-3">[3]</a></sup> It is administered by the provinces of New Brunswick and Nova Scotia, and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Wildlife_Service" title="Canadian Wildlife Service">Canadian Wildlife Service</a>, and is managed in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Ducks_Unlimited" title="Ducks Unlimited">Ducks Unlimited</a> Canada and the <a href="/wiki/Nature_Conservancy_of_Canada" title="Nature Conservancy of Canada">Nature Conservancy of Canada</a>.</p>
<p>In July 2009, the Bay of Fundy was named as a finalist for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Seven_Wonders_of_Nature" title="New Seven Wonders of Nature">New 7 Wonders of Nature</a> contest that ended in November 2011. It was not chosen as a wonder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent">Extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> defines the southwest limit of the Bay of Fundy as "A line running northwesterly from <a href="/wiki/Cape_St._Marys,_Nova_Scotia" title="Cape St. Marys, Nova Scotia">Cape St. Mary</a> (44°05'N) <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Machias_Seal_Island" title="Machias Seal Island">Machias Seal Island</a> (67°06'W) and on to Little River Head (44°39'N) in the State of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tides">Tides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Bay of Fundy is known for its high <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal range</a>. The quest for world tidal dominance has led to a rivalry between the <a href="/wiki/Minas_Basin" title="Minas Basin">Minas Basin</a> in the Bay of Fundy and the Leaf Basin in <a href="/wiki/Ungava_Bay" title="Ungava Bay">Ungava Bay</a>, over which body of water lays claim to the highest tides in the world, with supporters in each region claiming the record.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hydrographic_Service" title="Canadian Hydrographic Service">Canadian Hydrographic Service</a> finally declared it a statistical tie, with measurements of a 16.8-metre (55-foot) tidal range in Leaf Basin for Ungava Bay and 17 meters (56 feet) at <a href="/wiki/Burntcoat_Head,_Nova_Scotia" title="Burntcoat Head, Nova Scotia">Burntcoat Head</a> for the Bay of Fundy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The highest water level ever recorded in the Bay of Fundy system occurred at the head of the Minas Basin on the night of October 4–5, 1869 during a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> named the “<a href="/wiki/1869_Saxby_Gale" title="1869 Saxby Gale">Saxby Gale</a>”. The water level of 21.6 meters (71 feet) resulted from the combination of high winds, abnormally low <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_tide" title="Spring tide">spring tide</a>.</p>
<p>Leaf Basin has only been measured in recent years, whereas the Fundy system has been measured for many decades. The tide at Leaf Basin is higher on average than tides at Minas Basin; however, the highest recorded tidal ranges ever measured are at Burntcoat Head and result from spring tides measured at the peak of the tidal cycle every 18 years.</p>
<p>Traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mi%27kmaq_people" title="Mi'kmaq people">Mi'kmaq</a> folklore states that the tides in the Bay of Fundy are caused by a giant whale splashing in the water. <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">Oceanographers</a> attribute it to <a href="/wiki/Tidal_resonance" title="Tidal resonance">tidal resonance</a> resulting from a coincidence of timing: the time it takes a large wave to go from the mouth of the bay to the inner shore and back is practically the same as the time from one high tide to the next. During the 12.4-hour tidal period, 115 billion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> of water flow in and out of the bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tides" title="Tides">tides</a> in the Bay of Fundy are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semidiurnal_tides" title="Semidiurnal tides">semidiurnal</a>, which means that they have two highs and two lows each day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The height that the water rises and falls to each day during these tides are approximately equal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein.2C_G.D._1963.2C_p._844-854_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klein.2C_G.D._1963.2C_p._844-854-9">[9]</a></sup> There are approximately six hours and thirteen minutes between each high and low tide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_electrical_power_generation">Tidal electrical power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tidal electrical power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternative forms of energy are being explored in depth in a number of unique areas. Tidal energy harnesses the movement of ocean water to generate electricity through a number of mechanisms. Currently a process of gathering tidal energy called "<a href="/wiki/Tidal_stream_generator" title="Tidal stream generator">In-stream turbine technology</a>" is being tested in the Minas Passage, Nova Scotia. This project is being spearheaded by the Fundy Ocean Research Center for Energy or FORCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In-stream tidal turbine technology is a relatively simple design. An elevated turbine is submerged under water in a location that enables its movement with tidal cycles. As the blades of the turbine move they create energy that powers an electric generator at the base. From here the power travels to a cable attached to the seafloor and back to an offsite facility where it can be added to the power grid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While this technology has shown to be successful in its early stages of testing, FORCE has not officially begun the process for energy collection. However, the installation of the undersea cable in December 2013 indicates that the project is moving along swiftly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A megawatt-scale turbine was installed at Cape Sharp near <a href="/wiki/Partridge_Island_(Nova_Scotia)#Tidal_statistics" title="Partridge Island (Nova Scotia)">Partridge Island</a> in November 2016. Project managers intend to install 16 MW by 2017, and 300 MW by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Bay of Fundy lies in a <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Fundy_Basin" title="Fundy Basin">Fundy Basin</a>; as the <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rift</a> began to separate from mainland North America, volcanic activity occurred, forming <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalts</a>. These flood basalts poured out over the landscape, covering much of southern Nova Scotia. Sections of the flood basalts have been eroded away, but still form a <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a> known as <a href="/wiki/North_Mountain_(Nova_Scotia)" title="North Mountain (Nova Scotia)">North Mountain</a>. As a result, much of the basin floor is made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tholeiitic_basalt" title="Tholeiitic basalt">tholeiitic basalts</a> giving its brown color. The rift valley eventually failed (<i>see</i> <a href="/wiki/Aulacogen" title="Aulacogen">aulacogen</a>) as the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> continued to separate North America, Europe, and Africa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-basins">Sub-basins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sub-basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The upper part of the bay splits into <a href="/wiki/Chignecto_Bay" title="Chignecto Bay">Chignecto Bay</a> in the northeast and the <a href="/wiki/Minas_Basin" title="Minas Basin">Minas Basin</a> in the east. Chignecto Bay is further subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Basin_(Canada)" title="Cumberland Basin (Canada)">Cumberland Basin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shepody_Bay" title="Shepody Bay">Shepody Bay</a> and the extreme eastern portion of <a href="/wiki/Minas_Basin" title="Minas Basin">Minas Basin</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Cobequid_Bay" title="Cobequid Bay">Cobequid Bay</a>. Some of these upper reaches exhibit exposed red <a href="/wiki/Bay_mud" title="Bay mud">bay muds</a>, for which the Bay of Fundy is noted (for their appearance and biological productivity).</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Cape_Chignecto" title="Cape Chignecto">Cape Chignecto</a> defines <a href="/wiki/Chignecto_Bay" title="Chignecto Bay">Chignecto Bay</a> whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Split,_Nova_Scotia" title="Cape Split, Nova Scotia">Cape Split</a> defines the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minas_Channel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minas Channel (page does not exist)">Minas Channel</a>, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Minas_Basin" title="Minas Basin">Minas Basin</a>. The Minas Channel connects the Minas Basin with the main body of the bay. The channel is 5.6 kilometers across and approximately 106.7 meters deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein.2C_G.D._1963.2C_p._844-854_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klein.2C_G.D._1963.2C_p._844-854-9">[9]</a></sup> The tides that flow through the channel are very powerful. They are as powerful as 8000 train engines or 25 million horses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Facing Cape Split at the entrance to the Minas Channel are the basalt cliffs of <a href="/wiki/Cape_d%27Or" title="Cape d'Or">Cape d'Or</a>.</p>

<p>The lower part of the bay is also home to four important sub-basins: <a href="/wiki/Passamaquoddy_Bay" title="Passamaquoddy Bay">Passamaquoddy Bay</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Back_Bay_(New_Brunswick)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Back Bay (New Brunswick) (page does not exist)">Back Bay</a> on the New Brunswick shore, <a href="/wiki/Cobscook_Bay" title="Cobscook Bay">Cobscook Bay</a> on the Maine shore, and the <a href="/wiki/Annapolis_Basin" title="Annapolis Basin">Annapolis Basin</a> on the Nova Scotia shore.</p>
<p>The bay is home to several islands, the largest of which is <a href="/wiki/Grand_Manan" title="Grand Manan">Grand Manan</a> at the boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Maine" title="Gulf of Maine">Gulf of Maine</a>. Other important islands on the north side of the bay include <a href="/wiki/Campobello_Island" title="Campobello Island">Campobello Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moose_Island,_Maine" title="Moose Island, Maine">Moose Island</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deer_Island,_New_Brunswick" title="Deer Island, New Brunswick">Deer Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Passamaquoddy_Bay" title="Passamaquoddy Bay">Passamaquoddy Bay</a> area. <a href="/wiki/Brier_Island" title="Brier Island">Brier Island</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Island,_Nova_Scotia" title="Long Island, Nova Scotia">Long Island</a> can be found on the south side of the bay while <a href="/wiki/Isle_Haute" title="Isle Haute">Isle Haute</a> is in the upper bay off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Chignecto,_Nova_Scotia" title="Cape Chignecto, Nova Scotia">Cape Chignecto</a>. Smaller islands and islets also exist in Passamaquoddy Bay, Back Bay, and <a href="/wiki/Annapolis_Basin" title="Annapolis Basin">Annapolis Basin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Five_Islands,_Nova_Scotia" title="Five Islands, Nova Scotia">Five Islands</a>, in the Minas Basin, are particularly scenic.</p>

<p>The Bay of Fundy is also home to another interesting geologic feature, the <a href="/wiki/Hopewell_Rocks" title="Hopewell Rocks">Hopewell Rocks</a> formation. This formation is where the “famous flower-pot rocks” are located on a short, one kilometer long portion of <a href="/wiki/Shepody_Bay" title="Shepody Bay">Shepody Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trenhaile.2C_A.S._1998.2C_p._975-988_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trenhaile.2C_A.S._1998.2C_p._975-988-16">[16]</a></sup> These rocks are shaped by the tides, the strength of the rocks, and the presence of the joints in the rocks. The rock in the cliffs the stacks are being cut from are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arkosic" title="Arkosic">arkosic</a> sandstone and coarse poorly sorted conglomerates. The tides that are eroding away the cliffs are between 10.7 meters and 14.7 meters high on average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trenhaile.2C_A.S._1998.2C_p._975-988_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trenhaile.2C_A.S._1998.2C_p._975-988-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The bay's extreme tidal range causes several interesting phenomena in the various rivers which empty into it.</p>
<p>Near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_John_River_(Bay_of_Fundy)" title="Saint John River (Bay of Fundy)">Saint John River</a>, where it passes through the city of <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John</a>, is the famous <a href="/wiki/Reversing_Falls" title="Reversing Falls">Reversing Falls</a>. Here the changing tide causes alternating strong upriver and downriver flows several times a day as the tidal cycle changes. A narrow granite gorge and rocky bottom heavily restricts waterflow and creates strong rapids that flow upriver at high tide, and downriver at low tide.</p>
<p>Rivers in the upper Bay of Fundy have a smaller flow-rate than the Saint John, and a shallower slope. As a result, extensive mud flats are deposited throughout the tidal range of the rivers.</p>

<p>Another phenomenon which occurs in these rivers of the upper bay is a "<a href="/wiki/Tidal_bore" title="Tidal bore">tidal bore</a>", where a wave front of water "bores" its way up a river against its normal flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The narrow and shallow river estuaries around the upper Bay of Fundy regularly produce bores when the advancing tide is slowed by shallow waters creating a build-up with a steep surge of advancing water. Tidal bores are found on the Salmon River at <a href="/wiki/Truro,_Nova_Scotia" title="Truro, Nova Scotia">Truro</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Petitcodiac_River" title="Petitcodiac River">Petitcodiac River</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moncton,_New_Brunswick" title="Moncton, New Brunswick">Moncton</a> as well as on the <a href="/wiki/Shubenacadie_River" title="Shubenacadie River">Shubenacadie River</a>, the <a href="/wiki/St._Croix_River_(Nova_Scotia)" title="St. Croix River (Nova Scotia)">St. Croix River</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maccan_River" title="Maccan River">Maccan River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kennetcook_River" title="Kennetcook River">Kennetcook River</a> and at River Herbert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the better examples of a tidal bore can be seen on the Shubenacadie River near the town of <a href="/wiki/Truro,_Nova_Scotia" title="Truro, Nova Scotia">Truro</a> and the village of Maitland, where local <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism" title="Ecotourism">ecotourism</a> operators offer the chance to experience rafting the bore upriver. Another good example of a tidal bore may be viewed on the Salmon River in the town of <a href="/wiki/Truro,_Nova_Scotia" title="Truro, Nova Scotia">Truro</a>. The once-famous tidal bores on the Petitcodiac and Avon rivers have been severely disrupted as a result of <a href="/wiki/Causeway" title="Causeway">causeway</a> construction in the 1960s–1970s which have caused excessive siltation.</p>

<p>The Mi'kmaq fished in the Bay of Fundy and lived in communities around the bay for centuries before the first Europeans arrived and continue to live and work around the Bay. The first European settlement was at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-Saint-Croix" title="Île-Saint-Croix">Île-Saint-Croix</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Port_Royal,_Annapolis_County,_Nova_Scotia" title="Port Royal, Annapolis County, Nova Scotia">Port Royal</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Dugua,_Sieur_de_Mons" title="Pierre Dugua, Sieur de Mons">Pierre Dugua, Sieur de Mons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a> in 1605. The village was the first permanent European settlement north of <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine,_Florida" title="St. Augustine, Florida">St. Augustine, Florida</a>. (Two years later, the English made their first permanent settlement in <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown, Virginia</a>.) Approximately seventy-five years after Port Royal was founded, <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">Acadians</a> migrated from the capital and established what would become the other major Acadian settlements before the <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_the_Acadians" title="Expulsion of the Acadians">Expulsion of the Acadians</a>: <a href="/wiki/Grand-Pr%C3%A9,_Nova_Scotia" title="Grand-Pré, Nova Scotia">Grand-Pré</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Chignecto" title="Isthmus of Chignecto">Chignecto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobequid" title="Cobequid">Cobequid</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Truro,_Nova_Scotia" title="Truro, Nova Scotia">Truro, Nova Scotia</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Pisiguit" title="Pisiguit">Pisiguit</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Nova_Scotia" title="Windsor, Nova Scotia">Windsor, Nova Scotia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faragher.2C_John_Mack_2005._pp._110_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Faragher.2C_John_Mack_2005._pp._110-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were numerous naval battles as well as naval attacks on the settlements surrounding the Bay of Fundy. Port Royal was raided numerous times during the <a href="/wiki/Acadian_Civil_War" title="Acadian Civil War">Acadian Civil War</a>. There were also numerous battles and raids during six wars that engulfed the region over a period of 75 years: the four <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_Wars" title="French and Indian Wars">French and Indian Wars</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Father_Rale%27s_War" title="Father Rale's War">Father Rale's War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Father_Le_Loutre%27s_War" title="Father Le Loutre's War">Father Le Loutre's War</a>. During <a href="/wiki/King_William%27s_War" title="King William's War">King William's War</a>, there was the naval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_of_July_14,_1696" title="Action of July 14, 1696">Action of July 14, 1696</a> off <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John, New Brunswick</a> and Major <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Church_(ranger)" title="Benjamin Church (ranger)">Benjamin Church</a> arrived by sea from Boston and raided various Acadian communities around the Bay (See <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Chignecto_(1696)" title="Raid on Chignecto (1696)">Raid on Chignecto (1696)</a>). During <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_War" title="Queen Anne's War">Queen Anne's War</a>, Church attacked the Acadian communities around the Bay again (See <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Grand_Pr%C3%A9" title="Raid on Grand Pré">Raid on Grand Pré</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faragher.2C_John_Mack_2005._pp._110_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Faragher.2C_John_Mack_2005._pp._110-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Father_Le_Loutre%27s_War" title="Father Le Loutre's War">Father Le Loutre's War</a>, there were naval assaults on the communities of Chignecto (See <a href="/wiki/Battle_at_Chignecto" title="Battle at Chignecto">Battle at Chignecto</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Acadian_Exodus" title="Acadian Exodus">Acadian Exodus</a> from the Cobequid. Finally, during the last <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>, the British expelled the Acadians in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy_Campaign_(1755)" title="Bay of Fundy Campaign (1755)">Bay of Fundy Campaign (1755)</a>, which was followed three years later with campaigns which targeted the <a href="/wiki/St._John_River_Campaign" title="St. John River Campaign">Saint John River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Petitcodiac_River_Campaign" title="Petitcodiac River Campaign">Petitcodiac River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, the upper reaches of the Basin saw a significant rebellion and battle at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Fort_Cumberland" title="Siege of Fort Cumberland">Siege of Fort Cumberland</a> and American attacks at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Saint_John_(1777)" title="Siege of Saint John (1777)">Saint John, New Brunswick</a> as well as numerous privateering raids against towns like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annapolis_Royal,_Nova_Scotia" title="Annapolis Royal, Nova Scotia">Annapolis Royal, Nova Scotia</a>. After the War, the boundary between the United States and the newly created province of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> remained uncertain, resulting in a thriving <a href="/wiki/Smuggling" title="Smuggling">smuggling</a> trade in the region, especially on the waters of <a href="/wiki/Passamaquoddy_Bay" title="Passamaquoddy Bay">Passamaquoddy Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Bay saw some action during the War of 1812 as well, mostly in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privateering" title="Privateering">privateering</a> and small patrol ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> After the War of 1812, the smuggling resumed and culminated in the so-called "plaster war" of 1820.</p>
<p>The Bay of Fundy's ports and basins became major shipbuilding and shipping centres in the 19th Century. Led at first by shipbuilders like <a href="/wiki/James_Moran_(shipbuilder)" title="James Moran (shipbuilder)">James Moran</a> of <a href="/wiki/St._Martins,_New_Brunswick" title="St. Martins, New Brunswick">St. Martins, New Brunswick</a>, shipbuilding to all shores of the Bay of Fundy and eventually included shipbuilders such as <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Salter" title="Joseph Salter">Joseph Salter</a> in Moncton and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_D._Lawrence" title="William D. Lawrence">William D. Lawrence</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maitland,_Nova_Scotia" title="Maitland, Nova Scotia">Maitland, Nova Scotia</a> who build some of the larges sailing ships ever constructed in Canada. Among other accomplishments, Fundy ports produced the fastest ship in the world, the ship <i><a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo_(1851_ship)" title="Marco Polo (1851 ship)">Marco Polo</a></i>; the largest wooden ship ever built in Canada, the ship <i><a href="/wiki/William_D._Lawrence_(ship)" title="William D. Lawrence (ship)">William D. Lawrence</a></i>; and the first female sea captain in the western world, <a href="/wiki/Molly_Kool" title="Molly Kool">Molly Kool</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alma,_New_Brunswick" title="Alma, New Brunswick">Alma, New Brunswick</a>. The fabled mystery ship the <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Celeste" title="Mary Celeste">Mary Celeste</a></i> was also built on the Bay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ports_and_shipping">Ports and shipping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ports and shipping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The port of Saint John gives access to the <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">pulp and paper industry</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Irving_Oil" title="Irving Oil">Irving</a> <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hantsport,_Nova_Scotia" title="Hantsport, Nova Scotia">Hantsport, Nova Scotia</a>, on the Avon River is also home to a pulp and paper mill and is the shipment point for raw <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> exports to the United States. The ports of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayside,_Charlotte_County" title="Bayside, Charlotte County">Bayside, Charlotte County</a>, (near St. Andrews) and <a href="/wiki/Eastport,_Maine" title="Eastport, Maine">Eastport, Maine</a>, are important local ports.</p>
<p>A result of shipping traffic has been the potential for increased collisions between ships and the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_right_whale" title="North Atlantic right whale">North Atlantic right whale</a>. In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Coast_Guard" title="Canadian Coast Guard">Canadian Coast Guard</a> adjusted shipping lanes crossing prime whale feeding areas at the entrance to the Bay of Fundy to lessen the risk of collision.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_protected_areas">Parks and protected areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Fundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Parks and protected areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A number of parks preserve and interpret the Bay of Fundy's coastal ecosystem. They include <a href="/wiki/Fundy_National_Park" title="Fundy National Park">Fundy National Park</a> in New Brunswick and <a class="external text" href="http://www.capechignecto.net/" rel="nofollow">Cape Chignecto Provincial Park</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Islands_Provincial_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Five Islands Provincial Park (page does not exist)">Five Islands Provincial Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blomidon_Provincial_Park" title="Blomidon Provincial Park">Blomidon Provincial Park</a> in Nova Scotia. The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Wildlife_Service" title="Canadian Wildlife Service">Canadian Wildlife Service</a> maintains a number of National Wildlife Areas in the Bay of Fundy including a proposed designation of <a href="/wiki/Isle_Haute" title="Isle Haute">Isle Haute</a> in the middle of the Bay.</p>
