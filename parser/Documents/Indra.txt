<p><b><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Indra</span></b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>: इन्द्र) is a <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedic</a> deity in <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berry1996p20_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berry1996p20-1">[1]</a></sup> a guardian deity in <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baroni2002p153_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baroni2002p153-2">[2]</a></sup> and the king of the highest heaven called <i>Saudharmakalpa</i> in <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Owen2012p25_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Owen2012p25-3">[3]</a></sup> His mythologies and powers are similar, though not identical to those of the Indo-European deities such as <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perun" title="Perun">Perun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin (Wotan)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berry1996p20_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Berry1996p20-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Vedas, Indra is the king of <a href="/wiki/Svarga" title="Svarga">Svarga</a> (Heaven) and the <a href="/wiki/Deva_(Hinduism)" title="Deva (Hinduism)">Devas</a>. He is the god of lightning, thunder, storms, rains and river flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Indra is the most referred to <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> in the <i>Rigveda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonda1989p3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gonda1989p3-7">[7]</a></sup> He is celebrated for his powers, and the one who kills the great symbolic evil (<a href="/wiki/Asura" title="Asura">Asura</a>) named <a href="/wiki/Vritra" title="Vritra">Vritra</a> who obstructs human prosperity and happiness. Indra destroys Vritra and his "deceiving forces", and thereby brings rains and the sunshine as the friend of mankind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berry1996p20_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Berry1996p20-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His importance diminishes in the post-Vedic Indian literature where he is depicted as a powerful hero but one who is getting in trouble with his drunken, hedonistic and adulterous ways, and the god who disturbs Hindu monks as they meditate because he fears self-realized human beings may become more powerful than him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berry1996p20_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Berry1996p20-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Buddhism, Indra has been a popular deity, referred by many names and particularly <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aakra_(Buddhism)" title="Śakra (Buddhism)">Shakra</a></i> (Pali: <i>Sakka</i>). He is featured in Buddhism somewhat differently than Hinduism, such as being shown as less war oriented and one paying homage to the Buddha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buswelllopez739_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-buswelllopez739-10">[10]</a></sup> Indra rules over the much sought <i>Devas</i> realm of rebirth within the <a href="/wiki/Sa%E1%B9%83s%C4%81ra_(Buddhism)" title="Saṃsāra (Buddhism)">Samsara</a> doctrine of Buddhist traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buswelllopez739_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-buswelllopez739-10">[10]</a></sup> However, like the Hindu texts, Indra also is a subject of ridicule and reduced to a figurehead status in Buddhist texts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donigerindra_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-donigerindra-11">[11]</a></sup> shown as a god that suffers rebirth and redeath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buswelllopez739_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-buswelllopez739-10">[10]</a></sup> In the Jainism traditions, like Buddhism and Hinduism, Indra is the king of gods and a part of Jain rebirth cosmology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He is also the god who appears with his wife Indrani to celebrate the auspicious moments in the life of a Jain <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">Tirthankara</a>, an iconography that suggests the king and queen of gods reverentially marking the spiritual journey of a Jina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wileyj99_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wileyj99-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra's iconography shows him wielding a lightning thunderbolt known as <i><a href="/wiki/Vajra" title="Vajra">Vajra</a></i>, riding on a <a href="/wiki/White_elephant" title="White elephant">white elephant</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Airavata" title="Airavata">Airavata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In Buddhist iconography the elephant sometimes features three heads, while Jaina icons sometimes show the elephant with five heads. Sometimes a single elephant is shown with four symbolic tusks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup> Indra's heavenly home is on or near <a href="/wiki/Mount_Meru" title="Mount Meru">Mount Meru</a> (also called Sumeru).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buswelllopez739_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-buswelllopez739-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkings188252_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkings188252-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The etymological roots of Indra are unclear, and it has been a contested topic among scholars since the 19th-century, one with many proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCller1903p395_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCller1903p395-18">[18]</a></sup> The significant proposals have been:</p>
<ul>
<li>root <i>ind-u</i>, or "rain drop", based on the Vedic mythology that he conquered rain and brought it down to earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCller1903p395_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCller1903p395-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>root <i>ind</i>, or "equipped with great power". This was proposed by Vopadeva.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>root <i>idh</i> or "kindle", and <i>ina</i> or "strong".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griswold1971p177_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griswold1971p177-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>root <i>indha</i>, or "igniter", for his ability to bring light and power (<i>indriya</i>) that ignites the vital forces of life (<i>prana</i>). This is based on <a href="/wiki/Shatapatha_Brahmana" title="Shatapatha Brahmana">Shatapatha Brahmana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>root <i>idam-dra</i>, or "It seeing" which is a reference to the one who first perceived the self-sufficient metaphysical <a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a>. This is based on <a href="/wiki/Aitareya_Upanishad" title="Aitareya Upanishad">Aitareya Upanishad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>roots in ancient Indo-European, Indo-Aryan deities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colarusso329_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Colarusso329-22">[22]</a></sup> For example, states John Colarusso, as a reflex of proto-Indo-European <i>*ə2n-(ə)r-</i>, Greek <i>anēr</i>, Sabine <i>nerō</i>, Avestan <i>nar-</i>, Umbrian <i>nerus</i>, Old Irish <i>nert</i>, Ossetic <i>nart</i>, and others which all refer to "most manly" or "hero".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colarusso329_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Colarusso329-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Colonial era scholarship proposed that Indra shares etymological roots with <i>Zend Andra</i> derived from Old High German <i>Antra</i>, or <i>Jedru</i> of Old Slavonic, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Muller" title="Max Muller">Max Muller</a> critiqued these proposals as untenable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCller1903p395_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCller1903p395-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Later scholarship has linked Vedic Indra to the European <i>Aynar</i> (the Great One), <i>Abaza</i>, <i>Ubykh</i> and <i>Innara</i> of Hittite mythology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colarusso329_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Colarusso329-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Colarusso suggests a Pontic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[note 1]</a></sup> origin and that both the phonology and the context of Indra in Indian religions is best explained from Indo-Aryan roots and a <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassian</a> etymology (i.e. <i>*inra</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colarusso329_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Colarusso329-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra is also called <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śakra</span> frequently in the Vedas and in Buddhism (<a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>: <i>Sakka</i>). He is known in <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a> as <span lang="my" xml:lang="my">သိကြားမင်း</span>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Burmese" title="Help:IPA/Burmese">[ðadʑá mɪ́ɴ]</a></span>; in <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> as <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">พระอินทร์</span> <i>Phra In</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a> as <span lang="km" xml:lang="km">ព្រះឥន្ទ្រា</span> <small>pronounced</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Khmer" title="Help:IPA/Khmer">[preah ʔəntraa]</a></span>, in <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> as <i>Indera</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a> as <span lang="jv" xml:lang="jv">ꦧꦛꦫꦲꦶꦤ꧀ꦢꦿ</span> <i>Bathara Indra</i>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kannada_language" title="Kannada language">Kannada</a> as <span lang="kn" xml:lang="kn">ಇಂದ್ರ</span> <i>Indra</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a> as <span lang="te" xml:lang="te">ఇంద్రుడు</span> <i>Indrudu</i> or <i>Indra</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malayalam_language" title="Malayalam language">Malayalam</a> as <span lang="ml" xml:lang="ml">ഇന്ദ്രന്</span> <i>Indran</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a> as <span lang="ta" xml:lang="ta">இந்திரன்</span> <i>Inthiran</i>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> as 帝释天 <i>Dìshìtiān</i>, and in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> as <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">帝釈天</span> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taishakuten" title="Taishakuten">Taishakuten</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra has many epithets in the Indian religions, notably Śakra (शक्र, powerful one), Vṛṣan (वृषन्, mighty), Vṛtrahan (वृत्रहन्, slayer of <a href="/wiki/Vritra" title="Vritra">Vṛtra</a>), Meghavāhana (मेघवाहन, he whose cloud is vehicle), Devarāja (देवराज, king of deities), Devendra (देवेन्द्र, the lord of deities),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkings188253_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkings188253-27">[26]</a></sup> Surendra (सुरेन्द्र, chief of deities), Svargapati (स्वर्गपति, the lord of heaven), Vajrapāṇī (वज्रपाणि, he who has thunderbolt (Vajra) in his hand) and Vāsava (वासव, lord of Vasus).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Indra is of ancient but unclear origin. Aspects of Indra as a deity are cognate to other Indo-European gods; they are either <a href="/wiki/List_of_thunder_gods" title="List of thunder gods">thunder gods</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perun" title="Perun">Perun</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> who share parts of his heroic mythologies, act as king of gods, and all are linked to "rain and thunder".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> The similarities between Indra of Hindu mythologies and of <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a> of Nordic and Germanic mythologies are significant, states Max Muller. Both Indra and Thor are storm gods, with powers over lightning and thunder, both carry hammer or equivalent, for both the weapon returns to their hand after they hurl it, both are associated with bulls in the earliest layer of respective texts, both use thunder as a battle-cry, both are heroic leaders, both protectors of mankind, both are described with legends about "milking the cloud-cows", both are benevolent giants, gods of strength, of life, of marriage and the healing gods, both are worshipped in respective texts on mountains and in forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Michael Janda suggests that Indra has origins in the Indo-European <i>*trigw-welumos</i> [or rather <i>*trigw-t-welumos</i>] "smasher of the enclosure" (of <a href="/wiki/Vritra" title="Vritra">Vritra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vala_(Vedic)" title="Vala (Vedic)">Vala</a>) and <i>diye-snūtyos</i> "impeller of streams" (the liberated rivers, corresponding to Vedic <i>apam ajas</i> "agitator of the waters").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> Brave and heroic Innara or Inra, which sounds like Indra, is mentioned among the gods of the <a href="/wiki/Mitanni" title="Mitanni">Mitanni</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hurrians" title="Hurrians">Hurrian-speaking people</a> of Hittite region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra as a deity had a presence in northeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_minor" title="Asia minor">Asia minor</a>, as evidenced by the inscriptions on the Boghaz-köi clay tablets dated to about 1400 BCE. This tablet mentions a treaty, but its significance is in four names it includes reverentially as <i>Mi-it-ra</i>, <i>U-ru-w-na</i>, <i>In-da-ra</i> and <i>Na-sa-at-ti-ia</i>. These are respectively, Mitra, Varuna, Indra and Nasatya-Asvin of the Vedic pantheon as revered deities, and these are also found in Avestan pantheon but with Indra and Naonhaitya as demons. This at least suggests that Indra and his fellow deities were in vogue in South Asia and Asia minor by about mid 2nd-millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griswold1971p177_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Griswold1971p177-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra is praised as the highest god in 250 hymns of the <i><a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">Rigveda</a></i> – a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> scripture dated to have been composed sometime between 1700 and 1100 BCE. He is co-praised as the supreme in another 50 hymns, thus making him one of the most celebrated Vedic deities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griswold1971p177_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Griswold1971p177-19">[19]</a></sup> He is also mentioned in ancient Indo-Iranian literature, but with a major inconsistency when contrasted with the Vedas. In the Vedic literature, Indra is a heroic god. In the Avestan (ancient, pre-Islamic Iranian) texts such as <i><a href="/wiki/Vendidad" title="Vendidad">Vd.</a></i> 10.9, <i><a href="/wiki/Denkard" title="Denkard">Dk.</a></i> 9.3 and <i><a href="/wiki/Bundahishn" title="Bundahishn">Gbd</a></i> 27.6-34.27, Indra – or accurately Andra<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCller1897p757_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCller1897p757-33">[32]</a></sup> – is a gigantic demon who opposes truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colarusso329_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Colarusso329-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[note 2]</a></sup> In the Vedic texts, Indra kills the archenemy and demon Vritra who threatens mankind. In the Avestan texts, Vritra is not found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCller1897p757_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCller1897p757-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra is called <i>vrtrahan-</i> (literally, "slayer of Vritra demon") in the Vedas, which corresponds to <a href="/wiki/Verethragna" title="Verethragna">Verethragna</a> of the Zoroastrian noun <i>verethragna-</i>. According to David Anthony, the Old Indic religion probably emerged among Indo-European immigrants in the contact zone between the <a href="/wiki/Zeravshan_River" title="Zeravshan River">Zeravshan River</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>) and (present-day) Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007462_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007462-35">[33]</a></sup> It was "a syncretic mixture of old Central Asian and new Indo-European elements",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007462_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007462-35">[33]</a></sup> which borrowed "distinctive religious beliefs and practices"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200932_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200932-36">[34]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Bactria%E2%80%93Margiana_Archaeological_Complex" title="Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex">Bactria–Margiana Culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200932_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200932-36">[34]</a></sup> At least 383 non-Indo-European words were borrowed from this culture, including the god Indra and the ritual drink <a href="/wiki/Soma_(drink)" title="Soma (drink)">Soma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007454-455_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007454-455-37">[35]</a></sup> According to Anthony,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Many of the qualities of Indo-Iranian god of might/victory, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verethraghna" title="Verethraghna">Verethraghna</a>, were transferred to the adopted god Indra, who became the central deity of the developing Old Indic culture. Indra was the subject of 250 hymns, a quarter of the <i>Rig Veda</i>. He was associated more than any other deity with <i>Soma</i>, a stimulant drug (perhaps derived from <i>Ephedra</i>) probably borrowed from the BMAC religion. His rise to prominence was a peculiar trait of the Old Indic speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007454_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnthony2007454-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Indra was a prominent deity in the Vedic era of Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griswold1971p177_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Griswold1971p177-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vedic_texts">Vedic texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vedic texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over a quarter of the 1,208 hymns of the <i>Rigveda</i> mention Indra, making him the most referred to deity than any other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griswold1971p177_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-Griswold1971p177-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106-39">[37]</a></sup> These hymns present a complex picture of Indra, but some aspects of Indra are oft repeated. Of these, the most common theme is where he as the god with thunderbolt kills the evil serpent Vritra that held back rains, and thus released rains and land nourishing rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCller1903p395_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCller1903p395-18">[18]</a></sup> For example, the Rigvedic hymn 1.32 dedicated to Indra reads:</p>
<table role="presentation" style="margin-left:3em;">
<tr style="vertical-align: top;">
<td>
<div class="poem" lang="" style="font-style:roman;text-align:left" xml:lang="">
<p><span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">इन्द्रस्य नु वीर्याणि प्र वोचं यानि चकार प्रथमानि वज्री ।<br/>
अहन्नहिमन्वपस्ततर्द प्र वक्षणा अभिनत्पर्वतानाम् ॥१।।<br/>
अहन्नहिं पर्वते शिश्रियाणं त्वष्टास्मै वज्रं स्वर्यं ततक्ष ।<br/>
वाश्रा इव धेनवः स्यन्दमाना अञ्जः समुद्रमव जग्मुरापः ॥२।।</span></p>
</div>
</td>
<td style="padding-left: 2em;">
<div class="poem">
<p>Let me tell you the manly deeds of Indra, which he first accomplished, bolt-weaponed,<br/>
He slew the serpent, opened up waters, cleft in twain the belly of mountains, ॥1।।<br/>
He slew the serpent on the mountain, with heavenly bolt made by Tvastar,<br/>
Like lowing cattle downward sped the waters, then flowed to the ocean. ॥2।।<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
</div>
</td>
</tr>
<tr style="vertical-align: top; font-size:88%">

<td style="padding-left: 2em;"></td>

<p>The hymns of <i>Rigveda</i> declare him to be the "king that moves and moves not", the friend of mankind who holds the different tribes on earth together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup> In one interpretation by Oldenberg, the hymns are referring to the snaking thunderstorm clouds that gather with bellowing winds (Vritra), Indra is then seen as the storm god who intervenes in these clouds with his thunderbolts, which then release the rains nourishing the parched land, crops and thus humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griswold180_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-griswold180-43">[41]</a></sup> In another interpretation by Hillebrandt, Indra is a symbolic sun god (<a href="/wiki/Surya" title="Surya">Surya</a>) and Vritra is a symbolic winter-giant (historic mini cycles of ice age, cold) in the earliest, not the later, hymns of <i>Rigveda</i>. The Vritra is an ice-demon of colder central Asia and northern latitudes, who holds back the water. Indra is the one who releases the water from the winter demon, an idea that later metamorphosed into his role as storm god.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griswold180_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-griswold180-43">[41]</a></sup> According to Griswold, this is not a completely convincing interpretation, because Indra is simultaneously a lightning god, a rain god and a river-helping god in the Vedas. Further, the Vritra demon that Indra slew is best understood as any obstruction, whether it be clouds that refuse to release rain or mountains or snow that hold back the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griswold180_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-griswold180-43">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even though Indra is declared as the king of gods in some verses, there is no consistent subordination of other gods to Indra. In Vedic thought, all gods and goddesses are equivalent and aspects of the same eternal abstract <a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a>, none consistently superior, none consistently inferior. All gods obey Indra, but all gods also obey Varuna, Vishnu, Rudra and others when the situation arises. Further, Indra also accepts and follows the instructions of Savitr (sun-god).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keith1925p93_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keith1925p93-44">[42]</a></sup> Indra, like all Vedic deities, is a part of <a href="/wiki/Henotheism" title="Henotheism">henotheistic</a> theology of ancient India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra is not a visible object of nature in the Vedic texts, nor is he a personification of any object, but that agent which causes the lightning, the rains and the rivers to flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup> His myths and adventures in the Vedic literature are numerous, ranging from harnessing the rains, cutting through mountains to help rivers flow, helping land becoming fertile, unleashing sun by defeating the clouds, warming the land by overcoming the winter forces, winning the light and dawn for mankind, putting milk in the cows, rejuvenating the immobile into something mobile and prosperous, and in general, he is depicted as removing any and all sorts of obstacles to human progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup> The Vedic prayers to Indra, states <a href="/wiki/Jan_Gonda" title="Jan Gonda">Jan Gonda</a>, generally ask "produce success of this rite, throw down those who hate the materialized <a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra is often presented as the twin brother of <a href="/wiki/Agni" title="Agni">Agni</a> (fire) – another major Vedic deity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup> Yet, he is also presented to be the same, states Max Muller, as in Rigvedic hymn 2.1.3, which states, "Thou Agni, art Indra, a bull among all beings; thou art the wide-ruling Vishnu, worthy of adoration. Thou art the Brahman, (...)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[48]</a></sup> He is also part of one of many Vedic trinities as "Agni, Indra and Surya", representing the "creator-maintainer-destroyer" aspects of existence in Hindu thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106-39">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[note 3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upanishads">Upanishads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Upanishads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ancient <i><a href="/wiki/Aitareya_Upanishad" title="Aitareya Upanishad">Aitareya Upanishad</a></i> equates Indra, along with other deities, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atman_(Hinduism)" title="Atman (Hinduism)">Atman</a> (soul, self) in the Vedanta's spirit of internalization of rituals and gods. It begins with its cosmological theory in verse 1.1.1 by stating that, "in the beginning, Atman, verily one only, was here - no other blinking thing whatever; he bethought himself: let me now create worlds".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deussen1997p15_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deussen1997p15-56">[53]</a></sup> This soul, which the text refers to as Brahman as well, then proceeds to create the worlds and beings in those worlds wherein all Vedic gods and goddesses such as sun-god, moon-god, Agni and other divinities become active cooperative organs of the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deussen1997p15_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deussen1997p15-56">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[55]</a></sup> The Atman thereafter creates food, and thus emerges a sustainable non-sentient universe, according to the Upanishad. The eternal Atman then enters each living being making the universe full of sentient beings, but these living beings fail to perceive their Atman. The first one to see the Atman as Brahman, asserts the Upanishad, said, "<i>idam adarsha</i> or "I have seen It".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deussen1997p15_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Deussen1997p15-56">[53]</a></sup> Others then called this first seer as <i>Idam-dra</i> or "It-seeing", which over time came to be cryptically known as "Indra", because, claims <i>Aitareya Upanishad</i>, everyone including the gods like short nicknames.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[56]</a></sup> The passing mention of Indra in this Upanishad, states Alain Daniélou, is a symbolic folk etymology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The section 3.9 of the <i><a href="/wiki/Brihadaranyaka_Upanishad" title="Brihadaranyaka Upanishad">Brihadaranyaka Upanishad</a></i> connects Indra to thunder, thunderbolt and release of waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[57]</a></sup> In section 5.1 of the <i><a href="/wiki/Avyakta_Upanishad" title="Avyakta Upanishad">Avyakta Upanishad</a></i>, Indra is praised as he who is embodies the qualities of all gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106-39">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-vedic_texts">Post-vedic texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-vedic texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In post-Vedic texts, Indra is depicted as an intoxicated hedonistic god, his importance declines, and he evolves into a minor deity in comparison to others in the Hindu pantheon, such as <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Devi" title="Devi">Devi</a>. In medieval Hindu texts, Indra is an aspect of <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p106-39">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is depicted as the father of <a href="/wiki/Vali_(Ramayana)" title="Vali (Ramayana)">Vali</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Arjuna" title="Arjuna">Arjuna</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donigerindra_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-donigerindra-11">[11]</a></sup> He becomes a source of nuisance rains in the Puranas, out of anger and with an intent to hurt mankind. But, Krishna as an avatar of Vishnu, comes to the rescue by lifting Mount Govardhana on his fingertip, and letting mankind shelter under the mountain till Indra exhausts his anger and relents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donigerindra_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-donigerindra-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Sangam_literature_(300_BCE–300_AD)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sangam_literature_.28300_BCE.E2.80.93300_AD.29">Sangam literature (300 BCE–300 AD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sangam literature (300 BCE–300 AD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil language</a> contains more stories about Indra by various authors. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silapathikaram" title="Silapathikaram">Silapathikaram</a> Indra is described as <i>Maalai venkudai mannavan</i> (மாலைவெண் குடை மன்னவன்), literally meaning Indra with the pearl-garland and white umbrella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a> also describes Indhira Vizha (festival for Indra), the festival for want of rain, celebrated for one full month starting from the full moon in Ootrai (later name – Cittirai) and completed on the full moon in Puyaazhi (Vaikaasi) (which coincides with Buddhapurnima). It is described in the epic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cilapatikaram" title="Cilapatikaram">Cilapatikaram</a> in detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_other_gods">Relations with other gods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relations with other gods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Hindu religion, he is married to Shachi, also known as Indrani or Pulomaja.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkings1882_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkings1882-63">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indra and Shachi have a son, <a href="/wiki/Jayanta" title="Jayanta">Jayanta</a>, and daughters called <a href="/wiki/Jayanti_(Hinduism)" title="Jayanti (Hinduism)">Jayanti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devasena" title="Devasena">Devasena</a>. Goddess Jayanti is the spouse of <a href="/wiki/Shukra" title="Shukra">Shukra</a>, while goddess Devasena marries the war-god <a href="/wiki/Kartikeya" title="Kartikeya">Kartikeya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalal2010_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalal2010-64">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <i>Brahmavaivarta Purana</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[62]</a></sup> Indra defeats Vṛtrá and releases the waters. Indra asks Vishvakarma to build him a palace, but ultimately decides to leave his life of luxury to become a hermit and seek wisdom. Horrified, Indra's wife Shachi asks the priest Brihaspati to change her husband's mind. He teaches Indra to see the virtues of both the spiritual life and the worldly life. Thus, at the end of the story, Indra learns how to pursue wisdom while still fulfilling his kingly duties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In <i>Rigveda</i>, Indra is described as strong willed, armed with a thunderbolt, riding a chariot:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>May the strong Heaven make thee the Strong wax stronger: Strong, for thou art borne by thy two strong Bay Horses. So, fair of cheek, with mighty chariot, mighty, uphold us, strong-willed, thunder armed, in battle.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>RigVeda, Book 5, Hymn XXXVI: Griffith<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.sacred-texts.com.2Fhin.2Frigveda.2Frv05036.htm_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.sacred-texts.com.2Fhin.2Frigveda.2Frv05036.htm-66">[63]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Indra's weapon, which he used to kill evil Vritra, is the <a href="/wiki/Vajra" title="Vajra">Vajra</a> or thunderbolt. Other alternate iconographic symbolism for him includes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_(weapon)" title="Bow (weapon)">bow</a> (sometimes as a colorful rainbow), a sword, a <a href="/wiki/Indra%27s_net" title="Indra's net">net</a>, a noose, a hook, or a conch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p110_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p110-67">[64]</a></sup> The thunderbolt of Indra is called Bhaudhara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madan_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madan-68">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the post-Vedic period, he rides a large, four-tusked white elephant called <a href="/wiki/Airavata" title="Airavata">Airavata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup> In sculpture and relief artworks in temples, he typically sits on an elephant or is near one. When he is shown to have two, he holds the Vajra and a bow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Shatapatha_Brahmana" title="Shatapatha Brahmana">Shatapatha Brahmana</a></i> and in Shaktism traditions, Indra is stated to be same as goddess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shodashi" title="Shodashi">Shodashi</a> (Tripura Sundari), and her iconography is described similar to those of Indra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbow</a> is called Indra's <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_arrow" title="Bow and arrow">Bow</a> (Sanskrit: <i>indradhanusha</i> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">इन्द्रधनुष</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p110_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p110-67">[64]</a></sup></p>

<p>Indra is a popular guardian deity in Buddhism, who protects Buddhist teachings and believers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baroni2002p153_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baroni2002p153-2">[2]</a></sup> He is commonly found in the Buddhist art works in both the Theravada and Mahayana traditions all over South Asia, Southeast Asia and East Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoltKinnard2012p45_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoltKinnard2012p45-71">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowellDavis1969p2_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowellDavis1969p2-72">[69]</a></sup> He also appears extensively in ancient and medieval Buddhist literature such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Buddhacarita" title="Buddhacarita">Buddhacarita</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahavamsa" title="Mahavamsa">Mahavamsa</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baroni2002p153_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baroni2002p153-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoltKinnard2012p45_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-HoltKinnard2012p45-71">[68]</a></sup> Indra is the god who urges the Buddha to go teach mankind, after he had achieved his enlightenment under a bodhi tree but had doubts whether he should share his knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baroni2002p153_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baroni2002p153-2">[2]</a></sup> Other texts depict Indra as the most devoted of divine disciples of the Buddha, who listens and follows the Buddha, and the deity who renders assistance to the Buddha and his followers on numerous occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buswelllopez739_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-buswelllopez739-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Buddhist cosmology places Indra above Mount Sumeru, in Trayastrimsha heaven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baroni2002p153_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Baroni2002p153-2">[2]</a></sup> He resides and rules over one of the six realms of rebirth, the <i>Devas</i> realm of <a href="/wiki/Sa%E1%B9%83s%C4%81ra_(Buddhism)" title="Saṃsāra (Buddhism)">Saṃsāra</a>, that is widely sought in the Buddhist tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrainor200462_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrainor200462-73">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[note 4]</a></sup> Rebirth in the realm of Indra is a consequence of very good <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">Karma</a> (Pali: <i>kamma</i>) and accumulated merit during a human life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, Indra is commonly called by his other name, <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aakra_(Buddhism)" title="Śakra (Buddhism)">Śakra</a> or Sakka, ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Tr%C4%81yastri%E1%B9%83%C5%9Ba" title="Trāyastriṃśa"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Trāyastriṃśa</span></a> heaven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoltKinnard2012p45_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-HoltKinnard2012p45-71">[68]</a></sup> Śakra is sometimes referred to as <i>Devānām Indra</i> or "Lord of the Devas". Buddhist texts also refer to Indra by numerous names and epithets, as is the case with Hindu and Jain texts. For example, Asvaghosha's <i>Buddhacarita</i> in different sections refers to Indra with terms such as "the thousand eyed",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis19695.2C_21_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis19695.2C_21-78">[74]</a></sup> <i>Puramdara</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis196944_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis196944-79">[75]</a></sup> <i>Lekharshabha</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis196971_footnote_1_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis196971_footnote_1-80">[76]</a></sup> <i>Mahendra</i>, <i>Marutvat</i>, <i>Valabhid</i> and <i>Maghavat</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis1969205_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEE._B._CowellFrancis_A._Davis1969205-81">[77]</a></sup> Elsewhere, he is known as <i>Devarajan</i> (literally, "the king of gods"). These names reflect a large overlap between Hinduism and Buddhism, and the adoption of many Vedic terminology and concepts into Buddhist thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[78]</a></sup> Even the term <i>Śakra</i>, which means "mighty", appears in the Vedic texts such as in hymn 5.34 of the <i>Rigveda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Dani.C3.A9lou1991p108-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[79]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bimaran_Casket" title="Bimaran Casket">Bimaran Casket</a> made of gold inset with garnet, dated to be around 60 CE, but some proposals dating it to the 1st century BCE, is among the earliest archaeological evidences available that establish the importance of Indra in Buddhist mythology. The artwork shows the Buddha flanked by gods <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a> and Indra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopez37_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-lopez37-84">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, he is known by the characters 帝釋天 (Chinese: 釋提桓因, pinyin: shì dī huán yīn, Korean: "Je-seok-cheon" or 桓因 <i>Hwan-in</i>, Japanese: "Tai-shaku-ten", <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a>: 帝釈天). In Japan, Indra always appears opposite <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a> (<b>梵天</b>, Japanese: "Bonten") in Buddhist art. Brahma and Indra are revered together as protectors of the historical Buddha (Chinese: <b>釋迦</b>, <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a>: 釈迦, also known as <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Shakyamuni</a>), and are frequently shown giving the infant Buddha his first bath. Although Indra is often depicted like a <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattva</a> in the Far East, typically in <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> costume, his iconography also includes a martial aspect, wielding a thunderbolt from atop his elephant mount.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huayan_school" title="Huayan school">Huayan school</a> of Buddhism and elsewhere, the image of <a href="/wiki/Indra%27s_net" title="Indra's net">Indra's net</a> is a metaphor for the emptiness of all things.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>, the legend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tirta_Empul_Temple" title="Tirta Empul Temple">Tirta Empul Temple</a> origin is related to Indra. The sacred spring was created by the Indra, whose soldiers were poisoned at one time by Mayadanawa. Indra pierced the earth to create a fountain of immortality to revive them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Japan, Indra is one of the twelve Devas, as guardian deities, who are found in or around Buddhist temples (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%8D%81%E4%BA%8C%E5%A4%A9" title="ja:十二天">Jūni-ten</a>, 十二天).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[83]</a></sup> In Japan, Indra has been called "Taishaku-ten".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biswas184_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-biswas184-88">[84]</a></sup> He joins these other eleven Devas of Buddhism, found in Japan and other parts of southeast Asia: Indra (Taishaku-ten), Agni (Ka-ten), Yama (Enma-ten), Nirrti (Rasetsu-ten), Vayu (Fu-ten), Ishana (Ishana-ten), Kubera (Tamon-ten), Varuna (Sui-ten), Brahma (Bon-ten), Prithvi (Chi-ten), Surya (Nit-ten), Chandra (Gat-ten).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biswas184_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-biswas184-88">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ceremonial name of <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> claims that the city was "given by Indra and built by <a href="/wiki/Vishvakarman" title="Vishvakarman">Vishvakarman</a>." The provincial seal of <a href="/wiki/Surin_Province" title="Surin Province">Surin Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> is an image of Indra atop <a href="/wiki/Airavata" title="Airavata">Airavata</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jainism">Jainism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jainism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:338px;max-width:338px">
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:112px;max-width:112px">

<p>Indra in <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jain</a> mythology always serves the <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">Tirthankara</a> teachers. Indra most commonly appears in stories related to Tirthankaras, in which Indra himself manages and celebrates the <a href="/wiki/Panch_Kalyanaka" title="Panch Kalyanaka">five auspicious events</a> in that Tirthankara's life, such as Chavan kalyanak, <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">Janma</a> kalyanak, <a href="/wiki/Diksha" title="Diksha">Diksha</a> kalyanak, <a href="/wiki/Kevala_Jnana" title="Kevala Jnana">Kevala Jnana</a> kalyanak, and <a href="/wiki/Moksha_(Jainism)" title="Moksha (Jainism)">moksha</a> kalyanak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoswamy2014245_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoswamy2014245-91">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are sixty four Indras in Jaina literature, each ruling over different heavenly realms where heavenly souls who have not yet gained Kaivalya (<a href="/wiki/Moksha" title="Moksha">moksha</a>) are reborn according to Jainism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wileyj99_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wileyj99-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owenj64_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-owenj64-92">[88]</a></sup> Among these many Indras, the ruler of the first Kalpa heaven is the Indra who is known as <i>Saudharma</i> in <a href="/wiki/Digambara" title="Digambara">Digambara</a>, and <i>Sakra</i> in <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Av%C4%93t%C4%81mbara" title="Śvētāmbara">Śvētāmbara</a> tradition. He is most preferred, discussed and often depicted in Jaina caves and marble temples, often with his wife Indrani.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owenj64_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-owenj64-92">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[89]</a></sup> They greet the devotee as he or she walks in, flank the entrance to an idol of <a href="/wiki/Arihant_(Jainism)" title="Arihant (Jainism)">Jina</a> (conqueror), and lead the gods as they are shown celebrating the five auspicious moments in a Jina's life, including his birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wileyj99_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wileyj99-13">[13]</a></sup> These Indra-related stories are enacted by laypeople in Jainism tradition during special Puja (worship) or festive remembrances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wileyj99_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wileyj99-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Jaina <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a> for Indra, he is sometimes represented to be dancing, just like the <a href="/wiki/Nataraja" title="Nataraja">Nataraja</a> – the classic Hindu icon for a dancing Shiva. Some symbolic overlaps between Jainism and Hinduism are widespread with regards to Indra mythologies and iconography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>In south Indian <a href="/wiki/Digambara" title="Digambara">Digambara</a> Jaina community, Indra is also the title of hereditary priests who preside over Jain temple functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wileyj99_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wileyj99-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
