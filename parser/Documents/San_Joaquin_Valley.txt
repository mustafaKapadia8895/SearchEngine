<p>The <b>San Joaquin Valley</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/hw/: 'wh' in 'why'">hw</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> is the area of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a> of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> that lies south of the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento%E2%80%93San_Joaquin_River_Delta" title="Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta">Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta</a> and is drained by the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_River" title="San Joaquin River">San Joaquin River</a>. It comprises seven <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a> counties—all of <a href="/wiki/Kings_County,_California" title="Kings County, California">Kings County</a>; a majority of <a href="/wiki/Fresno_County,_California" title="Fresno County, California">Fresno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merced_County,_California" title="Merced County, California">Merced</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_County,_California" title="Stanislaus County, California">Stanislaus</a> counties; segments of <a href="/wiki/Madera_County,_California" title="Madera County, California">Madera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tulare_County,_California" title="Tulare County, California">Tulare</a> counties—and a majority of <a href="/wiki/Kern_County,_California" title="Kern County, California">Kern County</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Although a majority of the valley is rural, it does contain cities such as <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">Stockton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modesto,_California" title="Modesto, California">Modesto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turlock,_California" title="Turlock, California">Turlock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porterville,_California" title="Porterville, California">Porterville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visalia,_California" title="Visalia, California">Visalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merced,_California" title="Merced, California">Merced</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hanford,_California" title="Hanford, California">Hanford</a>.</p>
<p>San Joaquin Valley was originally inhabited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yokuts_people" title="Yokuts people">Yoktus</a>- and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miwok_people" title="Miwok people">Miwok peoples</a>. The first European to enter the valley was <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fages" title="Pedro Fages">Pedro Fages</a> in 1772.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The San Joaquin Valley extends from the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento%E2%80%93San_Joaquin_River_Delta" title="Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta">Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta</a> in the north to the <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Mountains" title="Tehachapi Mountains">Tehachapi Mountains</a> in the south, and from the various California coastal ranges (from the <a href="/wiki/Diablo_Range" title="Diablo Range">Diablo Range</a> in the north to the <a href="/wiki/Temblor_Range" title="Temblor Range">Temblor Range</a> in the south) in the west to the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> in the east. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Valley" title="Sacramento Valley">Sacramento Valley</a>, the river system for which the San Joaquin Valley is named does not extend very far along the valley. Most of the valley south of Fresno, instead, drains into <a href="/wiki/Tulare_Lake" title="Tulare Lake">Tulare Lake</a>, which no longer exists continuously due to diversion of its sources. The valley's primary river is the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_River" title="San Joaquin River">San Joaquin</a>, which drains north through about half of the valley into the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento%E2%80%93San_Joaquin_River_Delta" title="Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta">Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kings_River_(California)" title="Kings River (California)">Kings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kern_River" title="Kern River">Kern</a> Rivers are in the southern <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basin</a> of the valley, all of which have been largely diverted for agricultural uses and are mostly dry in their lower reaches.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_history">Geological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The San Joaquin Valley began to form about 66 million years ago during the early Paleocene era. Broad fluctuations in the sea level caused various areas of the valley to be flooded with ocean water for the next 60 million years. About 5 million years ago, the marine outlets began to close due to uplift of the coastal ranges and the deposition of sediment in the valley. Starting 2 million years ago, a series of glacial episodes periodically caused much of the valley to become a fresh water lake. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Corcoran" title="Lake Corcoran">Lake Corcoran</a> was the last widespread lake to fill the valley about 700,000 years ago. At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> there were three major lakes remaining in the southern part of the Valley, <a href="/wiki/Tulare_Lake" title="Tulare Lake">Tulare Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buena_Vista_Lake" title="Buena Vista Lake">Buena Vista Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kern_Lake_(Kern_County)" title="Kern Lake (Kern County)">Kern Lake</a>. In the late 19th and in the 20th century, agricultural diversion of the <a href="/wiki/Kern_River" title="Kern River">Kern River</a> eventually dried out these lakes. Today, only a fragment of Buena Vista Lake remains as two small lakes <a href="/wiki/Lake_Webb_(California)" title="Lake Webb (California)">Lake Webb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Evans_(California)" title="Lake Evans (California)">Lake Evans</a> in a portion of the former Buena Vista Lakebed</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_California#Drought" title="Climate change in California">Climate change in California § Drought</a></div>
<p>The San Joaquin Valley has hot, dry summers and has historically enjoyed cool rainy winters characterized by dense <a href="/wiki/Tule_fog" title="Tule fog">tule fog</a>. Its rainy season normally runs from November through April, but since 2011 when a drought became evident it generally received minimal to no rain at all. The drought was still extant by mid-August 2014 with scientists saying it would likely continue indefinitely, for anywhere from several years to several decades to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valley_Drought_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Valley_Drought-3">[3]</a></sup> However, as of February 2017 the drought is mostly over for a majority of the Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2015, the Director of the <a href="/wiki/California_Department_of_Water_Resources" title="California Department of Water Resources">California Department of Water Resources</a> stated, "Because of increased pumping, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> levels are reaching record lows—up to 100 feet lower than previous records."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dwrsink_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dwrsink-5">[5]</a></sup> Research from NASA shows that parts of the San Joaquin Valley sank as much as 8 inches (0.20 m) in a four-month period, and land near <a href="/wiki/Corcoran,_California" title="Corcoran, California">Corcoran</a> sank 13 inches (0.33 m) in 8 months. The sinking has destroyed thousands of groundwater well casings and has the potential to damage aqueducts, roads, bridges, and flood-control structures. In the long term, the <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groundwater_exploitation" title="Groundwater exploitation">extracting groundwater</a> could irreversibly reduce the underground aquifer's water storage capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dwrsink_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-dwrsink-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> Forecast Office for the San Joaquin Valley is located in <a href="/wiki/Hanford,_California" title="Hanford, California">Hanford</a> and includes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_weather_radar" title="Doppler weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a>. Weather forecasts and climatological information for the San Joaquin Valley are available from its official website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The total population of the eight counties comprising the San Joaquin Valley at the time of the 2011-2015 American Community Survey 5-year Estimates by United States Census Bureau reported a population of 4,080,509.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> The racial composition of San Joaquin Valley was 2,775,074 (68.0%) White, 193,694 (4.7%) Black or African American, 40,911 (1.0%) American Indian and Alaska Native, 310, 557 (7.6%) Asian, 13,000 (0.32%) Native Hawaiian and other Pacific Islander, and 2,048,280 (50.2%) Hispanic or Latino.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The educational attainment of high school graduate or higher is 72.7%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By some estimates, federal restrictions on shallow well irrigation systems threaten the productivity of the San Joaquin Valley, which produces the majority of the 12.8% of the United States' agricultural production (as measured by dollar value) that comes from California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapes" title="Grapes">Grapes</a>—table, <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">raisin</a>, and to a lesser extent wine—are perhaps the valley's highest-profile product, but equally (if not more) important are <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, nuts (especially <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pistachio" title="Pistachio">pistachios</a>), <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a>, and vegetables. Though it has been called "The food basket of the World", the San Joaquin Valley has not been nationally recognized for the diversity of its produce. Walnuts, oranges, peaches, garlic, tangerines, tomatoes, kiwis, hay, alfalfa and numerous other crops have been harvested with great success. DeRuosi Nut, a large walnut processing plant in <a href="/wiki/Escalon,_California" title="Escalon, California">Escalon</a>, has been in the valley since 1947.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Certain places are identified quite strongly with a given crop: Stockton produces the majority of the domestic <a href="/wiki/Asparagus" title="Asparagus">asparagus</a> consumed in the United States, and Fresno is the largest producer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raisins" title="Raisins">raisins</a>.</p>
<p>In spite of its agricultural productivity, the San Joaquin Valley has the state's highest rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_insecurity" title="Food insecurity">food insecurity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MakingContact_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MakingContact-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cattle and sheep ranching are also vitally important to the valley's economy. During the late 19th century and early 20th century, the Miller &amp; Lux corporation built an agricultural monopoly centered around cattle. The corporation can be characterized as a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Corporate_farming" title="Corporate farming">corporate farming</a> transforming the yeoman farmer into wage workers. The success of the business can be attributed to his direct management style which is reflected in his detailed correspondences to his subordinates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> During recent years, dairy farming has greatly expanded in importance. As areas such as <a href="/wiki/Chino,_California" title="Chino, California">Chino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corona,_California" title="Corona, California">Corona</a> have become absorbed into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban">suburban</a> sprawl of Los Angeles, many dairy farmers have cashed out and moved their herds to Kings, Tulare, and Kern counties.</p>
<p>Between 1990 and 2004, 28,092 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hectares" title="Hectares">hectares</a> (70,231 acres) of agricultural land was lost to urban development in the San Joaquin Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Although there have been some token efforts at confronting the problems of (sub)urban sprawl, the politically Conservative climate of the Valley generally prefers traditional suburban sprawl type of growth such as low density housing and strip malls anchored by so-called big box stores and opposes measures such as "Smart Growth", "Transit Oriented Development", "High Density Housing", and increased public transit such as light and commuter rail. By August 2014, a three-year drought was prompting changes to the agriculture industry in the valley. Farmers began using complex irrigation systems and using treated waste water to feed crops, while many were switching from farming cotton to other commodities, chief among them, almonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valley_Drought_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Valley_Drought-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum">Petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Petroleum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>California has long been one of the nation's most important oil-producing states, and the San Joaquin Valley has long since eclipsed the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Basin" title="Los Angeles Basin">Los Angeles Basin</a> as the state's primary oil production region. Scattered oil wells on small <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a> are found throughout the region, and several enormous extraction facilities – most notably near <a href="/wiki/Lost_Hills,_California" title="Lost Hills, California">Lost Hills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taft,_California" title="Taft, California">Taft</a>, including the enormous <a href="/wiki/Midway-Sunset_Oil_Field" title="Midway-Sunset Oil Field">Midway-Sunset Oil Field</a>, the third-largest oil field in the United States – are veritable forests of pumps.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Shell</a> operated a major refinery in Bakersfield; it was sold in 2005 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_J_(1968%E2%80%932010)" title="Flying J (1968–2010)">Flying J</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_Utah" title="Salt Lake City, Utah">Salt Lake</a>-based firm that operates truck stops and refineries. Flying J's bankruptcy in 2009 resulted in the refinery being shut down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oil and gas fields in Kern County are receiving increased attention since the July 2009 announcement by Occidental Petroleum of significant discovery of oil and gas reserves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Even prior to this discovery the region retains more oil reserves than any other part of California. Of California fields outside of the San Joaquin Valley, only the <a href="/wiki/Wilmington_Oil_Field" title="Wilmington Oil Field">Wilmington Oil Field</a> in Los Angeles County has untapped reserves greater than 100,000,000 barrels (16,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>), while six fields in the San Joaquin Valley (Midway-Sunset, <a href="/wiki/Kern_River_Oil_Field" title="Kern River Oil Field">Kern River</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Belridge_Oil_Field" title="South Belridge Oil Field">South Belridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elk_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Elk Hills Oil Field">Elk Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cymric_Oil_Field" title="Cymric Oil Field">Cymric</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lost_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Lost Hills Oil Field">Lost Hills</a>) each have reserves exceeding 100,000,000 barrels (16,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The San Joaquin Valley has been a major influence in American <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nu_metal" title="Nu metal">nu metal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a> particularly through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakersfield_Sound" title="Bakersfield Sound">Bakersfield Sound</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doowop" title="Doowop">Doowop</a> <a href="/wiki/Era" title="Era">Era</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_hip_hop_genres" title="List of hip hop genres">Bakersfield Rap Scene</a>, also referred to as <a href="/wiki/List_of_hip_hop_genres" title="List of hip hop genres">Central California Hip Hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_hip_hop_genres" title="List of hip hop genres">Central Valley Hip Hop</a>, or <a href="/wiki/List_of_hip_hop_genres" title="List of hip hop genres">Central California Rap</a>, which are all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenres" title="Subgenres">subgenres</a> of <a href="/wiki/List_of_hip_hop_genres" title="List of hip hop genres">Gangsta rap</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_hip_hop_genres" title="List of hip hop genres">Bass-Rap</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-Funk" title="G-Funk">West Coast G-funk</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Coast_Hip_hop" title="West Coast Hip hop">West Coast Hip hop</a> which are all different styles of West Coast Rap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Valley has been the home to many country, nu metal, and doo-wop musicians and singers, such as <a href="/wiki/Buck_Owens" title="Buck Owens">Buck Owens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard" title="Merle Haggard">Merle Haggard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Mize" title="Billy Mize">Billy Mize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korn" title="Korn">Korn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Simpson" title="Red Simpson">Red Simpson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Payne" title="Dennis Payne">Dennis Payne</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Maddox_Brothers_and_Rose" title="Maddox Brothers and Rose">Maddox Brothers and Rose</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Paradons" title="The Paradons">The Paradons</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Colts_(Vocal_Group)" title="The Colts (Vocal Group)">The Colts</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_the_San_Joaquin" title="Sons of the San Joaquin">Sons of the San Joaquin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The San Joaquin Valley is also home to many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indie_Hip_Hop" title="Indie Hip Hop">Indie Hip Hop</a> labels, R&amp;B singers, Jazz &amp; Funk musicians, and Hip Hop artist such as: <a href="/wiki/Fashawn" title="Fashawn">Fashawn</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Moment_of_Truth_(C-Bo_and_Killa_Tay_album)" title="The Moment of Truth (C-Bo and Killa Tay album)">Killa Tay</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Def_Dames" title="The Def Dames">The Def Dames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cali_Agents" title="Cali Agents">Cali Agents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet_Asia" title="Planet Asia">Planet Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Valley also has a strong literary tradition, heavy in poetry, producing many famous poets such as <a href="/wiki/Sherley_Anne_Williams" title="Sherley Anne Williams">Sherley Anne Williams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gary_Soto" title="Gary Soto">Gary Soto</a>, current U.S. Poet Laureate <a href="/wiki/Juan_Felipe_Herrera" title="Juan Felipe Herrera">Juan Felipe Herrera</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_St._John" title="David St. John">David St. John</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> An MFA Creative Writing program started by former US Poet Laureate Philip Levine, resides at Fresno State, continuing to cultivate the region's literary talent.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_major_industries_and_employers">Other major industries and employers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other major industries and employers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The isolation and vastness of the San Joaquin Valley, as well as its poverty and need for jobs, have led the state to build numerous <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prisons</a> in the area. The most notable of these is <a href="/wiki/Corcoran,_California" title="Corcoran, California">Corcoran</a>, whose inmates include <a href="/wiki/Charles_Manson" title="Charles Manson">Charles Manson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Corona" title="Juan Corona">Juan Corona</a>. Other correctional facilities in the valley are at <a href="/wiki/Avenal,_California" title="Avenal, California">Avenal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chowchilla,_California" title="Chowchilla, California">Chowchilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tracy,_California" title="Tracy, California">Tracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delano,_California" title="Delano, California">Delano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coalinga,_California" title="Coalinga, California">Coalinga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wasco,_California" title="Wasco, California">Wasco</a>.</p>
<p>The only significant military base in the region is <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Lemoore" title="Naval Air Station Lemoore">Naval Air Station Lemoore</a>, a vast air base located 25 kilometres (16 mi) WSW of <a href="/wiki/Hanford,_California" title="Hanford, California">Hanford</a>. Unlike many of California's other military installations, NAS Lemoore's operational importance has increased in the 1990s and 2000s. The other, <a href="/wiki/Castle_Air_Force_Base" title="Castle Air Force Base">Castle Air Force Base</a>, located near <a href="/wiki/Atwater,_California" title="Atwater, California">Atwater</a> was closed during the <a href="/wiki/Base_Realignment_and_Closure" title="Base Realignment and Closure">Base Realignment and Closure</a> of the 1990s. Although both are in Kern County, <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China_Lake_Naval_Air_Weapons_Station" title="China Lake Naval Air Weapons Station">China Lake Naval Air Weapons Station</a> are located in the <a href="/wiki/High_Desert_(California)" title="High Desert (California)">High Desert</a> area of that county.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty">Poverty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Poverty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> issued a report entitled the <a href="/wiki/American_Community_Survey" title="American Community Survey">American Community Survey</a> in 2007, which found that six San Joaquin Valley counties had the highest percentage of residents living below the federal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a> in 2006 of any counties in California. The report also revealed that the same six counties were among the 52 counties with the highest poverty rate in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The median income for a household in the valley was $46,713.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> The poverty rate for individuals below the poverty level is 23.7%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> In most Valley cities crime rates such as burglary, theft, and assault tend to be significantly higher than the national averages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_and_cultural_groups">Ethnic and cultural groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ethnic and cultural groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Mexicans/Chicanos"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexicans.2FChicanos">Mexicans/Chicanos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mexicans/Chicanos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1249" data-file-width="833" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg/170px-Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg/255px-Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg/340px-Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cesar_chavez_crop2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>While the <i>barrios</i> of <a href="/wiki/East_Los_Angeles,_California" title="East Los Angeles, California">East Los Angeles</a> are California's most famous areas dominated by persons of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> ancestry, both first-generation Mexican immigrants and well-established <a href="/wiki/Chicano" title="Chicano">Chicanos</a> are important populations in the San Joaquin Valley. Since not long after the onset of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bracero" title="Bracero">bracero</a></i> program during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, all but a minor percentage of the farmworkers in the region have been of Mexican ancestry. Ethnic and economic friction between Mexican-Americans and the valley's predominantly white farming elite manifested itself most notably during the 1960s and 1970s, when the <a href="/wiki/United_Farm_Workers" title="United Farm Workers">United Farm Workers</a>, led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="César Chávez">César Chávez</a>, went on numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_strike" title="Labor strike">strikes</a> and called for boycotts of table grapes. The UFW generated enormous sympathy throughout the United States, even managing to terminate several agricultural mechanization projects at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a>. However, from the 1970s onward, landlords and large corporations have also hired <a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigration" title="Illegal immigration">undocumented immigrants</a>, because of the ability to work long hours for low pay due to no income tax on undocumented salaries.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="European_and_Asian_groups">European and Asian groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: European and Asian groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The San Joaquin Valley has—by California standards—an unusually large number of European, Middle Eastern, and Asian ethnicities in the heritage of its citizens. These communities are often quite large and, relative to Americans immigration patterns, quite eclectic: for example, there are more <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azorean</a> Portuguese in the San Joaquin Valley than in the Azores. Many groups are found in majorities in specific cities, and hardly anywhere else in the region. For example, <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a> are concentrated in <a href="/wiki/Turlock,_California" title="Turlock, California">Turlock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ripon,_California" title="Ripon, California">Ripon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikhs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">Stockton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Livingston,_California" title="Livingston, California">Livingston</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croatian_American" title="Croatian American">Croats</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Delano,_California" title="Delano, California">Delano</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kingsburg,_California" title="Kingsburg, California">Kingsburg</a> is famous for its distinctly <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> air, having been founded by immigrants from that country. Ethnic groups found in a broader area are <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">Basques</a>, and the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okies" title="Okies">Okies</a>" who migrated to California from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_South" title="American South">South</a>. Since the early 1970s East Indians of predominantly Punjabi, Gujrati and Southern India have settled in the valley communities. Most recently large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Pakistanis" title="Pakistanis">Pakistanis</a> have settled in Modesto and <a href="/wiki/Lodi,_California" title="Lodi, California">Lodi</a>. In addition, the late 1970s and 80s saw an influx of immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Vietnam" title="War in Vietnam">War in Vietnam</a>. These immigrants, the majority of whom are <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thai_people" title="Thai people">Thai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lao_people" title="Lao people">Laotian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khmer_people" title="Khmer people">Cambodian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Vietnamese</a>, have settled in the communities of Stockton, Modesto, <a href="/wiki/Merced,_California" title="Merced, California">Merced</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_American" title="Filipino American">Filipino American</a> population is concentrated in Delano and <a href="/wiki/Lathrop,_California" title="Lathrop, California">Lathrop</a>. Filipinos have a strong history in Stockton. Filipino organizations in Stockton are reflected in various commercial buildings identified as Filipino. Filipinos fought for the U.S. against Japan in WWII, in exchange for favorable immigration status. Stockton has been an adjunct to the San Francisco Bay Area, which was a major military production and transit area during WWII. Filipino emigration to Stockton followed.</p>
<p>These cultures are often the result of established ethnic communities and groups of immigrants coming to the United States at once. This is in part due to the founding of religious communes in the San Joaquin Valley: for example, the first permanent Sikh <a href="/wiki/Gurdwara" title="Gurdwara">Gurdwara</a> was founded in Stockton in 1915. These cultures are very different and unique.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="African_Americans">African Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: African Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Colonel_Allensworth_State_Historic_Park" title="Colonel Allensworth State Historic Park">Colonel Allensworth State Historic Park</a> marks the location of the only California town to be founded, financed and governed by African Americans. The small farming community was founded in 1908 by Lt. Colonel <a href="/wiki/Allen_Allensworth" title="Allen Allensworth">Allen Allensworth</a>, Professor William Payne, William Peck, a minister, John W. Palmer, a miner, and Harry A. Mitchell, a real estate agent, dedicated to improving the economic and social status of African Americans. Uncontrollable circumstances, including a drop in the area's water table, resulted in the town's demise. The "Allensworth Historic District" is listed on the National Register of Historic Places. The park is in <a href="/wiki/Allensworth,_California" title="Allensworth, California">Allensworth, California</a>, an unincorporated area in Tulare County, California, United States.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Okies_and_Arkies">Okies and Arkies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Okies and Arkies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Depression</a>-era migrants to the San Joaquin Valley from the South and Midwest are one of the more well-known groups in the Central Valley, in large part due to the popularity of <a href="/wiki/John_Steinbeck" title="John Steinbeck">John Steinbeck</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grapes_of_Wrath" title="The Grapes of Wrath">The Grapes of Wrath</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fonda" title="Henry Fonda">Henry Fonda</a> movie made from it. By 1910, agriculture in the southern <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> had become nearly unviable due to soil erosion and poor rainfall. Much of the rural population of states such as <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> left at this time, selling their land and moving to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit</a>, and fast-growing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a>. Those who remained experienced continuing deterioration of conditions, which reached their nadir during the drought that began in the late 1920s and created the infamous <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a>. (Small cotton farmers in states such as <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> suffered similar problems from the first major infestation of the <a href="/wiki/Boll_weevil" title="Boll weevil">boll weevil</a>.) When the onset of the Great Depression created a national banking crisis, family farmers—usually heavily in debt—often had their mortgages foreclosed by banks desperate to shore up their balance sheets. In response, many farmers loaded their families and portable possessions into their automobiles and drove west.</p>
<p>Taking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Route_66_(California)" title="U.S. Route 66 (California)">Route 66</a> to <a href="/wiki/Barstow,_California" title="Barstow, California">Barstow</a> or Los Angeles and crossing the <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Pass" title="Tehachapi Pass">Tehachapi</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tejon_Pass" title="Tejon Pass">Tejon</a> passes, they began new lives as fruit and vegetable pickers on truck farms in the San Joaquin Valley. Having gone from the relative independence of <a href="/wiki/Homesteading" title="Homesteading">homesteading</a> to a condition that was essentially peasantry, many of them lived in squalid agricultural camps and were deeply unhappy with their economic plight; domestic disputes, crime, and suicide were rampant, and occasional riots broke out. <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> measures alleviated some of these problems, albeit belatedly: by the time that <i>The Grapes of Wrath</i> drew public attention to the Okies' plight, many of them had already left the valley. Those who didn't were gradually assimilated into California culture and society where many of them and their descendants became noted artists, tradesmen, educators, legislators and professional business people, and their influence remains strong in many parts of the Valley today, especially in the south near Bakersfield.</p>
<p>Many of the Okies and Arkies left the San Joaquin Valley during World War II, most of them going to Los Angeles, San Francisco and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" title="San Diego, California">San Diego</a> to work in war related industries. Many of those who stayed ended up in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakersfield" title="Bakersfield">Bakersfield</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oildale" title="Oildale">Oildale</a>, as the southern San Joaquin Valley became an important area of oil production after major Southern California oil fields such as <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach_Oil_Field" title="Long Beach Oil Field">Signal Hill</a> began to dry up. Their influence remains strong: Bakersfield resembles a <a href="/wiki/West_Texas" title="West Texas">West Texas</a> town such as <a href="/wiki/Odessa,_Texas" title="Odessa, Texas">Odessa</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lubbock,_Texas" title="Lubbock, Texas">Lubbock</a> far more than it resembles most other places in California. <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">Country music</a> legends <a href="/wiki/Buck_Owens" title="Buck Owens">Buck Owens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard" title="Merle Haggard">Merle Haggard</a> came out of Bakersfield's <a href="/wiki/Honky-tonk" title="Honky-tonk">honky-tonk</a> scene and created a hard-driving sound that is still deeply associated with the city.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_changes">Recent changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Recent changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The California real estate boom that began in the late 1990s has significantly changed the San Joaquin Valley. Once distinctly and fiercely independent of Los Angeles and San Francisco, the area has seen increasing <a href="/wiki/Commuter_town" title="Commuter town">exurban</a> development as the cost of living forces young families and small businesses further and further away from the coastal urban cores. Stockton, Modesto, Tracy, Manteca, and Los Banos are increasingly dominated by commuters to San Francisco and Silicon Valley, and the small farming towns to the south are finding themselves in the Bay Area's orbit as well. Bakersfield, traditionally a boom-bust oil town once described by urban scholar <a href="/wiki/Joel_Kotkin" title="Joel Kotkin">Joel Kotkin</a> as an "American <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>," has seen a massive influx of former Los Angeles business owners and commuters, to the extent that <a href="/wiki/Gated_community" title="Gated community">gated communities</a> containing million-dollar homes are going up on the city's outskirts. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a>, <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a>, <a href="/wiki/CVS_Pharmacy" title="CVS Pharmacy">CVS Pharmacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restoration_Hardware" title="Restoration Hardware">Restoration Hardware</a>, and other various large shipping firms have built huge distribution centers both in the southern end of the valley and northern part of the valley because of quick access to major interstates and low local wages. Further integration with the rest of the state is likely to continue for the foreseeable future.</p>
<p>See: <a href="/wiki/Mountain_House,_San_Joaquin_County,_California" title="Mountain House, San Joaquin County, California">Mountain House</a> (new town planned for 45,000).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_5_(California)" title="Interstate 5 (California)">Interstate 5</a> (I-5) and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_99" title="California State Route 99">State Route 99</a> (SR 99, or just "99") each run along for almost the entire length of the San Joaquin Valley. I-5 runs in the western part of the valley, bypassing the major population centers (including Fresno, currently the largest U.S. city without an Interstate highway), while 99 runs through them. Both highways then merge at the southern end of valley en route to Los Angeles. When the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a> was created in the 1950s, the decision was made to build I-5 as an entirely then-new freeway bypass instead of upgrading the then-existing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._99" title="U.S. 99">U.S. 99</a>. Since then, state and federal representatives have pushed to convert 99 to an Interstate, although this cannot occur until all of the portions of 99 between I-5 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._50" title="U.S. 50">U.S. 50</a> junction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento" title="Sacramento">Sacramento</a> are upgraded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeway" title="Freeway">freeway</a> standards.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_58" title="California State Route 58">State Route 58</a> (SR 58), which is a freeway in Bakersfield and along most of its route until its terminus in <a href="/wiki/Barstow,_California" title="Barstow, California">Barstow</a>, is an extremely important and very heavily traveled route for truckers from the valley and the Bay Area who want to cross the Sierra Nevada and leave California (by way of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_15_(California)" title="Interstate 15 (California)">Interstate 15</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_40_(California)" title="Interstate 40 (California)">Interstate 40</a>) without having to climb <a href="/wiki/Donner_Pass" title="Donner Pass">Donner Pass</a> or brave the traffic congestion of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. Proposals have also been made to designate this highway as a western extension of I-40 once the entirety of the route between <a href="/wiki/Mojave,_California" title="Mojave, California">Mojave</a> and Barstow has been upgraded to a freeway. This would provide an Interstate connection for Bakersfield, currently the second-largest U.S. city without an Interstate. The most recent additions to this system are State Highway 168 and 180. Route 168 begins at Fresno on Route 180 linking to Huntington Lake in the mountains through Clovis and many smaller communities. This route is part of the California Freeway and Expressway System[2] and is eligible for the State Scenic Highway System[3]. State Route 180 is a state highway in California, United States, which runs through the heart of the San Joaquin Valley from Mendota through Fresno to Kings Canyon National Park. A short piece near the eastern end, through the Grant Grove section of Kings Canyon National Park, is not state-maintained. The part east of unbuilt State Route 65 near Minkler is eligible for the State Scenic Highway System; the road east of Dunlap is the Kings Canyon Scenic Byway, a Forest Service Byway.</p>
<p>Other important highways in the valley include <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_46" title="California State Route 46">State Route 46</a> (SR 46) and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_41" title="California State Route 41">State Route 41</a> (SR 41), which respectively link the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_Central_Coast" title="California Central Coast">California Central Coast</a> with Bakersfield and Fresno; <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_33" title="California State Route 33">State Route 33</a>, which runs south to north along the valley's western rim and provides a connection to <a href="/wiki/Ventura,_California" title="Ventura, California">Ventura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Ynez_Mountains" title="Santa Ynez Mountains">Santa Ynez Mountains</a>; and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_152" title="California State Route 152">State Route 152</a> (SR 152), an important commuter route linking <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> with its fast-growing exurbs such as <a href="/wiki/Los_Banos,_California" title="Los Banos, California">Los Banos</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> provides rail service through the San Joaquin Valley. There are also plans for a <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail" title="High-speed rail">high-speed rail</a> line that will link the valley with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" title="San Diego, California">San Diego</a>. While many valley politicians and businesses are eager supporters of the line, eager to provide better connections to the larger and wealthier cities to the north and south, factions in cities such as Modesto and Stockton have opposed the line due to adverse impacts such as increased noise.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Even if the project were to be approved, construction would probably not begin until 2013 at the earliest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_Pollution">Air Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Air Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hemmed in by mountains and rarely having strong winds to disperse <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>, the San Joaquin Valley has long suffered from some of the United States' worst <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>. This pollution, exacerbated by stagnant weather, comes mainly from <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> fueled vehicles and agricultural operations. Population growth has caused the San Joaquin Valley to rank with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a> in most measures of air pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimespollution_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimespollution-22">[22]</a></sup> Only the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inland_Empire_(California)" title="Inland Empire (California)">Inland Empire</a> region east of Los Angeles has worse overall air quality, and the San Joaquin Valley led the nation in 2004 in the number of days with quantities of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> considered unhealthy by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimespollution_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimespollution-22">[22]</a></sup> Although industrial activity, as well as driving, occurs year-round, the air pollution is worse in the winter. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_County" title="San Joaquin County">San Joaquin County</a> has better air quality than any other region in the San Joaquin Valley, while the Sacramento region and Stanislaus County have the worst.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_Pollution">Water Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Water Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Less water is due to Federal restrictions on shallow water irrigation which results in concentrated Water pollution. Air pollution is not properly diluted with adequate water supply. Lack of adequate irrigation presents another significant challenge to food production capability in the valley. Because of federal restrictions on irrigation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_salination" title="Soil salination">soil salination</a> is no longer diluted with adequate moisture in fertile areas. Reduced irrigation has significantly reduced the viability of some of the valley's most fertile tracts, especially those in the Tulare lake bed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrates_in_Groundwater">Nitrates in Groundwater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Nitrates in Groundwater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>San Joaquin Valley, within the Central Valley of California, is made up of eight counties that are well known for agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-23">[23]</a></sup> Overuse of nitrogen fertilizers and irrigated agriculture is common, and according to Thomas Harter, the Chair for Water Resources Management and Policy at UC Davis, “more than 80 pounds of nitrogen per acre per year may leach into groundwater beneath irrigated lands, usually as nitrates”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Between the 1950s and 1980s, when nitrogen fertilizer use grew sixfold, nitrate concentrations in groundwater increased 2.5 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Fertilizer runoffs contributes roughly 90% of all nitrate inputs to the alluvial groundwater system. Within agriculture, the two major factors are <i>High-Intensity Crop Production</i> and <i>Large Dairy Herds.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Because these communities are cut off from larger water distribution, they are dependent on wells,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-:32-27">[27]</a></sup> making groundwater a source of drinking water for 90% of San Joaquin Valley's residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="High-intensity_crop_production">High-intensity crop production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: High-intensity crop production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Within the past century, farmers have been increasing their production to meet the high demand that a developed country requires. To help increase output and efficiency, farmers have been increasing the amount of fertilizers used, which means increasing the level of nitrates being used. However, only a fraction of the nitrogen in fertilizers is efficiently used to help produce crops. This has led to a greater concentration of nitrates and phosphates in the waters, contaminating and causing eutrophication of possible drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Large_dairy_herds">Large dairy herds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Large dairy herds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Roaming dairy herds before the exponential demand of meat products and dairy has contributed an insignificant amount of nitrate pollution to the underlying groundwater systems. However, within the past few decades, the increasing amount of cattle has been one of the main contributors of nitrate contamination in the groundwater systems of California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Roughly around 1960, cattle were openly grazing pastures, and because of the large amount of lands which they roamed manure was not intensively managed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, even though manure was not closely managed, "Nitrogen excretion and deposition in pastures likely did not exceed pasture buffering capacity and had no significant leaching to groundwater"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-:42-32">[32]</a></sup> It was not until the mid 1970s when the transition to dry-lot and free stall-based dairy farming, coupled with irrigated forage crops, that dairy herds were a contributor to nitrogen contamination.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_solutions_and_alternatives">Possible solutions and alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Possible solutions and alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The large dairy herds create manure, which is used to create the fertilizers that is applied to the crop fields. Because of the exponential demand for crops, farmers have been looking to lower the costs of production. Using manure based fertilizers is cost effective since manure is a by-product of large dairy farms and herds. The alternative to manure, which contains a high level of nitrates, is composting. Composting however is relatively expensive compared to using manure as fertilizer, since it is not as effective and is more timely/costly to make because of the large amount of aeration needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_of_Nitrates_in_Groundwater">Incidents of Nitrates in Groundwater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Incidents of Nitrates in Groundwater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Nitrates have found their way into the aquifers around the San Joaquin Valley, affecting over 250,000 people in communities that are poor and rural.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-:32-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2006, the State Water Resources Board took samples from domestic wells in Tulare County; they found that 40% of 181 domestic wells had nitrate levels above the 10 mg/L legal limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:43_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:43-34">[34]</a></sup> Though locals have typically used filters for their water, the filters need to be installed correctly and replaced frequently, which may not be economically feasible for the residents in Orosi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Table 2 on page 20 from <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Institute" title="Pacific Institute">Pacific Institute</a>'s "The Human Costs of Nitrate-contaminated Drinking Water in the San Joaquin Valley" indicates the water systems that were contaminated with nitrates over the legal limit, the percentage of the population affected that are non-white and that are below or near poverty-level, and the year since the violations began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:43_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:43-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Turlock_Basin" title="Turlock Basin">Turlock Basin</a> is a sub-basin of the San Joaquin Valley groundwater basin found within Stanislaus County and Merced County in the Central Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-36">[36]</a></sup> In California's Groundwater Bulletin 118, a chart, linked below, illustrates the number of non-compliant public supply wells with nitrates over the MCL. According to this chart, there were eight wells which had nitrate levels above the 10 mg/L MCL, out of 90 sampled for nitrates in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">Nitrates</a> are of concern because it interferes with the blood's ability to carry oxygen, and can have severe health effects on pregnant women, infants under 6 months, and children using tap water for their formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-37">[37]</a></sup> Because nitrates interfere with blood's capacity to carry oxygen, infants are at high risk of death from <a href="/wiki/Blue_baby_syndrome" title="Blue baby syndrome">blue-baby syndrome</a>, which can occur when there are high nitrate levels in the blood that are untreated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_interest">Medical interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Joaquin_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Medical interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>San Joaquin Valley Fever</b> is one common term for <a href="/wiki/Coccidioidomycosis" title="Coccidioidomycosis">coccidioidomycosis</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungal" title="Fungal">fungal</a> infection caused by <i>Coccidioides immitis</i> through the <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inhalation</a> of airborne dust or dirt. The valley is just one of the areas in the southwestern United States where the illness is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> due to C. immitis residing in the soil.</p>
