<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">United States Hydrographic Office</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Navy_Hydrographic_Office&amp;redirect=no" title="United States Navy Hydrographic Office">United States Navy Hydrographic Office</a>)</span></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>United States Hydrographic Office</b> prepared and published maps, charts, and nautical books required in <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>. The office was established by an act of 21 June 1866 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Navigation_(United_States_Navy)" title="Bureau of Navigation (United States Navy)">Bureau of Navigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Navy" title="United States Department of the Navy">Department of the Navy</a>. It was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> on 10 August 1949. The office was abolished on 10 July 1962, replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Oceanographic_Office" title="Naval Oceanographic Office">Naval Oceanographic Office</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERecords..._Archives_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERecords..._Archives-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Hydrographic_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the hydrographic office was established in 1866, U.S. navigators were almost entirely dependent on British charts. A few private enterprises had prepared and published charts, but had not been able to do so profitably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE1874_Annual_report...17_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE1874_Annual_report...17-2">[2]</a></sup> The Hydrographic Office was established "for the improvement of the means for navigating safely the vessels of the Navy and of the mercantile marine, by providing, under the authority of the Secretary of the Navy, accurate and cheap nautical charts, sailing directions, navigators and manuals of instructions for the use of all vessels of the United States, and for the benefit and use of navigators generally".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnited_States_Congress_188664_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnited_States_Congress_188664-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Hydrographic_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The impetus for establishing the Hydrographical Office came from a petition submitted to Congress in 1863 by the American Shipmasters Association. A Senate committee prepared a report, and a Senate bill was passed on 24 June 1864. The purpose was to empower the Navy Department to give navy and merchant ships the results of surveys and explorations by naval officers in foreign waters. The office was not envisioned as being a rival to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Hydrographic_Office" title="United Kingdom Hydrographic Office">British Admiralty hydrographic office</a> or the French depot of charts, but as an office that could publish charts and directions where there was sufficient information available, priced to cover the cost of paper and printing but not the cost of preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPresident_of_the_USA_1866108.E2.80.93109_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPresident_of_the_USA_1866108.E2.80.93109-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1873 the office prepared the instruments needed to determine by using the electric telegraph the longitude of West Indian islands and of points on the northern coast of South America where telegraph cables had been laid. A survey of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> had found many errors. Some surveying had been carried out in the Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAmerican_Geog._Society_1874246_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAmerican_Geog._Society_1874246-5">[5]</a></sup> In July 1875 the Commodore responsible for the office, describing the work that had been accomplished in the previous year, called for a permanent building with proper fireproofing instead of the temporary rented premises, and asked for funding to conduct a proper survey of the Pacific Ocean, for which the charts were in many areas inadequate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE1874_Annual_report...72.E2.80.9374_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE1874_Annual_report...72.E2.80.9374-6">[6]</a></sup> That year a great deal had been achieved in charting the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE1874_Annual_report...15_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE1874_Annual_report...15-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1880 the office was divided into the Division of Archives, Chart Division, Meteorological Division, Division of Drafting and Engraving and Division of Longitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENaval_encyclop.C3.A6dia_1880354_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENaval_encyclop.C3.A6dia_1880354-8">[8]</a></sup> The office had published about 700 charts of foreign coasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENaval_encyclop.C3.A6dia_1880355_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENaval_encyclop.C3.A6dia_1880355-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1881 the office employed 22 naval officers and 28 civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELamphere1881195_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELamphere1881195-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>An 1889 report described the function of the office as mainly being reconnaissance of foreign coasts, office duties and publication of compiled maps. The Coast and Geodetic Survey of the Treasury Department was responsible for the systematic hydrographic survey of the coasts of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeographical_Surveys_1889468_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeographical_Surveys_1889468-11">[11]</a></sup> That year the office employed 39 officers and 40 civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeographical_Surveys_1889475_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeographical_Surveys_1889475-12">[12]</a></sup> The office gave out instruments for meteorological observations to the masters of vessels willing to record and report their findings, requiring only that they take reasonable care of the instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWheatley1890763_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWheatley1890763-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1894 the hydrographic office paid the sum of $20,000 for patents taken out by a former employee for engraving machines, which would greatly reduce the time and cost of engraving soundings, compasses and border shadings. A number of these machines were in use by 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung188820.E2.80.9321_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung188820.E2.80.9321-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1946 the U.S. Navy Hydrographic Office published an ice atlas covering the North American Arctic on a scale of about 1:20,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOffice_of_Naval_Research_195822_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOffice_of_Naval_Research_195822-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1954 aerial observation of sea ice moved from development into operations, with navy weathermen trained by the Hydrographic Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOffice_of_Naval_Research_1958265_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOffice_of_Naval_Research_1958265-16">[16]</a></sup> Information was transmitted to the Hydrographic office, which prepared forecasts used in planning movement of shipping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOffice_of_Naval_Research_1958266_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOffice_of_Naval_Research_1958266-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
