<p><b>Hephaestus</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hephaestus#Alternative_forms" title="wikt:Hephaestus">eight spellings</a>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἥφαιστος</span> <i>Hēphaistos</i>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_god" title="Greek god">Greek god</a> of <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenters" title="Carpenters">carpenters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craftsmen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">fire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> Hephaestus' <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman</a> equivalent is <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a>. In Greek mythology, Hephaestus was the son of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>, the king and queen of the gods. In another version, he was Hera's <a href="/wiki/Parthenogenesis" title="Parthenogenesis">parthenogenous</a> child, rejected by his mother because of his deformity and thrown off Mount Olympus and down to earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a smithing god, Hephaestus made all the weapons of the gods in Olympus. He served as the blacksmith of the gods, and was worshipped in the manufacturing and industrial centers of Greece, particularly <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. The cult of Hephaestus was based in <a href="/wiki/Lemnos" title="Lemnos">Lemnos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> Hephaestus' symbols are a smith's <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a>, and a pair of <a href="/wiki/Tongs" title="Tongs">tongs</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hephaestus is probably associated with the <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenean_Greek" title="Mycenean Greek">Mycenean Greek</a>) inscription <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐀀𐀞𐀂𐀴𐀍</span>, <i>A-pa-i-ti-jo</i>, found at Knossos; the inscription indirectly attests his worship at that time because it is believed that it reads the <a href="/wiki/Theophoric_name" title="Theophoric name">theophoric name</a> <i>Haphaistios</i> or <i>Haphaistion</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chawick_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chawick-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnthologyM_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnthologyM-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The name of the god in Greek (<i>Hēphaistos</i>) has a root which can be observed in names of places of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Greek" title="Pre-Greek">Pre-Greek</a> origin, like <a href="/wiki/Phaistos" title="Phaistos">Phaistos</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (<i>Pa-i-to</i> in <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epithets">Epithets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Epithets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hephaestus is given many <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithets</a>. The meaning of each epithet is:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Amphigúeis</b> "the lame one" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀμφιγύεις</span>)</li>
<li><b>Kullopodíōn</b> "the halting" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Κυλλοποδίων</span>)</li>
<li><b>Khalkeús</b> "coppersmith" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Χαλκεύς</span>)</li>
<li><b>Klutotékhnēs</b> "renowned artificer" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Κλυτοτέχνης</span>)</li>
<li><b>Polúmētis</b> "shrewd, crafty" or "of many devices" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Πολύμητις</span>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aetnaeus" title="Aetnaeus">Aitnaîos</a></b> "Aetnaean" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Αἰτναῖος</span>), owing to his workshop being supposedly located below <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Aetna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Craft_of_Hephaestus">Craft of Hephaestus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Craft of Hephaestus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Hephaestus had his own palace on Olympus, containing his workshop with anvil and twenty bellows that worked at his bidding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Hephaestus crafted much of the magnificent equipment of the gods, and almost any finely wrought metalwork imbued with powers that appears in Greek myth is said to have been forged by Hephaestus. He designed <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a>' winged helmet and <a href="/wiki/Talaria" title="Talaria">sandals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aegis" title="Aegis">Aegis</a> <a href="/wiki/Breastplate" title="Breastplate">breastplate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a>'s famed <a href="/wiki/Girdle" title="Girdle">girdle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agamemnon" title="Agamemnon">Agamemnon</a>'s staff of office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a>' armor, <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>' bronze <a href="/wiki/Clapper_(musical_instrument)" title="Clapper (musical instrument)">clappers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">Helios</a>'s chariot, the shoulder of <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">Pelops</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eros_(mythology)" title="Eros (mythology)">Eros</a>'s bow and arrows. In later accounts, Hephaestus worked with the help of the <a href="/wiki/Chthonic" title="Chthonic">chthonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cyclops" title="Cyclops">Cyclopes</a>—among them his assistants in the forge, Brontes, Steropes and Pyracmon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graves_1960_150_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graves_1960_150-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hephaestus built <a href="/wiki/Automaton" title="Automaton">automatons</a> of metal to work for him. This included tripods that walked to and from <a href="/wiki/Mount_Olympus" title="Mount Olympus">Mount Olympus</a>. He gave to the blinded <a href="/wiki/Orion_(mythology)" title="Orion (mythology)">Orion</a> his apprentice <a href="/wiki/Cedalion" title="Cedalion">Cedalion</a> as a guide. In some versions of the myth<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a> stole the fire that he gave to man from Hephaestus's forge. Hephaestus also created the gift that the gods gave to man, the woman <a href="/wiki/Pandora" title="Pandora">Pandora</a> and her <a href="/wiki/Pithos" title="Pithos">pithos</a>. Being a skilled blacksmith, Hephaestus created all the thrones in the Palace of Olympus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graves_1960_150_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graves_1960_150-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Greek myths and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeric" title="Homeric">Homeric</a> poems sanctified in stories that Hephaestus had a special power to produce motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He made the golden and silver lions and dogs at the entrance of the palace of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkinoos" title="Alkinoos">Alkinoos</a> in such a way that they could bite the invaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Greeks maintained in their civilization an animistic idea that statues are in some sense alive. This kind of art and the animistic belief goes back to the <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> period, when <a href="/wiki/Daedalus" title="Daedalus">Daedalus</a>, the builder of the <a href="/wiki/Labyrinth" title="Labyrinth">labyrinth</a>, made images which moved of their own accord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A statue of the god was somehow the god himself, and the image on a man's tomb indicated somehow his presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parentage">Parentage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parentage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> have Hephaestus being born of the union of Zeus and Hera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In another tradition, attested by Hesiod, Hera bore Hephaestus alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>'s Zeus-centered cosmology, Hera gave birth to Hephaestus as revenge for Zeus giving birth to <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> without her. Several later texts follow Hesiod's account, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliotheke" title="Bibliotheke">Bibliotheke</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyginus" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a>, and the preface to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabulae" title="Fabulae">Fabulae</a></i>. In the account of Attic vase painters, Hephaestus was present at the birth of Athena and wields the axe with which he split Zeus' head to free her. In the latter account, Hephaestus is there represented as older than Athena, so the mythology of Hephaestus is inconsistent in this respect.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_from_Olympus">Fall from Olympus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fall from Olympus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In one branch of Greek mythology, Hera ejected Hephaestus from the heavens because he was "shrivelled of foot". He fell into the ocean and was raised by <a href="/wiki/Thetis" title="Thetis">Thetis</a> (mother of Achilles) and the <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Oceanid</a> <a href="/wiki/Eurynome_(Oceanid)" title="Eurynome (Oceanid)">Eurynome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In another account, Hephaestus, attempting to rescue his mother from Zeus' advances, was flung down from the heavens by Zeus. He fell for an entire day and landed on the island of <a href="/wiki/Lemnos" title="Lemnos">Lemnos</a>, where he was cared for and taught to be a master craftsman by the <a href="/wiki/Sintians" title="Sintians">Sintians</a> – an ancient tribe native to that island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Later writers describe his lameness as the consequence of his second fall, while Homer makes him lame and weak from his birth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Olympus">Return to Olympus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Return to Olympus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hephaestus was the only Olympian to have returned to Olympus after being exiled.</p>
<p>In an archaic story,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[a]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hedreen_2004_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hedreen_2004-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerenyi_1951_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerenyi_1951-26">[25]</a></sup> Hephaestus gained revenge against Hera for rejecting him by making her a magical golden throne, which, when she sat on it, did not allow her to stand up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[b]</a></sup> The other gods begged Hephaestus to return to Olympus to let her go, but he refused, saying "I have no mother".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerenyi_1951_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kerenyi_1951-26">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p>At last, <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a> fetched him, intoxicated him with wine, and took the subdued smith back to Olympus on the back of a mule accompanied by revelers – a scene that sometimes appears on painted pottery of Attica and of Corinth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eldridge_1917_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eldridge_1917-30">[28]</a></sup> In the painted scenes, the padded dancers and phallic figures of the Dionysan throng leading the mule show that the procession was a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dithyrambic" title="Dithyrambic">dithyrambic</a> celebrations that were the forerunners of the <a href="/wiki/Satyr_play" title="Satyr play">satyr plays</a> of fifth century Athens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Webster_1958_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Webster_1958-32">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The theme of the <i>return of Hephaestus</i>, popular among the Attic vase-painters whose wares were favored among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscans" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a>, may have introduced this theme to Etruria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[c]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petersen_1902_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Petersen_1902-34">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harmon_1912_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harmon_1912-35">[32]</a></sup> In the vase-painters' portrayal of the procession, Hephaestus was mounted on a mule or a horse, with Dionysus holding the bridle and carrying Hephaestus' tools (including a <a href="/wiki/Labrys" title="Labrys">double-headed axe</a>).</p>
<p>The traveller <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a> reported seeing a painting in the temple of Dionysus in Athens, which had been built in the 5th century but may have been decorated at any time before the 2nd century CE. When Pausanias saw it, he said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There are paintings here – Dionysus bringing Hephaestus up to heaven. One of the Greek legends is that Hephaestus, when he was born, was thrown down by Hera. In revenge he sent as a gift a golden chair with invisible fetters. When Hera sat down she was held fast, and Hephaestus refused to listen to any other of the gods except Dionysus – in him he reposed the fullest trust – and after making him drunk Dionysus brought him to heaven.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, 1.20.3</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consorts_and_children">Consorts and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Consorts and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to most versions, Hephaestus's consort is <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a>, who is unfaithful to Hephaestus with a number of gods and mortals, including <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a>. However, in Book XVIII of Homer's <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, the consort of Hephaestus is a lesser Aphrodite, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charis" title="Charis">Charis</a> ("the grace") or <a href="/wiki/Aglaea" title="Aglaea">Aglaia</a> ("the glorious") – the youngest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graces" title="Graces">Graces</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> calls her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, there is a <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Hephaestus" title="Temple of Hephaestus">Temple of Hephaestus</a>, the <i>Hephaesteum</i> (miscalled the "Theseum") near the <a href="/wiki/Agora" title="Agora">agora</a>. An Athenian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_myth" title="Founding myth">founding myth</a> tells that the city's patron goddess, <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, refused a union with Hephaestus because of his unsightly appearance and crippled nature, and that when he became angry and forceful with her, she disappeared from the bed. His ejaculate fell on the earth, impregnating <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a>, who subsequently gave birth to <a href="/wiki/Erichthonius_of_Athens" title="Erichthonius of Athens">Erichthonius of Athens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[34]</a></sup> A surrogate mother later gave the child to Athena to foster, guarded by a <a href="/wiki/Serpent_(symbolism)" title="Serpent (symbolism)">serpent</a>.</p>
<p>On the island of Lemnos, Hephaestus' consort was the sea <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymph</a> <a href="/wiki/Cabeiro" title="Cabeiro">Cabeiro</a>, by whom he was the father of two metalworking gods named the <a href="/wiki/Cabeiri" title="Cabeiri">Cabeiri</a>. In Sicily, his consort was the nymph <a href="/wiki/Aetna_(nymph)" title="Aetna (nymph)">Aetna</a>, and his sons were two gods of Sicilian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geysers" title="Geysers">geysers</a> called <a href="/wiki/Palici" title="Palici">Palici</a>. With <a href="/wiki/Thalia_(nymph)" title="Thalia (nymph)">Thalia</a>, Hephaestus was sometimes considered the father of the <a href="/wiki/Palici" title="Palici">Palici</a>.</p>
<p>Hephaestus fathered several children with mortals and immortals alike. One of those children was the robber <a href="/wiki/Periphetes" title="Periphetes">Periphetes</a>.</p>

<p>Though married to Hephaestus, Aphrodite had an affair with Ares, the god of war. Eventually, Hephaestus discovered Aphrodite’s affair through <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">Helios</a>, the all-seeing Sun, and planned a trap during one of their trysts. While Aphrodite and Ares lay together in bed, Hephaestus ensnared them in an unbreakable chain-link net so small as to be invisible and dragged them to Mount Olympus to shame them in front of the other gods for retribution.</p>
<p>The gods laughed at the sight of these naked lovers, and <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> persuaded Hephaestus to free them in return for a guarantee that Ares would pay the adulterer's fine. Hephaestus states in <i>The Odyssey</i> that he would return Aphrodite to her father and demand back his bride price.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thebes_(Greece)" title="Thebes (Greece)">Thebans</a> told that the union of Ares and Aphrodite produced <a href="/wiki/Harmonia_(mythology)" title="Harmonia (mythology)">Harmonia</a>. However, of the union of Hephaestus with Aphrodite, there was no issue unless <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> was serious when he said that <a href="/wiki/Eros" title="Eros">Eros</a> was their child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[35]</a></sup> Later authors explain this statement by saying that Eros was sired by Ares but passed off to Hephaestus as his own son.</p>
<p>Hephaestus was somehow connected with the archaic, pre-Greek <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thracian</a> mystery cult of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kabeiroi" title="Kabeiroi">Kabeiroi</a>, who were also called the <i>Hephaistoi</i>, "the Hephaestus-men", in Lemnos. One of the three Lemnian tribes also called themselves Hephaestion and claimed direct descent from the god.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hephaestus_and_Athena">Hephaestus and Athena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hephaestus and Athena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hephaestus is to the male gods as Athena is to the females, for he gives skill to mortal artists and was believed to have taught men the arts alongside Athena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup> He was nevertheless believed to be far inferior to the sublime character of Athena. At Athens they had temples and festivals in common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[d]</a></sup> Both were believed to have great healing powers, and Lemnian earth (terra Lemnia) from the spot on which Hephaestus had fallen was believed to cure madness, the bites of snakes, and haemorrhage, and priests of Hephaestus knew how to cure wounds inflicted by snakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was represented in the temple of Athena Chalcioecus (Athena of the Bronze House<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[38]</a></sup>) at Sparta, in the act of delivering his mother;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[39]</a></sup> on the chest of Cypselus, giving Achilles's armour to Thetis;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[40]</a></sup> and at Athens there was the famous statue of Hephaestus by Alcamenes, in which his lameness was only subtly portrayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[41]</a></sup> The Greeks frequently placed small dwarf-like statues of Hephaestus near their hearths, and these figures are the oldest of all his representations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[42]</a></sup> During the best period of Grecian art he was represented as a vigorous man with a beard, and is characterized by his hammer or some other crafting tool, his oval cap, and the <a href="/wiki/Chiton_(costume)" title="Chiton (costume)">chiton</a>.</p>
<p>Athena is sometimes thought to be "the ‘soul-mate’ of [Hephaestus]. Yet a kind of cloudy mysteriousness shrouds their relationship; no single tradition was ever clearly established on this subject, and so what confronts us is a blurred image based on rumors and conflicting reports." Nonetheless, he "seeks impetuously and passionately to make love to Athene: at the moment of climax she pushes him aside, and his semen falls to the earth where it impregnates Gaia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcano_god">Volcano god</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Volcano god">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hephaestus was associated by Greek colonists in southern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> gods <a href="/wiki/Adranus" title="Adranus">Adranus</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Etna</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcanus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lipari" title="Lipari">Lipari</a> islands. The first-century sage <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana" title="Apollonius of Tyana">Apollonius of Tyana</a> is said to have observed, "there are many other mountains all over the earth that are on fire, and yet we should never be done with it if we assigned to them giants and gods like Hephaestus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mythology">Other mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Trojan war, Hephaestus sided with the Greeks, but was also worshiped by the Trojans and saved one of their men from being killed by Diomedes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[45]</a></sup> Hephaestus’ favourite place in the mortal world was the island of <a href="/wiki/Lemnos" title="Lemnos">Lemnos</a>, where he liked to dwell among the Sintians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[48]</a></sup> but he also frequented other volcanic islands such as Lipara, Hiera, Imbros and Sicily, which were called his abodes or workshops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The epithets and surnames by which Hephaestus is known by the poets generally allude to his skill in the <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">plastic arts</a> or to his figure or lameness. The Greeks frequently placed small dwarf-like statues of Hephaestus near their hearths, and these figures are the oldest of all his representations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">Symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hephaestus was sometimes portrayed as a vigorous man with a beard and was characterized by his hammer or some other crafting tool, his oval cap, and the <a href="/wiki/Chiton_(costume)" title="Chiton (costume)">chiton</a>.</p>
<p>Hephaestus is described in mythological sources as "lame" (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">cholōs</span>), and "halting" (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">ēpedanos</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[58]</a></sup> He was depicted with crippled feet and as misshapen, either from birth or as a result of his fall from Olympus. In vase paintings, Hephaestus is usually shown lame and bent over his anvil, hard at work on a metal creation, and sometimes with his feet back-to-front: <i>Hephaistos amphigyēeis</i>. He walked with the aid of a stick. The <a href="/wiki/Argonauts" title="Argonauts">Argonaut</a> Palaimonius, "son of Hephaestus" (i.e. a bronze-smith) was also lame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other "sons of Hephaestus" were the <a href="/wiki/Cabeiri" title="Cabeiri">Cabeiri</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Samothrace" title="Samothrace">Samothrace</a>, who were identified with the <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crab</a> (<i>karkinos</i>) by the lexicographer <a href="/wiki/Hesychius_of_Alexandria" title="Hesychius of Alexandria">Hesychius</a>. The adjective <i>karkinopous</i> ("crab-footed") signified "lame", according to Detienne and Vernant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[60]</a></sup> The Cabeiri were also lame.</p>
<p>In some myths, Hephaestus built himself a "wheeled chair" or chariot with which to move around, thus helping him overcome his lameness while demonstrating his skill to the other gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[61]</a></sup> In the <i>Iliad</i> 18.371, it is stated that Hephaestus built twenty bronze wheeled tripods in order assist him in moving around.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hephaestus’s ugly appearance and lameness is taken by some to represent <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenicosis</a>, an effect of high levels of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> exposure that would result in lameness and <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancers</a>. In place of <a href="/wiki/Tin_sources_and_trade_in_ancient_times" title="Tin sources and trade in ancient times">less easily available</a> <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, arsenic was added to <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> to harden it; like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hatter" title="Hatter">hatters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning#Signs_and_symptoms" title="Mercury poisoning">crazed</a> by their exposure to <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, who inspired <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s famous character of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Hatter" title="Mad Hatter">Mad Hatter</a>, most smiths of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> would have suffered from chronic poisoning as a result of their livelihood. Consequently, the mythic image of the lame smith is widespread. As Hephaestus was an iron-age smith, not a bronze-age smith, the connection is one from ancient folk memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_mythology">Comparative mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Comparative mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parallels in other mythological systems for Hephaestus's symbolism include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a> craftsman-god <a href="/wiki/Kothar-wa-Khasis" title="Kothar-wa-Khasis">Kothar-wa-Khasis</a>, who is identified from afar by his distinctive walk – possibly suggesting that he limps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>As <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> was given to understand, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a> craftsman-god <a href="/wiki/Ptah" title="Ptah">Ptah</a> was a dwarf, naked, and deformed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Norse mythology, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wayland_Smith" title="Wayland Smith">Weyland the Smith</a> was a lame bronzeworker.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_planet">Minor planet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hephaestus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Minor planet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a> <a href="/wiki/2212_Hephaistos" title="2212 Hephaistos">2212 Hephaistos</a> discovered in 1978 by <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> astronomer <a href="/wiki/Lyudmila_Chernykh" title="Lyudmila Chernykh">Lyudmila Chernykh</a> was named in Hephaestus' honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[66]</a></sup></p>
