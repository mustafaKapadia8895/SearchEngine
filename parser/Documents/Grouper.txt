<p><b>Groupers</b> are <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> of any of a number of <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> in the subfamily <b>Epinephelinae</b> of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Serranidae" title="Serranidae">Serranidae</a>, in the order <a href="/wiki/Perciformes" title="Perciformes">Perciformes</a>.</p>
<p>Not all serranids are called groupers; the family also includes the <b><a href="/wiki/Bass_(fish)" title="Bass (fish)">sea basses</a></b>. The common name <b>grouper</b> is usually given to fish in one of two large <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Epinephelus" title="Epinephelus">Epinephelus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mycteroperca" title="Mycteroperca">Mycteroperca</a></i>. In addition, the species classified in the small genera <i>Anyperidon</i>, <i>Cromileptes</i>, <i>Dermatolepis</i>, <i>Gracila</i>, <i>Saloptia</i>, and <i>Triso</i> are also called groupers. Fish in the genus <i>Plectropomus</i> are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Plectropomus" title="Plectropomus">coralgroupers</a>. These genera are all classified in the subfamily Epiphelinae. However, some of the hamlets (genus <i>Alphestes</i>), the hinds (genus <i>Cephalopholis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-edged_lyretail" title="Yellow-edged lyretail">lyretails</a> (genus <i>Variola</i>) and some other small genera (<i>Gonioplectrus</i>, <i>Niphon</i>, <i>Paranthias</i>) are also in this subfamily, and occasional species in other serranid genera have common names involving the word "grouper". Nonetheless, the word "grouper" on its own is usually taken as meaning the subfamily Epinephelinae.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_origin">Name origin</span></h2>
<p>The word "grouper" is from the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> name, <i>garoupa</i>. The origin of this name in Portuguese is believed to be from an indigenous South American language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Australia, "groper" is used instead of "grouper" for several species, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queensland_grouper" title="Queensland grouper">Queensland grouper</a> (<i>Epinephelus lanceolatus</i>). In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, it is named <i>lapu-lapu</i> in <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, while in the <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a> it goes by the name <i>pugapo</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In New Zealand, "groper" refers to a type of wreckfish, <i>Polyprion oxygeneios</i>, which goes by the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a> name <i><a href="/wiki/H%C4%81puku" title="Hāpuku">hāpuku</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the Middle East, the fish is known as <i>hammour</i>, and is widely eaten, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better!_What_about_you%3F_(3852325083).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="777" data-file-width="1260" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better%21_What_about_you%3F_%283852325083%29.jpg/220px-Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better%21_What_about_you%3F_%283852325083%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better%21_What_about_you%3F_%283852325083%29.jpg/330px-Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better%21_What_about_you%3F_%283852325083%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better%21_What_about_you%3F_%283852325083%29.jpg/440px-Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better%21_What_about_you%3F_%283852325083%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Only_today_I_got_to_know_this_Fish_better!_What_about_you%3F_(3852325083).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anatomy of a grouper</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Groupers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teleostei" title="Teleostei">teleosts</a>, typically having a stout body and a large mouth. They are not built for long-distance, fast swimming. They can be quite large, and lengths over a meter and weights up to 100 kg are not uncommon<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, though obviously in such a large group, species vary considerably. They swallow prey rather than biting pieces off it. They do not have many teeth on the edges of their jaws, but they have heavy crushing tooth plates inside the <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a>. They habitually eat <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopuses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>. Some species prefer to <a href="/wiki/Ambush_predator" title="Ambush predator">ambush their prey</a>, while other species are active predators. Reports of fatal attacks on humans by the largest species, the <a href="/wiki/Giant_grouper" title="Giant grouper">giant grouper</a> (<i>Epinephelus lanceolatus</i>) are unconfirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their mouths and <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> form a powerful sucking system that sucks their prey in from a distance. They also use their mouths to dig into sand to form their shelters under big rocks, jetting it out through their gills.</p>
<p>Research indicates <a href="/wiki/Roving_coralgrouper" title="Roving coralgrouper">roving coralgroupers</a> (<i>Plectropomus pessuliferus</i>) sometimes cooperate with <a href="/wiki/Giant_moray" title="Giant moray">giant morays</a> in hunting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h2>
<p>Groupers are mostly monandric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protogynous_hermaphrodite" title="Protogynous hermaphrodite">protogynous hermaphrodites</a>, i.e. they mature only as females and have the ability to change sex after sexual maturity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeMartini_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeMartini-9">[9]</a></sup> Some species of groupers grow about a kilogram per year and are generally adolescent until they reach three kilograms, when they become female. The largest males often control harems containing three to 15 females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2009_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sadovy_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sadovy-10">[10]</a></sup> Groupers often pair spawn, which enables large males to competitively exclude smaller males from reproducing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2009_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allsop_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allsop-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munoz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Munoz-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuwamura_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuwamura-13">[13]</a></sup> As such, if a small female grouper were to change sex before it could control a harem as a male, its fitness would decrease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allsop_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allsop-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munoz_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Munoz-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuwamura_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kuwamura-13">[13]</a></sup> If no male is available, the largest female that can increase fitness by changing sex will do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munoz_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Munoz-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, some groupers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonochoristic" title="Gonochoristic">gonochoristic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2009_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2009-8">[8]</a></sup> Gonochorism, or a reproductive strategy with two distinct sexes, has evolved independently in groupers at least five times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2009_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2009-8">[8]</a></sup> The evolution of gonochorism is linked to group spawning high amounts of habitat cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2009_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munoz_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Munoz-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erisman2008_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erisman2008-14">[14]</a></sup> Both group spawning and habitat cover increase the likelihood of a smaller male to reproduce in the presence of large males. Fitness of male groupers in environments where competitive exclusion of smaller males is not possible is correlated with sperm production and thus <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testicle</a> size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sadovy_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sadovy-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munoz_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Munoz-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molloy_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Molloy-15">[15]</a></sup> Gonochoristic groupers have larger testes than protogynous groupers (10% of body mass compared to 1% of body mass), indicating the evolution of gonochorism increased male grouper fitness in environments where large males were unable to competitively exclude small males from reproducing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sadovy_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sadovy-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1059" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg/100px-Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg/150px-Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg/200px-Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pseudorhabdosynochus_morrhua.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Monogenea" title="Monogenea">monogenean</a> parasitic on the gill of a grouper</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As other fish, groupers harbour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Digenea" title="Digenea">digeneans</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cestode" title="Cestode">cestodes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monogenea" title="Monogenea">monogeneans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isopod" title="Isopod">isopods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a>. A study conducted in <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> has shown that <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a>-associated groupers have about 10 species of parasites per fish species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Species of <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudorhabdosynochus" title="Pseudorhabdosynochus">Pseudorhabdosynochus</a></i>, monogeneans of the family <a href="/wiki/Diplectanidae" title="Diplectanidae">Diplectanidae</a> are typical of and especially numerous on groupers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">Modern use</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gulai_kerapu.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Gulai_kerapu.JPG/220px-Gulai_kerapu.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Gulai_kerapu.JPG/330px-Gulai_kerapu.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Gulai_kerapu.JPG/440px-Gulai_kerapu.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gulai_kerapu.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Gulai kerapu</i>, a grouper-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Padang_food" title="Padang food">Padang food</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many groupers are important food fish, and some of them are now <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">farmed</a>. Unlike most other fish species which are chilled or frozen, groupers are usually sold live in markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Many species are popular fish for <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">sea-angling</a>. Some species are small enough to be kept in <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a>, though even the small species are inclined to grow rapidly.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Groupers are commonly reported as a source of <a href="/wiki/Ciguatera" title="Ciguatera">Ciguatera</a> fish poisoning. <a href="/wiki/DNA_barcoding" title="DNA barcoding">DNA barcoding</a> of grouper species might help in controlling Ciguatera fish poisoning since fish are easily identified, even from meal remnants, with molecular tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span></h2>
<p>Malaysian newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Star_(Malaysia)" title="The Star (Malaysia)">The Star</a></i> reported a 180-kg grouper being caught off the waters near Pulau Sembilan in the Straits of Malacca in January 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Shenzhen News in China reported that a 1.8-m grouper swallowed a 1.0-m <a href="/wiki/Whitetip_reef_shark" title="Whitetip reef shark">whitetip reef shark</a> at the Fuzhou Sea World aquarium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2010, a Costa Rican newspaper reported a 2.3-m (7.5-ft) grouper in Cieneguita, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lim%C3%B3n_province" title="Limón province">Limón</a>. The weight of the fish was 250 kg (550 lb) and it was lured using one kilogram of bait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In November 2013, a 310-kg (686-lb) grouper had been caught and sold to a hotel in <a href="/wiki/Dongyuan_County" title="Dongyuan County">Dongyuan</a>, China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2014 off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonita_Springs" title="Bonita Springs">Bonita Springs</a> in Florida (USA) a big grouper took in one gulp a 4-foot shark which an angler had caught.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
