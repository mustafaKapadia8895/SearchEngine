<p><b>Tropical Storm Zeta</b> was a very late-developing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> over the central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> that formed after the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a> had officially ended (on November 30) and continued into January 2006. Becoming a tropical depression at approximately midnight on December 30 (UTC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup> it became the record-breaking thirtieth tropical cyclone of the 2005 Atlantic hurricane season and after intensifying into Tropical Storm Zeta six hours later, it became the season's twenty-seventh named storm. Zeta was one of only two Atlantic tropical cyclones to span two calendar years (the other being <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> in 1954–55).</p>
<p>Zeta originated from an area of low pressure on December 29, which previously developed within an upper-level trough. After becoming a tropical storm, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> continually predicted it would weaken rapidly. Like the previous tropical cyclone, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Epsilon_(2005)" title="Hurricane Epsilon (2005)">Hurricane Epsilon</a>, Zeta defied these predictions. The storm reached its peak strength on January 2, 2006 before finally dissipating on January 6. As Zeta never approached land there was no impact from the storm other than minor shipping problems. Several ships encountered the storm, and several crews in the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Rowing_Race" title="Atlantic Rowing Race">Atlantic Rowing Race</a> were affected by rough seas and high winds.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Zeta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zeta_2005_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Zeta_2005_track.png/220px-Zeta_2005_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Zeta_2005_track.png/330px-Zeta_2005_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Zeta_2005_track.png/440px-Zeta_2005_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zeta_2005_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tropical Storm Zeta originated from an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> that interacted with a weakening <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontal_system" title="Frontal system">frontal system</a> in late-December 2005. By December 28, the trough had developed into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a> roughly 750 mi (1,210 km) west-northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a>. The following day, a low-level circulation developed and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convective activity</a> began to increase around the low. By December 30, sufficient shower and <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity had developed for the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) to classify it as a tropical cyclone. Over the following several hours, <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">convective banding</a> developed around the storm's center and by 0600 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>, the depression intensified into Tropical Storm Zeta, the record 28th <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> to form during the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Operationally, however, the NHC did not begin issuing advisories on Zeta until 1700 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Traveling slowly northwestward in response to a mid-level low to the southwest, the storm gradually intensified in a region of favorable <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the afternoon of December 30, dry air caused the system to become slightly disorganized and led forecasters to anticipate further weakening of the storm and dissipating within three days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The following day, a mid-level <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to the north caused Zeta turn westward before nearly stalling due to upper-level <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a>. By this time, the storm attained winds of 60 mph (95 km/h) but increasing <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> caused convection to become disorganized, resulting in a brief weakening period. Despite the shear, the shallow structure of Zeta allowed convection to regenerate and the storm gradually re-intensified. Around 1800 UTC on January 1, 2006, Zeta attained its peak intensity with winds of 65 mph (100 km/h) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 994 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 29.35 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a>) roughly 1,035 mi (1,665 km) northwest of the Cape Verde Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup> By January 2, the storm began tracking towards the southwest in response to a mid-level trough located east of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Tropical Storm Zeta continued to defy forecasts of weakening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC-disc21_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC-disc21-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The effects of wind shear finally took their toll late on January 4, and Zeta's convection began to die down. This led to Tropical Storm Zeta weakening to a minimal tropical storm. The NHC continued to overestimate how quickly Zeta would dissipate and early on January 5 they operationally downgraded Zeta to a depression, which was later confirmed to have been an error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC-disc24_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC-disc24-6">[6]</a></sup> Zeta continued to move west-northwestward, barely holding on to tropical storm status before becoming disorganized again. Zeta weakened into a tropical depression on January 6 and dissipated into a remnant later that day. With this transition, the very active 2005 hurricane season finally came to a close. The remnant low retained its identity for a further day before it dissipated 660 miles (1060 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_and_records">Impact and records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Zeta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact and records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="4000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg/220px-TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg/330px-TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg/440px-TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TS_Zeta_30_dec_2005_1535Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical Storm Zeta shortly after being named on December 30</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several of the crews that were taking part in the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Rowing_Race" title="Atlantic Rowing Race">Atlantic Rowing Race</a> were affected with heavy seas and strong adverse winds from Tropical Storm Zeta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowing_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowing-7">[7]</a></sup> Several teams encountered winds up to 50 mph (85 km/h) and large <a href="/wiki/Swell_(ocean)" title="Swell (ocean)">swells</a> up to 12 ft (3.7 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Although battered by torrential rain and high winds, the direction of the winds assisted some of the ships in the race by speeding them closer to <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> One <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> vessel was blown 25 mi (40 km) off-course, ending with an encounter with a 12 ft (3.7 m) shark that battered their boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The ship <i>Liberty Star</i> made several reports of strong winds from Tropical Storm Zeta, including one of 40 mph (65 km/h) winds early on December 31, when the ship was about 45 miles (75 km) north of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Zeta formed at 0600 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on December 30, it became the second latest-forming tropical cyclone ever recorded in the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Only <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> of December 1954 formed later than Zeta, becoming a tropical storm around 1200 UTC. Additionally, Zeta became the second known Atlantic tropical cyclone, along with Alice, to exist in two calendar years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Tropical Storm Zeta also extended the record number of storms to form in the 2005 season to twenty-eight, seven more than the previous record held by the <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-11">[11]</a></sup> Also, if an unnamed subtropical storm had been named, the storm would have been named "Eta" accordingly.</p>
<p>Although the storm affected several vessels during its existence, no land areas were threatened by Zeta. As a result, the NHC did not issue any <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watches or warnings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
