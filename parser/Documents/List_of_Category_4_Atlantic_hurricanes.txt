<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>Category 4 hurricanes are <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> that reach Category 4 intensity on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. Category 4 hurricanes that later attained Category 5 strength are not included in this list. The Atlantic basin includes the open waters of the Atlantic Ocean, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. Category 4 is the second-highest hurricane classification category on the Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale, and storms that are of this intensity maintain <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 113–136 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (130–156 mph, 209–251 km/h). Based on the Atlantic hurricane database, 94 hurricanes have attained Category 4 hurricane status since 1851, the start of modern meteorological record keeping. Category 4 storms are considered extreme hurricanes. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a>, which was a Category 4 storm, brought on a 24 ft storm surge, the third greatest storm surge ever recorded (after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Hurricane Camille</a>, respectively).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Category 4 hurricanes have <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 113–136 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (130–156 mph, 209–251 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-1">[1]</a></sup> "Sustained winds" refers to the average wind speed observed over one minute at a height of 10 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">meters</a> (33 ft) above ground. Gust can be 30% higher than the sustained winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-2">[2]</a></sup> Mobile homes and other buildings without fixed structures can be completely destroyed, and the lower floors of sturdier structures usually sustain major damage. In addition to the winds, the cyclones generally produce a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of 13–18 feet (4–5.5 m) above normal, potentially causing major beach erosion. Heavy, irreparable damage and/or near complete destruction of gas station canopies and other wide span overhang type structures are also very common, and mobile and manufactured homes are often completely destroyed. Low-level terrain may be flooded well inland, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition, Category 4 hurricanes are very often Cape Verde type hurricanes. Cape Verde hurricanes are usually the strongest, and their track sometimes points them towards the United States, or other land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies have shown that the number of Category 4 and 5 hurricanes nearly doubled in occurrence in from 1970 to 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-5">[5]</a></sup> Also, due to growing population in major coastal cities, many areas have become more vulnerable to strong hurricanes, especially categories 4 and 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_measurements">Meteorological measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meteorological measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All of the storms listed in this analysis are listed in chronological order, but they also list the minimum central pressure and maximum sustained winds. Each of these meteorological readings are taken using a specific meteorological instrument. For modern storms, the minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> measurements are taken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Reconnaissance Aircraft</a> using <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsondes</a>, or by determining it from satellite imagery using the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a>. For older storms, pressures are often incomplete, typically being provided by ship-reports or land-observations. None of these methods can provide constant pressure measurements; thus it is possible the only measurement occurred when the cyclone was at a lesser strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc7_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc7-7">[7]</a></sup> Sustained winds are taken using an <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">Anemometer</a> at 10 meters (33 ft) above the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FEMA_glossary_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FEMA_glossary-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A total of 95 hurricanes in the Atlantic Ocean Basin, including the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, have reached Category 4 status as their peak intensity. (Note that Category 4 storms that intensified later to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_5" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 5</a> status are not included in this analysis.)</p>
<p>Most Category 4 hurricanes occur during September, with 51 storms occurring in that month. This coincides with the average peak of the Atlantic hurricane season, which occurs on September 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Atl_peak_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Atl_peak-9">[9]</a></sup> Most Category 4 hurricanes develop in the warm waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. Several Category 4 hurricanes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricanes</a>. There have been no Category 4 hurricanes to form in either May or December, or in any other month outside the traditional bounds of the Atlantic hurricane season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landfalls">Landfalls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Landfalls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following hurricanes made <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfall</a> at some location at any strength. Due to inaccuracies in data, tropical depression landfalls are not included. Several of these storms weakened slightly after attaining Category 4 status as they approached land; this is usually a result of dry air, shallower water due to shelving, cooler waters, or interaction with land.</p>
