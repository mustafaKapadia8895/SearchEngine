<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gas giant</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <b>gas giant</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planet</a> composed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> are the gas giants of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. The term "gas giant" was originally synonymous with "giant planet", but in the 1990s it became known that <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> are really a distinct class of giant planet, being composed mainly of heavier volatile substances (which are referred to as "ices"). For this reason, Uranus and Neptune are now often classified in the separate category of <a href="/wiki/Ice_giant" title="Ice giant">ice giants</a>.</p>
<p>Jupiter and Saturn consist mostly of hydrogen and helium, with heavier elements making up between 3 and 13 percent of the mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillot_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guillot-1">[1]</a></sup> They are thought to consist of an outer layer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a> surrounding a layer of liquid <a href="/wiki/Metallic_hydrogen" title="Metallic hydrogen">metallic hydrogen</a>, with probably a molten rocky core. The outermost portion of their hydrogen atmosphere is characterized by many layers of visible clouds that are mostly composed of water and ammonia. The layer of metallic hydrogen makes up the bulk of each planet, and is referred to as "metallic" because the very large pressure turns hydrogen into an electrical conductor. The gas giants' cores are thought to consist of heavier elements at such high temperatures (20,000 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) and pressures that their properties are poorly understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillot_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guillot-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The defining differences between a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf#Low-mass_brown_dwarfs_vs._high-mass_planets" title="Brown dwarf">very low-mass brown dwarf</a> and a gas giant (estimated at about 13 Jupiter masses) are debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PT-June2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PT-June2008-2">[2]</a></sup> One school of thought is based on formation; the other, on the physics of the interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PT-June2008_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-PT-June2008-2">[2]</a></sup> Part of the debate concerns whether "brown dwarfs" must, by definition, have experienced <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> at some point in their history.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_giant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>gas giant</i> was coined in 1952 by the science fiction writer <a href="/wiki/James_Blish" title="James Blish">James Blish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and was originally used to refer to all <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a>. It is, arguably, something of a misnomer because throughout most of the volume of all giant planets, the pressure is so high that matter is not in gaseous form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddl2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ddl2011-4">[4]</a></sup> Other than solids in the core and the upper layers of the atmosphere, all matter is above the <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)#Pure_substances:_vapor.E2.80.93liquid_critical_point" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a>, where there is no distinction between liquids and gases. The term has nevertheless caught on, because planetary scientists typically use "rock", "gas", and "ice" as shorthands for classes of elements and compounds commonly found as planetary constituents, irrespective of what <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_of_matter" title="Phase of matter">phase</a> the matter may appear in. In the outer Solar System, hydrogen and helium are referred to as "gases"; water, methane, and ammonia as "ices"; and silicates and metals as "rock". Because Uranus and Neptune are primarily composed of, in this terminology, ices, not gas, they are increasingly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ice_giant" title="Ice giant">ice giants</a> and separated from the gas giants.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_giant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sudarsky%27s_gas_giant_classification" title="Sudarsky's gas giant classification">Sudarsky's gas giant classification</a></div>
<p>Gas giants can, theoretically, be divided into five distinct classes according to their modeled physical atmospheric properties, and hence their appearance: ammonia clouds (I), water clouds (II), cloudless (III), alkali-metal clouds (IV), and silicate clouds (V). Jupiter and Saturn are both class I. <a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">Hot Jupiters</a> are class IV or V.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extrasolar">Extrasolar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_giant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extrasolar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_gas_giants">Cold gas giants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_giant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cold gas giants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A cold hydrogen-rich gas giant more massive than Jupiter but less than about 500 <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass"><i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub></a> (<span class="nowrap">1.6</span> <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass"><i>M</i><sub>J</sub></a>) will only be slightly larger in volume than Jupiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seager2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seager2007-5">[5]</a></sup> For masses above 500 <i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> will cause the planet to shrink (see <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_matter" title="Degenerate matter">degenerate matter</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seager2007_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seager2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_mechanism" title="Kelvin–Helmholtz mechanism">Kelvin–Helmholtz heating</a> can cause a gas giant to radiate more energy than it receives from its host star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bagenal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bagenal-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_dwarfs">Gas dwarfs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_giant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gas dwarfs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gas_dwarf" title="Gas dwarf">Gas dwarf</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mini-Neptune" title="Mini-Neptune">Mini-Neptune</a></div>
<p>Although the words "gas" and "giant" are often combined, hydrogen planets need not be as large as the familiar gas giants from the Solar System. However, smaller gas planets and planets closer to their star will lose atmospheric mass more quickly via <a href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic_escape" title="Hydrodynamic escape">hydrodynamic escape</a> than larger planets and planets farther out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A gas dwarf could be defined as a planet with a rocky core that has accumulated a thick envelope of hydrogen, helium and other volatiles, having as result a total radius between 1.7 and 3.9 Earth-radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dangelo_bodenheimer_2016_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dangelo_bodenheimer_2016-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The smallest known extrasolar planet that is likely a "gas planet" is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler-138d" title="Kepler-138d">Kepler-138d</a>, which has the same mass as Earth but is 60% larger and therefore has a density that indicates a thick gas envelope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A low-mass gas planet can still have a radius resembling that of a gas giant if it has the right temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
