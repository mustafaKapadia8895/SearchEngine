<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dry thunderstorm</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p>A <b>dry thunderstorm</b> or heat storm, is a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> that produces thunder and lightning, but most or <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">all of its precipitation evaporates</a> before reaching the ground,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and <b>dry lightning</b> is the term which is used to refer to lightning strikes occurring in this situation. Both are so common in the American West that they are sometimes used interchangeably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drycb_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-drycb-2">[2]</a></sup> The latter term is a technical <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">misnomer</a> since <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> itself is neither wet nor dry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drycb_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-drycb-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dry thunderstorms occur essentially in dry conditions, and their lightning is a major cause of wildfires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSL_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSL-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Because of that, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>, and other agencies around the world, issue forecasts for its likelihood over large areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSL_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSSL-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Where_dry_thunderstorms_occur">Where dry thunderstorms occur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_thunderstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Where dry thunderstorms occur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dry thunderstorms generally occur in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserts" title="Deserts">deserts</a> or places where the lower layers of the atmosphere usually contain little <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>. Any precipitation that falls from elevated thunderstorms can be entirely evaporated as it falls through the lower dry layers. They are common during the summer months across much of western North America and other arid areas. The shaft of precipitation that can be seen falling from a cloud without reaching the ground is called "<a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A thunderstorm does not have to be completely dry to be considered dry; in many areas 0.1 inches (2.5 mm) is the threshold between a "wet" and "dry" thunderstorm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_thunderstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dry thunderstorms are notable for two reasons: they are the most common natural origin of wildland fires, and they can produce strong gusty surface winds that can fan flames.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_storms">Dust storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_thunderstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dust storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">Dust storm</a></div>
<p>Strong winds often develop around dry thunderstorms as the evaporating precipitation causes excessive cooling of the air beneath the storm, which increases its density and thereby its weight relative to the surrounding air. This cool air then descends rapidly and fans out upon impacting the ground, an event often described as a <a href="/wiki/Microburst#Dry_microbursts" title="Microburst">dry microburst</a>. As the gusty winds expand outward from the storm, dry soil and sand are often picked up by the strong winds, creating dust and sand storms known as <a href="/wiki/Haboob" title="Haboob">haboobs</a>.</p>

<p>In areas where trees or other vegetation are present, there is little to no rain that can prevent the lightning from causing them to catch fire. Storm winds also fan the <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">fire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Firestorm" title="Firestorm">firestorm</a>, causing it to spread more quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrocumulus_cloud" title="Pyrocumulus cloud">Pyrocumulonimbus</a> are cumuliform clouds that can form over a large fire and that are particularly dry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When the higher levels of the atmosphere are cooler, and the surface is thus warmed to extreme temperatures due to a wildfire, volcano, or other event, convection will occur, and produce clouds and lightning. They are similar to any cumulus cloud but ingest extra <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a> from the fire. This increases the voltage difference between the base and the top of the cloud, helping to produce lightning.</p>
