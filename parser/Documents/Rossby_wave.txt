<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rossby wave</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Rossby waves</b>, also known as <b>planetary waves</b>, are a natural phenomenon in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and oceans of planets that largely owe their properties to rotation of the planet. Rossby waves are a subset of <a href="/wiki/Inertial_wave" title="Inertial wave">inertial waves</a>.</p>
<p><b><i>Atmospheric</i> Rossby waves</b> on Earth are giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meanders" title="Meanders">meanders</a> in high-<a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> that have a major influence on <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>. These waves are associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_systems" title="Pressure systems">pressure systems</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <b><i>Oceanic</i> Rossby waves</b> move along the <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>: the boundary between the warm upper layer and the cold deeper part of the ocean.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rossby_wave_types">Rossby wave types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rossby wave types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_waves">Atmospheric waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atmospheric waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="253" data-file-width="1042" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg/440px-Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg/660px-Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg/880px-Jetstream_-_Rossby_Waves_-_N_hemisphere.svg.png 2x" width="440"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Atmospheric Rossby waves result from the conservation of <a href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity" title="Potential vorticity">potential vorticity</a> and are influenced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_acceleration" title="Coriolis acceleration">Coriolis force</a> and pressure gradient. A fluid, on the Earth, that moves toward the pole will deviate toward the east; a fluid moving toward the equator will deviate toward the west (true in either hemisphere). The deviations are caused by the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> and conservation of potential vorticity which leads to changes of relative vorticity. This is analogous to conservation of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> in mechanics. In <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planetary</a> atmospheres, including Earth, Rossby waves are due to the variation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> with <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl-Gustaf_Arvid_Rossby" title="Carl-Gustaf Arvid Rossby">Carl-Gustaf Arvid Rossby</a> first identified such waves in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> in 1939 and went on to explain their motion.</p>
<p>One can identify a terrestrial Rossby wave as its <a href="/wiki/Phase_velocity" title="Phase velocity">phase velocity</a>, marked by its wave crest, always has a westward component. However, the collected set of Rossby waves may appear to move in either direction with what is known as its <a href="/wiki/Group_velocity" title="Group velocity">group velocity</a>. In general, shorter waves have an eastward group velocity and long waves a westward group velocity.</p>
<p>The terms "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">barotropic</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroclinic" title="Baroclinic">baroclinic</a>" are used to distinguish the vertical structure of Rossby waves. Barotropic Rossby waves do not vary in the vertical, and have the fastest propagation <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speeds</a>. The baroclinic wave modes, on the other hand, do vary in the vertical. They are also slower, with speeds of only a few centimeters per second or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shepherd2006_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shepherd2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most investigations of Rossby waves has been done on those in Earth's atmosphere. Rossby waves in the Earth's atmosphere are easy to observe as (usually 4-6) large-scale meanders of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>. When these deviations become very pronounced, masses of cold or warm air detach, and become low-strength <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticyclones" title="Anticyclones">anticyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, respectively, and are responsible for day-to-day weather patterns at mid-latitudes. The action of Rossby waves partially explains why eastern continental edges, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_United_States" title="Northeast United States">Northeast United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canada" title="Eastern Canada">Eastern Canada</a>, are colder than <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> at the same <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poleward-propagating_atmospheric_waves">Poleward-propagating atmospheric waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Poleward-propagating atmospheric waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Deep <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> (<a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>) to the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> is enhanced over very warm sea surfaces in the tropics, such as during <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events. This tropical forcing generates atmospheric Rossby waves that have a poleward and eastward migration.</p>
<p>Poleward-propagating Rossby waves explain many of the observed statistical connections between low- and high-latitude climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> One such phenomenon is <a href="/wiki/Sudden_stratospheric_warming" title="Sudden stratospheric warming">sudden stratospheric warming</a>. Poleward-propagating Rossby waves are an important and unambiguous part of the variability in the Northern Hemisphere, as expressed in the Pacific North America pattern. Similar mechanisms apply in the Southern Hemisphere and partly explain the strong variability in the <a href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea" title="Amundsen Sea">Amundsen Sea</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2011, a <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Geoscience" title="Nature Geoscience">Nature Geoscience</a></i> study using <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">general circulation models</a> linked Pacific Rossby waves generated by increasing central tropical Pacific temperatures to warming of the Amundsen Sea region, leading to winter and spring continental warming of <a href="/wiki/Ellsworth_Land" title="Ellsworth Land">Ellsworth Land</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marie_Byrd_Land" title="Marie Byrd Land">Marie Byrd Land</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctica" title="West Antarctica">West Antarctica</a> via an increase in <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rossby_waves_on_other_planets">Rossby waves on other planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rossby waves on other planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Atmospheric Rossby waves, like Kelvin waves, can occur on any rotating planet with an atmosphere. The Y-shaped cloud feature on Venus is attributed to Kelvin and Rossby waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_waves">Oceanic waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oceanic waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oceanic Rossby waves are large-scale waves within an ocean basin. They have a low amplitude, on the order of centimetres (at the surface) to metres (at the thermocline), compared to a very long wavelength, on the order of hundreds of kilometres of atmospheric Rossby waves. They may take months to cross an ocean basin. They gain <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wind_stress" title="Wind stress">wind stress</a> at the ocean surface layer and are thought to communicate climatic changes due to variability in <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator" title="Harmonic oscillator">forcing</a>, due to both the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>. Both barotropic and baroclinic waves cause variations of the sea surface height, although the length of the waves made them difficult to detect until the advent of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altimetry" title="Altimetry">altimetry</a>. <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">Satellite</a> observations have confirmed the existence of oceanic Rossby waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chelton1996_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chelton1996-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baroclinic waves also generate significant displacements of the oceanic <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>, often of tens of meters. Satellite observations have revealed the stately progression of Rossby waves across all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">ocean basins</a>, particularly at low- and mid-latitudes. These waves can take months or even years to cross a basin like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>.</p>
<p>Rossby waves have been suggested as an important mechanism to account for the heating of <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)#Subsurface_ocean" title="Europa (moon)">the ocean on Europa</a>, a moon of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyler2008_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler2008-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waves_in_astrophysical_discs">Waves in astrophysical discs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Waves in astrophysical discs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave_instability_in_astrophysical_discs" title="Rossby wave instability in astrophysical discs">Rossby wave instabilities</a> are also thought to be found in astrophysical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">discs</a>, for example, around newly forming stars. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quasiresonant_amplification_of_Rossby_waves">Quasiresonant amplification of Rossby waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Quasiresonant amplification of Rossby waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Planetary_wave_resonance"></span><span id="Planetary_wave_quasiresonance"></span><span id="Quasiresonant_amplification"></span></p>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>

<p>It has been proposed that a number of regional weather extremes in the Northern Hemisphere associated with blocked atmospheric circulation patterns may have been caused by <b>quasiresonant amplification of Rossby waves</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Examples include the <a href="/wiki/2013_European_floods" title="2013 European floods">2013 European floods</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=2012_China_floods&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="2012 China floods (page does not exist)">2012 China floods</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_Russian_heat_wave" title="2010 Russian heat wave">2010 Russian heat wave</a>, the <a href="/wiki/2010_Pakistan_floods" title="2010 Pakistan floods">2010 Pakistan floods</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2003_European_heat_wave" title="2003 European heat wave">2003 European heat wave</a>. Even taking <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> into account, the 2003 heat wave would have been highly unlikely without such a mechanism.</p>
<p>Normally freely travelling <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a>-scale Rossby waves and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quasistationary&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quasistationary (page does not exist)">quasistationary</a> planetary-scale Rossby waves exist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-latitudes" title="Mid-latitudes">mid-latitudes</a> with only weak interactions. The hypothesis, proposed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Petoukhov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vladimir Petoukhov (page does not exist)">Vladimir Petoukhov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Rahmstorf" title="Stefan Rahmstorf">Stefan Rahmstorf</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stefan_Petri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stefan Petri (page does not exist)">Stefan Petri</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Joachim_Schellnhuber" title="Hans Joachim Schellnhuber">Hans Joachim Schellnhuber</a>, is that under some circumstances these waves interact to produce the static pattern. For this to happen, they suggest, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zonal_(Earth_sciences)" title="Zonal (Earth sciences)">zonal</a> (east-west) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_number" title="Wave number">wave number</a> of both types of wave should be in the range 6–8, the synoptic waves should be arrested within the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> (so that energy does not escape to the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>) and mid-latitude <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waveguides" title="Waveguides">waveguides</a> should trap the quasistationary components of the synoptic waves. In this case the planetary-scale waves may respond unusually strongly to <a href="/wiki/Orography" title="Orography">orography</a> and thermal sources and sinks because of "quasiresonance".</p>
<p>It is also suggested that the phenomenon is made more likely by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_global_warming" title="Anthropogenic global warming">anthropogenic global warming</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> has cautioned that more data would be needed to confirm that specific events such as flooding were caused by global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>This research was furthered in 2017 by <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Mann" title="Michael E. Mann">Michael E. Mann</a>, Stefan Rahmstorf, et al. study in <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_Reports" title="Scientific Reports">Scientific Reports</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rossby_waves_and_climate_networks">Rossby waves and climate networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rossby_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rossby waves and climate networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The connectivity pattern of climate networks based on ground level temperature records shows a dense stripe of links in the extra tropics of the southern hemisphere. It is found that statistical categorization of these links yields a clear association with the pattern of the atmospheric Rossby waves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WangGozolchiani2013_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-WangGozolchiani2013-15">[15]</a></sup>. It is found that alternating densities of negative and positive links are arranged in half Rossby wave distances around 3500, 7000, and 10 000 km and are aligned with the expected direction of energy flow, distribution of time delays, and the seasonality of these waves. In addition, long distance links that are associated with Rossby waves are the most dominant in the climate network. On the relation between Rossby waves and climate networks during El-Nino see Wang <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WangGozolchiani2016_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-WangGozolchiani2016-16">[16]</a></sup>.</p>
