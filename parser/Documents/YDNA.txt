<p>In <a href="/wiki/Human_genetics" title="Human genetics">human genetics</a>, a <b>human Y-chromosome DNA haplogroup</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup" title="Haplogroup">haplogroup</a> defined by mutations in the non-<a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">recombining</a> portions of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> (called Y-DNA). Mutations that are shared by many people are called <a href="/wiki/Single-nucleotide_polymorphism" title="Single-nucleotide polymorphism">single-nucleotide polymorphisms</a> (SNPs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTDNAUnderstandingHaplogroups_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTDNAUnderstandingHaplogroups-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The human Y-chromosome accumulates roughly two mutations per generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureHumanMutationRateRevealed_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatureHumanMutationRateRevealed-2">[2]</a></sup> Y-DNA haplogroups represent major branches of the Y-chromosome <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a> that share hundreds or even thousands of mutations unique to each haplogroup.</p>
<p>The Y-chromosomal most recent common ancestor (Y-MRCA, informally known as Y-chromosomal Adam) is the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> (MRCA) from whom all currently living men are descended <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineally</a>. Y-chromosomal Adam is estimated to have lived roughly 236,000 years ago in Africa. By examining other bottlenecks all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasian" title="Eurasian">Eurasian</a> men are descended from a man who lived 69,000 years ago. Another major bottleneck occurred about 5,000 years ago and subsequently most Eurasian men can trace their ancestry back to a dozen ancestors who lived 5,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bottleneck_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bottleneck-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YFullYTree_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-YFullYTree-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_convention">Naming convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Naming convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1154" data-file-width="1033" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png/220px-Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png/330px-Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png/440px-Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Molecular_lineage_haplogroups.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Schematic illustration of Y-DNA haplogroups naming convention. Haplogroups are defined through mutations (SNPs).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Y-DNA haplogroups are defined by the presence of a series of Y-DNA SNP <a href="/wiki/Genetic_marker" title="Genetic marker">markers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Subclade" title="Subclade">Subclades</a> are defined by a <i>terminal SNP</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Single-nucleotide_polymorphism" title="Single-nucleotide polymorphism">SNP</a> furthest down in the Y-chromosome phylogenetic tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Y_Chromosome_Consortium" title="Y Chromosome Consortium">Y Chromosome Consortium</a> (YCC) developed a system of naming major Y-DNA haplogroups with the capital letters A through T, with further subclades named using numbers and lower case letters (YCC longhand <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature" title="Nomenclature">nomenclature</a>). YCC shorthand nomenclature names Y-DNA haplogroups and their subclades with the first letter of the major Y-DNA haplogroup followed by a dash and the name of the defining terminal SNP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Y-DNA haplogroup nomenclature is changing over time to accommodate the increasing number of SNPs being discovered and tested, and the resulting expansion of the Y-chromosome phylogenetic tree. This change in nomenclature has resulted in inconsistent nomenclature being used in different sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTDNAUnderstandingHaplogroups_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FTDNAUnderstandingHaplogroups-1">[1]</a></sup> This inconsistency, and increasingly cumbersome longhand nomenclature, has prompted a move towards using the simpler shorthand nomenclature. In September 2012, <a href="/wiki/Family_Tree_DNA" title="Family Tree DNA">Family Tree DNA</a> provided the following explanation of its changing Y-DNA haplogroup nomenclature to individual customers on their Y-DNA results pages (note that the haplogroup mentioned below relates to a specific individual):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>Long time customers of Family Tree DNA have seen the YCC-tree of Homo Sapiens evolve over the past several years as new SNPs have been discovered. Sometimes these new SNPs cause a substantial change in the "longhand" explanation of your terminal Haplogroup. Because of this confusion, we introduced a shorthand version a few years ago that lists the branch of the tree and your terminal SNP, i.e. J-L147, in lieu of J1c3d. Therefore, in the very near term, Family Tree DNA will discontinue showing the current "longhand" on the tree and we will focus all of our discussions around your terminal defining SNP.<br/>
This changes no science – it just provides an easier and less confusing way for us all to communicate.</p>
</div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_Y-DNA_haplogroups">Major Y-DNA haplogroups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major Y-DNA haplogroups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Haplogroups_A_&amp;_B"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroups_A_.26_B">Haplogroups A &amp; B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Haplogroups A &amp; B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using fast evolving SNPs, haplogroup A is the NRY macrohaplogroup from which all modern paternal haplogroups descend. It is sparsely distributed in Africa, being concentrated among Khoisan populations in the southwest and Nilotic populations toward the northast in the Nile Valley. BT is a subclade of haplogroup A; more precisely of the A1b clade (A2-T in Cruciani et al. 2011), as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup A (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup A</a>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A00" title="Haplogroup A00">Haplogroup A00</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A0" title="Haplogroup A0">Haplogroup A0</a> (formerly also A1b)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A1" title="Haplogroup A1">Haplogroup A1</a> (also A1a-T)
<ul>
<li>Haplogroup A1a (M31)</li>
<li>Haplogroup A1b (also A2-T; P108, V221)

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_BT_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup BT (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup BT</a> (M91, M42, M94, M139, M299)

<h3><span id="Haplogroup_CT_(P143)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_CT_.28P143.29">Haplogroup CT (P143)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Haplogroup CT (P143)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_CT_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup CT (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup CT (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p>The defining mutations separating CT (all haplogroups except for A and B) are M168 and M294. The site of origin is unknown but likely in either Asia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> or Africa approximately 88,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karafet2008_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet2008-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_DE_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup DE (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup DE</a> (M1, M145, M203)
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_D_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup D (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup D</a> (M174) <i>Found in Asia</i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_E_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup E (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup E</a> (E-M96) <i>Found in Africa, Middle East, and Southern Europe</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_CF_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup CF (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup CF</a> (P143)
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_C_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup C (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup C</a> (M130) <i>Found in Asia, Oceania, and North America</i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_F_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup F (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup F</a> (M89) <i>Found in Europe, Asia, Oceania, and the Americas</i></li>
</ul>

<h3><span id="Haplogroup_F_(M89)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_F_.28M89.29">Haplogroup F (M89)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Haplogroup F (M89)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_F_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup F (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup F (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haplogroup_F_(Y-DNA).PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="726" data-file-width="1144" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Haplogroup_F_%28Y-DNA%29.PNG/300px-Haplogroup_F_%28Y-DNA%29.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Haplogroup_F_%28Y-DNA%29.PNG/450px-Haplogroup_F_%28Y-DNA%29.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Haplogroup_F_%28Y-DNA%29.PNG/600px-Haplogroup_F_%28Y-DNA%29.PNG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Haplogroup_F_(Y-DNA).PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A hypothetical diversion of Haplogroup F and its descendants.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The groups descending from haplogroup F are found in some 90% of the world's population, but almost exclusively outside of sub-Saharan Africa.</p>
<p>F xG,H,I,J,K is rare in modern populations and peaks in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isogg2015_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-isogg2015-9">[9]</a></sup> It also appears to have long been present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" title="South East Asia">South East Asia</a>. has been reported at rates of 4–5% in <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lembata" title="Lembata">Lembata</a>. One study, which did not comprehensively screen for other subclades of F-M89 (including some subclades of GHIJK), found that Indonesian men with the <a href="/wiki/Single-nucleotide_polymorphism" title="Single-nucleotide polymorphism">SNP</a> P14/PF2704 (which is equivalent to M89), comprise 1.8% of men in <a href="/wiki/West_Timor" title="West Timor">West Timor</a>, 1.5% of <a href="/wiki/Flores" title="Flores">Flores</a> 5.4% of <a href="/wiki/Lembata" title="Lembata">Lembata</a> 2.3% of <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a> and 0.2% in <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiaroni2009_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiaroni2009-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> F* (F xF1,F2,F3) has been reported among 10% of males in Sri Lanka and <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>, 5% in Pakistan, as well as lower levels among the <a href="/wiki/Tamang_people" title="Tamang people">Tamang people</a> (Nepal), and in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. F1 (P91), F2 (M427) and F3 (M481; previously F5) are all highly rare and virtually exclusive to regions/ethnic minorities in Sri Lanka, India, Nepal, <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">South China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. In such cases, however, the possibility of misidentification is considered to be relatively high and some may belong to misidentified subclades of <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_GHIJK" title="Haplogroup GHIJK">Haplogroup GHIJK</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Haplogroup_G_(M201)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_G_.28M201.29">Haplogroup G (M201)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Haplogroup G (M201)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_G_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup G (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup G</a> (M201) originated some 48,000 years ago and its most recent common ancestor likely lived 26,000 years ago in the Middle East. It spread to Europe with the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a>.</p>
<p>It is found in many ethnic groups in Eurasia; most common in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>. Found in almost all European countries, but most common in <a href="/wiki/Gagauzia" title="Gagauzia">Gagauzia</a>, southeastern <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> with highest concentrations on some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> islands; uncommon in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Passarino2002_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Passarino2002-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karlsson2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karlsson2006-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>G-M201 is also found in small numbers in northwestern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.</p>

<h3><span id="Haplogroup_H_(M69)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_H_.28M69.29">Haplogroup H (M69)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Haplogroup H (M69)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_H_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup H (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup H (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p>Haplogroup H (M69) probably emerged in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, about 48,000 years BP, and remains prevalent there, in the forms of H1 (M69) and H3 (Z5857).</p>

<li>Haplogroup J* (J-M304*) is rare outside the island of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a>.

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_J1_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup J1 (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup J1</a> (M267) is associated with <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">Northeast Caucasian</a> peoples in <a href="/wiki/Dagestan" title="Dagestan">Dagestan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Semitic-speaking_peoples" title="Ancient Semitic-speaking peoples">Semitic languages speaking people</a> in the Middle East, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and also found in Mediterranean Europe in smaller frequencies much like haplogroup T.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_J2_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup J2 (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup J2</a> (M172) is found mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Semitic_people" title="Semitic people">Semitic-speaking peoples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a>/<a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="Haplogroup_K_(M9)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_K_.28M9.29">Haplogroup K (M9)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Haplogroup K (M9)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_K_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup K (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup K (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p>Haplogroup K (M9) is spread all over <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a> and among <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a>.</p>
<p>K(xLT,K2a,K2b) – that is, K*, K2c, K2d or K2e – is found mainly in <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">Aboriginal Australians</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_South_East_Asia" title="Island South East Asia">Island South East Asia</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Haplogroups_L_&amp;_T_(K1)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroups_L_.26_T_.28K1.29">Haplogroups L &amp; T (K1)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Haplogroups L &amp; T (K1)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_LT" title="Haplogroup LT">Haplogroup LT</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_L-M20" title="Haplogroup L-M20">Haplogroup L</a> (M20) is found in South Asia, Central Asia, South-West Asia, and the Mediterranean.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_T_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup T (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup T</a> (M184, M70, M193, M272) is found at high levels in the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> (mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic_languages" title="Afro-Asiatic languages">Afro-Asiatic</a>-speaking peoples), parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. T-M184 is also found in significant minorities of <a href="/wiki/Sciacca" title="Sciacca">Sciaccensi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stilfs" title="Stilfs">Stilfser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fula_people" title="Fula people">Fulbe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_people" title="Egyptian people">Egyptians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Omanis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">Ibizans</a> (Eivissencs), and <a href="/wiki/Toubou_people" title="Toubou people">Toubou</a>. It is also found at low frequencies in other parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Haplogroup_K2_(K-M526)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_K2_.28K-M526.29">Haplogroup K2 (K-M526)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Haplogroup K2 (K-M526)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_K2" title="Haplogroup K2">Haplogroup K2</a></div>
<p>The only living males reported to carry the basal <a href="/wiki/Paragroup" title="Paragroup">paragroup</a> K2* are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australian" title="Indigenous Australian">indigenous Australians</a>. Major studies published in 2014 and 2015 suggest that up to 27% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australian" title="Aboriginal Australian">Aboriginal Australian</a> males carry K2*, while others carry a subclade of K2.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_N">Haplogroup N</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Haplogroup N">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_N_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup N (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup N (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_N_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup N (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup N</a> (M231) is found through northern Eurasia, especially among the <a href="/wiki/Uralic_peoples" title="Uralic peoples">Uralic peoples</a>.</p>
<p>Haplogroup N possibly originated in eastern Asia and spread both west into <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and north, being the most common group found in some <a href="/wiki/Uralic_peoples" title="Uralic peoples">Uralic speaking peoples</a>. Haplogroup O is found at its highest frequency in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, with lower frequencies in the <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">South Pacific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.</p>

<h3><span id="Haplogroup_P_(K2b2)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_P_.28K2b2.29">Haplogroup P (K2b2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Haplogroup P (K2b2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_P_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup P (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup P (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<p>Haplogroup P (P295) has two primary branches: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_P1_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup P1 (Y-DNA)">P1</a> (P-M45) and the extremely rare <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Haplogroup_P2_(Y-DNA)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Haplogroup P2 (Y-DNA) (page does not exist)">P2</a> (P-B253).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isogg2016_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-isogg2016-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>P*, P1* and P2 are found together only on the island of <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Philippines" title="The Philippines">The Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isogg2016_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-isogg2016-18">[18]</a></sup> In particular, P* and P1* are found at significant rates among members of the <a href="/wiki/Aeta_people" title="Aeta people">Aeta</a> (or Agta) people of Luzon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tumonggor_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-tumonggor-19">[19]</a></sup> While, P1* is now more common among living individuals in <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Eastern Siberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, it is also found at low levels in mainland <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" title="South East Asia">South East Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. Considered together, these distributions tend to suggest that P* emerged from K2b in South East Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tumonggor_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-tumonggor-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karafet_2014_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet_2014-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>P1 is also the parent node of two primary clades:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_Q_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup Q (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup Q</a> (Q-M242) and;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup R (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup R</a> (R-M207). These share the common marker M45 in addition to at least 18 other SNPs.</li>
</ul>
<p>Haplogroup Q (MEH2, M242, P36) <i>found in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a></i> Haplogroup R (M207, M306): <i>found in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Asia" title="West Asia">West Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a></i></p>

<h3><span id="Haplogroup_R_(M207)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haplogroup_R_.28M207.29">Haplogroup R (M207)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Y-chromosome_DNA_haplogroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Haplogroup R (M207)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup R (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup R (Y-DNA)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haplogroup_R_(Y-DNA).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="800" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Haplogroup_R_%28Y-DNA%29.jpg/300px-Haplogroup_R_%28Y-DNA%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Haplogroup_R_%28Y-DNA%29.jpg/450px-Haplogroup_R_%28Y-DNA%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Haplogroup_R_%28Y-DNA%29.jpg/600px-Haplogroup_R_%28Y-DNA%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

The hypothetical divergence of Haplogroup R and its descendants.</div>

<p>Haplogroup R is defined by the SNP M207. The bulk of <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup R (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup R</a> is represented in descendant subclade <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1" title="Haplogroup R1">R1</a>, which likely originated on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasian_Steppes" title="Eurasian Steppes">Eurasian Steppes</a>. R1 has two descendant subclades: <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1a" title="Haplogroup R1a">R1a</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1b" title="Haplogroup R1b">R1b</a>.</p>
<p>R1a is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Iranian_language" title="Proto-Indo-Iranian language">proto-Indo-Iranian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balto-Slavic_languages" title="Balto-Slavic languages">Balto-Slavic</a> peoples, and is now found primarily in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>.</p>
<p>Haplogroup R1b is the dominant haplogroup of Western Europe and also found sparsely distributed among various peoples of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Its subclade R1b1a2 (M269) is the haplogroup that is most commonly found among modern Western European populations, and has been associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italo-Celtic_languages" title="Italo-Celtic languages">Italo-Celtic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> peoples.</p>
