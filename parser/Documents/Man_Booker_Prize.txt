<p>The <b>Man Booker Prize for Fiction</b> (formerly known as the <b>Booker-McConnell Prize</b> and commonly known simply as the <b>Booker Prize</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Literary_award" title="Literary award">literary prize</a> awarded each year for the best original novel, written in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> and published in the UK. The winner of the Man Booker Prize is generally assured international renown and success; therefore, the prize is of great significance for the book trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> From its inception, only <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>, Irish, and South African (and later Zimbabwean) citizens were eligible to receive the prize; in 2014, however, this eligibility was widened to any English-language novel—a change which proved controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_rulechange2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_rulechange2-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/2016_Man_Booker_Prize" title="2016 Man Booker Prize">2016</a> and <a href="/wiki/2017_Man_Booker_Prize" title="2017 Man Booker Prize">2017</a>, the prize was won by writers from the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A high-profile literary award in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_culture" title="British culture">British culture</a>, the Booker Prize is greeted with great anticipation and fanfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is also a mark of distinction for authors to be selected for inclusion in the <a href="/wiki/Short_list" title="Short list">shortlist</a> or even to be nominated for the "longlist".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_administration">History and administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Booker_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prize was originally known as the Booker–McConnell Prize, after the company <a href="/wiki/Booker_Group" title="Booker Group">Booker, McConnell Ltd</a> began sponsoring the event in 1968;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup> it became commonly known as the "Booker Prize" or simply "the Booker".</p>
<p>When administration of the prize was transferred to the Booker Prize Foundation in 2002, the title sponsor became the investment company <a href="/wiki/Man_Group" title="Man Group">Man Group</a>, which opted to retain "Booker" as part of the official title of the prize. The foundation is an independent registered charity funded by the entire profits of Booker Prize Trading Ltd, of which it is the sole shareholder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The prize money awarded with the Booker Prize was originally £21,000, and was subsequently raised to £50,000 in 2002 under the sponsorship of the Man Group, making it one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_the_world%27s_richest_literary_prizes" title="List of the world's richest literary prizes">world's richest literary prizes</a>.</p>
<p>In 1970, <a href="/wiki/Bernice_Rubens" title="Bernice Rubens">Bernice Rubens</a> became the first woman to win the Booker Prize, for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elected_Member" title="The Elected Member">The Elected Member</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The rules of the Booker changed in 1971; previously, it had been awarded retrospectively to books published prior to the year in which the award was given. In 1971 the year of eligibility was changed to the same as the year of the award; in effect, this meant that books published in 1970 were not considered for the Booker in either year. The Booker Prize Foundation announced in January 2010 the creation of a special award called the "<a href="/wiki/Lost_Man_Booker_Prize" title="Lost Man Booker Prize">Lost Man Booker Prize</a>," with the winner chosen from a longlist of 22 novels published in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alice_Munro" title="Alice Munro">Alice Munro</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Beggar_Maid" title="The Beggar Maid">The Beggar Maid</a></i> was shortlisted in 1980, and remains the only short story collection to be shortlisted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before 2001, each year's longlist of nominees was not publicly revealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Sutherland_(author)" title="John Sutherland (author)">John Sutherland</a>, who was a judge for the 1999 prize, has said,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There is a well-established London literary community. <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Rushdie</a> doesn't get shortlisted now because he has attacked that community. That is not a good game plan if you want to win the Booker. <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a> has found the same thing in the US – you have to "be a citizen" if you want to win prizes. The real scandal is that [Martin] <a href="/wiki/Martin_Amis" title="Martin Amis">Amis</a> has never won the prize. In fact, he has only been shortlisted once and that was for <i><a href="/wiki/Time%27s_Arrow_(novel)" title="Time's Arrow (novel)">Time's Arrow</a></i>, which was not one of his strongest books. That really is suspicious. He pissed people off with <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Babies_(novel)" title="Dead Babies (novel)">Dead Babies</a></i> and that gets lodged in the culture. There is also the feeling that he has always looked towards America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-booker_fixed_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-booker_fixed-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1972, the winning writer <a href="/wiki/John_Berger" title="John Berger">John Berger</a>, known for his <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> worldview, protested during his acceptance speech against Booker McConnell. He blamed Booker's 130 years of sugar production in the Caribbean for the region's modern poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-white-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Berger_on_the_Booker_Prize_.281972.29_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Berger_on_the_Booker_Prize_.281972.29-14">[14]</a></sup> Berger donated half of his £5,000 prize to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Panther_Party#Groups_and_movements_inspired_by_the_Black_Panthers" title="Black Panther Party">British Black Panther movement</a>, because they had a socialist and revolutionary perspective in agreement with his own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-white-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Speech_by_John_Berger_on_accepting_the_Booker_Prize_for_Fiction_at_the_Caf.C3.A9_Royal_in_London_on_23_November_1972_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Speech_by_John_Berger_on_accepting_the_Booker_Prize_for_Fiction_at_the_Caf.C3.A9_Royal_in_London_on_23_November_1972-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Burgess" title="Anthony Burgess">Anthony Burgess</a>, writer of <i><a href="/wiki/Earthly_Powers" title="Earthly Powers">Earthly Powers</a></i>, refused to attend the ceremony unless it was confirmed to him in advance whether he had won.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup> His was one of two books considered likely to win, the other being <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rites_of_Passage_(novel)" title="Rites of Passage (novel)">Rites of Passage</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Golding" title="William Golding">William Golding</a>. The judges decided only 30 minutes before the ceremony, giving the prize to Golding. Both novels had been seen as favourites to win leading up to the prize, and the dramatic "literary battle" between two senior writers made front page news.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Judging for the 1983 award produced a draw between <a href="/wiki/J._M._Coetzee" title="J. M. Coetzee">J. M. Coetzee</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Life_%26_Times_of_Michael_K" title="Life &amp; Times of Michael K">Life &amp; Times of Michael K</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Shame_(Rushdie_novel)" title="Shame (Rushdie novel)">Shame</a></i>, leaving chair of judges <a href="/wiki/Fay_Weldon" title="Fay Weldon">Fay Weldon</a> to choose between the two. According to Stephen Moss in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, "Her arm was bent and she chose Rushdie" only to change her mind as the result was being phoned through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-booker_fixed_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-booker_fixed-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, two of the judges threatened to walk out when <i><a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(novel)" title="Trainspotting (novel)">Trainspotting</a></i> appeared on the longlist; <a href="/wiki/Irvine_Welsh" title="Irvine Welsh">Irvine Welsh</a>'s novel was pulled from the shortlist to satisfy them. The novel would later receive critical acclaim, and is now considered Welsh's masterpiece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The award has been criticised for the types of books it covers. In 1981, nominee <a href="/wiki/John_Banville" title="John Banville">John Banville</a> wrote a letter to <i>The Guardian</i> requesting that the prize be given to him so that he could use the money to buy every copy of the longlisted books in Ireland and donate them to libraries, "thus ensuring that the books not only are bought but also read — surely a unique occurrence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1994, Guardian literary editor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gott" title="Richard Gott">Richard Gott</a>, citing the lack of objective criteria and the exclusion of American authors, described the prize as "a significant and dangerous iceberg in the sea of British culture that serves as a symbol of its current malaise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, the decision to award <a href="/wiki/Arundhati_Roy" title="Arundhati Roy">Arundhati Roy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_God_of_Small_Things" title="The God of Small Things">The God of Small Things</a></i> proved controversial. <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Callil" title="Carmen Callil">Carmen Callil</a>, chair of the previous year's Booker judges, called it an "execrable" book and said on television that it shouldn't even have been on the shortlist. Booker Prize chairman <a href="/wiki/Martyn_Goff" title="Martyn Goff">Martyn Goff</a> said Roy won because nobody objected, following the rejection by the judges of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_MacLaverty" title="Bernard MacLaverty">Bernard MacLaverty</a>'s shortlisted book due to their dismissal of him as "a wonderful short-story writer and that <i><a href="/wiki/Grace_Notes" title="Grace Notes">Grace Notes</a></i> was three short stories strung together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/A._L._Kennedy" title="A. L. Kennedy">A. L. Kennedy</a>, who was a judge in 1996, called the prize "a pile of crooked nonsense" with the winner determined by "who knows who, who's sleeping with who, who's selling drugs to who, who's married to who, whose turn it is".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-booker_fixed_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-booker_fixed-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Booker prized created a permanent home for the archives from 1968 to present at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Brookes_University" title="Oxford Brookes University">Oxford Brookes University</a> Library. The Archive, which encompasses the administrative history of the Prize from 1968 to date, collects together a diverse range of material, including correspondence, publicity material, copies of both the Longlists and the Shortlists, minutes of meetings, photographs and material relating to the awards dinner (letters of invitation, guest lists, seating plans). Embargoes of ten or twenty years apply to certain categories of material; examples include all material relating to the judging process and the Longlist prior to 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 2005 and 2008, the Booker Prize alternated between writers from Ireland and India. "Outsider" <a href="/wiki/John_Banville" title="John Banville">John Banville</a> began this trend in 2005 when his novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sea_(novel)" title="The Sea (novel)">The Sea</a></i> was selected as a surprise winner:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Boyd Tonkin, literary editor of <i>The Independent</i>, famously condemned it as "possibly the most perverse decision in the history of the award" and rival novelist <a href="/wiki/Tibor_Fischer" title="Tibor Fischer">Tibor Fischer</a> poured scorn on Banville's victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kiran_Desai" title="Kiran Desai">Kiran Desai</a> of India won in 2006. <a href="/wiki/Anne_Enright" title="Anne Enright">Anne Enright</a>'s 2007 victory came about due to a jury badly split over <a href="/wiki/Ian_McEwan" title="Ian McEwan">Ian McEwan</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/On_Chesil_Beach" title="On Chesil Beach">On Chesil Beach</a></i>. The following year it was India's turn again, with <a href="/wiki/Aravind_Adiga" title="Aravind Adiga">Aravind Adiga</a> narrowly defeating Enright's fellow Irishman <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Barry" title="Sebastian Barry">Sebastian Barry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, the winner of the Man Booker Prize had been required to be a citizen of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>, the Republic of Ireland, or Zimbabwe. It was announced on 18 September 2013 that future Man Booker Prize awards would consider authors from anywhere in the world, so long as their work was in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and published in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This change proved controversial in literary circles. Former winner <a href="/wiki/A._S._Byatt" title="A. S. Byatt">A. S. Byatt</a> and former judge <a href="/wiki/John_Mullan" title="John Mullan">John Mullan</a> said the prize risked diluting its identity, whereas former judge A. L. Kennedy welcomed the change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_rulechange2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_rulechange2-3">[3]</a></sup> Following this expansion, the first winner not from the Commonwealth, Ireland, or Zimbabwe was American <a href="/wiki/Paul_Beatty" title="Paul Beatty">Paul Beatty</a> in 2016. Another American, <a href="/wiki/George_Saunders" title="George Saunders">George Saunders</a>, won the following year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judging">Judging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Booker_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Judging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The selection process for the winner of the prize commences with the formation of an advisory committee, which includes a writer, two publishers, a literary agent, a bookseller, a librarian, and a chairperson appointed by the Booker Prize Foundation. The advisory committee then selects the judging panel, the membership of which changes each year, although on rare occasions a judge may be selected a second time. Judges are selected from amongst leading literary critics, writers, academics and leading public figures.</p>
<p>The winner is usually announced at a ceremony in London's <a href="/wiki/Guildhall,_London" title="Guildhall, London">Guildhall</a>, usually in early October.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Winners">Winners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Booker_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Winners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_winners_and_shortlisted_authors_of_the_Booker_Prize_for_Fiction" title="List of winners and shortlisted authors of the Booker Prize for Fiction">List of winners and shortlisted authors of the Booker Prize for Fiction</a></div>
<p>The 2017 prize was awarded to <a href="/wiki/George_Saunders" title="George Saunders">George Saunders</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_in_the_Bardo" title="Lincoln in the Bardo">Lincoln in the Bardo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, to mark the prize's 25th anniversary, a <i>"Booker of Bookers" Prize</i> was given. Three previous judges of the award, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Bradbury" title="Malcolm Bradbury">Malcolm Bradbury</a>, David Holloway and W. L. Webb, met and chose <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Midnight%27s_Children" title="Midnight's Children">Midnight's Children</a></i>, the 1981 winner, as "the best novel out of all the winners."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/The_Best_of_the_Booker" title="The Best of the Booker">The Best of the Booker</a> was awarded in 2008 to celebrate the prize's 40th anniversary. A shortlist of six winners was chosen and the decision was left to a public vote; the winner was again <i>Midnight's Children</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_awards">Related awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Booker_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Related awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A separate prize for which any living writer in the world may qualify, the <a href="/wiki/Man_Booker_International_Prize" title="Man Booker International Prize">Man Booker International Prize</a> was inaugurated in 2005. Until 2015, it was given every two years to a living author of any nationality for a body of work published in English or generally available in English translation. In 2016, the award was significantly reconfigured, and is now given annually to a single book in English translation, with a £50,000 prize for the winning title, shared equally between author and translator.</p>
<p>A Russian version of the Booker Prize was created in 1992 called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booker-Open_Russia_Literary_Prize" title="Booker-Open Russia Literary Prize">Booker-Open Russia Literary Prize</a>, also known as the Russian Booker Prize. In 2007, Man Group plc established the <a href="/wiki/Man_Asian_Literary_Prize" title="Man Asian Literary Prize">Man Asian Literary Prize</a>, an annual literary award given to the best novel by an Asian writer, either written in English or translated into English, and published in the previous calendar year.</p>
<p>As part of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a>'</i> <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Literature_Festival" title="Cheltenham Literature Festival">Literature Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>, a Booker event is held on the last Saturday of the festival. Four guest speakers/judges debate a shortlist of four books from a given year from before the introduction of the Booker prize, and a winner is chosen. Unlike the real Man Booker (1969 through 2014), writers from outside the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">Commonwealth</a> are also considered. In 2008, the winner for 1948 was <a href="/wiki/Alan_Paton" title="Alan Paton">Alan Paton</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Cry,_the_Beloved_Country" title="Cry, the Beloved Country">Cry, the Beloved Country</a></i>, beating <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Naked_and_the_Dead" title="The Naked and the Dead">The Naked and the Dead</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heart_of_the_Matter" title="The Heart of the Matter">The Heart of the Matter</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Loved_One" title="The Loved One">The Loved One</a></i>. In 2015, the winner for 1915 was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_Maddox_Ford" title="Ford Maddox Ford">Ford Maddox Ford</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_Soldier" title="The Good Soldier">The Good Soldier</a></i>, beating <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thirty-Nine_Steps" title="The Thirty-Nine Steps">The Thirty-Nine Steps</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/John_Buchan" title="John Buchan">John Buchan</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Of_Human_Bondage" title="Of Human Bondage">Of Human Bondage</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somerset_Maugham" title="Somerset Maugham">Somerset Maugham</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Psmith,_Journalist" title="Psmith, Journalist">Psmith, Journalist</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/P._G._Wodehouse" title="P. G. Wodehouse">P. G. Wodehouse</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_Out" title="The Voyage Out">The Voyage Out</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup></p>
