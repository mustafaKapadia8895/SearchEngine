<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mesocyclone</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

Storm relative motion of a tornado-producing mesocyclone over Greensburg, Kansas on May 4 2007. The storm was producing an EF5 tornado at the time of the image.</div>

<p>A <b>mesocyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortex</a> of air within a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convective</a> <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MesocyloneDef_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MesocyloneDef-1">[1]</a></sup> It is air that rises and rotates around a vertical axis, usually in the same direction as low pressure systems in a given hemisphere. They are most often cyclonic, that is, associated with a localized <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure region</a> within a severe thunderstorm. Such <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> can feature strong surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_wind" title="Straight-line wind">winds</a> and severe <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>. Mesocyclones often occur together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updrafts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a>, within which <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> may form at the interchange with certain downdrafts.</p>
<p>Mesocyclones are localized, approximately 2 km (1.2 mi) to 10 km (6.2 mi) in diameter within strong thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MesocyloneDef_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MesocyloneDef-1">[1]</a></sup> Thunderstorms containing persistent mesocyclones are supercell thunderstorms. Mesocyclones occur on the "<a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a>" from a few kilometers to hundreds of kilometers. <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">Doppler radar</a> is used to identify mesocyclones. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesovortex" title="Mesovortex">mesovortex</a> is a similar but typically smaller and weaker rotational feature associated with <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesocyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mesocyclones form when strong changes of wind speed and/or direction with height ("<a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>") sets parts of the lower part of the atmosphere spinning in invisible tube-like rolls. The convective updraft of a thunderstorm then draw up this spinning air, tilting the rolls' orientation upward (from parallel to the ground to perpendicular) and causing the entire updraft to rotate as a vertical column.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the updraft rotates and ingests cooler moister air from the forward flank downdraft (FFD), it may form a <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a>, a spinning layer of clouds lowered from ambient storm <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a> under the mid-level mesocyclone. The wall cloud tends to form closer to the center of the mesocyclone. As it descends, a <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">funnel cloud</a> may form near its center. This is the first visible stage of <a href="/wiki/Tornadogenesis" title="Tornadogenesis">tornadogenesis</a>.</p>

<p>The best way to detect and verify the presence of a mesocyclone is by <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a>. Nearby high values of opposite sign within velocity data are how they are detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus the word <i>mesocyclone</i> is associated with weather radar terminology. Mesocyclones are most often identified in the right-rear flank of supercell thunderstorms and squall lines, and may be distinguished by a <a href="/wiki/Hook_echo" title="Hook echo">hook echo</a> rotation signature on a weather radar map. Visual cues such as a rotating wall cloud or tornado may also hint at the presence of a mesocyclone. This is why the term has entered into wider usage in connection with rotating features in severe storms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornado_formation">Tornado formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesocyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tornado formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dszpics1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="679" data-file-width="1024" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Dszpics1.jpg/250px-Dszpics1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Dszpics1.jpg/375px-Dszpics1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Dszpics1.jpg/500px-Dszpics1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dszpics1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A tornado near <a href="/wiki/Anadarko,_Oklahoma" title="Anadarko, Oklahoma">Anadarko, Oklahoma</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></div>
<p>Tornado formation generally occurs in one of two ways.</p>
<p>In the first method, two conditions must be first satisfied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssl.noaa.gov_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nssl.noaa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup> One, an invisible horizontal spinning effect must be first formed upon the Earth's surface. This is usually formed by sudden changes in wind direction or speed, known as wind shear. Second, a thundercloud, or occasionally a cumulus cloud, must be present. During a thunderstorm, the updrafts presented are occasionally powerful enough to lift the horizontal spinning row of air upwards, turning it into a vertical air column. This vertical air column then becomes the basic structure for the tornado. Tornadoes that form in this way are often classified as weak tornadoes and [generally] last for less than 10 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssl.noaa.gov_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nssl.noaa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second method of formation occurs during the occurrence of a supercell thunderstorm. This type of tornado forms by the updrafts present in the supercell thunderstorm. When winds occurring during this phenomenon increase and intensify, the force released can cause the updrafts to rotate. This rotating updraft is known as a mesocyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For a tornado to form in this manner, a downdraft called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear-flank_downdraft" title="Rear-flank downdraft">rear-flank downdraft</a> enters the center of the mesocyclone from the back. Cold air, being denser than warm air is able to penetrate through the updraft. The combination of the updraft and downdraft completes the development of a tornado. Tornadoes that form in this method are often classified as violent and are capable of lasting for more than one hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssl.noaa.gov_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-nssl.noaa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscale_convective_vortex">Mesoscale convective vortex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesocyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mesoscale convective vortex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_vortex" title="Mesoscale convective vortex">Mesoscale convective vortex</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_vortex" title="Mesoscale convective vortex">mesoscale convective vortex</a> (MCV), also known as a mesoscale vorticity center or Neddy eddy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cimss_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cimss-6">[6]</a></sup> is a mesocyclone within an <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_system" title="Mesoscale convective system">mesoscale convective system</a> (MCS) that pulls winds into a circling pattern, or vortex, at the mid levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and is normally associated with anticyclonic outflow aloft. With a core only 30 to 60 miles wide and 1 to 3 miles deep, an MCV is often overlooked in standard <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather maps</a>. MCVs can persist for up to 2 days after its parent mesoscale convective system has dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cimss_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-cimss-6">[6]</a></sup> The orphaned MCV can become the seed of the next thunderstorm outbreak. An MCV that moves into tropical waters, such as the Gulf of Mexico, can serve as the nucleus for a tropical storm or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a>. MCVs can produce very large wind storms; sometimes winds can reach over 100 mph. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_2009_Southern_Midwest_Derecho" title="May 2009 Southern Midwest Derecho">May 2009 Southern Midwest Derecho</a> was an extreme progressive <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a> and mesoscale convective vortex event that struck Southeastern Kansas, Southern Missouri, and Southwestern Illinois on May 8, 2009.</p>
