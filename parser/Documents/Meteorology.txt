<p><b>Meteorology</b> is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">atmospheric sciences</a> which includes <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_physics" title="Atmospheric physics">atmospheric physics</a>, with a major focus on <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>. The study of meteorology dates back <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennia" title="Millennia">millennia</a>, though significant progress in meteorology did not occur until the 18th century. The 19th century saw modest progress in the field after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_observation" title="Weather observation">weather observation</a> networks were formed across broad regions. Prior attempts at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_prediction" title="Weather prediction">prediction of weather</a> depended on historical data. It wasn't until after the elucidation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_of_physics" title="Laws of physics">laws of physics</a> and, more particularly, the development of the computer, allowing for the automated solution of a great many equations that model the weather, in the latter half of the 20th century that significant breakthroughs in weather forecasting were achieved.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_meteorological_phenomena" title="List of meteorological phenomena">Meteorological phenomena</a> are observable weather events that are explained by the science of meteorology. Meteorological phenomena are described and quantified by the variables of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>: <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pressure" title="Air pressure">air pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow" title="Mass flow">mass flow</a>, and the variations and interactions of those variables, and how they change over time. Different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(spatial)" title="Scale (spatial)">spatial scales</a> are used to describe and predict weather on local, regional, and global levels.</p>
<p>Meteorology, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_physics" title="Atmospheric physics">atmospheric physics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a> are sub-disciplines of the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">atmospheric sciences</a>. Meteorology and <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> compose the interdisciplinary field of <a href="/wiki/Hydrometeorology" title="Hydrometeorology">hydrometeorology</a>. The interactions between Earth's atmosphere and its oceans are part of a coupled ocean-atmosphere system. Meteorology has application in many diverse fields such as the military, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_production" title="Energy production">energy production</a>, transport, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>.</p>
<p>The word "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meteorology" title="wikt:meteorology">meteorology</a>" is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μετέωρος</span> <i>metéōros</i> "lofty; high (in the sky)" (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μετα-</span> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meta-" title="wikt:meta-">meta-</a></i> "above" and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀείρω</span> <i>aeiro</i> "I lift up") and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-λογία</span> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logia" title="wikt:-logia">-logia</a></i> "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logy" title="wikt:-logy">-(o)logy</a>", i.e. "the study of things in the air".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_meteorology" title="Timeline of meteorology">Timeline of meteorology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AlpineRainbow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1684" data-file-width="2605" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/AlpineRainbow.jpg/220px-AlpineRainbow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/AlpineRainbow.jpg/330px-AlpineRainbow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/AlpineRainbow.jpg/440px-AlpineRainbow.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AlpineRainbow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parhelion" title="Parhelion">Parhelion (sundog)</a> in <a href="/wiki/Savoie" title="Savoie">Savoie</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ability to predict rains and floods based on annual cycles was evidently used by humans at least from the time of agricultural settlement if not earlier. Early approaches to predicting weather were based on <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a> and were practiced by priests. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">Cuneiform</a> inscriptions on <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylonian</a> tablets included associations between thunder and rain. The <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Chaldeans</a> differentiated the <a href="/wiki/22%C2%B0_halo" title="22° halo">22°</a> and <a href="/wiki/46%C2%B0_halo" title="46° halo">46° halos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ancient <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a> contain mentions of clouds and seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imd_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-imd-2">[2]</a></sup> The Samaveda mentions sacrifices to be performed when certain phenomena were noticed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Var%C4%81hamihira" title="Varāhamihira">Varāhamihira</a>'s classical work <i>Brihatsamhita</i>, written about 500 AD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imd_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-imd-2">[2]</a></sup> provides evidence of weather observation.</p>
<p>In 350 BC, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Meteorology_(Aristotle)" title="Meteorology (Aristotle)">Meteorology</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Aristotle is considered the founder of meteorology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> One of the most impressive achievements described in the <i>Meteorology</i> is the description of what is now known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_Cycle" title="Water Cycle">hydrologic cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aristotle_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aristotle-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Mundo" title="De Mundo">De Mundo</a> (composed before 250 BC or between 350 and 200 BC) noted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1908DeMundo_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-1908DeMundo-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>If the flashing body is set on fire and rushes violently to the Earth it is called a thunderbolt; if it is only half of fire, but violent also and massive, it is called a <i>meteor</i>; if it is entirely free from fire, it is called a smoking bolt. They are all called 'swooping bolts' because they swoop down upon the Earth. Lightning is sometimes smoky, and is then called 'smoldering lightning"; sometimes it darts quickly along, and is then said to be <i>vivid</i>. At other times, it travels in crooked lines, and is called <i>forked lightning</i>. When it swoops down upon some object it is called 'swooping lightning'.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> scientist <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> compiled a book on weather forecasting, called the <i>Book of Signs</i>. The work of Theophrastus remained a dominant influence in the study of weather and in weather forecasting for nearly 2,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 25 AD, <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a>, a geographer for the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, formalized the climatic zone system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> According to Toufic Fahd, around the 9th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Dinawari" title="Al-Dinawari">Al-Dinawari</a> wrote the <i>Kitab al-Nabat</i> (<i>Book of Plants</i>), in which he deals with the application of meteorology to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_Agricultural_Revolution" title="Muslim Agricultural Revolution">Muslim Agricultural Revolution</a>. He describes the meteorological character of the sky, the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a>, the sun and <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phases</a> indicating <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> and rain, the <i>anwa</i> (<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">heavenly bodies</a> of rain), and atmospheric phenomena such as winds, thunder, lightning, snow, floods, valleys, rivers, lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fahd-815_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fahd-815-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (September 2010)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Early attempts at predicting weather were often related to prophesy and divining and sometimes based on astrological ideas. Admiral FitzRoy tried to separate scientific approaches from prophetic ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_of_visual_atmospheric_phenomena">Research of visual atmospheric phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research of visual atmospheric phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1374" data-file-width="2000" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg/220px-Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg/330px-Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg/440px-Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baker_beach_at_twilight_41.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Twilight at <a href="/wiki/Baker_Beach" title="Baker Beach">Baker Beach</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">Rainbow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">Twilight</a></div>
<p>Ptolemy wrote on the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a> of light in the context of astronomical observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1021, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alhazen" title="Alhazen">Alhazen</a> showed that atmospheric refraction is also responsible for <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>; he estimated that twilight begins when the sun is 19 degrees below the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>, and also used a geometric determination based on this to estimate the maximum possible height of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> as 52,000 <i>passim</i> (about 49 miles, or 79 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Albert_the_Great" title="St. Albert the Great">St. Albert the Great</a> was the first to propose that each drop of falling rain had the form of a small sphere, and that this form meant that the rainbow was produced by light interacting with each raindrop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a> was the first to calculate the angular size of the rainbow. He stated that a rainbow summit can not appear higher than 42 degrees above the horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the late 13th century and early 14th century, <a href="/wiki/Kam%C4%81l_al-D%C4%ABn_al-F%C4%81ris%C4%AB" title="Kamāl al-Dīn al-Fārisī">Kamāl al-Dīn al-Fārisī</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodoric_of_Freiberg" title="Theodoric of Freiberg">Theodoric of Freiberg</a> were the first to give the correct explanations for the primary <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbow</a> phenomenon. Theoderic went further and also explained the secondary rainbow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1716, Edmund Halley suggested that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurorae</a> are caused by "magnetic effluvia" moving along the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> lines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments_and_classification_scales">Instruments and classification scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Instruments and classification scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Scale" title="Beaufort Scale">Beaufort Scale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wea00920.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1740" data-file-width="1184" height="323" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Wea00920.jpg/220px-Wea00920.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Wea00920.jpg/330px-Wea00920.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Wea00920.jpg/440px-Wea00920.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wea00920.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A hemispherical cup anemometer</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1441, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejong_the_Great_of_Joseon" title="Sejong the Great of Joseon">King Sejong</a>'s son, Prince Munjong, invented the first standardized <a href="/wiki/Rain_gauge" title="Rain gauge">rain gauge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These were sent throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> as an official tool to assess land taxes based upon a farmer's potential harvest. In 1450, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leone_Battista_Alberti" title="Leone Battista Alberti">Leone Battista Alberti</a> developed a swinging-plate <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a>, and was known as the first <i>anemometer</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1607, <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> constructed a <a href="/wiki/Galileo_thermometer" title="Galileo thermometer">thermoscope</a>. In 1611, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> wrote the first scientific treatise on snow crystals: "Strena Seu de Nive Sexangula (A New Year's Gift of Hexagonal Snow)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1643, <a href="/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli" title="Evangelista Torricelli">Evangelista Torricelli</a> invented the mercury <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1662, Sir <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a> invented the mechanical, self-emptying, tipping bucket rain gauge. In 1714, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabriel_Fahrenheit" title="Gabriel Fahrenheit">Gabriel Fahrenheit</a> created a reliable scale for measuring temperature with a mercury-type thermometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1742, <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a>, a Swedish astronomer, proposed the "centigrade" temperature scale, the predecessor of the current <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1783, the first hair <a href="/wiki/Hygrometer" title="Hygrometer">hygrometer</a> was demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Horace-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9dict_de_Saussure" title="Horace-Bénédict de Saussure">Horace-Bénédict de Saussure</a>. In 1802–1803, <a href="/wiki/Luke_Howard" title="Luke Howard">Luke Howard</a> wrote <i>On the Modification of Clouds</i>, in which he assigns <a href="/wiki/List_of_cloud_types" title="List of cloud types">cloud types</a> <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1806, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a> introduced his <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">system for classifying wind speeds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Near the end of the 19th century the first <a href="/wiki/Cloud_atlas" title="Cloud atlas">cloud atlases</a> were published, including the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Cloud_Atlas" title="International Cloud Atlas">International Cloud Atlas</a></i>, which has remained in print ever since. The April 1960 launch of the first successful <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellite</a>, <a href="/wiki/TIROS-1" title="TIROS-1">TIROS-1</a>, marked the beginning of the age where weather information became available globally.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_composition_research">Atmospheric composition research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atmospheric composition research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1648, <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a> rediscovered that <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> decreases with height, and deduced that there is a vacuum above the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1738, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">Daniel Bernoulli</a> published <i>Hydrodynamics</i>, initiating the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">Kinetic theory of gases</a> and established the basic laws for the theory of gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1761, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Black" title="Joseph Black">Joseph Black</a> discovered that <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> absorbs <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> without changing its temperature when melting. In 1772, Black's student <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Rutherford" title="Daniel Rutherford">Daniel Rutherford</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, which he called <i>phlogisticated air</i>, and together they developed the <a href="/wiki/Phlogiston_theory" title="Phlogiston theory">phlogiston theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1777, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and developed an explanation for combustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1783, in Lavoisier's essay "Reflexions sur le phlogistique",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> he deprecates the phlogiston theory and proposes a <a href="/wiki/Caloric_theory" title="Caloric theory">caloric theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 1804, Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Leslie_(physicist)" title="John Leslie (physicist)">John Leslie</a> observed that a matte black surface radiates heat more effectively than a polished surface, suggesting the importance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_body_radiation" title="Black body radiation">black body radiation</a>. In 1808, <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a> defended caloric theory in <i>A New System of Chemistry</i> and described how it combines with matter, especially gases; he proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> of gases varies inversely with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a>. In 1824, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">Sadi Carnot</a> analyzed the efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> using caloric theory; he developed the notion of a <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible process</a> and, in postulating that no such thing exists in nature, laid the foundation for the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_into_cyclones_and_air_flow">Research into cyclones and air flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Research into cyclones and air flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">Prevailing winds</a></div>
<p>In 1494, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> experienced a tropical cyclone, which led to the first written European account of a hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 1686, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmund_Halley" title="Edmund Halley">Edmund Halley</a> presented a systematic study of the <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a> and identified solar heating as the cause of atmospheric motions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1735, an <i>ideal</i> explanation of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">global circulation</a> through study of the <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> was written by <a href="/wiki/George_Hadley" title="George Hadley">George Hadley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1743, when <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> was prevented from seeing a lunar eclipse by a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a>, he decided that cyclones move in a contrary manner to the winds at their periphery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorst_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dorst-34">[34]</a></sup> Understanding the kinematics of how exactly the rotation of the Earth affects airflow was partial at first. Gaspard-Gustave Coriolis published a paper in 1835 on the energy yield of machines with rotating parts, such as waterwheels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corps_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-corps-35">[35]</a></sup> In 1856, <a href="/wiki/William_Ferrel" title="William Ferrel">William Ferrel</a> proposed the existence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrel_cell" title="Ferrel cell">circulation cell</a> in the mid-latitudes, and the air within deflected by the Coriolis force resulting in the prevailing westerly winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Late in the 19th century, the motion of air masses along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">isobars</a> was understood to be the result of the large-scale interaction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a> and the deflecting force. By 1912, this deflecting force was named the Coriolis effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Just after World War I, a group of meteorologists in Norway led by <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Bjerknes" title="Vilhelm Bjerknes">Vilhelm Bjerknes</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_cyclone_model" title="Norwegian cyclone model">Norwegian cyclone model</a> that explains the generation, intensification and ultimate decay (the life cycle) of <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">mid-latitude cyclones</a>, and introduced the idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Front_(meteorology)" title="Front (meteorology)">fronts</a>, that is, sharply defined boundaries between <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air masses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorCycMod_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-NorCycMod-38">[38]</a></sup> The group included <a href="/wiki/Carl-Gustaf_Rossby" title="Carl-Gustaf Rossby">Carl-Gustaf Rossby</a> (who was the first to explain the large scale atmospheric flow in terms of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tor_Bergeron" title="Tor Bergeron">Tor Bergeron</a> (who first determined how rain forms) and <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Bjerknes" title="Jacob Bjerknes">Jacob Bjerknes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_networks_and_weather_forecasting">Observation networks and weather forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Observation networks and weather forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wolkenstockwerke.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="912" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Wolkenstockwerke.png/300px-Wolkenstockwerke.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Wolkenstockwerke.png/450px-Wolkenstockwerke.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Wolkenstockwerke.png/600px-Wolkenstockwerke.png 2x" width="300"/></a>

</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_surface_weather_analysis" title="History of surface weather analysis">History of surface weather analysis</a></div>
<p>In 1654, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinando_II_de_Medici" title="Ferdinando II de Medici">Ferdinando II de Medici</a> established the first <i>weather observing</i> network, that consisted of meteorological stations in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cutigliano" title="Cutigliano">Cutigliano</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vallombrosa" title="Vallombrosa">Vallombrosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parma" title="Parma">Parma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Osnabrück">Osnabrück</a>, Paris and <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>. The collected data were sent to Florence at regular time intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 1832, an electromagnetic telegraph was created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baron_Schilling" title="Baron Schilling">Baron Schilling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">electrical telegraph</a> in 1837 afforded, for the first time, a practical method for quickly gathering <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface weather observations</a> from a wide area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This data could be used to produce maps of the state of the atmosphere for a region near the Earth's surface and to study how these states evolved through time. To make frequent weather forecasts based on these data required a reliable network of observations, but it was not until 1849 that the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> began to establish an observation network across the United States under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Henry" title="Joseph Henry">Joseph Henry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Similar observation networks were established in Europe at this time. The Reverend <a href="/wiki/William_Clement_Ley" title="William Clement Ley">William Clement Ley</a> was key in understanding of cirrus clouds and early understandings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_Stream" title="Jet Stream">Jet Streams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Later after this <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Kenneth_Mackinnon_Douglas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles Kenneth Mackinnon Douglas (page does not exist)">Charles Kenneth Mackinnon Douglas</a> known as 'CKM' Douglas read Ley's papers after his death and carried on the early study of weather systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Nineteenth century researchers in meteorology were drawn from military or medical backgrounds, rather than trained as dedicated scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1854, the United Kingdom government appointed <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a> to the new office of <i>Meteorological Statist to the Board of Trade</i> with the task of gathering weather observations at sea. FitzRoy's office became the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">United Kingdom Meteorological Office</a> in 1854, the second oldest national meteorological service in the world (the <a href="/wiki/Central_Institution_for_Meteorology_and_Geodynamics" title="Central Institution for Meteorology and Geodynamics">Central Institution for Meteorology and Geodynamics</a> (ZAMG) in Austria was founded in 1851 and is the oldest weather service in the world). The first daily weather forecasts made by FitzRoy's Office were published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> newspaper in 1860. The following year a system was introduced of hoisting storm warning cones at principal ports when a gale was expected.</p>
<p>Over the next 50 years many countries established national meteorological services. The <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (1875) was established to follow tropical cyclone and <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The Finnish Meteorological Central Office (1881) was formed from part of Magnetic Observatory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helsinki_University" title="Helsinki University">Helsinki University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Japan's Tokyo Meteorological Observatory, the forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a>, began constructing surface weather maps in 1883.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> (1890) was established under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Australian Bureau of Meteorology</a> (1906) was established by a Meteorology Act to unify existing state meteorological services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_weather_prediction">Numerical weather prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Numerical weather prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">Numerical weather prediction</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1180" data-file-width="1504" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg/220px-Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg/330px-Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg/440px-Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weather_Bureau_1965.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>In 1904, Norwegian scientist <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Bjerknes" title="Vilhelm Bjerknes">Vilhelm Bjerknes</a> first argued in his paper <i>Weather Forecasting as a Problem in Mechanics and Physics</i> that it should be possible to forecast weather from calculations based upon <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">natural laws</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was not until later in the 20th century that advances in the understanding of atmospheric physics led to the foundation of modern <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a>. In 1922, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson" title="Lewis Fry Richardson">Lewis Fry Richardson</a> published "Weather Prediction By Numerical Process",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> after finding notes and derivations he worked on as an ambulance driver in World War I. He described how small terms in the prognostic fluid dynamics equations that govern atmospheric flow could be neglected, and a numerical calculation scheme that could be devised to allow predictions. Richardson envisioned a large auditorium of thousands of people performing the calculations. However, the sheer number of calculations required was too large to complete without electronic computers, and the size of the grid and time steps used in the calculations led to unrealistic results. Though numerical analysis later found that this was due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_instability" title="Numerical instability">numerical instability</a>.</p>
<p>Starting in the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numerical</a> forecasts with computers became feasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_forecast" title="Weather forecast">weather forecasts</a> derived this way used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">barotropic</a> (single-vertical-level) models, and could successfully predict the large-scale movement of midlatitude <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby waves</a>, that is, the pattern of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">atmospheric lows</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">highs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In 1959, the UK Meteorological Office received its first computer, a <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Mercury" title="Ferranti Mercury">Ferranti Mercury</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> nature of the atmosphere was first observed and mathematically described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Lorenz" title="Edward Lorenz">Edward Lorenz</a>, founding the field of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> These advances have led to the current use of <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_forecasting" title="Ensemble forecasting">ensemble forecasting</a> in most major forecasting centers, to take into account uncertainty arising from the chaotic nature of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCens_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPCens-57">[57]</a></sup> Mathematical models used to predict the long term weather of the Earth (<a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate models</a>), have been developed that have a resolution today that are as coarse as the older weather prediction models. These climate models are used to investigate long-term <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> shifts, such as what effects might be caused by human emission of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorologists">Meteorologists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Meteorologists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">Weather forecasting</a></div>
<p><b>Meteorologists</b> are scientists who study meteorology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The American Meteorological Society published and continually updates an authoritative electronic <i>Meteorology Glossary</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Meteorologists work in <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government agencies</a>, private consulting and <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> services, industrial enterprises, utilities, radio and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_stations" title="Television stations">television stations</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>. In the United States, meteorologists held about 9,400 jobs in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLS-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meteorologists are best known by the public for <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>. Some radio and television weather forecasters are professional meteorologists, while others are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reporters" title="Reporters">reporters</a> (weather specialist, weatherman, etc.) with no formal meteorological training. The <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Association" title="National Weather Association">National Weather Association</a> issue "Seals of Approval" to weather broadcasters who meet certain requirements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_instrumentation" title="Meteorological instrumentation">Meteorological instrumentation</a></div>
<p>Each science has its own unique sets of laboratory equipment. In the atmosphere, there are many things or qualities of the atmosphere that can be measured. Rain, which can be observed, or seen anywhere and anytime was one of the first atmospheric qualities measured historically. Also, two other accurately measured qualities are wind and humidity. Neither of these can be seen but can be felt. The devices to measure these three sprang up in the mid-15th century and were respectively the <a href="/wiki/Rain_gauge" title="Rain gauge">rain gauge</a>, the anemometer, and the hygrometer. Many attempts had been made prior to the 15th century to construct adequate equipment to measure the many atmospheric variables. Many were faulty in some way or were simply not reliable. Even <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> noted this in some of his work as the difficulty to measure the air.</p>
<p>Sets of surface measurements are important data to meteorologists. They give a snapshot of a variety of weather conditions at one single location and are usually at a <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather station</a>, a ship or a <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">weather buoy</a>. The measurements taken at a weather station can include any number of atmospheric observables. Usually, temperature, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a>, wind measurements, and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> are the variables that are measured by a thermometer, barometer, anemometer, and hygrometer, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Professional stations may also include air quality sensors (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ceilometer" title="Ceilometer">ceilometer</a> (cloud ceiling), falling precipitation sensor, <a href="/wiki/Level_sensor" title="Level sensor">flood sensor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning sensor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> (<a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic booms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a>), <a href="/wiki/Pyranometer" title="Pyranometer">pyranometer</a>/<a href="/wiki/Pyrheliometer" title="Pyrheliometer">pyrheliometer</a>/<a href="/wiki/Spectroradiometer" title="Spectroradiometer">spectroradiometer</a> (IR/Vis/UV <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photodiodes" title="Photodiodes">photodiodes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Rain_gauge" title="Rain gauge">rain gauge</a>/<a href="/wiki/Snow_gauge" title="Snow gauge">snow gauge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scintillation_counter" title="Scintillation counter">scintillation counter</a> (<a href="/wiki/Background_radiation" title="Background radiation">background radiation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fallout" title="Fallout">fallout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">radon</a>), <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismometer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> and tremors), <a href="/wiki/Transmissometer" title="Transmissometer">transmissometer</a> (visibility), and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS_clock" title="GPS clock">GPS clock</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_logging" title="Data logging">data logging</a>. Upper air data are of crucial importance for weather forecasting. The most widely used technique is launches of <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosondes</a>. Supplementing the radiosondes a <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_Meteorological_Data_Relay" title="Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay">network of aircraft collection</a> is organized by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">Remote sensing</a>, as used in meteorology, is the concept of collecting data from remote weather events and subsequently producing weather information. The common types of remote sensing are <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">Lidar</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">satellites</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Photogrammetry" title="Photogrammetry">photogrammetry</a>). Each collects data about the atmosphere from a remote location and, usually, stores the data where the instrument is located. Radar and Lidar are not passive because both use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EM_radiation" title="EM radiation">EM radiation</a> to illuminate a specific portion of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Weather satellites along with more general-purpose Earth-observing satellites circling the earth at various altitudes have become an indispensable tool for studying a wide range of phenomena from forest fires to <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spatial_scales">Spatial scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Spatial scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The study of the atmosphere can be divided into distinct areas that depend on both time and spatial scales. At one extreme of this scale is climatology. In the timescales of hours to days, meteorology separates into micro-, meso-, and synoptic scale meteorology. Respectively, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geospatial" title="Geospatial">geospatial</a> size of each of these three scales relates directly with the appropriate timescale.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microscale">Microscale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Microscale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Microscale_meteorology" title="Microscale meteorology">Microscale meteorology</a></div>
<p>Microscale meteorology is the study of atmospheric phenomena on a scale of about 1 kilometre (0.62 mi) or less. Individual thunderstorms, clouds, and local turbulence caused by buildings and other obstacles (such as individual hills) are modeled on this scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscale">Mesoscale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mesoscale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">Mesoscale meteorology</a></div>
<p>Mesoscale meteorology is the study of atmospheric phenomena that has horizontal scales ranging from 1 km to 1000 km and a vertical scale that starts at the Earth's surface and includes the atmospheric boundary layer, troposphere, <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, and the lower section of the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Mesoscale timescales last from less than a day to weeks. The events typically of interest are <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">fronts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbands" title="Rainbands">precipitation bands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, and topographically generated weather systems such as mountain waves and <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea and land breezes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synoptic_scale">Synoptic scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Synoptic scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">Synoptic scale meteorology</a></div>
<p>Synoptic scale meteorology predicts atmospheric changes at scales up to 1000 km and 10<sup>5</sup> sec (28 days), in time and space. At the synoptic scale, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_acceleration" title="Coriolis acceleration">Coriolis acceleration</a> acting on moving air masses (outside of the tropics) plays a dominant role in predictions. The phenomena typically described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_meteorology" title="Synoptic meteorology">synoptic meteorology</a> include events such as extratropical cyclones, baroclinic troughs and ridges, <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal zones</a>, and to some extent <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet streams</a>. All of these are typically given on <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather maps</a> for a specific time. The minimum horizontal scale of synoptic phenomena is limited to the spacing between <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">surface observation stations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_scale">Global scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Global scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Annual mean sea surface temperatures.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Global scale meteorology is the study of weather patterns related to the transport of heat from the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a>. Very large scale oscillations are of importance at this scale. These oscillations have time periods typically on the order of months, such as the <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden–Julian oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</a>, or years, such as the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_decadal_oscillation" title="Pacific decadal oscillation">Pacific decadal oscillation</a>. Global scale meteorology pushes into the range of climatology. The traditional definition of climate is pushed into larger timescales and with the understanding of the longer time scale global oscillations, their effect on climate and weather disturbances can be included in the synoptic and mesoscale timescales predictions.</p>
<p>Numerical Weather Prediction is a main focus in understanding air–sea interaction, tropical meteorology, atmospheric predictability, and tropospheric/stratospheric processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="Naval Research Laboratory">Naval Research Laboratory</a> in Monterey, California, developed a global atmospheric model called <a href="/wiki/Navy_Operational_Global_Atmospheric_Prediction_System" title="Navy Operational Global Atmospheric Prediction System">Navy Operational Global Atmospheric Prediction System</a> (NOGAPS). NOGAPS is run operationally at <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Numerical_Meteorology_and_Oceanography_Center" title="Fleet Numerical Meteorology and Oceanography Center">Fleet Numerical Meteorology and Oceanography Center</a> for the United States Military. Many other global atmospheric models are run by national meteorological agencies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_meteorological_principles">Some meteorological principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Some meteorological principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_layer_meteorology">Boundary layer meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Boundary layer meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">Boundary layer</a> meteorology is the study of processes in the air layer directly above Earth's surface, known as the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">atmospheric boundary layer</a> (ABL). The effects of the surface – heating, cooling, and <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> – cause <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent mixing</a> within the air layer. Significant movement of <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> on time scales of less than a day are caused by turbulent motions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Boundary layer meteorology includes the study of all types of surface–atmosphere boundary, including ocean, lake, urban land and non-urban land for the study of meteorology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_meteorology">Dynamic meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Dynamic meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dynamic meteorology generally focuses on the <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> of the atmosphere. The idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">air parcel</a> is used to define the smallest element of the atmosphere, while ignoring the discrete molecular and chemical nature of the atmosphere. An air parcel is defined as a point in the fluid continuum of the atmosphere. The fundamental laws of fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, and motion are used to study the atmosphere. The physical quantities that characterize the state of the atmosphere are temperature, density, pressure, etc. These variables have unique values in the continuum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_forecasting">Weather forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Weather forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Day5pressureforecast.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="613" data-file-width="1024" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Day5pressureforecast.png/300px-Day5pressureforecast.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Day5pressureforecast.png/450px-Day5pressureforecast.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Day5pressureforecast.png/600px-Day5pressureforecast.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Day5pressureforecast.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Forecast of surface pressures five days into the future for the north Pacific, North America, and north Atlantic Ocean</div>

</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">Weather forecasting</a></div>
<p>Weather forecasting is the application of science and technology to predict the state of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> at a future time and given location. Humans have attempted to predict the weather informally for millennia and formally since at least the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Weather forecasts are made by collecting quantitative <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> about the current state of the atmosphere and using scientific understanding of atmospheric processes to project how the atmosphere will evolve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once an all-human endeavor based mainly upon changes in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a>, current weather conditions, and sky condition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">forecast models</a> are now used to determine future conditions. Human input is still required to pick the best possible forecast model to base the forecast upon, which involves pattern recognition skills, <a href="/wiki/Teleconnection" title="Teleconnection">teleconnections</a>, knowledge of model performance, and knowledge of model biases. The <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> nature of the atmosphere, the massive computational power required to solve the equations that describe the atmosphere, error involved in measuring the initial conditions, and an incomplete understanding of atmospheric processes mean that forecasts become less accurate as the difference in current time and the time for which the forecast is being made (the <i>range</i> of the forecast) increases. The use of ensembles and model consensus help narrow the error and pick the most likely outcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klaus_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klaus-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TBK_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are a variety of end uses to weather forecasts. Weather warnings are important forecasts because they are used to protect life and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Forecasts based on temperature and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> are important to agriculture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> and therefore to commodity traders within stock markets. Temperature forecasts are used by utility companies to estimate demand over coming days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> On an everyday basis, people use weather forecasts to determine what to wear. Since outdoor activities are severely curtailed by heavy rain, snow, and <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">wind chill</a>, forecasts can be used to plan activities around these events, and to plan ahead and survive them.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation_meteorology">Aviation meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aviation meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aviation meteorology deals with the impact of weather on <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic management</a>. It is important for air crews to understand the implications of weather on their flight plan as well as their aircraft, as noted by the <a href="/wiki/Aeronautical_Information_Manual" title="Aeronautical Information Manual">Aeronautical Information Manual</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>The effects of ice on aircraft are cumulative—thrust is reduced, drag increases, lift lessens, and weight increases. The results are an increase in stall speed and a deterioration of aircraft performance. In extreme cases, 2 to 3 inches of ice can form on the leading edge of the airfoil in less than 5 minutes. It takes but 1/2 inch of ice to reduce the lifting power of some aircraft by 50 percent and increases the frictional drag by an equal percentage.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_meteorology">Agricultural meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Agricultural meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meteorologists, <a href="/wiki/Soil_science" title="Soil science">soil scientists</a>, agricultural hydrologists, and <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomists</a> are persons concerned with studying the effects of weather and climate on plant distribution, <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">crop yield</a>, water-use efficiency, <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenology</a> of plant and animal development, and the energy balance of managed and natural ecosystems. Conversely, they are interested in the role of vegetation on climate and weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrometeorology">Hydrometeorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hydrometeorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrometeorology" title="Hydrometeorology">Hydrometeorology</a> is the branch of meteorology that deals with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrologic_cycle" title="Hydrologic cycle">hydrologic cycle</a>, the water budget, and the rainfall statistics of <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> A hydrometeorologist prepares and issues forecasts of accumulating (quantitative) precipitation, heavy rain, heavy snow, and highlights areas with the potential for flash flooding. Typically the range of knowledge that is required overlaps with climatology, mesoscale and synoptic meteorology, and other geosciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>The multidisciplinary nature of the branch can result in technical challenges, since tools and solutions from each of the individual disciplines involved may behave slightly differently, be optimized for different hard- and software platforms and use different data formats. There are some initiatives - such as the DRIHM project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> - that are trying to address this issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_meteorology">Nuclear meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Nuclear meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nuclear meteorology investigates the distribution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_meteorology">Maritime meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Maritime meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maritime meteorology deals with air and wave forecasts for ships operating at sea. Organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Prediction_Center" title="Ocean Prediction Center">Ocean Prediction Center</a>, Honolulu <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> forecast office, United Kingdom <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">JMA</a> prepare high seas forecasts for the world's oceans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_meteorology">Military meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Military meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Military_meteorology" title="Military meteorology">Military meteorology</a></div>
<p>Military meteorology is the research and application of meteorology for <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> purposes. In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander,_Naval_Meteorology_and_Oceanography_Command" title="Commander, Naval Meteorology and Oceanography Command">Commander, Naval Meteorology and Oceanography Command</a> oversees meteorological efforts for the Navy and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a> while the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Weather_Agency" title="Air Force Weather Agency">Air Force Weather Agency</a> is responsible for the Air Force and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_meteorology">Environmental meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Environmental meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental meteorology mainly analyzes industrial pollution dispersion physically and chemically based on meteorological parameters such as temperature, humidity, wind, and various weather conditions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">Renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meteorology applications in renewable energy includes basic research, "exploration", and potential mapping of wind power and solar radiation for wind and solar energy.</p>
