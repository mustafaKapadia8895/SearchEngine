<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Convergent boundary</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, a <b>convergent boundary</b>, also known as a <b>destructive plate boundary</b>, is a region of active deformation where two or more tectonic plates or fragments of the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> are near the end of their life cycle. This is in contrast to a constructive plate boundary (also known as a <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridge</a> or spreading center). As a result of pressure, friction, and plate material melting in the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> are common near destructive boundaries, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">subduction zones</a> or an area of <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">continental collision</a> (depending on the nature of the plates involved) occurs. The subducting plate in a subduction zone is normally <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>, and moves beneath the other plate, which can be made of either oceanic or <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>. During collisions between two continental plates, large mountain ranges, such as the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> are formed. In other regions, a <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">divergent boundary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform faults</a> may be present.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-ocean_ridges">Mid-ocean ridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mid-ocean ridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_internal_heat_budget" title="Earth's internal heat budget">Earth's internal heat budget</a></div>
<p>The reason for the existence of convergent boundaries are the <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridges</a> that were only beginning to be understood with the acceptance of the theory of plate tectonics in the mid-1960s. The <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of elements within the rocks in the <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth" title="Structure of the Earth">interior of the earth</a> generates large amounts of heat that moves magma to the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> in a process called <a href="/wiki/Mantle_convection" title="Mantle convection">mantle convection</a>. This results in new oceanic crust or <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">sea floor</a> being formed that slowly moves away from the ridges (sometimes called spreading centers) towards the subduction zones. In other words, new sea floor originates from the upwelling rock as it emerges from the depths of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and it slowly cools as it is transported over the millennia to the collision or subduction zones where it is consumed or recycled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subduction_zones">Subduction zones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subduction zones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Forearc" title="Forearc">Forearc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wadati%E2%80%93Benioff_zone" title="Wadati–Benioff zone">Wadati–Benioff zone</a></div>
<p>Large, shallow earthquakes on subduction zone interfaces are the largest types of earthquakes. They accounted for 90% of the total <a href="/wiki/Seismic_moment" title="Seismic moment">seismic moment</a> released for the period 1900–1989, but these events usually occur offshore and far from population centers. Non-subduction interface earthquakes also occur in the region of the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">trench</a> including shallow upper plate shocks, deeper events within the downgoing <a href="/wiki/Slab_(geology)" title="Slab (geology)">slab</a>, and events that are related to <a href="/wiki/Outer_trench_swell" title="Outer trench swell">outer trench swell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_arcs">Volcanic arcs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Volcanic arcs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A volcanic arc is formed on the continental plate, above the location of the downgoing oceanic slab. The volcanic arc is the surface expression of the magma that is generated by hydrous melting of the mantle above the downgoing slab. Hydrated minerals (e.g., phlogopite, lawsonite, amphibole) within the oceanic lithosphere become unstable at certain depths due to increased temperature and pressure, causing the crystal structure of the hydrated minerals break down and release water. The buoyant fluids then rise into the asthenosphere, where they lower the melting temperature of the mantle and cause partial melting. These melts rise to the surface and are the source of some of the most explosive volcanism on Earth because of their high volumes of extremely pressurized gases.</p>
<p>When two plates with oceanic crust converge, they typically create an <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arc</a> as one plate is subducted below the other. The arc is formed from volcanoes which erupt through the overriding plate as the descending plate melts below it. The arc shape occurs because of the spherical surface of the earth (nick the peel of an orange with a knife and note the arc formed by the straight-edge of the knife). A deep <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trench</a> is located in front of such arcs where the descending slab dips downward, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Back_arc_basins">Back arc basins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Back arc basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_region" title="Back-arc region">Back-arc region</a></div>
<p>Some convergent margins have zones of active seafloor spreading behind the island arc, known as <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">back-arc basins</a>. When one plate is composed of oceanic lithosphere and the other is composed of continental lithosphere, the denser oceanic plate is subducted, often forming an <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenic belt</a> and associated mountain range. This type of convergent boundary is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> or the Cascade Range in North America.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_trenches">Oceanic trenches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oceanic trenches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trench</a> is found where the denser plate is subducted underneath the other plate. There is water in the rocks of the oceanic plate (because they are underwater), and as this plate moves further down into the subduction zone, much of the water contained in the plate is squeezed out when the plate begins to subduct. However, the recrystallization of ocean floor rocks, such as <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a>, which are unstable in the upper mantle, recrystallize into <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>, causing dehydration through loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl groups</a>. This addition of water to the mantle causes partial melting of the mantle, generating magma, which then rises, and which normally results in volcanoes. This normally happens at a certain depth, about 70 to 80 miles below the Earth's surface, and so volcanoes are formed fairly close to, but not right next to, the trench.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The collision between the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Indian Plate</a> that is forming the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>.</li>
<li>The collision between the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Plate" title="Australian Plate">Australian Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> that formed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Alps_(New_Zealand)" title="Southern Alps (New Zealand)">Southern Alps</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a></li>
<li>Subduction of the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> and the NW <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a> that is forming the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>.</li>
<li>Subduction of the <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Plate" title="Nazca Plate">Nazca Plate</a> beneath the <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plate</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>.</li>
<li>Subduction of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> beneath the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Australian Plate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonga_Plate" title="Tonga Plate">Tonga Plate</a>, forming the complex <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> subduction/transform boundaries.</li>
<li>Collision of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African Plate</a> formed the <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Mountains" title="Pontic Mountains">Pontic Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a></li>
