<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bahama Banks</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the sand bank in the Irish Sea near the Isle of Man, see <a href="/wiki/Bahama_Bank" title="Bahama Bank">Bahama Bank</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bahamabank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="642" data-file-width="564" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Bahamabank.jpg/290px-Bahamabank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Bahamabank.jpg/435px-Bahamabank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/Bahamabank.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bahamabank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Bahama Banks: Little Bahama Bank in the north and Great Bahama Bank in the south. The Cay Sal Bank is also visible.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Bahama Banks</b> are the submerged <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_platform" title="Carbonate platform">carbonate platforms</a> that make up much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahama" title="Bahama">Bahama</a> Archipelago. The term is usually applied in referring to either the Great Bahama Bank around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andros_Island" title="Andros Island">Andros Island</a>, or the Little Bahama Bank of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bahama" title="Grand Bahama">Grand Bahama</a> Island and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Abaco" title="Great Abaco">Great Abaco</a>, which are the largest of the platforms, and the <a href="/wiki/Cay_Sal_Bank" title="Cay Sal Bank">Cay Sal Bank</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. The islands of these banks are politically part of the Bahamas. Other banks are the three banks of the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>, namely the Caicos Bank of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caicos_Islands" title="Caicos Islands">Caicos Islands</a>, the bank of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_Islands" title="Turks Islands">Turks Islands</a>, and wholly submerged <a href="/wiki/Mouchoir_Bank" title="Mouchoir Bank">Mouchoir Bank</a>. Further southeast are the equally wholly submerged <a href="/wiki/Silver_Bank" title="Silver Bank">Silver Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navidad_Bank" title="Navidad Bank">Navidad Bank</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geologic_history_and_structure">Geologic history and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bahama_Banks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geologic history and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> that comprises the Banks has been accumulating since at least the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period, and perhaps as early as the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>; today the total thickness under the Great Bahama Bank is over 4.5 kilometres (2.8 miles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup> As the limestone was deposited in shallow water, the only way to explain this massive column is to estimate that the entire platform has subsided under its own weight at a rate of roughly 3.6 centimetres (2 inches) per 1,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The waters of the Bahama Banks are very shallow; on the Great Bahama Bank they are generally no deeper than 25 meters (80 feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boss_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boss-2">[2]</a></sup> The slopes around them however, such as the border of the <a href="/wiki/Tongue_of_the_Ocean" title="Tongue of the Ocean">Tongue of the Ocean</a> in the Great Bahama Bank, are very steep. The Banks were dry land during past <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>, when sea level was as much as 120 meters (390 feet) lower than at present; the area of the Bahamas today thus represents only a small fraction of their prehistoric extent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boss_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boss-2">[2]</a></sup> When they were exposed to the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, the limestone structure was subjected to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" title="Chemical weathering">chemical weathering</a> that created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caves" title="Caves">caves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinkholes" title="Sinkholes">sinkholes</a> common to <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> terrain, resulting in structures like <a href="/wiki/Blue_hole" title="Blue hole">blue holes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
