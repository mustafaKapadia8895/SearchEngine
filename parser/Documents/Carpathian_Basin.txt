<p>The <b>Pannonian Basin</b> or <b>Carpathian Basin</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is a large <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphological</a> term <b>Pannonian Plain</b> is more widely used for roughly the same region though with a somewhat different sense, with only the lowlands, the <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plain</a> that remained when the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> Epoch <i><a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Sea" title="Pannonian Sea">Pannonian Sea</a></i> dried out.</p>
<p>It is a geomorphological subsystem of the <a href="/wiki/Alpide_belt" title="Alpide belt">Alps-Himalaya system</a>, specifically a sediment-filled <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">back-arc basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Most of the plain consists of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hungarian_Plain" title="Great Hungarian Plain">Great Hungarian Plain</a> (in the south and east, including the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slovak_Lowland" title="Eastern Slovak Lowland">Eastern Slovak Lowland</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Little_Hungarian_Plain" title="Little Hungarian Plain">Little Hungarian Plain</a> (in the northwest), divided by the <a href="/wiki/Transdanubian_Mountains" title="Transdanubian Mountains">Transdanubian Mountains</a>.</p>
<p>The Pannonian Basin lies in the southeastern part of <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>. It forms a topographically discrete unit set in the European landscape, surrounded by imposing geographic boundaries - the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. The Rivers <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tisza" title="Tisza">Tisza</a> divide the basin roughly in half. It extends roughly between <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a> in the southwest, <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a> in the southeast and <a href="/wiki/Satu_Mare" title="Satu Mare">Satu Mare</a> in the northeast.</p>
<p>In terms of modern state boundaries, the basin centres on the territory of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, but it also covers <a href="/wiki/Vojvodina" title="Vojvodina">Vojvodina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ma%C4%8Dva" title="Mačva">Mačva</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> regions of western <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slovak_Lowland" title="Eastern Slovak Lowland">Eastern Slovak Lowland</a>), southwestern <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vojvodina" title="Vojvodina">Vojvodina</a>), and the tip of northeast <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slavonia" title="Slavonia">Slavonia</a>). The name "Pannonian" comes from <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a>, a province of the Roman Empire. Only the western part of the territory of modern Hungary (the so-called <a href="/wiki/Transdanubia" title="Transdanubia">Transdanubia</a>) formed part of the ancient Roman Province of Pannonia; this comprises less than 29% of modern Hungary, therefore Hungarian geographers avoid the terms "Pannonian Basin" and "Pannonian Plain".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In English, the terms "Pannonian Basin" and "Carpathian Basin" are used synonymously. The name "Pannonian" is taken from that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pannonia_province" title="Pannonia province">Pannonia</a>, a province of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. The historical province overlapped but was not coterminous with the geographical plain or basin. <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Inferior" title="Pannonia Inferior">Pannonia Inferior</a> covered much of the western half of the basin, as far as the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Superior" title="Pannonia Superior">Pannonia Superior</a> included the western fringe of the basin as well as part of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Alps" title="Eastern Alps">Eastern Alps</a>, as far as <a href="/wiki/Virunum" title="Virunum">Virunum</a>. The southern fringe of the basin was in <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia_(Roman_province)" title="Dalmatia (Roman province)">Dalmatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>. The eastern half of the basin was not conquered by the Romans, and was considered part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatia" title="Sarmatia">Sarmatia</a>, inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Iazyges" title="Iazyges">Iazyges</a>. Likewise, the parts north of the Danube (now in western Slovakia) were not in the empire; they were considered part of <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a>, inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Quadi" title="Quadi">Quadi</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>Pannonian Plain</i> refers to the lowland parts of the Pannonian Basin as well as those of some adjoining regions like <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia</a>. The lands adjoining the plain proper are sometimes also called <i>peri-Pannonian</i>.</p>
<p>The term <i>Carpathian Basin</i> is used in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> literature, while the <a href="/wiki/South_Slavic_languages" title="South Slavic languages">South Slavic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> use <i>Pannonian</i>: In <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, the basin is known as <i>Kárpát-medence</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_language" title="Bosnian language">Bosnian</a> as <i>Панонски басен/ Panonski basen</i> and <i>Panonska nizija</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a> as <i>Panonska nizina</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a> as <i>Panónska kotlina</i>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovenian_language" title="Slovenian language">Slovenian</a> as <i>Panonska kotlina</i> and in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> as <i>Pannonisches Becken</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> as <i>Câmpia Panonică</i> or <i>Bazinul Panonic</i>.</p>
<p>In Hungarian geographical literature various subdivisions of the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a> (<a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Carpathians" title="Divisions of the Carpathians">Inner Western Carpathians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Carpathians" title="Divisions of the Carpathians">Inner Eastern Carpathians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Carpathians" title="Divisions of the Carpathians">Southern Carpathians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Carpathians" title="Western Carpathians">Western Carpathians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transylvanian_Plateau" title="Transylvanian Plateau">Transylvanian Plateau</a>) are also considered parts of the Carpathian Basin on the basis of traditional geopolitical divisions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pannonii" title="Pannonii">Pannonii</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_natural_resources">Climate and natural resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate and natural resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although rain is not plentiful, it usually falls when necessary and the plain is a major <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> area; it is sometimes said that these fields of rich <a href="/wiki/Loam" title="Loam">loamy</a> <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> could feed the whole of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. For its early settlers, the plain offered few sources of metals or stone. Thus when archaeologists come upon objects of <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a>, copper or gold, they have almost unparalleled opportunities to interpret ancient pathways of trade.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Pannonian Basin has its geological origins in the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Sea" title="Pannonian Sea">Pannonian Sea</a>, a shallow sea that reached its greatest extent during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pliocene_Epoch" title="Pliocene Epoch">Pliocene Epoch</a>, when three to four kilometres of sediments were deposited.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a></div>
<p>The plain was named after the Pannon named Medes. Various different peoples inhabited the plain during its history. In the first century BC, the eastern parts of the plain belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">Dacian</a> state, and in the first century AD its western parts were subsumed into the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. The Roman province named <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a> was established in the area, and the city of <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a>, today <a href="/wiki/Sremska_Mitrovica" title="Sremska Mitrovica">Sremska Mitrovica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, became one of the four capital cities of the Roman Empire in the 3rd century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_Migrations" title="Age of Migrations">Age of Migrations</a> and the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the region belonged to several realms such as the <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Hun Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gepids" title="Gepids">Kingdom of the Gepids</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Kingdom of the Ostrogoths</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Kingdom of the Lombards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Avar_Khaganate" title="Avar Khaganate">Avar Khaganate</a>, the Slavic state of <a href="/wiki/Samo" title="Samo">Samo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Empire" title="Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarian Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Moravia" title="Great Moravia">Great Moravia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balaton_Principality" title="Balaton Principality">Balaton Principality</a>, the Pannonian Principality and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Syrmia" title="Kingdom of Syrmia">Kingdom of Syrmia</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Hungary" title="Principality of Hungary">Principality of Hungary</a> established in 895 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magyars" title="Magyars">Magyars</a> was centered on the plain and included almost all of it (as did the former Avar Kingdom as well). It was established as the Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Medieval kingdom of Hungary">kingdom of Hungary</a> in AD 1000, with the coronation of <a href="/wiki/Stephen_I_of_Hungary" title="Stephen I of Hungary">Stephen I of Hungary</a>.</p>

</div>
<p>The kingdom of Hungary by the 11th century comprised the entire Pannonian basin, but the changing fates of this part of Europe during the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_wars_in_Europe" title="Ottoman wars in Europe">Ottoman wars</a> of the 14th to 17th centuries left the Pannonian basin divided between numerous political entities. After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moh%C3%A1cs" title="Battle of Mohács">Battle of Mohács</a> in 1526, the central and eastern regions of the kingdom and the plain on which they lay were incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, while the remainder to the north-west was subsumed into the holdings of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg Monarchy</a> and retitled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Hungary" title="Royal Hungary">Royal Hungary</a>. Under Ottoman administration, the plain was reorganized into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyalet_of_Budim" title="Eyalet of Budim">Eyalet of Budim</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyalet_of_Egri" title="Eyalet of Egri">Eyalet of Egri</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyalet_of_Sigetvar" title="Eyalet of Sigetvar">Eyalet of Sigetvar</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teme%C5%9Fvar_Province,_Ottoman_Empire" title="Temeşvar Province, Ottoman Empire">Eyalet of Temeşvar</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pannonian_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Pannonian Plain was frequently a scene of conflict between the two empires. At the end of the 17th century the Habsburgs won decisive battles against the Ottomans, and most of the plain gradually came under Habsburg rule. Under Habsburg rule the region was eventually reorganized into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Banat_of_Temeswar" title="Banat of Temeswar">Banat of Temeswar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Military_Frontier" title="Military Frontier">Military Frontier</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Croatia_(Habsburg)" title="Kingdom of Croatia (Habsburg)">Kingdom of Croatia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Slavonia" title="Kingdom of Slavonia">Kingdom of Slavonia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voivodeship_of_Serbia_and_Temes_Banat" title="Voivodeship of Serbia and Temes Banat">Voivodeship of Serbia and Temes Banat</a>.</p>
<p>The Habsburg Monarchy was subsequently transformed into the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a> (in 1804) and later became <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> (in 1867). Most of the plain was located within the Hungarian part of Austria-Hungary, since all other Habsburg possessions in the plain were integrated into the Kingdom of Hungary until 1882. The autonomous Kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croatia-Slavonia" title="Croatia-Slavonia">Croatia-Slavonia</a>, which was one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Crown_of_St._Stephen" title="Lands of the Crown of St. Stephen">Lands of the Crown of St. Stephen</a>, comprised the south-western portion of the plain.</p>
<p>With the dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> after World War I, the region was divided between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary_(1920%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Hungary (1920–1946)">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Romania" title="Kingdom of Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Austria" title="German Austria">Austria</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbs,_Croats_and_Slovenes" title="Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes">Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes</a> (renamed to <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> in 1929). The borders drawn in 1918 and 1919 are mostly preserved as those of the contemporary states of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.</p>
