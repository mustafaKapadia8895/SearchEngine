<p><b>Yamagata Prefecture</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span lang="ja">山形県</span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>Yamagata-ken</i>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_Japan" title="Prefectures of Japan">prefecture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku_region" title="Tōhoku region">Tōhoku region</a> on <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Yamagata,_Yamagata" title="Yamagata, Yamagata">Yamagata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_Sites_of_Yamagata_Prefecture" title="Historic Sites of Yamagata Prefecture">Historic Sites of Yamagata Prefecture</a></div>
<p>The aboriginal <a href="/wiki/Ezo" title="Ezo">Ezo</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span lang="ja">蝦夷</span>)</span> people once inhabited the area now known as Yamagata. Yamagata and <a href="/wiki/Akita_Prefecture" title="Akita Prefecture">Akita Prefecture</a> were known as <a href="/wiki/Dewa_Province" title="Dewa Province">Dewa Province</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">Heian Period</a> (794–1185), the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_clan" title="Fujiwara clan">Fujiwara</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span lang="ja">藤原</span>)</span> family ruled the area. Yamagata City flourished during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edo_Period" title="Edo Period">Edo Period</a> (1603–1867) due to its status as a castle town and post station, famous for <i>beni</i> (red <a href="/wiki/Safflower" title="Safflower">safflower</a> dye used in the production of handspun silk). In 1689, the famous <a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a> poet, <a href="/wiki/Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D" title="Matsuo Bashō">Matsuo Bashō</a> visited Yamagata during his five-month trip to the northern regions of Japan.</p>

<p>Yamagata Prefecture is located in the southwest corner of Tōhoku, facing the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a>. It borders <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Prefecture" title="Niigata Prefecture">Niigata Prefecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Prefecture" title="Fukushima Prefecture">Fukushima Prefecture</a> on the south, <a href="/wiki/Miyagi_Prefecture" title="Miyagi Prefecture">Miyagi Prefecture</a> on the east, and <a href="/wiki/Akita_Prefecture" title="Akita Prefecture">Akita Prefecture</a> on the north. All of these boundaries are marked by mountains, with most of the population residing in a limited central plain.</p>
<p>As of 1 April 2012, 17% of the total land area of the prefecture was designated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefectural_Natural_Park" title="Prefectural Natural Park">Natural Parks</a>, namely the <a href="/wiki/Bandai-Asahi_National_Park" title="Bandai-Asahi National Park">Bandai-Asahi</a> National Park; <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dkai_Quasi-National_Park" title="Chōkai Quasi-National Park">Chōkai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurikoma_Quasi-National_Park" title="Kurikoma Quasi-National Park">Kurikoma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Za%C5%8D_Quasi-National_Park" title="Zaō Quasi-National Park">Zaō</a> Quasi-National Parks; and six Prefectural Natural Parks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_mergers_in_Yamagata_Prefecture" title="List of mergers in Yamagata Prefecture">List of mergers in Yamagata Prefecture</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The climate of Yamagata Prefecture is characterized by long hot humid summers and long snowy winters. Both spring and autumn are short, the former often cold, the latter often warm, but both quite dry and sunny. Yamagata Prefecture, along with northern parts of Miyagi and Iwate are the transition areas from <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Koppen</a> <i>Dfa</i>/<i>Dfb</i>) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humid_continental" title="Humid continental">humid continental</a> within the Japan mainland. Winter temperatures rarely fall below -10 °C in populated areas; they frequently rise above 30 °C in July and August. Precipitation falls all year round and the remnants of one or perhaps two <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a> usually pass through between August and October. The winters see heavy snowfall especially at higher elevations, though the Japan Sea coast (<a href="/wiki/Sakata,_Yamagata" title="Sakata, Yamagata">Sakata</a>) is milder and has more rain. Snowfall for <a href="/wiki/Shinj%C5%8D,_Yamagata" title="Shinjō, Yamagata">Shinjō</a> is typical of populated mountainous areas, snowfall for <a href="/wiki/Yamagata,_Yamagata" title="Yamagata, Yamagata">Yamagata City</a> typical of the valleys. The central mountains around <a href="/wiki/Mount_Gassan" title="Mount Gassan">Gassan</a> may see as much as 3000 cm of snow in a season with depths up to 8 metres at higher elevations.</p>

<p>Yamagata Prefecture is the largest producer of cherries and pears in Japan. A large quantity of other kinds of fruits such as grapes, apples, peaches, melons, persimmons and watermelons are also produced.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of October 2012, Yamagata Prefecture had a population of 1,152,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2014)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> The prefecture also has one of the oldest populations in Japan. As of 2004, 12.8% of the population was aged between 65 and 74, and 12.1% was over 75 (the fourth and third highest in Japan, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Over 40% of households in Yamagata contain one or more relatives aged 65 or over (one of the three highest in Japan).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals_and_events">Festivals and events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Festivals and events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yamagata Prefecture has a number of annual festivals and events.</p>
<p>The largest is the <i>hanagasa matsuri</i> (花笠祭り) which takes place in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamagata_City" title="Yamagata City">Yamagata City</a> on the first weekend in August, when thousands of people perform the hanagasa dance in the city centre and attracts up to 300,000 spectators. Yamagata City is the home of the bi-annual <a href="/wiki/Yamagata_International_Documentary_Film_Festival" title="Yamagata International Documentary Film Festival">Yamagata International Documentary Film Festival</a> in October.</p>
<p>In February, a snow lantern festival is held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yonezawa" title="Yonezawa">Yonezawa</a> at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uesugi_Shrine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uesugi Shrine (page does not exist)">Uesugi Shrine</a>. Hundreds of candle-lit lanterns light pathways dug into the snow around the shrine. Yonezawa is also the site of the <i>Uesugi Festival</i> (上杉祭り, <i>uesugi matsuri</i>) in mid-spring. The festival's highlight is a re-enactment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kawanakajima" title="Battle of Kawanakajima">Battle of Kawanakajima</a> on the banks of the Matsukawa River.</p>
<p>In September, Yamagata Prefecture is famous for its <a href="/wiki/Imoni" title="Imoni">imoni</a>, a potato stew popular in Northern Japan during the autumn. Imonikai, potato soup parties, are very popular during this season, and many tourists come to Yamagata Prefecture specifically for its particular style of imoni.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 2003, Yamagata city officials with the aid of Tōhoku University of Art and Design began a three-year project in which the Buddhist art of the city’s temples would be catalogued and compared to a set of guidelines in order to identify “cultural assets.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> One hundred and ninety temples have had their works of art examined and several significant examples of Buddhist sculpture have been discovered. At Heisenji Temple, in the Hirashimizu district, a particularly rare statue, a seated <a href="/wiki/Vairocana" title="Vairocana">Vairocana</a> Buddha made from <a href="/wiki/Zelkova" title="Zelkova">zelkova</a> wood, was found. Other significant works include sculptures from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">Heian Period</a> (794–1185) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamakura_Period" title="Kamakura Period">Kamakura Period</a> (1192–1333).</p>
<p>The Yamagata Museum of Art, located in Yamagata City, was opened in 1964 through the efforts of a foundation led by Yoshio Hattori, the president of Yamagata Shimbun and Yamagata Broadcasting Co, Ltd. The permanent collection consists of three types of art: Japanese and Asian, regional, and French. Special exhibitions are held periodically.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yamagata Prefecture is known for its local dialect <a href="/wiki/Yamagata_dialect" title="Yamagata dialect">Yamagata-ben</a>, sometimes thought of as backward sounding in other parts of Japan. The 2004 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Swing_Girls" title="Swing Girls">Swing Girls</a></i> (スウィングガールズ), co-written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Shinobu_Yaguchi" title="Shinobu Yaguchi">Shinobu Yaguchi</a>, is set in Yamagata and makes use of Yamagata-ben for comedic purposes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamagata_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The temple of <a href="/wiki/Yama-dera" title="Yama-dera">Yama-dera</a>, carved into the mountainside near the city of <a href="/wiki/Yamagata,_Yamagata" title="Yamagata, Yamagata">Yamagata</a>, is a major attraction.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewa_Sanzan" title="Dewa Sanzan">Dewa Sanzan</a> are three holy mountains that form a traditional pilgrimage for followers of the <a href="/wiki/Shugend%C5%8D" title="Shugendō">Shugendō</a> branch of Shintō. The famous Gojudo (five-story pagoda) is at the base of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Haguro" title="Mount Haguro">Mount Haguro</a>, the lowest of the three mountains.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mount_Za%C5%8D" title="Mount Zaō">Mount Zaō</a> is a famous winter ski resort, also known for its <i>snow monsters</i> (frozen snow-covered trees) in the winter, and the <i>Okama crater lake</i>, also known as the <i>Goshiki Numa</i> (Five Color Lake) because its colour changes according to the weather.</p>
