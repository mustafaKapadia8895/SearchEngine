<p>A <b>rift valley</b> is a linear-shaped lowland between several highlands or mountain ranges created by the action of a geologic <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rift</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault</a>. A rift valley is formed on a divergent plate boundary, a crustal extension, a spreading apart of the surface, which is subsequently further deepened by the forces of erosion. When the tensional forces were strong enough to cause the plate to split apart, a center block dropped between the two blocks at its flanks, forming a <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">graben</a>. The drop of the center creates the nearly parallel steeply dipping walls of a rift valley when it is new. That feature is the beginning of the rift valley, but as the process continues, the valley widens, until it becomes a large basin that fills with sediment from the rift walls and the surrounding area. One of the best known examples of this process is the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> On Earth, rifts can occur at all elevations, from the sea floor to plateaus and mountain ranges in <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>. They are often associated with a number of adjoining subsidiary or co-extensive valleys, which are typically considered part of the principal rift valley geologically.</p>

<h2><span id="Earth's_rift_valleys"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_rift_valleys">Earth's rift valleys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rift_valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Earth's rift valleys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most extensive rift valley is located along the crest of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridge</a> system and is the result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor_spreading" title="Sea floor spreading">sea floor spreading</a>. Examples of this type of rift include the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_Pacific_Rise" title="East Pacific Rise">East Pacific Rise</a>.</p>
<p>Many existing continental rift valleys are the result of a failed arm (<a href="/wiki/Aulacogen" title="Aulacogen">aulacogen</a>) of a <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">triple junction</a>, although there are two, the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baikal_Rift_Zone" title="Baikal Rift Zone">Baikal Rift Zone</a>, which are currently active, as well as a third which may be, the <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_Rift" title="West Antarctic Rift">West Antarctic Rift</a>. In these instances, not only the crust, but also entire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plates</a>, are in the process of breaking apart to create new plates. If they continue, continental rifts will eventually become oceanic rifts.</p>
<p>Other rift valleys are the result of bends or discontinuities in horizontally-moving (strike-slip) faults. When these bends or discontinuities are in the same direction as the relative motions along the fault, extension occurs. For example, for a right lateral-moving fault, a bend to the right will result in stretching and consequent subsidence in the area of the irregularity. In the view of many geologists today, the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> lies in a rift which results from a leftward discontinuity in the left lateral-moving <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Transform" title="Dead Sea Transform">Dead Sea Transform</a> fault. Where a fault breaks into two strands, or two faults run close to each other, crustal extension may also occur between them, as a result of differences in their motions. Both types of fault-caused extension commonly occur on a small scale, producing such features as <a href="/wiki/Sag_pond" title="Sag pond">sag ponds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rift_valley_lakes">Rift valley lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rift_valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rift valley lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rift_lake" title="Rift lake">Rift lake</a></div>
<p>Many of the world's largest lakes are located in rift valleys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> lies in an active rift valley. Baikal is both the deepest lake in the world and, with 20% of all of the liquid freshwater on earth, has the greatest volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>, second by both measures, is in the <i><a href="/wiki/Albertine_Rift" title="Albertine Rift">Albertine Rift</a></i>, the westernmost arm of the active <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> by area, lies in the ancient and dormant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midcontinent_Rift" title="Midcontinent Rift">Midcontinent Rift</a>. The largest subglacial lake, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Vostok" title="Lake Vostok">Lake Vostok</a>, may also lie in an ancient rift valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nipissing" title="Lake Nipissing">Lake Nipissing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Timiskaming" title="Lake Timiskaming">Lake Timiskaming</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> lie inside a rift valley called the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa-Bonnechere_Graben" title="Ottawa-Bonnechere Graben">Ottawa-Bonnechere Graben</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Eingvallavatn" title="Þingvallavatn">Þingvallavatn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland's</a> largest natural lake, is also an example of a rift lake.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_rift_valleys">Extraterrestrial rift valleys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rift_valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial rift valleys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Extraterrestrial rift valleys are also known to occur on other terrestrial planets and natural satellites. The 4,000 km long <a href="/wiki/Valles_Marineris" title="Valles Marineris">Valles Marineris</a> on Mars is believed by planetary geologists to be a large rift system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AndersonGrimm1998_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AndersonGrimm1998-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrews-Hanna2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andrews-Hanna2012-8">[8]</a></sup> Some features of Venus, most notably, the 4000 km <a href="/wiki/Devana_Chasma" title="Devana Chasma">Devana Chasma</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-9">[9]</a></sup> and a part of the western Eistla, and possibly also Alta and Bell Regio have been interpreted by some planetary geologists as a rift valleys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SenskeSchaber1992_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SenskeSchaber1992-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SolomonSmrekar1992_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SolomonSmrekar1992-11">[11]</a></sup> Some natural satellites also have prominent rift valleys. The 2,000 km long <a href="/wiki/Ithaca_Chasma" title="Ithaca Chasma">Ithaca Chasma</a> on Tethys in the Saturn system is a prominent example. Charon's <a href="/wiki/Nostromo_Chasma" title="Nostromo Chasma">Nostromo Chasma</a> is the first confirmed in the Pluto system, however large chasms up to 950 km wide observed on the Charon have also been tentatively interpreted by some as giant rifts and similar formations have also been noted on Pluto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A recent study suggests a complex system of ancient lunar rift valleys including <a href="/wiki/Vallis_Rheita" title="Vallis Rheita">Vallis Rheita</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vallis_Alpes" title="Vallis Alpes">Vallis Alpes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrews-HannaBesserer2014_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andrews-HannaBesserer2014-13">[13]</a></sup> The Uranus system also has prominent examples, with large 'chasma' believed to be giant rift valley systems, most notably the 1492 km long <a href="/wiki/Messina_Chasma" title="Messina Chasma">Messina Chasma</a> on Titania, 622 km <a href="/wiki/Kachina_Chasmata" title="Kachina Chasmata">Kachina Chasmata</a> on Ariel, <a href="/wiki/Verona_Rupes" title="Verona Rupes">Verona Rupes</a> on Miranda,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chaikin2001a_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-chaikin2001a-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mommur_Chasma" title="Mommur Chasma">Mommur Chasma</a> on Oberon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
