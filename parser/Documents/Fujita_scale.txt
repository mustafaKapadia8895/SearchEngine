<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fujita scale</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Fujita scale</b> (<b>F-Scale</b>), or <b>Fujita–Pearson scale</b> (<b>FPP scale</b>), is a scale for rating <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> intensity, based primarily on the damage tornadoes inflict on human-built structures and vegetation. The official Fujita scale category is determined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">engineers</a> after a ground or aerial damage survey, or both; and depending on the circumstances, ground-swirl patterns (<a href="/wiki/Cycloid" title="Cycloid">cycloidal</a> marks), <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> data, witness testimonies, media reports and damage imagery, as well as <a href="/wiki/Photogrammetry" title="Photogrammetry">photogrammetry</a> or <a href="/wiki/Videogrammetry" title="Videogrammetry">videogrammetry</a> if motion picture recording is available. The F-Scale was replaced with the <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_scale" title="Enhanced Fujita scale">Enhanced Fujita scale</a> (EF-Scale) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in February 2007. In April 2013, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> adopted the EF-Scale over the Fujita scale along with 31 "Specific Damage Indicators" used by the EC (Environment Canada) in their ratings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA:_Fujita_Tornado_Damage_Scale_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA:_Fujita_Tornado_Damage_Scale-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tornadofacts.net_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tornadofacts.net-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fujita_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scale was introduced in 1971 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetsuya_Fujita" title="Tetsuya Fujita">Tetsuya Fujita</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Allen_Pearson" title="Allen Pearson">Allen Pearson</a>, head of the National Severe Storms Forecast Center/NSSFC (currently the <a href="/wiki/Storm_Prediction_Center" title="Storm Prediction Center">Storm Prediction Center</a>/SPC). The scale was updated in 1973, taking into account path length and width. In the United States, starting in 1973, tornadoes were rated soon after occurrence. The Fujita scale was applied retroactively to tornadoes reported between 1950 and 1972 in the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)</a> National Tornado Database. Fujita rated tornadoes from 1916–1992<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Grazulis" title="Tom Grazulis">Tom Grazulis</a> of The Tornado Project retroactively rated all known significant tornadoes (F2–F5 or causing a fatality) in the U.S. back to 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-significant_tornadoes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-significant_tornadoes-3">[3]</a></sup> The Fujita scale was adopted in most areas outside of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2007, the Fujita scale was updated, and the Enhanced Fujita Scale was introduced in the United States. The new scale more accurately matches wind speeds to the severity of damage caused by the tornado.</p>
<p>Though each damage level is associated with a wind speed, the Fujita scale is effectively a damage scale, and the wind speeds associated with the damage listed aren't rigorously verified. The Enhanced Fujita Scale was formulated due to research which suggested that the wind speeds required to inflict damage by intense tornadoes on the Fujita scale are greatly overestimated. A process of <a href="/wiki/Expert_elicitation" title="Expert elicitation">expert elicitation</a> with top engineers and meteorologists resulted in the EF scale wind speeds, however, these are biased to United States construction practices. The EF scale also improved damage parameter descriptions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivation">Derivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fujita_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Derivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fujita_scale_technical.svg"><img alt="Fujita scale technical.svg" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="428" height="393" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Fujita_scale_technical.svg/300px-Fujita_scale_technical.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Fujita_scale_technical.svg/450px-Fujita_scale_technical.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Fujita_scale_technical.svg/600px-Fujita_scale_technical.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p>The original scale as derived by Fujita was a theoretical 13-level scale (F0–F12) designed to smoothly connect the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach number</a> scale. F1 corresponds to the twelfth level of the Beaufort scale, and F12 corresponds to Mach number 1.0. F0 was placed at a position specifying no damage (approximately the eighth level of the Beaufort scale), in analogy to how the Beaufort's zeroth level specifies little to no wind. From these wind speed numbers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qualitative_data" title="Qualitative data">qualitative</a> descriptions of damage were made for each category of the Fujita scale, and then these descriptions were used to classify tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enhancedIntro_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-enhancedIntro-4">[4]</a></sup> The diagram on the right illustrates the relationship between the Beaufort, Fujita, and Mach number scales.</p>
<p>At the time Fujita derived the scale, little information was available on damage caused by wind, so the original scale presented little more than educated guesses at <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> ranges for specific tiers of damage. Fujita intended that only F0–F5 be used in practice, as this covered all possible levels of damage to frame homes as well as the expected estimated bounds of wind speeds. He did, however, add a description for F6, which he phrased as "inconceivable tornado", to allow for wind speeds exceeding F5 and for possible future advancements in damage analysis which might show it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPC_FAQ_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPC_FAQ-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, the original wind speed numbers have since been found to be higher than the actual wind speeds required to incur the damage described at each category. The error manifests itself to an increasing degree as the category increases, especially in the range of F3 through F5. NOAA notes that "... precise wind speed numbers are actually guesses and have never been scientifically verified. Different wind speeds may cause similar-looking damage from place to place—even from building to building. Without a thorough engineering analysis of tornado damage in any event, the actual wind speeds needed to cause that damage are unknown."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPC_FAQ_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SPC_FAQ-5">[5]</a></sup> Since then, the Enhanced Fujita Scale has been created using better wind estimates by engineers and meteorologists.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Some sources add level F+", meaning a tornado with winds below 39 mph; on rare occasions tornadoes this weak are observed covering a wide range of path widths and lengths.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parameters">Parameters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fujita_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parameters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The five categories are listed here, in order of increasing intensity.</p>
<ol>
<li>Where the relative frequency of tornadoes is mentioned, it is the relative frequency in the United States, for which the best data exists. Strong tornadoes (F2 or greater) occur less often elsewhere in the world. The ratio seems to be similar except for some areas where it's less such as the United Kingdom, but because of few overall number of tornadoes there are few strong tornadoes. Areas of eastern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and possibly a few other areas do have frequent severe tornadoes; however, data are scarce and statistics in these countries have not been studied thoroughly.</li>
<li>The rating of any given tornado is of the most severe damage to any well-built frame home or comparable level of damage from engineering analysis of other damage.</li>
</ol>

<p>Since the Fujita scale is based on the severity of damage resulting from high winds, an F6 or an F7 tornado is a theoretical construct. Structural damage cannot exceed total destruction, which constitutes an F5. A tornado with wind speeds greater than 319 miles per hour (513 km/h) is theoretically possible, and the <a href="/wiki/1999_Bridge_Creek%E2%80%93Moore_tornado" title="1999 Bridge Creek–Moore tornado">1999 Bridge Creek-Moore Tornado</a> may have been such an event. However, no such wind speed has ever been recorded and that measurement was not near ground level.</p>

<p>For purposes such as <a href="/wiki/Tornado_climatology" title="Tornado climatology">tornado climatology</a> studies, Fujita scale ratings may be grouped into classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-significant_tornadoes_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-significant_tornadoes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decommission_in_the_US">Decommission in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fujita_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decommission in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_scale" title="Enhanced Fujita scale">Enhanced Fujita scale</a></div>
<p>The Fujita scale, introduced in 1971 as a means to differentiate tornado intensity and path area, assigned wind speeds to damage that were, at best, educated guesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujita_scale_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fujita_scale-9">[9]</a></sup> Fujita and others recognized this immediately and intensive engineering analysis was conducted through the rest of the 1970s. This research, as well as subsequent research, showed that tornado wind speeds required to inflict the described damage were actually much lower than the F-scale indicated, particularly for the upper categories. Also, although the scale gave general descriptions for the type of damage a tornado could cause, it gave little leeway for strength of construction and other factors that might cause a building to receive higher damage at lower wind speeds. Fujita tried to address these problems somewhat in 1992 with the Modified Fujita Scale,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> but by then he was semi-retired and the National Weather Service was not in a position for the undertaking of updating to an entirely new scale, so it went largely unenacted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujita_Memoirs_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fujita_Memoirs-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, on February 1, 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA:_Fujita_Tornado_Damage_Scale_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA:_Fujita_Tornado_Damage_Scale-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tornadofacts.net_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tornadofacts.net-2">[2]</a></sup> the Fujita scale was decommissioned in favor of what these scientists believe is a more accurate Enhanced Fujita Scale, which replaces it. The meteorologists and engineers who designed the EF Scale believe it an improvement on the F-scale on many counts—it accounts for different degrees of damage that occur with different types of structures, both man-made and natural. The expanded and refined damage indicators and degrees of damage standardize what was somewhat ambiguous. It also is thought to provide a much better estimate for wind speeds, and sets no upper limit on the wind speeds for the strongest level, EF5. Several countries<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2014)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> continue to use the original Fujita Scale. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a> has begun using the Enhanced Fujita scale in Canada as of April 18, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
