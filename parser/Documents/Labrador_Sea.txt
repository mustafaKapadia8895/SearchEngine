<p>The <b>Labrador Sea</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i>mer du Labrador</i>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <i>Labradorhavet</i>) is an arm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Peninsula" title="Labrador Peninsula">Labrador Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. The sea is flanked by <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelves</a> to the southwest, northwest, and northeast. It connects to the north with <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Bay" title="Baffin Bay">Baffin Bay</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Davis_Strait" title="Davis Strait">Davis Strait</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup> It has been described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marginal_sea" title="Marginal sea">marginal sea</a> of the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calow1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Calow1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sea formed upon separation of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Plate" title="Greenland Plate">Greenland Plate</a> that started about 60 million years ago and stopped about 40 million years ago. It contains one of the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Turbidity_current" title="Turbidity current">turbidity current</a> channel systems, the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Atlantic_Mid-Ocean_Channel" title="Northwest Atlantic Mid-Ocean Channel">Northwest Atlantic Mid-Ocean Channel</a> (NAMOC), that runs for thousands of kilometers along the sea bottom toward the Atlantic Ocean.</p>
<p>The Labrador Sea is a major source of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>, a cold water mass that flows at great depth along the western edge of the North Atlantic, spreading out to form the largest identifiable water mass in the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Labrador Sea formed upon separation of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Plate" title="Greenland Plate">Greenland Plate</a> that started about 60 million years ago (<a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a>) and stopped about 40 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-r1-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basin</a>, which is now buried under the continental shelves, formed during the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-r1-2">[2]</a></sup> Onset of magmatic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea-floor_spreading" title="Sea-floor spreading">sea-floor spreading</a> was accompanied by volcanic eruptions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picrite" title="Picrite">picrites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalts</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> at the Davis Strait and Baffin Bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-r1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between about 500 BC and 1300 AD, the southern coast of the sea contained <a href="/wiki/Dorset_culture" title="Dorset culture">Dorset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> settlements; Dorset tribes were later replaced by <a href="/wiki/Thule_people" title="Thule people">Thule</a> people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> defines the limits of the Labrador Sea as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span id="Geography,_geology_and_bathymetry"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography.2C_geology_and_bathymetry">Geography, geology and bathymetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography, geology and bathymetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="555" data-file-width="600" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg/180px-LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg/270px-LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg/360px-LabradorCurrentus-coastguard.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/East_Greenland_Current" title="East Greenland Current">East Greenland Current</a></div>
<p>The Labrador Sea is about 3,400 m (1,859 <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathoms</a>; 11,155 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a>) deep and 1,000 km (621 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a>; 540 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a>) wide where it joins the Atlantic Ocean. It becomes shallower, to less than 700 m (383 fathoms; 2,297 ft) towards Baffin Bay (see <a href="/wiki/File:IBCAO_betamap.jpg" title="File:IBCAO betamap.jpg">depth map</a>) and passes into the 300 km (190 mi; 160 nmi) wide <a href="/wiki/Davis_Strait" title="Davis Strait">Davis Strait</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-r1-2">[2]</a></sup> A 100–200 m (55–109 fathoms; 330–660 ft) deep <a href="/wiki/Turbidity_current" title="Turbidity current">turbidity current</a> channel system, which is about 2–5 km (1.2–3.1 mi; 1.1–2.7 nmi) wide and 3,800 km (2,400 mi; 2,100 nmi) long, runs on the bottom of the sea, near its center from the Hudson Strait into the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is called the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Atlantic_Mid-Ocean_Channel" title="Northwest Atlantic Mid-Ocean Channel">Northwest Atlantic Mid-Ocean Channel</a> (NAMOC) and is one of the world's longest drainage systems of Pleistocene age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-r2-10">[10]</a></sup> It appears as a submarine river bed with numerous tributaries and is maintained by high-density turbidity currents flowing within the <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The water temperature varies between −1 °C (30 °F) in winter and 5–6 °C (41–43 °F) in summer. The salinity is relatively low, at 31–34.9 parts per thousand. Two-thirds of the sea is covered in ice in winter. Tides are <a href="/wiki/Earth_tide" title="Earth tide">semi-diurnal</a> (i.e. occur twice a day), reaching 4 m (2.2 fathoms; 13 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bse-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is an anticlockwise water circulation in the sea. It is initiated by the <a href="/wiki/East_Greenland_Current" title="East Greenland Current">East Greenland Current</a> and continued by the <a href="/wiki/West_Greenland_Current" title="West Greenland Current">West Greenland Current</a>, which brings warmer, more saline waters northwards, along the Greenland coasts up to the Baffin Bay. Then, the <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island_Current" title="Baffin Island Current">Baffin Island Current</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Current" title="Labrador Current">Labrador Current</a> transport cold and less saline water southward along the Canadian coast. These currents carry numerous icebergs and therefore hinder navigation and exploration of the gas fields beneath the sea bed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-can_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-can-12">[12]</a></sup> The speed of the Labrador current is typically 0.3–0.5 m/s (0.98–1.64 ft/s), but can reach 1 m/s (3.3 ft/s) in some areas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> whereas the Baffin Current is somewhat slower at about 0.2 m/s (0.66 ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Labrador Current maintains the water temperature at 0 °C (32 °F) and salinity between 30 and 34 parts per thousand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sea provides a significant part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a> (NADW) – a cold water mass that flows at great depth along the western edge of the North Atlantic, spreading out to form the largest identifiable water mass in the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The NADW consists of three parts of different origin and salinity, and the top one, the Labrador Sea Water (LSW), is formed in the Labrador Sea. This part occurs at a medium depth and has a relatively low salinity (34.84–34.89 parts per thousand), low temperature (3.3–3.4 °C (37.9–38.1 °F)) and high oxygen content compared to the layers above and below it. LSW also has a relatively low vorticity, i.e. the tendency to form vortices, than any other water in North Atlantic that reflects its high homogeneity. It has a <a href="/wiki/Potential_density" title="Potential density">potential density</a> of 27.76–27.78 mg/cm<sup>3</sup> relatively to the surface layers, meaning it is denser, and thus sinks under the surface and remains homogeneous and unaffected by the surface fluctuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern and western parts of the Labrador Sea are covered in ice between December and June. The <a href="/wiki/Drift_ice" title="Drift ice">drift ice</a> serves as a breeding ground for seals in early spring. The sea is also a feeding ground for <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_salmon" title="Atlantic salmon">Atlantic salmon</a> and several marine mammal species. <a href="/wiki/Shrimp_fishery" title="Shrimp fishery">Shrimp fisheries</a> began in 1978 and intensified toward 2000, as well as cod fishing. However, the cod fishing rapidly depleted the fish population in the 1990s near the Labrador and West Greenland banks and was therefore halted in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-can_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-can-12">[12]</a></sup> Other fishery targets include <a href="/wiki/Haddock" title="Haddock">haddock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_herring" title="Atlantic herring">Atlantic herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a> and several species of <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_fish" title="Pelagic fish">pelagic fish</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sand_lance" title="Sand lance">sand lance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capelin" title="Capelin">capelin</a>. They are most abundant in the southern parts of the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beluga_whales" title="Beluga whales">Beluga whales</a>, while abundant to the north, in the <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Bay" title="Baffin Bay">Baffin Bay</a>, where their population reaches 20,000, are rare in the Labrador Sea, especially since the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The sea contains one of the two major stocks of <a href="/wiki/Sei_whale" title="Sei whale">Sei whales</a>, the other one being the <a href="/wiki/Scotian_Shelf" title="Scotian Shelf">Scotian Shelf</a>. Also common are <a href="/wiki/Minke_whale" title="Minke whale">minke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_whale" title="Bottlenose whale">bottlenose whales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Labrador_duck" title="Labrador duck">Labrador duck</a> was a common bird on the Canadian coast until the 19th century, but is now extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Other coastal animals include the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_wolf" title="Labrador wolf">Labrador wolf</a> (<i>Canis lupus labradorius</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribou" title="Caribou">caribou</a> (<i>Rangifer</i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">moose</a> (<i>Alces alces</i>), <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bear</a> (<i>Ursus americanus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a> (<i>Vulpes vulpes</i>), <a href="/wiki/Arctic_fox" title="Arctic fox">Arctic fox</a> (<i>Alopex lagopus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Wolverine" title="Wolverine">wolverine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe_hare" title="Snowshoe hare">snowshoe hare</a> (<i>Lepus americanus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">grouse</a> (<i>Dendragapus</i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Osprey" title="Osprey">osprey</a> (<i>Pandion haliaetus</i>), raven (<i>Corvus corax</i>), ducks, geese, partridge and American wild pheasant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-f-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Coastal vegetation includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_spruce" title="Black spruce">black spruce</a> (<i>Picea mariana</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamarack" title="Tamarack">tamarack</a>, <a href="/wiki/White_spruce" title="White spruce">white spruce</a> (<i>P. glauca</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwarf_birch" title="Dwarf birch">dwarf birch</a> (<i>Betula</i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Aspen" title="Aspen">aspen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> (<i>Salix</i> spp.), ericaceous shrubs (<i><a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">Ericaceae</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cottongrass" title="Cottongrass">cottongrass</a> (<i>Eriophorum</i> spp.), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedge" title="Sedge">sedge</a> (<i>Carex</i> spp.), lichens and moss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-f-25">[25]</a></sup> Evergreen bushes of <a href="/wiki/Labrador_tea" title="Labrador tea">Labrador tea</a>, which is used to make <a href="/wiki/Herbal_tea" title="Herbal tea">herbal teas</a>, are common in the area, both on the Greenland and Canadian coasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
