<p>The <b>tropopause</b> is the boundary in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. It is a thermodynamic gradient stratification layer, marking the end of <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropopause&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_Atmosphere.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="622" data-file-width="355" height="385" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Earth_Atmosphere.svg/220px-Earth_Atmosphere.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Earth_Atmosphere.svg/330px-Earth_Atmosphere.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Earth_Atmosphere.svg/440px-Earth_Atmosphere.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Going upward from the surface, it is the point where air ceases to cool with height, and becomes almost completely dry. More formally, the tropopause is the region of the atmosphere where the <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">environmental lapse rate</a> changes from positive, as it behaves in the troposphere, to the stratospheric negative one. Following is the exact definition used by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The boundary between the troposphere and the stratosphere, where an abrupt change in lapse rate usually occurs. It is defined as the lowest level at which the lapse rate decreases to 2 °C/km or less, provided that the average lapse rate between this level and all higher levels within 2 km does not exceed 2 °C/km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The tropopause as defined above renders as a <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">first-order discontinuity</a> surface, that is, <i>temperature</i> as a function of height varies continuously through the atmosphere but the <i>temperature gradient</i> does not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanchev1985129_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanchev1985129-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropopause&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The troposphere is one of the lowest layers of the Earth's atmosphere; it is located right above the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer</a>, and is the layer in which most <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> phenomena take place. The troposphere extends upwards from right above the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">boundary layer</a>, and ranges in height from an average of 9 km (5.6 mi; 30,000 ft) at the poles, to 17 km (11 mi; 56,000 ft) at the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the absence of <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversions</a> and not considering <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_lapse_rate" title="Temperature lapse rate">temperature lapse rate</a> for this layer is 6.5 °C per kilometer, on average, according to the <i>U.S. Standard Atmosphere</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetty2008112_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetty2008112-5">[5]</a></sup> A measurement of both the tropospheric and the stratospheric lapse rates helps identifying the location of the tropopause, since temperature increases with height in the stratosphere, and hence the lapse rate becomes negative. The tropopause location coincides with the lowest point at which the lapse rate falls below a prescribed threshold.</p>
<p>Since the tropopause responds to the average temperature of the entire layer that lies underneath it, it is at its peak levels over the Equator, and reaches minimum heights over the poles. On account of this, the coolest layer in the atmosphere lies at about 17 km over the equator. Due to the variation in starting height, the tropopause extremes are referred to as the equatorial tropopause and the polar tropopause.</p>
<p>Given that the lapse rate is not a conservative quantity when the tropopause is considered for stratosphere-troposphere exchanges studies, there exists an alternative definition named <i>dynamic tropopause</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrewsHoltonLeovy1987371_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrewsHoltonLeovy1987371-6">[6]</a></sup> It is formed with the aid of <a href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity" title="Potential vorticity">potential vorticity</a>, which is defined as the product of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isentropic" title="Isentropic">isentropic</a> <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, i.e. the density that arises from using <a href="/wiki/Potential_temperature" title="Potential temperature">potential temperature</a> as the vertical coordinate, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_vorticity" title="Absolute vorticity">absolute vorticity</a>, given that this quantity attains quite different values for the troposphere and the stratosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Instead of using the vertical temperature gradient as the defining variable, the dynamic tropopause surface is expressed in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity_unit" title="Potential vorticity unit">potential vorticity units</a></i> (PVU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[nb 1]</a></sup> Given that the absolute vorticity is positive in the Northern Hemisphere and negative in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, the threshold value should be taken as positive north of the Equator and negative south of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoinka_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoinka-10">[9]</a></sup> Theoretically, to define a global tropopause in this way, the two surfaces arising from the positive and negative thresholds need to be matched near the equator using another type of surface such as a constant <a href="/wiki/Potential_temperature" title="Potential temperature">potential temperature</a> surface. Nevertheless, the dynamic tropopause is useless at equatorial latitudes because the isentropes are almost vertical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tuck_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tuck-8">[8]</a></sup> For the extratropical tropopause in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> the WMO established a value of 1.5 PVU,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tuck_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tuck-8">[8]</a></sup> but greater values ranging between 2 and 3.5 PVU have been traditionally used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Z.C3.A4ngl.26Hoinka_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Z.C3.A4ngl.26Hoinka-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also possible to define the tropopause in terms of chemical composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pan_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pan-12">[11]</a></sup> For example, the lower stratosphere has much higher <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> concentrations than the upper troposphere, but much lower <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> concentrations, so appropriate cutoffs can be used.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomena">Phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropopause&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tropopause is not a "hard" boundary. Vigorous <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>, for example, particularly those of tropical origin, will <a href="/wiki/Convective_overshoot" title="Convective overshoot">overshoot</a> <a href="/wiki/Overshooting_top" title="Overshooting top">into</a> the lower stratosphere and undergo a brief (hour-order or less) low-frequency vertical <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Such oscillation sets up a low-frequency atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Gravity_wave#Atmosphere_dynamics_on_Earth" title="Gravity wave">gravity wave</a> capable of affecting both atmospheric and oceanic currents in the region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Most commercial aircraft are flown in the lower stratosphere, just above the tropopause, where clouds are usually absent, as also are significant weather perturbations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetty200821_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetty200821-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
