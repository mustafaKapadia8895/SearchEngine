<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">VORTEX projects</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>V</b>erification of the <b>O</b>rigins of <b>R</b>otation in <b>T</b>ornadoes <b>Ex</b>periment or <b>VORTEX</b> are field projects that study <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. VORTEX1 was the first time scientists completely researched the entire evolution of a tornado with an array of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_instrument" title="Weather instrument">instrumentation</a>, enabling a greater understanding of the processes involved with <a href="/wiki/Tornadogenesis" title="Tornadogenesis">tornadogenesis</a>. A violent tornado near Union City, Oklahoma was documented in its entirety by chasers of the Tornado Intercept Project (TIP) in 1973 and visual observations led to advancement in understanding of tornado structure and life cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intercept_history_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-intercept_history-1">[1]</a></sup> VORTEX2 utilized enhanced technology allowing scientists to improve forecasting capabilities to improve advanced <a href="/wiki/Weather_warning" title="Weather warning">warnings</a> to residents. VORTEX2 is seeking to explain how tornadoes form, how long they last and why they last that long, and what causes them to dissipate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VORTEX2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-VORTEX2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The field research phase of the VORTEX2 project concluded on July 6, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The VORTEX1 project sought to understand how a tornado is produced by deploying around 18 vehicles that were equipped with customized instruments used to measure and analyze the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> around a tornado. The project has also stated that it is interested in why some <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclones</a> within such storms, produce tornadoes while others do not. It also concerned itself with why some supercells form violent tornadoes versus weak tornadoes.</p>
<p>The original project took place in 1994 and 1995, while several smaller studies, such as SUB-VORTEX and VORTEX-99, were conducted from 1996 to 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSL_projs_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSL_projs-4">[4]</a></sup> VORTEX1 documented the entire life cycle of a tornado with significant instrumentation surrounding it for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifty_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fifty-5">[5]</a></sup> Severe weather warnings improved after the research collected from VORTEX1 and many believe that VORTEX1 contributed to this improvement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Six_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Six-6">[6]</a></sup> “An important finding from the original VORTEX experiment was that the factors responsible for causing tornadoes happen on smaller time and space scales than scientists had thought. New advances will allow for a more detailed sampling of a storm’s <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and moisture environment and lead to a better understanding of why tornadoes form –-and how they can be more accurately predicted,” said Stephan Nelson, NSF program director for physical and dynamic meteorology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>VORTEX had the capability to fly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_weather_radar" title="Doppler weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a> above the tornado approximately every five minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lydersen_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-lydersen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>VORTEX research helped the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS) to provide <a href="/wiki/Tornado_warning" title="Tornado warning">tornado warnings</a> to residents with a lead time of 13 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBS-10">[10]</a></sup> A federal research <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_W._Burgess" title="Donald W. Burgess">Don Burgess</a>, estimates that the "false alarms" pertaining to severe weather by the National Weather Service have declined by 10 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The movie <i><a href="/wiki/Twister_(1996_film)" title="Twister (1996 film)">Twister</a></i> was at least partially inspired by the VORTEX project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="VORTEX2">VORTEX2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VORTEX_projects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: VORTEX2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>VORTEX2</b> was an expanded second VORTEX project with field phases from 10 May until 13 June 2009 and 1 May until 15 June 2010. VORTEX2's goals were studying why some <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> produce tornadoes while others do not, how to make more accurate and longer lead time tornado forecasts and warnings, and tornado structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> VORTEX2 was by far the largest and most ambitious tornado study ever with over 100 scientific participants from many different universities and research laboratories.</p>
<p>"We still do not completely understand the processes that lead to tornado formation and shape its development. We hope that VORTEX2 will provide the data we need to learn more about the development of tornadoes and in time help forecasters give people more advance warning before a tornado strikes," said <a href="/wiki/Roger_Wakimoto" title="Roger Wakimoto">Roger Wakimoto</a>, director of the Earth Observing Laboratory (EOL) at the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR), and a principal investigator for VORTEX2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBS_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBS-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Then you can get first responders to be better prepared—police, fire, medical personnel, even power companies. Now, that's not even remotely possible," said Stephan P. Nelson, a program director in the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">atmospheric sciences</a> division of the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lydersen_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-lydersen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joshua_Wurman" title="Joshua Wurman">Joshua Wurman</a>, president of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Center_for_Severe_Weather_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Center for Severe Weather Research (page does not exist)">Center for Severe Weather Research</a> (CSWR) in <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder, Colorado</a> proposes, "if we can increase that lead time from 13 minutes to half an hour, then the average person at home could do something different. Maybe they can seek a community shelter instead of just going into their bathtub. Maybe they can get their family to better safety if we can give them a longer warning and a more precise warning."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>VORTEX2 deployed 50 vehicles customized with mobile <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">radar</a>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_On_Wheels" title="Doppler On Wheels">Doppler On Wheels</a> (DOW) radars, SMART radars, the NOXP radar, a fleet of instrumented vehicles, <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a>, deployable instrument arrays called Sticknet and Podnet, and mobile <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a> launching equipment. Over 100 scientists and crew researched tornadoes and supercell thunderstorms in the "<a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>" region of the United States' <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> between <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>. A number of institutions and countries were involved in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>11.9 million project, including: <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, the National Weather Service, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology_(Australia)" title="Bureau of Meteorology (Australia)">Bureau of Meteorology in Australia</a>, Italy, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a>, universities across the United States, and the NOAA <a href="/wiki/Storm_Prediction_Center" title="Storm Prediction Center">Storm Prediction Center</a> (SPC).</p>
<p>The project included DOW6, DOW7, Rapid-Scan DOW, SMART-RADARs, NOXP, UMASS-X, UMASS-W, and CIRPAS for their mobile radar contingent. The Doppler on Wheels were supplied by the Center for Severe Weather Research, and the SMART-Radars from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a> (OU). <a href="/wiki/National_Severe_Storms_Laboratory" title="National Severe Storms Laboratory">National Severe Storms Laboratory</a> (NSSL) supplied the NOXP radar, as well as several other radar units from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Naval_Research" title="Office of Naval Research">Office of Naval Research</a> (ONR) and <a href="/wiki/Texas_Tech_University" title="Texas Tech University">Texas Tech University</a> (TTU). NSSL and CSWR supplied mobile <a href="/wiki/Mesonet" title="Mesonet">mesonets</a>. Mobile <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> launching vehicles are provided by NSSL, NCAR, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SUNY_Oswego" title="SUNY Oswego">State University of New York at Oswego</a> (SUNY Oswego). There were quite a few other deployable state of the art instrumentation, such as Sticknets from TTU, tornado PODS from CSWR, and four <a href="/wiki/Disdrometer" title="Disdrometer">disdrometers</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">University of Colorado CU</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a> (UIUC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCAR_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCAR-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-what-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>VORTEX2 technology allowed trucks with radar to be placed in and near tornadic storms and allowed continuous observations of the tornadic activity. <a href="/wiki/Howard_Bluestein" title="Howard Bluestein">Howard Bluestein</a>, a meteorology professor at the University of Oklahoma said, "We will be able to distinguish between rain, hail, dust, debris, flying cows."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lydersen_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-lydersen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Photogrammetry" title="Photogrammetry">photogrammetry</a> teams, damage survey teams, <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aircraft</a>, and weather balloon launching vans helped to surround the tornadoes and thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCAR_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCAR-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-what-15">[15]</a></sup> The equipment amassed enabled three dimensional <a href="/wiki/Data_set" title="Data set">data sets</a> of the storms to be collected with radars and other instruments every 75 seconds (more frequently for some individual instruments), and resolution of the tornado and tornadic storm cells as close as 200 feet (61 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBS_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBS-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flickinger_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flickinger-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists met May 10 and held a class which taught the crews how to launch the tornado pods which will need to be sent off within 45 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glamour_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glamour-17">[17]</a></sup> VORTEX2 was equipped with 12 tornado PODS which were instruments mounted onto 1 meter (3.3 ft) towers which measure wind velocity (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">speed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">direction</a>). The aim was that some of the measurements be taken in the centre of the tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-instruments_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-instruments-18">[18]</a></sup> Once the pods are deployed, the teams repeat the process at the next location until finally the teams return to the south of the tornado to retrieve the pods with the recorded data. The process is then repeated again. This happens within 2 miles (3.2 km) or 4 minutes away from the tornado itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glamour_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glamour-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The team had 24 2 metres (6.6 ft) high portable Sticknets which can be set up at various locations around tornado storm cells to measure wind fields, provide atmospheric readings, and record acoustically the <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flickinger_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flickinger-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-instruments_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-instruments-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists are still seeking to refine understanding of which supercell thunderstorms which form mesocyclones will further produce tornadoes, by which processes, <a href="/wiki/Storm-scale" title="Storm-scale">storm-scale</a> interactions, and within which atmospheric environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lydersen_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-lydersen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Updates on the progress of the project were posted on the <a class="external text" href="http://www.vortex2.org/" rel="nofollow">VORTEX2 home page</a>. The scientists also started a blog of live reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> "Even though this field phase seems to be the most spectacular and seems like it's a lot of work, by far the majority of what we're doing is when we go back to our labs, when we work with each other, when we work with our students to try to figure out just what is it that we've collected," Wurman said. "It's going to take years to digest this data and to really get the benefit of this." <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penn_State_University" title="Penn State University">Penn State University</a> will feature the public release of the initial scientific findings in the fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-AP-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The forecasters were determining the best probability of sighting a tornado. As the trucks traveled to <a href="/wiki/Clinton,_Oklahoma" title="Clinton, Oklahoma">Clinton, OK</a> from <a href="/wiki/Childress,_Texas" title="Childress, Texas">Childress, TX</a>, they found <a href="/wiki/Mammatus_cloud" title="Mammatus cloud">mammatus clouds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> at sundown on May 13, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The project finally encountered its first tornado on the afternoon of June 5 when they successfully intercepted a tornado in southern <a href="/wiki/Goshen_County,_Wyoming" title="Goshen County, Wyoming">Goshen County, Wyoming</a> which lasted for approximately 25 minutes. One of their vehicles, Probe 1, suffered hail damage during the intercept. Later that evening, embedded <a href="/wiki/The_Weather_Channel" title="The Weather Channel">Weather Channel</a> reporter <a href="/wiki/Mike_Bettes" title="Mike Bettes">Mike Bettes</a> reported that elements of VORTEX2 had intercepted a second tornado in Nebraska. Placement of the armada for this tornado was nearly ideal and it, too, was surrounded for its entire life cycle, making it the most thoroughly observed tornado in history.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
