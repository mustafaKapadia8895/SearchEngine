<p>A <b>funnel cloud</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Funnel" title="Funnel">funnel</a>-shaped <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> of condensed water droplets, associated with a rotating column of wind and extending from the base of a cloud (usually a <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus</a> or towering <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus cloud</a>) but not reaching the ground or a water surface. A funnel cloud is usually visible as a cone-shaped or needle like protuberance from the main <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a>. Funnel clouds form most frequently in association with <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>.</p>
<p>If a funnel cloud touches the ground it becomes a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>. Most tornadoes begin as funnel clouds, but many funnel clouds do not make ground contact and so do not become tornadoes. Also, a tornado does not necessarily need to have an associated <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> funnel—if strong <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclonic</a> winds are occurring at the surface (and connected to a cloud base, regardless of condensation), then the feature is a tornado. Some tornadoes may appear only as a debris swirl, with no obvious funnel cloud extending below the rotating cloud base.</p>
<p>In cloud nomenclature, any funnel- or inverted-funnel-shaped cloud depending from cumulus or cumulonimbus clouds is technically described as an accessory feature called <i>tuba</i>. The terms <i>tuba</i> and <i>funnel cloud</i> are nearly but not exactly synonymous; a <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a>, for example, is also a form of <i>tuba</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold-air_funnel_clouds">Cold-air funnel clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Funnel_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cold-air funnel clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cold-air funnel clouds (vortices) are usually short-lived and generally much weaker than the vortices produced by supercells. Although cold-air funnels rarely make ground contact, they may touch down briefly and become weak tornadoes or waterspouts.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1172" data-file-width="1816" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg/250px-Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg/375px-Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg/500px-Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Funnel_cloud3_-_NOAA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A shear funnel extending from a generic cumulus cloud. Observed in northern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> by a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VORTEX" title="VORTEX">VORTEX</a> project.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unlike the related phenomenon associated with severe thunderstorms, cold-air funnels are generally associated with partly cloudy skies in the wake of <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>, where atmospheric instability and moisture is sufficient to support towering <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus clouds</a> but not precipitation. The mixing of cooler air in the lower <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> with air flowing in a different direction in the middle troposphere causes the rotation on a horizontal axis, which, when deflected vertically by atmospheric conditions, can become a funnel cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooley_soderberg_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cooley_soderberg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are a common sight along the Pacific Coast of the United States, particularly in the spring or fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooley_soderberg_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cooley_soderberg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 29, 2013, a cold-core funnel cloud touched down as an EF0 tornado in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, causing extensive damage in the form of downed trees on a golf course. No advance weather watches or warnings were issued by Environment Canada, and the tornado was spawned from one of the few non-severe storm clouds moving through the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
