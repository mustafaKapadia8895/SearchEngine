<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Antarctic bottom water</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Antarctic bottom water</b> (<b>AABW</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Water_mass" title="Water mass">water mass</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> surrounding <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> with temperatures ranging from −0.8 to 2 °C (35 °F), <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinities</a> from 34.6 to 34.7 <a href="/wiki/Salinity#Definitions" title="Salinity">psu</a>. Being the densest water mass of the oceans, AABW is found to occupy the depth range below 4000 m of all ocean basins that have a connection to the Southern Ocean at that level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The major significance of Antarctic bottom water is that it is the coldest bottom water, giving it a significant influence on the movement of the world's oceans. Antarctic bottom water also has a high oxygen content relative to the rest of the oceans' deep waters. This is due to the oxidation of deteriorating organic content in the rest of the deep oceans. Antarctic bottom water has thus been considered the <i>ventilation of the deep ocean.</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_circulation">Formation and circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antarctic_bottom_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation and circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Antarctic bottom water is created in part due to the major overturning of ocean water.</p>
<p>Antarctic bottom water is formed in the <a href="/wiki/Weddell_Sea" title="Weddell Sea">Weddell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ross_Sea" title="Ross Sea">Ross Seas</a>, off the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ad%C3%A9lie_Coast" title="Adélie Coast">Adélie Coast</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Cape_Darnley_(Mac._Robertson_Land)" title="Cape Darnley (Mac. Robertson Land)">Cape Darnley</a> from surface water cooling in <a href="/wiki/Polynya" title="Polynya">polynyas</a> and below the <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A unique feature of Antarctic bottom water is the cold surface wind blowing off the Antarctic continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The surface wind creates the polynyas which opens up the water surface to more wind. This Antarctic wind is stronger during the winter months and thus the Antarctic bottom water formation is more pronounced during the Antarctic winter season. Surface water is enriched in salt from sea ice formation. Due to its increased density, it flows down the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Continental_margin" title="Continental margin">continental margin</a> and continues north along the bottom. It is the densest water in the free ocean, and underlies other bottom and intermediate waters throughout most of the southern hemisphere. The <a href="/wiki/Weddell_Sea_Bottom_Water" title="Weddell Sea Bottom Water">Weddell Sea Bottom Water</a> is the densest component of the Antarctic bottom water.</p>
<p>Evidence indicates that Antarctic bottom water production through the Holocene (last 10,000 years) is not in a steady-state condition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> that is to say that bottom water production sites shift along the Antarctic margin over decade to century timescales as conditions for the existence of <a href="/wiki/Polynya" title="Polynya">polynyas</a> change. For example, the calving of the Mertz Glacier, which occurred on 12–13 February 2010, dramatically changed the environment for producing bottom water, reducing export by up to 23% in the region of Adelie Land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Evidence from sediment cores, containing layers of cross-bedded sediments indicating phases of stronger bottom currents, collected on the Mac.Robertson shelf <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ad%C3%A9lie_Land" title="Adélie Land">Adélie Land</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> suggests that they have switched "on" and "off" again as important bottom water production sites over the last several thousand years.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antarctic_bottom_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atlantic Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Vema Channel, a deep trough in the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_Rise" title="Rio Grande Rise">Rio Grande Rise</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">South Atlantic</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Antarctic_bottom_water&amp;params=-31.3_N_-39.4_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">31°18′S</span> <span class="longitude">39°24′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">31.3°S 39.4°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-31.3; -39.4</span></span></span></a></span>, is an important conduit for Antarctic Bottom Water and <a href="/wiki/Weddell_Sea_Bottom_Water" title="Weddell Sea Bottom Water">Weddell Sea Bottom Water</a> migrating north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Upon reaching the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, about one-third of the northward flowing Antarctic bottom water enters the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guiana_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guiana Basin (page does not exist)">Guiana Basin</a>, mainly through the southern half of the Equatorial Channel at 35°W. The other part recirculates and some of it flows through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanche_Fracture_Zone" title="Romanche Fracture Zone">Romanche Fracture Zone</a> into the eastern Atlantic. In the Guiana Basin, west of 40°W, the sloping topography and the strong, eastward flowing deep western boundary current might prevent the Antarctic bottom water from flowing west: thus it has to turn north at the eastern slope of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cear%C3%A1_Rise&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ceará Rise (page does not exist)">Ceará Rise</a>. At 44°W, north of the Ceará Rise, Antarctic bottom water flows west in the interior of the basin. A large fraction of the Antarctic bottom water enters the eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Vema_Fracture_Zone" title="Vema Fracture Zone">Vema Fracture Zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monika_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-monika-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Ocean">Indian Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antarctic_bottom_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indian Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> the Crozet-Kerguelen Gap allows Antarctic bottom water to move toward the equator. This northward movement amounts to 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sv</a>. It takes the Antarctic Bottom Water 23 years to reach the Crozet-Kerguelen Gap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> South of Africa, Antarctic bottom water flow northwards through the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Basin" title="Agulhas Basin">Agulhas Basin</a> and then east through the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Passage" title="Agulhas Passage">Agulhas Passage</a> and over the southern margins of the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Plateau" title="Agulhas Plateau">Agulhas Plateau</a> from where it is transported to into the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mozambique Basin (page does not exist)">Mozambique Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
