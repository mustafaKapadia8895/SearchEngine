<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For subsidence of the ground, see <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>.</div>
<p><b>Subsidence</b>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, is most commonly caused by a low <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. As the air cools, it becomes denser and moves towards the ground, as warm air becomes less dense and moves upwards. Subsiding air, when cool, is subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic" title="Adiabatic">adiabatic</a> warming, which tends to cause the evaporation of any clouds that might be present.</p>
<p>Subsidence generally causes high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> as more air moves into the same space: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_high" title="Polar high">polar highs</a> are areas of almost constant subsidence, as are the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a>, and the areas of subsidence are the sources of much of the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prevailing_wind" title="Prevailing wind">prevailing wind</a>.</p>
<p>Subsidence also causes many smaller-scale <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> phenomena, such as morning <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>. An extreme form of subsidence is a <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a>, which can result in damage similar to that produced by a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>. A milder form of subsidence is referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">downdraft</a>.</p>
