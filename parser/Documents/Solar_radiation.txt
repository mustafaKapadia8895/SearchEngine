<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Solar irradiance</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Solar irradiance</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> per unit area received from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> range of the measuring instrument. Irradiance may be measured in <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> or at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_surface" title="Earth's surface">Earth's surface</a> after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric" title="Atmospheric">atmospheric</a> absorption and <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a>. It is measured <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to the incoming sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stickler_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stickler-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>Total solar irradiance</b> (TSI), is a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> over all wavelengths per unit area incident on the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_atmosphere" title="Upper atmosphere">upper atmosphere</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a> is a conventional measure of mean TSI at a distance of one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_Unit" title="Astronomical Unit">astronomical Unit</a> (AU). Irradiance is a function of distance from the Sun, the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a>, and cross-cycle changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boxwell_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boxwell-2">[2]</a></sup> Irradiance on Earth is also measured perpendicular to the incoming sunlight. <b>Insolation</b> is the power received on Earth per unit area on a horizontal surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It depends on the height of the Sun above the horizon and atmospheric conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stickler_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stickler-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insolation">Insolation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Insolation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solar irradiance integrated over time is called solar irradiation, <a href="/wiki/Irradiance#Solar_Energy" title="Irradiance">solar exposure</a>, or insolation. However, insolation is often used interchangeably with irradiance in practice.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units">Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SI unit of irradiance is <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> per square <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">meter</a> (W/m<sup>2</sup>).</p>
<p>An alternate unit of measure is the <a href="/wiki/Langley_(unit)" title="Langley (unit)">Langley</a> (1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermochemical_calorie" title="Thermochemical calorie">thermochemical calorie</a> per square centimeter or 41,840 J/m<sup>2</sup>) per unit time.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> industry uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-hour" title="Watt-hour">watt-hour</a> per square metre (Wh/m<sup>2</sup>) per unit time. The relation to the SI unit is thus: 1 kW/m<sup>2</sup> = 24 kWh/m<sup>2</sup>/day = 8760 kWh/m<sup>2</sup>/year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption_and_reflection">Absorption and reflection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Absorption and reflection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Spectrum.png" title="Solar irradiance spectrum above atmosphere and at surface"><img alt="Solar irradiance spectrum above atmosphere and at surface" data-file-height="595" data-file-width="800" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/350px-Solar_Spectrum.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/525px-Solar_Spectrum.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/700px-Solar_Spectrum.png 2x" width="350"/></a></div>
<p>Part of the radiation reaching an object is absorbed and the remainder reflected. Usually the absorbed radiation is converted to <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>, increasing the object's temperature. Manmade or natural systems, however, can convert part of the absorbed radiation into another form such as <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a>, as in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> cells or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a>. The proportion of reflected radiation is the object's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projection_effect">Projection effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Projection effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seasons.too.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="457" data-file-width="642" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Seasons.too.png/220px-Seasons.too.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Seasons.too.png/330px-Seasons.too.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Seasons.too.png/440px-Seasons.too.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seasons.too.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One sunbeam one mile wide shines on the ground at a 90° angle, and another at a 30° angle. The <a href="/wiki/Angle#Types_of_angles" title="Angle">oblique</a> sunbeam distributes its light energy over twice as much area.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Insolation onto a surface is largest when the surface directly faces (is normal to) the sun. As the angle between the surface and the Sun moves from normal, the insolation is reduced in proportion to the angle's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosine" title="Cosine">cosine</a>; see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">effect of sun angle on climate</a>.</p>
<p>In the figure, the angle shown is between the ground and the sunbeam rather than between the vertical direction and the sunbeam; hence the sine rather than the cosine is appropriate. A sunbeam one mile (1.6 km) wide arrives from directly overhead, and another at a 30° angle to the horizontal. The <a href="/wiki/Sine" title="Sine">sine</a> of a 30° angle is 1/2, whereas the sine of a 90° angle is 1. Therefore, the angled sunbeam spreads the light over twice the area. Consequently, half as much light falls on each square mile.</p>
<p>This 'projection effect' is the main reason why Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a> are much colder than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_region" title="Equatorial region">equatorial regions</a>. On an annual average the poles receive less insolation than does the equator, because the poles are always angled more away from the sun than the tropics, and moreover receive no insolation at all for the six months of their respective winters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption_effect">Absorption effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Absorption effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At a lower angle the light must also travel through more atmosphere. This attenuates it (by absorption and scattering) further reducing insolation at the surface.</p>
<p>Attenuation is governed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer-Lambert_Law" title="Beer-Lambert Law">Beer-Lambert Law</a>, namely that the <a href="/wiki/Transmittance" title="Transmittance">transmittance</a> or fraction of insolation reaching the surface decreases exponentially in the <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">absorbance</a> (the two notions differing only by a constant factor of <i>ln</i>(10) = 2.303) of the path of insolation through the atmosphere. For any given short length of the path the optical depth is proportional to the quantity of absorbers and scatterers along that length, typically increasing with decreasing altitude. The optical depth of the whole path is then the integral (sum) of those optical depths along the path.</p>
<p>When the density of absorbers is layered, that is, depends much more on vertical than horizontal position in the atmosphere, to a good approximation the optical depth is inversely proportional to the projection effect, that is, to the cosine of the zenith angle. Since transmittance decreases exponentially with increasing optical depth, as the sun approaches the horizon there comes a point when absorption dominates projection for the rest of the day. With a relatively high level of absorbers this can be a considerable portion of the late afternoon, and likewise of the early morning. Conversely in the (hypothetical) total absence of absorption the optical depth remains zero at all altitudes of the sun, that is, transmittance remains 1, and so only the projection effect applies.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Direct_insolation" title="Direct insolation">Direct insolation</a> is measured at a given location with a surface element perpendicular to the Sun. It excludes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_insolation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diffuse insolation (page does not exist)">diffuse insolation</a> (radiation that is scattered or reflected by atmospheric components). Direct insolation is equal to the irradiance above the atmosphere minus the atmospheric losses due to <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">scattering</a>. While the irradiance above the atmosphere varies with time of year (because the distance to the sun varies), losses depend on time of day (length of light's path through the atmosphere depending on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_elevation_angle" title="Solar elevation angle">Solar elevation angle</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">Cloud cover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">Moisture</a> content and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pollution" title="Atmospheric pollution">contents</a>. (See values for direct and total insolation further down.) Insolation affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_metabolism" title="Plant metabolism">plant metabolism</a> and animal behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diffuse insolation is the contribution of light scattered by the atmosphere to total insolation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg/220px-SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg/330px-SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg/440px-SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SR20_pyranometer_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Average annual solar radiation arriving at the top of the Earth's atmosphere is roughly 1361 W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The radiation is distributed across the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>. About half is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_light" title="Infrared light">infrared light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Sun's rays are <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuated</a> as they pass through the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, leaving maximum normal surface irradiance at approximately 1000 W /m<sup>2</sup> at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> on a clear day. When 1361 W/m<sup>2</sup> is arriving above the atmosphere (when the sun is at the zenith in a cloudless sky), direct sun is about 1050 W/m<sup>2</sup>, and global radiation on a horizontal surface at ground level is about 1120 W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_constant_at_ground_level_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_constant_at_ground_level-7">[7]</a></sup> The latter figure includes radiation scattered or reemitted by atmosphere and surroundings. The actual figure varies with the Sun's angle and atmospheric circumstances. Ignoring clouds, the daily average insolation for the Earth is approximately 6 kWh/m<sup>2</sup> = 21.6 MJ/m<sup>2</sup>.</p>
<p>The output of, for example, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> panel, partly depends on the angle of the sun relative to the panel. One Sun is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">power flux</a>, not a standard value for actual insolation. Sometimes this unit is referred to as a Sol, not to be confused with a <i>sol</i>, meaning <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars#Sols" title="Timekeeping on Mars">one solar day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variation">Variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_irradiance">Total irradiance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Total irradiance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Total solar irradiance (TSI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> changes slowly on decadal and longer timescales. The variation during <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_21" title="Solar cycle 21">solar cycle 21</a> was about 0.1% (peak-to-peak).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willson91_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willson91-15">[15]</a></sup> In contrast to older reconstructions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.nap.edu_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.nap.edu-16">[16]</a></sup> most recent TSI reconstructions point to an increase of only about 0.05% to 0.1% between the Maunder Minimum and the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultraviolet_irradiance">Ultraviolet irradiance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ultraviolet irradiance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ultraviolet irradiance (EUV) varies by approximately 1.5 percent from solar maxima to minima, for 200 to 300 nm wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, a proxy study estimated that UV has increased by 3.0% since the Maunder Minimum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milankovitch_cycles">Milankovitch cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Milankovitch cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Milankovitch_Variations.png"><img alt="Milankovitch Variations.png" data-file-height="363" data-file-width="479" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Milankovitch_Variations.png/300px-Milankovitch_Variations.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Milankovitch_Variations.png/450px-Milankovitch_Variations.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7e/Milankovitch_Variations.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p>Some variations in insolation are not due to solar changes but rather due to the Earth moving between its perigee and apogee, or changes in the latitudinal distribution of radiation. These orbital changes or <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a> have caused radiance variations of as much as 25% (locally; global average changes are much smaller) over long periods. The most recent significant event was an axial tilt of 24° during boreal summer near the <i><a href="/wiki/Holocene_climatic_optimum" title="Holocene climatic optimum">Holocene climatic optimum</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The space-based TSI record comprises measurements from more than ten radiometers spanning three solar cycles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All modern TSI satellite instruments employ <a href="/wiki/Active_cavity_radiometer" title="Active cavity radiometer">active cavity electrical substitution radiometry</a>. This technique applies measured electrical heating to maintain an absorptive blackened cavity in thermal equilibrium while incident sunlight passes through a precision <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a> of calibrated area. The aperture is modulated via a <a href="/wiki/Shutter_(photography)" title="Shutter (photography)">shutter</a>. Accuracy uncertainties of &lt;0.01% are required to detect long term solar irradiance variations, because expected changes are in the range 0.05 to 0.15 W m−2 per century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intertemporal_calibration">Intertemporal calibration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Intertemporal calibration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In orbit, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiometric" title="Radiometric">radiometric</a> calibrations drift for reasons including solar degradation of the cavity, electronic degradation of the heater, surface degradation of the precision aperture and varying surface emissions and temperatures that alter thermal backgrounds. These calibrations require compensation to preserve consistent measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>For various reasons, the sources do not always agree. The Solar Radiation and Climate Experiment/Total Irradiance Measurement (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SORCE" title="SORCE">SORCE</a>/TIM) TSI values are lower than prior measurements by the Earth Radiometer Budget Experiment (ERBE) on the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Radiation_Budget_Satellite" title="Earth Radiation Budget Satellite">Earth Radiation Budget Satellite</a> (ERBS), VIRGO on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar Heliospheric Observatory">Solar Heliospheric Observatory</a> (SoHO) and the ACRIM instruments on the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Maximum_Mission" title="Solar Maximum Mission">Solar Maximum Mission</a> (SMM), <a href="/wiki/Upper_Atmosphere_Research_Satellite" title="Upper Atmosphere Research Satellite">Upper Atmosphere Research Satellite</a> (UARS) and <a href="/wiki/ACRIMSAT" title="ACRIMSAT">ACRIMSat</a>. Pre-launch ground calibrations relied on component rather than system level measurements, since irradiance standards lacked absolute accuracies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Measurement stability involves exposing different radiometer cavities to different accumulations of solar radiation to quantify exposure-dependent degradation effects. These effects are then compensated for in final data. Observation overlaps permits corrections for both absolute offsets and validation of instrumental drifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Uncertainties of individual observations exceed irradiance variability (∼0.1%). Thus, instrument stability and measurement continuity are relied upon to compute real variations.</p>
<p>Long-term radiometer drifts can be mistaken for irradiance variations that can be misinterpreted as affecting climate. Examples include the issue of the irradiance increase between cycle minima in 1986 and 1996, evident only in the ACRIM composite (and not the model) and the low irradiance levels in the PMOD composite during the 2008 minimum.</p>
<p>Despite the fact that ACRIM I, ACRIM II, ACRIM III, VIRGO and TIM all track degradation with redundant cavities, notable and unexplained differences remain in irradiance and the modeled influences of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">sunspots</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faculae" title="Faculae">faculae</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persistent_inconsistencies">Persistent inconsistencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Persistent inconsistencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Disagreement among overlapping observations indicates unresolved drifts that suggest the TSI record is not sufficiently stable to discern solar changes on decadal time scales. Only the ACRIM composite shows irradiance increasing by ∼1 W m−2 between 1986 and 1996; this change is also absent in the model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recommendations to resolve the instrument discrepancies include validating optical measurement accuracy by comparing ground-based instruments to laboratory references, such as those at <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Science_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Science and Technology">National Institute of Science and Technology</a> (NIST); NIST validation of aperture area calibrations uses spares from each instrument; and applying <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> corrections from the view-limiting aperture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>For ACRIM, NIST determined that diffraction from the view-limiting aperture contributes a 0.13% signal not accounted for in the three ACRIM instruments. This correction lowers the reported ACRIM values, bringing ACRIM closer to TIM. In ACRIM and all other instruments, the aperture is deep inside the instrument, with a larger view-limiting aperture at the front. Depending on edge imperfections this can directly scatter light into the cavity. This design admits two to three times the amount of light intended to be measured; if not completely absorbed or scattered, this additional light produces erroneously high signals. In contrast, TIM's design places the precision aperture at the front so that only desired light enters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-6"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Variations from other sources likely include an annual cycle that is nearly in phase with the Sun-Earth distance in ACRIM III data and 90-day spikes in the VIRGO data coincident with SoHO spacecraft maneuvers that were most apparent during the 2008 solar minimum.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="TSI_Radiometer_Facility">TSI Radiometer Facility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: TSI Radiometer Facility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>TIM's high absolute accuracy creates new opportunities for measuring climate variables. TSI Radiometer Facility (TRF) is a cryogenic <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometer</a> that operates in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> with controlled light sources. L-1 Standards and Technology (LASP) designed and built the system, completed in 2008. It was calibrated for optical power against the NIST Primary Optical Watt Radiometer, a cryogenic radiometer that maintains the NIST radiant power scale to an uncertainty of 0.02% (1<i>σ</i>). As of 2011 TRF was the only facility that approached the desired &lt;0.01% uncertainty for pre-launch validation of solar radiometers measuring irradiance (rather than merely optical power) at solar power levels and under vacuum conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-7"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>TRF encloses both the reference radiometer and the instrument under test in a common vacuum system that contains a stationary, spatially uniform illuminating beam. A precision aperture with area calibrated to 0.0031% (1<i>σ</i>) determines the beam's measured portion. The test instrument's precision aperture is positioned in the same location, without optically altering the beam, for direct comparison to the reference. Variable beam power provides linearity diagnostics, and variable beam diameter diagnoses scattering from different instrument components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-8"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Glory/TIM and PICARD/PREMOS flight instrument absolute scales are now traceable to the TRF in both optical power and irradiance. The resulting high accuracy reduces the consequences of any future gap in the solar irradiance record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-9"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_reassessment">2011 reassessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 2011 reassessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most probable value of TSI representative of solar minimum is 1360.8 ± 0.5 W m−2, lower than the earlier accepted value of 1365.4 ± 1.3 W m−2, established in the 1990s. The new value came from SORCE/TIM and radiometric laboratory tests. Scattered light is a primary cause of the higher irradiance values measured by earlier satellites in which the precision aperture is located behind a larger, view-limiting aperture. The TIM uses a view-limiting aperture that is smaller than precision aperture that precludes this spurious signal. The new estimate is from better measurement rather than a change in solar output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-11"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>A regression model-based split of the relative proportion of sunspot and facular influences from SORCE/TIM data accounts for 92% of observed variance and tracks the observed trends to within TIM's stability band. This agreement provides further evidence that TSI variations are primarily due to solar surface magnetic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-12"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instrument inaccuracies add a significant uncertainty in determining Earth's <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">energy balance</a>. The energy imbalance has been variously measured (during a deep <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">solar minimum</a> of 2005–2010) to be +0.58 ± 0.15 W/m²),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imbalance_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imbalance-23">[23]</a></sup> +0.60 ± 0.17 W/m²<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and +0.85 W m−2. Estimates from space-based measurements range from +3 to 7 W m−2. SORCE/TIM's lower TSI value reduces this discrepancy by 1 W m−2. This difference between the new lower TIM value and earlier TSI measurements corresponds to a climate forcing of −0.8 W m−2, which is comparable to the energy imbalance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-13"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_reassessment">2014 reassessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 2014 reassessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014 a new ACRIM composite was developed using the updated ACRIM3 record. It added corrections for scattering and diffraction revealed during recent testing at TRF and two algorithm updates. The algorithm updates more accurately account for instrument thermal behavior and parsing of shutter cycle data. These corrected a component of the quasi-annual signal and increased the <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">signal to noise ratio</a>, respectively. The net effect of these corrections decreased the average ACRIM3 TSI value without affecting the trending in the ACRIM Composite TSI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Differences between ACRIM and PMOD TSI composites are evident, but the most significant is the solar minimum-to-minimum trends during <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_21" title="Solar cycle 21">solar cycles 21</a>-<a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_23" title="Solar cycle 23">23</a>. ACRIM established an increase of +0.037%/decade from 1980 to 2000 and a decrease thereafter. PMOD instead presents a steady decrease since 1978. Significant differences can also be seen during the peak of solar cycles 21 and 22. These arise from the fact that ACRIM uses the original TSI results published by the satellite experiment teams while PMOD significantly modifies some results to conform them to specific TSI proxy models. The implications of increasing TSI during the global warming of the last two decades of the 20th century are that solar forcing may be a significantly larger factor in climate change than represented in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMIP5" title="CMIP5">CMIP5</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_circulation_models" title="General circulation models">general circulation climate models</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In construction, insolation is an important consideration when designing a building for a particular site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Insolation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="632" data-file-width="540" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Insolation.gif/220px-Insolation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Insolation.gif/330px-Insolation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Insolation.gif/440px-Insolation.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Insolation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Insolation variation by month; 1984–1993 averages for January (top) and April (bottom)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The projection effect can be used to design buildings that are cool in summer and warm in winter, by providing vertical windows on the equator-facing side of the building (the south face in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a>, or the north face in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a>): this maximizes insolation in the winter months when the Sun is low in the sky and minimizes it in the summer when the Sun is high. (The <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">Sun's north/south path</a> through the sky spans 47 degrees through the year).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">Solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Insolation figures are used as an input to worksheets to size <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Because solar collectors panels are almost always mounted at an angle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> towards the sun, insolation must be adjusted to prevent estimates that are inaccurately low for winter and inaccurately high for summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This also means that the amount of sun falling on a solar panel at high latitude is not as low compared to one at the equator as would appear from just considering insolation on a horizontal surface. In many countries the figures can be obtained from an insolation map or from insolation tables that reflect data over the prior 30–50 years. Photovoltaic panels are rated under standard conditions to determine the Wp rating (<a href="/wiki/Solar_cell#Watts_peak" title="Solar cell">watts peak</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which can then be used with insolation to determine the expected output, adjusted by factors such as tilt, tracking and shading (which can be included to create the installed Wp rating).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Insolation values range from 800 to 950 kWh/(kWp·y) in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> to up to 2,900 in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_research">Climate research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Climate research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Irradiance plays a part in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_modeling" title="Climate modeling">climate modeling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>. A non-zero average global net radiation at the top of the atmosphere is indicative of Earth's thermal disequilibrium as imposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcing</a>.</p>
<p>The impact of the lower 2014 TSI value on climate models is unknown. A few tenths of a percent change in the absolute TSI level is typically considered to be of minimal consequence for climate simulations. The new measurements require climate model parameter adjustments.</p>
<p>Experiments with GISS Model 3 investigated the sensitivity of model performance to the TSI absolute value during present and pre-industrial epochs, and describe, for example, how the irradiance reduction is partitioned between the atmosphere and surface and the effects on outgoing radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-14"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Assessing the impact of long-term irradiance changes on climate requires greater instrument stability<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-15"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup> combined with reliable global surface temperature observations to quantify climate response processes to radiative forcing on decadal time scales. The observed 0.1% irradiance increase imparts 0.22 W m−2 climate forcing, which suggests a transient climate response of 0.6 °C per W m−2. This response is larger by a factor of 2 or more than in the IPCC-assessed 2008 models, possibly appearing in the models' heat uptake by the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp_22-16"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_travel">Space travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Space travel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Insolation is the primary variable affecting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrium_temperature" title="Equilibrium temperature">equilibrium temperature</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> design and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetology" title="Planetology">planetology</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_activity_and_irradiance_measurement" title="Solar activity and irradiance measurement">Solar activity and irradiance measurement</a> is a concern for space travel. For example, the American space agency, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, launched its <a href="/wiki/Solar_Radiation_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Solar Radiation and Climate Experiment">Solar Radiation and Climate Experiment</a> (SORCE) satellite with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Irradiance_Monitor" title="Solar Irradiance Monitor">Solar Irradiance Monitors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boxwell_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boxwell-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_engineering">Civil engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_irradiance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Civil engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, numerical models of <a href="/wiki/Snowmelt" title="Snowmelt">snowmelt</a> runoff use observations of insolation. This permits estimation of the rate at which water is released from a melting snowpack. Field measurement is accomplished using a <a href="/wiki/Pyranometer" title="Pyranometer">pyranometer</a>.</p>
