<p>The <b>Cape Floristic Region</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floristic_region" title="Floristic region">floristic region</a> located near the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. It is the only floristic region of the Cape (South African) Floristic Kingdom, and includes only one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floristic_province" title="Floristic province">floristic province</a>, known as the Cape Floristic Province.</p>
<p>The Cape Floristic Region, the smallest of the six recognised <a href="/wiki/Phytochorion" title="Phytochorion">floral kingdoms</a> of the world, is an area of extraordinarily high diversity and <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemism</a>, and is home to over 9,000 vascular plant species, of which 69 percent are endemic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odendaal_2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odendaal_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> Much of this diversity is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Fynbos" title="Fynbos">fynbos</a> biome, a Mediterranean-type, fire-prone shrubland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odendaal_2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Odendaal_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The economical worth of fynbos biodiversity, based on harvests of fynbos products (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wildflower" title="Wildflower">wildflowers</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eco-tourism" title="Eco-tourism">eco-tourism</a>, is estimated to be in the region of <a href="/wiki/South_African_rand" title="South African rand">R</a>77 million a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odendaal_2008_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Odendaal_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> Thus, it is clear that the Cape Floristic Region has both economic and intrinsic biological value as a <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">biodiversity hotspot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odendaal_2008_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Odendaal_2008-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_description">Location and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Floristic_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Home to the greatest non-tropical concentration of higher plant species in the world, the region is the only hotspot that encompasses an entire floral kingdom, and holds five of South Africa’s 12 endemic plant families and 160 endemic genera. Covering 78,555 km², Cape Floristic Region hotspot is located entirely within the borders of South Africa. It is one of the five temperate Mediterranean-type systems on the hotspots list, and is one of only two hotspots that encompass an entire floral kingdom (the other being <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The Region covers the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> region of South Africa in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape" title="Western Cape">Western Cape</a> in the southwestern corner of the country, and extends eastward into the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Cape" title="Eastern Cape">Eastern Cape</a>, a transitional zone between the winter rainfall region to the west and the summer-rainfall region to the east in <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Floristic_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the region is covered with <a href="/wiki/Fynbos" title="Fynbos">fynbos</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclerophyllous" title="Sclerophyllous">sclerophyllous</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a> occurring on acid sands or nutrient poor soils derived from Table Mountain Sandstones (Cape Supergroup). Fynbos is home to an amazing diversity of plant species including many members of the Protea family (<a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a>), Heath family (<a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">Ericaceae</a>), and Reed family of restios (<a href="/wiki/Restionaceae" title="Restionaceae">Restionaceae</a>). Other vegetation types are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandveld" title="Sandveld">sandveld</a>, a soft coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland">scrubland</a> found mostly on the west-facing coast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Cape_Province" title="Western Cape Province">Western Cape Province</a>, on tertiary sands. <a href="/wiki/Renosterveld" title="Renosterveld">Renosterveld</a> is a grassy shrubland dominated by members of the Daisy family (<a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a> – particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renosterbos" title="Renosterbos">renosterbos</a> (<i>Elytropappus rhinocerotis</i>), graminoids and geophytes, occurring on the base-rich shaley soils of the coastal forelands. Small pockets of <a href="/wiki/Afromontane" title="Afromontane">Afromontane</a> forest (<a href="/wiki/Southern_Afrotemperate_Forest" title="Southern Afrotemperate Forest">Southern Afrotemperate Forest</a>) can be found in humid and sheltered areas.</p>
<p>According to Takhtajan (1978), the following families are endemic or subendemic to the region: <a href="/wiki/Grubbiaceae" title="Grubbiaceae">Grubbiaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roridulaceae" title="Roridulaceae">Roridulaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruniaceae" title="Bruniaceae">Bruniaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penaeaceae" title="Penaeaceae">Penaeaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greyiaceae" title="Greyiaceae">Greyiaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geissolomataceae" title="Geissolomataceae">Geissolomataceae</a>, Retziaceae (<i><a href="/wiki/Retzia" title="Retzia">Retzia</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Stilbaceae" title="Stilbaceae">Stilbaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Floristic_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> divides the Cape floristic region into three <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowland_fynbos_and_renosterveld" title="Lowland fynbos and renosterveld">Lowland fynbos and renosterveld</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_fynbos_and_renosterveld" title="Montane fynbos and renosterveld">Montane fynbos and renosterveld</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Albany_thickets" title="Albany thickets">Albany thickets</a>.</p>
<p>The fynbos ecoregions are designated one of the <a href="/wiki/Global_200" title="Global 200">Global 200</a> priority ecoregions for conservation. <a href="/wiki/Conservation_International" title="Conservation International">Conservation International</a> declared the Cape floristic region to be a <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">biodiversity hotspot</a>.</p>
<p>It is thought that the Cape Floristic Region is experiencing one of the most rapid rates of extinction in the world due to habitat loss, land degradation, and invasive alien plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extincitionCap_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-extincitionCap-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
