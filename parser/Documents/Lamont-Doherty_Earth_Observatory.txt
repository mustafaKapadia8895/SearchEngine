<p>The <b>Lamont–Doherty Earth Observatory</b> (<b>LDEO</b>) is a research unit of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> located on a 157-acre (64 ha) campus in Palisades, N.Y., 18 miles (29 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lamont–Doherty Earth Observatory (LDEO) was established in 1949 as the Lamont Geological Observatory on the weekend estate of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_W._Lamont" title="Thomas W. Lamont">Thomas W.</a> and Florence Haskell Corliss Lamont, which was donated to the university for that purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Observatory’s founder and first director was <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ewing" title="Maurice Ewing">Maurice "Doc" Ewing</a>, a seismologist who is credited with advancing efforts to study the solid Earth, particularly in areas related to using sound waves to image rock and sediments beneath the ocean floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was also the first to collect sediment <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">core samples</a> from the bottom of the ocean,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> a common practice today that helps scientists study changes in the planet's climate and the ocean's <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>.</p>
<p>In 1969, the Observatory was renamed Lamont–Doherty in honor of a major gift from the Henry L. and Grace Doherty Charitable Foundation; in 1993, it was renamed the Lamont–Doherty Earth Observatory in recognition of its expertise in the broad range of Earth sciences. Lamont–Doherty Earth Observatory is Columbia University's Earth sciences research center and is a core component of the <a href="/wiki/The_Earth_Institute" title="The Earth Institute">Earth Institute</a>, a collection of academic and research units within the university that together address complex environmental issues facing the planet and its inhabitants, with particular focus on advancing scientific research to support <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> and the needs of the world's poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>To support its research and the work of the broader scientific community, Lamont–Doherty operates the 235-foot (72 m) research vessel, the <a href="/wiki/RV_Marcus_Langseth" title="RV Marcus Langseth">R/V <i>Marcus Langseth</i></a>, which is equipped to undertake a wide range of geological, seismological, oceanographic and biological studies. Lamont–Doherty also houses the world's largest collection of deep-sea and ocean-sediment cores as well as many specialized research laboratories.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_statement">Mission statement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mission statement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lamont–Doherty Earth Observatory at Columbia University is one of the world’s leading research centers developing fundamental knowledge about the origin, evolution and future of the natural world. More than 300 research scientists and students study the planet from its deepest interior to the outer reaches of its atmosphere, on every continent and in every ocean. From global climate change to earthquakes, volcanoes, nonrenewable resources, environmental hazards and beyond, Observatory scientists provide a rational basis for the difficult choices facing humankind in the planet’s stewardship.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_achievements">Major achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the many contributions Lamont–Doherty scientists have made to understanding of the Earth system over the years, they:</p>
<ul>
<li>Provided the first definitive evidence to support the theory of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>First explained the role of large-scale ocean circulation systems in <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Provided the first evidence that the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">inner core</a> is spinning faster than the rest of the planet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>First to systematically study and first to create a <a href="/wiki/Marie_Tharp" title="Marie Tharp">global bathymetric map</a> of the oceans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Demonstrated that changes in the Earth's past climate were linked to changes in the planet's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milankovich_cycles" title="Milankovich cycles">rotation and orbit</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">sun's output</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Made the first successful prediction of extreme weather associated with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a> event<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>First to detect nuclear explosions using seismometers and continue monitoring work as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CTBT" title="CTBT">Comprehensive Nuclear Test Ban Treaty</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Developed the first lunar seismometers and conducted some of the earliest analyses of the moon's structure and tectonic activity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_divisions">Major divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Major divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_paleo_environment">Biology and paleo environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biology and paleo environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/biology-paleo-environment" rel="nofollow">Division of Biology and Paleo Environment</a> (BPE) includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanographer" title="Oceanographer">oceanographers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geochemist" title="Geochemist">geochemists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_scientist" title="Environmental scientist">environmental scientists</a> who pursue research in two connected efforts. First, because all biological organisms record the environment in which they exist, BPE scientists use biology to uncover clues about earth’s past environment. They also attempt to understand how modern environmental conditions affect present-day biology. To do this, BPE scientists turn to a number of primary sources, including deep-sea sediment cores (and the fossils of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> they contain), samples from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coral_reefs" title="Coral reefs">coral reefs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">growth rings</a> of trees.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geochemistry">Geochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Researchers in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/geochemistry" rel="nofollow">Division of Geochemistry</a> study the processes, past and present, that have governed Earth’s many environments. Using <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">chemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isotope_analysis" title="Isotope analysis">isotopic</a> analyses, division scientists study samples of air, water, biological remains, rocks and meteorites in order to address a broad range of scientific issues, ranging from the particulate and chemical pollutants emitted by the collapse of the World Trade Center, to changes in Earth's past climate, to the fundamental chemical processes involved in the differentiation and formation of the planet's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and core.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_geology_and_geophysics">Marine geology and geophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marine geology and geophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As its name implies, scientists in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/marine-geology-geophysics" rel="nofollow">Division of Marine Geology and Geophysics</a> (MG&amp;G) are primarily concerned with studying the structure and evolution of the Earth's seafloor. To do so, they employ tools that include <a href="/wiki/Side-scan_sonar" title="Side-scan sonar">side-scan sonar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflection_seismology" title="Reflection seismology">multi-channel seismic imaging</a> to map the surface and sub-surface, as well as satellite-based <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a>. An early success of MG&amp;G researchers was the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a>, which led to the general acceptance of plate tectonics as the broad foundation for understanding earthquake generation. Other MG&amp;G scientists study the interface between ice sheets and bedrock, sediment transport in the Hudson River, and meteorite impacts in the deep ocean.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_and_climate_physics">Ocean and climate physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ocean and climate physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/ocean-climate-physics" rel="nofollow">Division of Ocean and Climate Physics</a> (OCP) focuses on the links between Earth’s climate system and its oceans, including interactions involving the atmosphere, ocean circulation, planetary volcanism, the cryosphere, the biosphere and external forces such as variability of solar radiation and even the occasional asteroid impact. Scientists are also increasingly interested in understanding the nature of past and present changes to Earth’s climate—whether abrupt or gradual, regional or global—and the potential for human activities to influence the natural system.</p>
<h3><span id="Division_of_seismology,_geology_and_tectonophysics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_of_seismology.2C_geology_and_tectonophysics">Division of seismology, geology and tectonophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Division of seismology, geology and tectonophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The researchers in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/seismology-geology-tectonophysics" rel="nofollow">Seismology, Geology and Tectonophysics Division</a> (SGT) study theoretical and observational <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a>, solid Earth dynamics, rock mechanics, structural geology and tectonics, and sedimentary geology. They also contribute to understanding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">earthquakes</a>; the structure of Earth’s <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, mantle, and core; and the large-scale motions and deformation of the tectonic plates. In addition, the division operates several facilities for the scientific community. In partnership with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Geological_Survey" title="U.S. Geological Survey">U.S. Geological Survey</a>, SGT operates a network of seismographs throughout the northeastern U.S., supports <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF) efforts to conduct ocean-bottom seismology research, and provides accurate earthquake source mechanisms to government and academic researchers around the world.</p>

<h3><span id="Office_of_Marine_Operations_and_R/V_Marcus_G._Langseth"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_of_Marine_Operations_and_R.2FV_Marcus_G._Langseth">Office of Marine Operations and R/V <i>Marcus G. Langseth</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Office of Marine Operations and R/V Marcus G. Langseth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of Lamont–Doherty's seagoing research began in 1953 with the acquisition of the <a href="/wiki/RV_Vema" title="RV Vema">R/V <i>Vema</i></a> and the formation of a group within the Observatory to organize and support research missions. Today, the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/office-of-marine-operations" rel="nofollow">Office of Marine Operations</a> oversees geophysical and oceanographic studies on board the Observatory's latest research vessel, the <a class="external text" href="https://www.webcitation.org/62u8VihFY?url=http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/res/fac/oma/langseth/index.html" rel="nofollow">R/V <i>Marcus Langseth</i></a>, and conducts its own research on the character and operation of various geophysical imaging systems employed by the scientific community.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Department_of_Earth_and_Environmental_Sciences">Department of Earth and Environmental Sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Department of Earth and Environmental Sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Department of Earth and Environmental Sciences (DEES) is the educational arm of Columbia University located on the Lamont–Doherty campus to train graduate and undergraduate students enrolled at the university and at Barnard College. The Lamont–Doherty office of DEES primarily supports the department's graduate program, though many undergraduate students take classes and conduct research at the Observatory. At any given time, between 80 and 90 Ph.D. students are working towards degrees in specialties that include aqueous geochemistry, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_science" title="Atmospheric science">atmospheric science</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_science" title="Climate science">climate science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecophysiology" title="Ecophysiology">ecophysiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">physical oceanography</a>, solid Earth <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, and solid Earth <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a>. Specific programs at Lamont–Doherty are conducted with many education-affiliates at Columbia and elsewhere in the New York metro area.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_Repository">Core Repository</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Core Repository">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/res/fac/CORE_REPOSITORY/RHP1.html" rel="nofollow">Lamont–Doherty Core Repository</a> is both an archive of sediment and rocks from on and beneath the ocean floor and an archive of digital data pertaining to the material. The repository contains more than 7,200 meters of core, from every ocean and sea, and many other samples. They are used to study Earth's climate and environment, in other research areas, and for educational purposes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree_Ring_Lab">Tree Ring Lab</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tree Ring Lab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/res/fac/trl/" rel="nofollow">Tree-Ring Lab</a> (TRL) is dedicated to the application of tree-ring research around the world to improve understanding of Earth's past climate and environmental history. Current research concentrates on the use of tree-ring data networks to study regional climate, global climate teleconnections and anthropogenic impacts on tree growth, and on developing new quantitative techniques.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_Bottom_Seismology_Lab">Ocean Bottom Seismology Lab</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ocean Bottom Seismology Lab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/seismology-geology-tectonophysics/ocean-bottom-seismology" rel="nofollow">Ocean Bottom Seismology (OBS) Lab</a> develops and operates instruments used to measure deformation of the ocean floor in cooperation with the NSF's <a class="external text" href="http://obsip.ucsd.edu/" rel="nofollow">National Ocean Bottom Seismograph Instrumentation Pool</a> (OBSIP). These instruments permit the study of such phenomena as seafloor spreading and the formation of new crust, convective and hydrothermal processes beneath the seafloor, and the fate of subducting slabs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lamont_Cooperative_Seismographic_Network">Lamont Cooperative Seismographic Network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Lamont Cooperative Seismographic Network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/LCSN/" rel="nofollow">Lamont–Doherty Cooperative Seismographic Network</a> (LCSN) operates 40 seismographic stations in seven states—Connecticut, Delaware, Maryland, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania and Vermont—to monitor earthquakes occurring primarily in the eastern U.S. The network is an active member of the <a class="external text" href="https://earthquake.usgs.gov/monitoring/anss/" rel="nofollow">Advanced National Seismic System</a> (ANSS), which is a key input to the USGS National Seismic Hazard Maps. It also provides professional development among station operators.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Databases_and_Repositories">Databases and Repositories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamont%E2%80%93Doherty_Earth_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Databases and Repositories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lamont–Doherty is home to a wide range of geophysical <a class="external text" href="http://www.ldeo.columbia.edu/research/databases-repositories" rel="nofollow">databases and repositories</a> that aid in scientific research, education and outreach. Many of the databases are available online, and the repositories of physical samples, which are open to access by researchers and students from around the world, also make an increasing portion of their holdings available digitally.</p>
