<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Squall line</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about a line of thunderstorms. For a sudden, sharp increase in wind speed, see <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squall</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pehrcn07212003.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="699" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pehrcn07212003.gif/220px-Pehrcn07212003.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pehrcn07212003.gif/330px-Pehrcn07212003.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pehrcn07212003.gif/440px-Pehrcn07212003.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_vortex" title="Mesoscale convective vortex">Cyclonic vortex</a> over Pennsylvania with a trailing squall line</div>

<p>A <b>squall line</b> (also known as a <b>quasi-linear convective system</b> or QLCS) is a line of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a> forming along or ahead of a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>. In the early 20th century, the term was used as a synonym for cold front. It contains heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, frequent <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_wind" title="Straight-line wind">straight-line winds</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterspouts" title="Waterspouts">waterspouts</a>. Strong straight-line winds can occur where the squall line is in the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornadoes</a> can occur along waves within a <a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">line echo wave pattern</a> (LEWP), where mesoscale <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure areas</a> are present. Some bow echoes which develop within the <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> season are known as <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a>, and they move quite fast through large sections of territory. On the back edge of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shield&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rain shield (page does not exist)">rain shield</a> associated with mature squall lines, a <a href="/wiki/Wake_low" title="Wake low">wake low</a> can be present, sometimes associated with a <a href="/wiki/Heat_burst" title="Heat burst">heat burst</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Polar front theory was developed by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Bjerknes" title="Jacob Bjerknes">Jacob Bjerknes</a>, derived from a dense network of observation sites in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. This theory proposed that the main inflow into a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> was concentrated along two lines of convergence, one ahead of the low and another trailing behind the low. The trailing convergence zone was referred to as the squall line or cold front. Areas of clouds and rainfall appeared to be focused along this convergence zone. The concept of frontal zones led to the concept of air masses. The nature of the three-dimensional structure of the cyclone was conceptualized after the development of the upper air network during the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OU_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OU-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bow_echo_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="294" data-file-width="542" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bow_echo_diagram.svg/220px-Bow_echo_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bow_echo_diagram.svg/330px-Bow_echo_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bow_echo_diagram.svg/440px-Bow_echo_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bow_echo_diagram.svg" title="Agrandir"></a></div>
Typical evolution of (a) into a bow echo (b, c) and into a comma echo (d). Dashed line indicates axis of greatest potential for <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a>. Arrows indicate wind flow relative to the storm. Area C is most prone to supporting tornado development.</div>

<p>Organized areas of thunderstorms activity reinforce pre-existing frontal zones, and they can outrun cold fronts. This outrunning occurs within the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> in a pattern where the upper level jet splits into two streams. The resultant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_System" title="Mesoscale Convective System">mesoscale convective system</a> (MCS) forms at the point of the upper level split in the wind pattern in the area of best low level inflow.</p>
<p>The convection then moves east and toward the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> into the warm sector, parallel to low-level thickness lines. When the convection is strong linear or curved, the MCS is called a squall line, with the feature placed at the leading edge of the significant wind shift and pressure rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This feature is commonly depicted in the warm season across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> on surface analyses, as they lie within sharp surface troughs.</p>
<p>If squall lines form over arid regions, a duststorm known as a <a href="/wiki/Haboob" title="Haboob">haboob</a> may result from the high winds in their wake picking up dust from the desert floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Well behind mature squall lines, a <a href="/wiki/Wake_low" title="Wake low">wake low</a> can develop on the back edge of the rain shield,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which can lead to a heat burst due to the warming up of the descending air mass which is no longer being rain-cooled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smaller cumulus or stratocumulus clouds, along with cirrus, and, sometimes, altocumulus or cirrocumulus, can be found ahead of the squall line. These clouds are the result of former cumulonimbus clouds having disintegrated, or an area of only minor instability ahead of the main squall line.</p>
<p>As supercells and multi-cell thunderstorms dissipate due to a weak shear force or poor lifting mechanisms, (e.g. considerable <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> or lack of daytime heating) the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">gust front</a> associated with them may outrun the squall line itself and the synoptic scale area of low pressure may then infill, leading to a weakening of the cold front; essentially, the thunderstorm has exhausted its updrafts, becoming purely a downdraft dominated system. The areas of dissipating squall line thunderstorms may be regions of low <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">CAPE</a>, low <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, insufficient wind shear, or poor synoptic dynamics (e.g. an upper level low filling) leading to <a href="/wiki/Frontolysis" title="Frontolysis">frontolysis</a>.</p>
<p>From here, a general thinning of a squall line will occur: with winds decaying over time, outflow boundaries weakening updrafts substantially and clouds losing their thickness.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Updrafts">Updrafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Updrafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The leading area of a squall line is composed primarily of multiple updrafts, or singular regions of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updraft</a>, rising from ground level to the highest extensions of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, condensing water and building a dark, ominous cloud to one with a noticeable overshooting top and anvil (thanks to <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> winds). Because of the chaotic nature of updrafts and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdrafts" title="Downdrafts">downdrafts</a>, pressure perturbations are important.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_perturbations">Pressure perturbations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pressure perturbations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pressure perturbations around thunderstorms are noteworthy. With <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> rapid within the lower and mid-levels of a mature thunderstorm, updraft and downdraft create distinct mesocenters of pressure. As thunderstorms organized in squall lines, the northern end of the squall line is commonly referred to as the cyclonic end, with the southern side rotating anticyclonically (in Northern hemisphere). Because of the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis force</a>, the northern end may evolve further, creating a "comma shaped" <a href="/wiki/Wake_low" title="Wake low">wake low</a>, or may continue in a squall-like pattern. The updraft ahead of the line create a mesolow too while the downdraft just behind the line will produce a mesohigh.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_shear">Wind shear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wind shear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">Wind shear</a> is an important aspect of a squall line. In low to medium shear environments, mature thunderstorms will contribute modest amounts of downdrafts, enough to help create a leading edge lifting mechanism – the gust front. In high shear environments created by opposing low level jet winds and synoptic winds, updrafts and consequential downdrafts can be much more intense (common in supercell <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclones</a>). The cold air <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> leaves the trailing area of the squall line to the mid-level jet, which aids in downdraft processes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severe_weather_indicators">Severe weather indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Severe weather indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Severe squall lines typically bow out due to the formation of a stronger mesoscale high-pressure system (a <a href="/wiki/Mesohigh" title="Mesohigh">mesohigh</a>) within the convective area due to strong descending motion behind the squall line, and could come in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-6">[6]</a></sup> The pressure difference between the mesoscale high and the lower pressures ahead of the squall line cause high winds, which are strongest where the line is most bowed out.</p>
<p>Another indication of the presence of severe weather along a squall line is its morphing into a <a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">line echo wave pattern</a>, or LEWP. A LEWP is a special configuration in a line of convective storms that indicates the presence of a low-pressure area and the possibility of damaging winds, large hail, and tornadoes. At each kink along the LEWP is a mesoscale low-pressure area, which could contain a tornado. In response to very strong <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> southwest of the mesoscale low, a portion of the line bulges outward forming a <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a>. Behind this bulge lies the mesoscale high-pressure area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Derecho">Derecho</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Derecho">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">Derecho</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DangerousShelfCloud.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="183" data-file-width="346" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/DangerousShelfCloud.jpg/220px-DangerousShelfCloud.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/DangerousShelfCloud.jpg/330px-DangerousShelfCloud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/DangerousShelfCloud.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DangerousShelfCloud.jpg" title="Agrandir"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">Shelf cloud</a> from a Derecho photographed in <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a></div>
</div>

<p>A derecho (<i>from Spanish</i>: "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/derecho" title="wikt:derecho">derecho</a>" meaning "straight")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> is a widespread and long-lived, violent convectively induced straight-line windstorm that is associated with a fast-moving band of severe thunderstorms usually taking the form of a <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a>. Derechos blow in the direction of movement of their associated storms, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">gust front</a>, except that the wind is sustained and generally increases in strength behind the "gust" front. A warm weather phenomenon, derechos occur mostly in summer, between May and August in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">Northern hemisphere</a>. They can occur at any time of the year and occur as frequently at night as in the daylight hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The traditional criteria that distinguish a derecho from a severe thunderstorm are <i>sustained</i> winds of 58 miles per hour (93 km/h) during the storm as opposed to gusts, high or rapidly increasing forward speed, and geographic extent (typically 250 nautical miles (460 km; 290 mi) in length.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition, they have a distinctive appearance on radar (bow echo); several unique features, such as the rear inflow notch and bookend vortex, and usually manifest two or more downbursts. Although these storms most commonly occur in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, derechos occur elsewhere in the world. Outside North America they may be called by different names. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and adjacent portions of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, a type of storm known as a "Nor'wester" may be a progressive derecho.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
