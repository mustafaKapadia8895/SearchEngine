<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">North Atlantic Deep Water</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW)</b> is a deep <a href="/wiki/Water_mass" title="Water mass">water mass</a> formed in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Thermohaline circulation</a> of the world's oceans involves the flow of warm surface waters from the southern hemisphere into the North Atlantic. Water flowing northward becomes modified through evaporation and mixing with other water masses, leading to increased salinity. When this water reaches the North Atlantic it cools and sinks through convection, due to its decreased temperature and increased salinity resulting in increased density. NADW is the outflow of this thick deep layer, which can be detected by its high salinity, high oxygen content, nutrient minima, high <sup>14</sup>C/<sup>12</sup>C,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broeker1991_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broeker1991-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talley_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talley-2">[2]</a></sup> CFCs are anthropogenic substances that enter the surface of the ocean from gas exchange with the atmosphere. This distinct composition allows its path to be traced as it mixes with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumpolar_Deep_Water" title="Circumpolar Deep Water">Circumpolar Deep Water</a> (CDW), which in turn fills the deep <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and part of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">South Pacific</a>. NADW and its formation is essential to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation" title="Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> (AMOC), which is responsible for transporting large amounts of water, heat, salt, carbon, nutrients and other substances from the Tropical Atlantic to the Mid and High Latitude Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the conveyor belt model of <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> of the world's oceans, the sinking of NADW pulls the waters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_drift" title="North Atlantic drift">North Atlantic drift</a> northward; however, this is almost certainly an oversimplification of the actual relationship between NADW formation and the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>/North Atlantic drift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>NADW has a temperature of 2-4 °C with a salinity of 34.9-35.0 psu found at a depth between 1500 and 4000m.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_sources">Formation and sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_Deep_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation and sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The NADW is a complex of several water masses formed by deep convection and also by overflow of dense water across the Greenland-Iceland-Scotland Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smethie_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smethie-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="548" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif/220px-North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif/330px-North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif/440px-North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Circulation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Circulation patterns in the North Atlantic Ocean. Cold, dense water is shown in blue, flowing south from upper latitudes, while warm, less dense water, shown in red flows north from low latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The upper layers are formed by deep open ocean convection during winter. <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea_Water" title="Labrador Sea Water">Labrador Sea Water</a> (LSW), formed in the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea" title="Labrador Sea">Labrador Sea</a> can reach depths of 2000 m as dense water sinks downward. Classical Labrador Sea Water (CLSW) production is dependent on preconditioning of water in the Labrador Sea from the previous year, and the strength of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a>. During a positive NAO phase, conditions exist for strong winter storms to develop. These storms freshen the surface water, and their winds increase cyclonic flow, which allows denser waters to sink. As a result, the temperature, salinity, and density vary yearly. In some years these conditions do not exist and CLSW is not formed. CLSW has characteristic potential temperature of 3 °C, salinity of 34.88 psu, and density of 34.66.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smethie_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smethie-5">[5]</a></sup> Another component of LSW is the Upper Labrador Sea Water (ULSW). ULSW forms at a density lower than CLSW and has a CFC maximum between 1200 and 1500 m in the subtropical North Atlantic. Eddies of cold less saline ULSW have similar densities of warmer saltier water and flow along the DWBC, but maintain their high CFCs. The ULSW eddies erode rapidly as they mix laterally with this warmer saltier water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smethie_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smethie-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lower waters mass of NADW form from overflow of the Greenland-Iceland-Scotland Ridge. They are Iceland-Scotland Overflow Water (ISOW) and Denmark Strait Overflow Water (DSOW). The overflows are a combination of dense Arctic Ocean water (18%), modified Atlantic water (32%), and intermediate water from the Nordic seas (20%), that entrain and mix with other water masses (contributing 30%) as they flow over the Greenland-Iceland-Scotland Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The formation of both of these waters involves the conversion of warm salty northward flowing surface waters to cold dense deep waters behind the Greenland-Iceland-Scotland Ridge. Water flow from the North Atlantic current enters the Arctic Ocean through the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Current" title="Norwegian Current">Norwegian Current</a> which splits into the <a href="/wiki/Fram_Strait" title="Fram Strait">Fram Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a> Branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Water from the Fram Strait recirculates, reaching a density of DSOW, sinks, and flows towards the Denmark Strait. Water flowing into the Barent Sea feeds ISOW.</p>
<p>ISOW enters the eastern North Atlantic over the Iceland-Scotland Ridge through the Faeroe Bank Channel at a depth of 850 m, with some water flowing over the shallower Iceland-Faeroe Rise. ISOW has a low CFC concentrations and it has been estimated from these concentrations that ISOW resides behind the ridge for 45 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smethie_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smethie-5">[5]</a></sup> As the water flows southward at the bottom of the channel, it entrains surrounding water of the eastern North Atlantic, and flows to the western North Atlantic through the <a href="/wiki/Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone" title="Charlie-Gibbs Fracture Zone">Charlie-Gibbs Fracture Zone</a>, entraining with LSW. This water is less dense than (DSOW) and lays above it as it flows cyclonically in the Irminger Basin.</p>
<p>DSOW is the coldest, densest, and freshest water mass of NADW. DSOW formed behind the ridge flows over the <a href="/wiki/Denmark_Strait" title="Denmark Strait">Denmark Strait</a> at a depth of 600m. The most significant water mass contributing to DSOW is Arctic Intermediate Water (AIW).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Winter cooling and convection allow AIW to sink and pool behind the Denmark Strait. Upper AIW has a high amount of anthropogenic tracers due its exposure to the atmosphere. AIW's tritium and CFC signature is observed in DSOW at the base of the Greenland continental slope. This also showed that the DSOW flowing 450 km to the south was no older than 2 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smethie_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smethie-5">[5]</a></sup> Both the DSOW and ISOW flow around the Irminger Basin and Labrador Sea in a deep boundary current. Leaving the Greenland Sea with 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sv</a> its flow increases to 10 Sv south of Greenland. It is cold and relatively fresh, flowing below 3500 m in the DWBC and spreading inward the deep Atlantic basins.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spreading_pathways">Spreading pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_Deep_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spreading pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The southward spread of NADW along the Deep Western <a href="/wiki/Boundary_current" title="Boundary current">Boundary current</a> (DWBC) can be traced by its high oxygen content, high CFCs, and density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Text_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Text-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>ULSW is the major source of upper NADW. ULSW advects southward from the Labrador Sea in small eddies that mix into the DWBC. A CFC maxima associated with ULSW has been observed along 24°N in the DWBC at 1500 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Text_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Text-10">[10]</a></sup> Some of the upper ULSW recirculates into the Gulf Stream, while some remains in the DWBC. High CFCs in the subtropics indicate recirculation in the subtropics. ULSW that remains in the DWBC dilutes as it moves equatorward. Deep convection in the Labrador Sea during the late 1980s and early 1990s resulted in CLSW with a lower CFC concentration due to downward mixing. However, convection allowed the CFCs to penetrate further downward to 2000m. These minimum could be tracked, and were first observed in the subtropics in the early 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smethie_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Smethie-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_bottom_water.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="470" data-file-width="745" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Antarctic_bottom_water.svg/300px-Antarctic_bottom_water.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Antarctic_bottom_water.svg/450px-Antarctic_bottom_water.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Antarctic_bottom_water.svg/600px-Antarctic_bottom_water.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_bottom_water.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The NADW flows southward through the Atlantic, approaching the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_Bottom_Water" title="Antarctic Bottom Water">Antarctic Bottom Water</a> past the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>.</div>

<p>ISOW and DSOW flow around the Irminger Basin and DSOW entering the DWBC. These are the two lower portions of the NADW. Another CFC maximum is seen at 3500 m in the subtropics from the DSOW contribution to NADW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Text_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Text-10">[10]</a></sup> Some of the NADW recirculates with the northern gyre. To the south of the gyre NADW flows under the Gulf Stream where it continues along the DWBC until it reaches another gyre in the subtropics.</p>
<p>Lower North Atlantic Deep Water (LNADW), originating in the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Sea" title="Greenland Sea">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Seas</a>, brings high salinity, oxygen, and freon concentrations towards to the <a href="/wiki/Romanche_Trench" title="Romanche Trench">Romanche Trench</a>, an equatorial fracture zone in the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> (MAR). Found at depths around 3,600–4,000 m (11,800–13,100 ft), LNADW flow east through the trench over AABW, the trench being the only opening in the MAR where inter-basin exchange is possible for these two water masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variability">Variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_Deep_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is believed that North Atlantic Deep Water formation has been dramatically reduced at times during the past (such as during the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> or during <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_event" title="Heinrich event">Heinrich events</a>), and that this might correlate with a decrease in the strength of the Gulf Stream and the North Atlantic drift, in turn cooling the climate of <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Europe" title="Northwestern Europe">northwestern Europe</a>. There is concern that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> might cause this to happen again. It is also hypothesized that during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> (LGM), NADW was replaced with an analogous watermass that occupied a shallower depth known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glacial_North_Atlantic_Intermediate_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glacial North Atlantic Intermediate Water (page does not exist)">Glacial North Atlantic Intermediate Water</a> (GNAIW).</p>
