<p><i><b>The Wizard of Oz</b></i> was a 1902 <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical</a> <a href="/wiki/Extravaganza" title="Extravaganza">extravaganza</a> based on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/L._Frank_Baum" title="L. Frank Baum">L. Frank Baum</a>, which was originally published in 1900. Much of the original music was by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Tietjens" title="Paul Tietjens">Paul Tietjens</a> and has been mostly forgotten, although it was still well-remembered and in discussion at MGM in 1939 when <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">the classic film version of the story</a> was made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Although Baum is the credited bookwriter, <a href="/wiki/Glen_MacDonough" title="Glen MacDonough">Glen MacDonough</a> was hired on as jokewriter after Baum had finished the script.</p>
<p>The show premiered at the Chicago Grand Opera House<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> on June 16, 1902 and later moved to the <a href="/wiki/Majestic_Theatre_(Columbus_Circle)" title="Majestic Theatre (Columbus Circle)">Majestic Theatre</a> on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> on January 21, 1903, where it ran for 293 performances until December 31, 1904, followed by travelling tours of the original cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It starred Anna Laughlin as <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Stone" title="Fred Stone">Fred Stone</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Scarecrow_(Oz)" title="Scarecrow (Oz)">Scarecrow</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_C._Montgomery" title="David C. Montgomery">David C. Montgomery</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">Tin Woodman</a> (who is called Niccolo Chopper in the musical; he had no name in the original book, but would be called Nick Chopper in the sequels). <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hill_(stage_actor)" title="Arthur Hill (stage actor)">Arthur Hill</a> (no relation to the Canadian actor) played the <a href="/wiki/Cowardly_Lion" title="Cowardly Lion">Cowardly Lion</a>, but in this version his role was reduced to a bit part. The <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> is mentioned but does not appear in this version, and <a href="/wiki/Toto_(Oz)" title="Toto (Oz)">Toto</a> is replaced by a cow named Imogene. An element from the show – the snowfall caused by the <a href="/wiki/Good_Witch_of_the_North" title="Good Witch of the North">Good Witch of the North</a>, which defeats the spell of the poppies that had put Dorothy and the Cowardly Lion to sleep – was later used in the classic 1939 movie. Other new characters in the piece are King <a href="/wiki/Pastoria" title="Pastoria">Pastoria</a> II and his girlfriend, Trixie Tryfle (a waitress), Cynthia Cynch (a lady lunatic), Sir Dashemoff Daily (the <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">poet laureate</a>), Sir Wiley Gyle, and General Riskitt. Dorothy Gale's surname was introduced in this piece. It was not mentioned in the original novel, though it is mentioned in <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i> (1907).</p>
<p>The main plot of the show, as recounted in newspapers of the time, is Pastoria's attempts to regain the throne from the Wizard of Oz. The original protagonists' search for the Wizard puts them on the wrong side of the law.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_One:_The_Storm">Act One: The Storm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Act One: The Storm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A young girl named <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a> lives on the great Kansas prairies with her <a href="/wiki/Aunt_Em" title="Aunt Em">Aunt Em</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Henry_(Oz)" title="Uncle Henry (Oz)">Uncle Henry</a>, and little dog, Toto. One day, while she is playing with her pet cow Imogene, a fierce whirlwind appears. Dorothy and Toto take shelter in the farmhouse, which is carried far away into the clouds.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, in the hamlet of Center Munch, the little <a href="/wiki/Munchkin" title="Munchkin">Munchkins</a> dance around their maypole, not noticing that Dorothy's house has fallen to earth and killed the Wicked Witch of the East. Dorothy opens the front door and marvels at the strange <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Oz" title="Land of Oz">Land of Oz</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Good_Witch_of_the_North" title="Good Witch of the North">Good Witch of the North</a> awards Dorothy with a magic ring that grants her three wishes and can summon <a href="/wiki/Glinda_the_Good_Witch" title="Glinda the Good Witch">Glinda the Good Witch of the South</a> at any time. The Good Witch then waves her wand and a pair of <a href="/wiki/Silver_Shoes" title="Silver Shoes">beautiful shoes</a> appear on Dorothy's feet. She tells Dorothy that if she wants to get home, she must ask the <a href="/wiki/Wizard_of_Oz_(character)" title="Wizard of Oz (character)">Wizard of Oz</a> to help her.</p>
<p>After a while everyone exits, and Dorothy is left alone with a <a href="/wiki/Scarecrow_(Oz)" title="Scarecrow (Oz)">Scarecrow</a> hung on a pole. She wishes she had someone to talk to, and the Scarecrow comes to life. He gets down from the pole and complains that he has no brain. Dorothy suggests that he join her on the road to the Emerald City, and he sings "Alas for the Man Without Brains". Dorothy and the Scarecrow come upon the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">Tin Woodman</a>, who has rusted playing his piccolo. As it turns out, the Woodman's real name is Niccolo Chopper. He explains that the Wicked Witch of the West took his heart, so he cannot love Cynthia, his girlfriend. He joins the others in the hope of receiving a heart from the Wizard, so that he can return to Cynthia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_Two:_The_Emerald_City">Act Two: The Emerald City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Act Two: The Emerald City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardian_of_the_Gates" title="Guardian of the Gates">Keeper of the Gates</a> patrols outside the Emerald City. Sir Wiley Gyle enters, a mad old inventor who has scorned magic since his mother died. He is sent to prison for murdering his wife, and the travelers enter the Emerald City.</p>
<p>The Wizard gives the Scarecrow a brain and the Tin Woodman a heart. He declares this the greatest of all his achievements and calls for a celebration. The Ball of All Nations is thrown, in which up to twelve songs are sung by various characters. The Wizard performs a basket trick in which King Pastoria is the mark. In the middle of the trick Pastoria claims his right to the throne and overthrows the Wizard. A great commotion breaks out, with the Wizard escaping in a hot air balloon. Dorothy, still longing for home, sets off with her companions to the castle of Glinda the Good Witch of the South.</p>
<h3><span id="Act_Three:_Glinda's_Palace"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_Three:_Glinda.27s_Palace">Act Three: Glinda's Palace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Act Three: Glinda's Palace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dorothy and her friends arrive at the palace and are welcomed. There are great celebrations, with Glinda promising to send Dorothy home. The whole cast rushes out from the wings and sings the finale.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception_and_script">Conception and script</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conception and script">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Hill_as_the_Cowardly_Lion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="342" data-file-width="242" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dc/Arthur_Hill_as_the_Cowardly_Lion.jpg/170px-Arthur_Hill_as_the_Cowardly_Lion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/dc/Arthur_Hill_as_the_Cowardly_Lion.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Hill_as_the_Cowardly_Lion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Arthur Hill as the Lion and Anna Laughlin as Dorothy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>L. Frank Baum decided to collaborate with his friend, composer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Tietjens" title="Paul Tietjens">Paul Tietjens</a>, and <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> illustrator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W._W._Denslow" title="W. W. Denslow">W. W. Denslow</a> as set and costume designer, to bring the book to the stage. They completed a script, score and designs in 1901, hewing fairly close to the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They submitted the package to the manager of the Chicago Grand Opera House, Fred R. Hamlin, who liked it and approached <a href="/wiki/Julian_P._Mitchell" title="Julian P. Mitchell">Julian P. Mitchell</a> to be director.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Mitchell did not like the script, criticizing its lack of spectacle, calling it too subdued and small-scale. However, he sent a wire to Hamlin with the message "Can see possibilities for extravaganza". When Mitchell accepted the project, he brought in new songwriters, cutting some of the original Tietjens numbers. He rewrote the script, together with <a href="/wiki/Glen_MacDonough" title="Glen MacDonough">Glen MacDonough</a>, introducing new characters and incidents, reducing the <a href="/wiki/Cowardly_Lion" title="Cowardly Lion">Cowardly Lion</a>'s role, deleting the appearance of the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> entirely, and substituting a cow for Toto as Dorothy's companion. Baum was anxious about this, but went along. He hoped Mitchell's experience in directing, as well as the casting of comedy team <a href="/wiki/Fred_Stone" title="Fred Stone">Fred Stone</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_C._Montgomery" title="David C. Montgomery">David C. Montgomery</a> as the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman, would make the show a hit. It turned out to be a roaring success, with 293 performances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_early_revivals">Production and early revivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production and early revivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In rewriting Baum's 1901 script, Mitchell hired MacDonough to add topical humor. Baum described MacDonough as a New York joke writer in a letter to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Record-Herald" title="The Chicago Record-Herald">The Chicago Record-Herald</a></i>, responding to criticism that the show "teemed with wild and woolly western puns and forced gags".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In a letter to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Tribune" title="The Chicago Tribune">The Chicago Tribune</a></i> published June 26, 1904, Baum decried rumors that he was "heartbroken and ashamed" with the final product of the musical: "I acknowledge that I was unwise enough to express myself as dissatisfied with the handling of my play on its first production ... few authors of successful books are ever fully satisfied with the dramatization of their work. They discern great gaps in the original story that are probably never noticed by playgoers." He admitted to protesting several innovations, but ultimately concluded: "The people will have what pleases them, and not what the author happens to favor, and I believe that one of the reasons why Julian Mitchell is regarded as a great producer is that he faithfully tries to serve the great mass of playgoers – and usually succeeds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Most of the original songs were written by Paul Tietjens on Baum's lyrics, except for three: "The Guardian of the Gate" (although it was attributed to Tietjens), which was cut after only a few performances, "The Different Ways of Making Love" (wooing) and "It Happens Every Day" were composed by <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_D._Mann" title="Nathaniel D. Mann">Nathaniel D. Mann</a>. Mann later wrote the score for Baum's 1908 film/theatrical presentation, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fairylogue_and_Radio-Plays" title="The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays">The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays</a></i>. Most of Baum's songs related to the story in some way, as in <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">operetta</a>, but as performed, the play was more like <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">vaudeville</a>, and new songs by other songwriters were frequently substituted. In fact, the first song interpolated into the musical was "The Traveler and the Pie", a major number for the Scarecrow. Baum and Tietjens had written it for a play called <i>The Octopus; or the Title Trust</i>, which was never produced and possibly never completed. The song stayed in the show. James O'Dea and Edward Hutchinson wrote one of the show's most celebrated songs, "Sammy", in which Tryxie Tryfle sings of a lost love before King Pastoria, though the only surviving recording of the piece was sung by a man (<a href="/wiki/Harry_Macdonough" title="Harry Macdonough">Harry Macdonough</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The witches are largely absent in this version; The Good Witch of the North appears, named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locasta" title="Locasta">Locasta</a>, and The Wicked Witch of the East is a special effect. Toto, Dorothy's dog, was replaced by a cow named Imogene. The Wicked Witch of the West does not appear, and Glinda the Good Witch of the South, who had appeared only in Act Three, was written out by Mitchell in 1903. His re-write of that act was set in the Borderland that divides Oz and Glinda's Domain, as Dorothy and her friends try to escape Pastoria.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>New characters include King <a href="/wiki/Pastoria" title="Pastoria">Pastoria</a> II, Oz's true king working as a Kansas motorman and his girlfriend, Trixie Tryfle, a waitress. There is also Cynthia Cynch, the Lady Lunatic, a prototype for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nimmie_Amee" title="Nimmie Amee">Nimmie Amee</a>, Nick (Niccolo) Chopper's girlfriend. Niccolo Chopper is renowned for his ability on the <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>, the subject of one of her songs, and he is shown playing a piccolo in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz_(1910_film)" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz (1910 film)">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i>, the first Oz film made without Baum's input, which was highly influenced by the popular play. The Wizard was presented as various ethnic <a href="/wiki/Stock_character" title="Stock character">stock character</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_stereotypes" title="Ethnic stereotypes">stereotypes</a>, depending upon who played him. He was assisted by Sir Wiley Gyle and General Riskitt. David L. Greene and <a href="/wiki/Dick_Martin_(artist)" title="Dick Martin (artist)">Dick Martin</a> erroneously captioned a picture of General Riskitt as "Sir Wiley Gyle" in <i>The Oz Scrapbook</i>, and Donald Abbott carried this mistake over into his illustrations for <i>How the Wizard Saved Oz</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The animals in the play, including the Cowardly Lion, did not speak, based on <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomime</a> tradition. Although the lion costume was realistic, far more so than <a href="/wiki/Bert_Lahr" title="Bert Lahr">Bert Lahr</a>'s in the MGM film, his main purpose was a bit of comic relief and scaring off the villains on occasion. His quest for courage is completely omitted, much as the other characters' quests are deemphasized in favor of various comic routines. Ultimately, though, their desire to seek the Wizard's aid gets them caught on the wrong side of the revolution, jailed and ultimately scheduled for execution. In a <a href="/wiki/Deus_ex_machina" title="Deus ex machina">deus ex machina</a>, another tornado arrives to sweep Dorothy home from the chopping block.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many new plot twists are virtually pointless. In addition to a kiss of protection, Dorothy gets three wishes, one of which is wasted on a triviality. The second is used to bring the Scarecrow to life, and the third is used so she can learn the song Sir Dashemoff Daily (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trouser_role" title="Trouser role">trouser role</a>) has written to his girlfriend, Carrie Barry. This song was written by Baum and Tietjens, but some programs credited the song to Glen MacDonough and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._Baldwin_Sloane" title="A. Baldwin Sloane">A. Baldwin Sloane</a> to make their connection to the play look greater.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Probably the biggest influence on the 1939 MGM film, aside from making the story into a musical (but not using the score created for the stage version), is the field of poppies sequence that ended Act I. In the novel, Baum imaginatively has a legion of field mice pull a cart with the Cowardly Lion out of the poppy field. This was deemed unfeasible (though the stage version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wiz" title="The Wiz">The Wiz</a></i> created a variation, with the mice as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_unit" title="Vice unit">vice cops</a>), and Baum, though he included it in the 1901 script, replaced the scene with that of the Snow Queen creating a storm that destroys the poppies, much as Glinda does in the 1939 movie.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This concluded Act I with an elaborate dance known as "Winter Jubilation", which James Patrick Doyle plays on synthesizers on the album, <i>Before the Rainbow: The Original Music of Oz</i>.</p>
<p>Because there were no cast albums in those days, theatre productions, including this production, often exceeded four hours in length because of multiple demands for encores, since many of the attendees knew they would never get to attend again. The most popular songs were often sung multiple times and this was often used to gauge whether a song should be retained or dropped. Two popular routines that were worked in include a sailing routine and a <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> routine, the latter parodying the level of violence in the sport, which had recently been lessened due to new regulations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The original cast included <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Laughlin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anna Laughlin (page does not exist)">Anna Laughlin</a> as Dorothy Gale, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Stone" title="Fred Stone">Fred Stone</a> as the Scarecrow, <a href="/wiki/David_C._Montgomery" title="David C. Montgomery">David C. Montgomery</a> as Nick Chopper (the Tin Man), Helen Byron as Cynthia Cynch, Bessie Wynn as Sir Dashemoff Daily, Gilbert Clayton as King Pastoria II, Bobby Gaylor as Oz, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hill_(stage_actor)" title="Arthur Hill (stage actor)">Arthur Hill</a> as the Cowardly Lion, Grace Kimball as Tryxie Tryffle, and Edwin J. Stone as Imogene the cow. The second theatre to house the production was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympia_Theatre_(New_York)" title="Olympia Theatre (New York)">New York Theatre</a>. It went on the road playing as far away as the Opera House in Hot Springs National Park, Arkansas in 1904. By 1905, the New York production had been moved to the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Music_(New_York_City)" title="Academy of Music (New York City)">Academy of Music</a> at 14th and Irving Place. Montgomery and Stone remained in the cast, but Dorothy was now played by Mona Desmond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Marion Stanley took over the role of Trixie Tryfle, <a href="/wiki/George_B._Field" title="George B. Field">George B. Field</a> played Sir Wiley Gyle, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_E._Mitchell" title="Charles E. Mitchell">Charles E. Mitchell</a> became the Wizard. The Snow Queen was played by Bert Dean <i>en travesti</i>.</p>
<p>The musical was revived as late as 1934, with <a href="/wiki/Charles_H._Pinkham" title="Charles H. Pinkham">Charles H. Pinkham</a> in the role of the Scarecrow.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> critic described the show as "the Darling of <a href="/wiki/David_Belasco" title="David Belasco">Mr. Belasco</a>'s Gods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Leone Langdon-Key loved the scenery, but found Baum's script commonplace, commenting that many lines start with, "Well, wouldn't that..." and deplored Tietjens's "fondness for a lack of contrast and rhythms. She also claims that the story of Pastoria trying to regain the lost throne from the Wizard was "as readers of the story remember".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required.">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_Boris_Vladimirovich_of_Russia" title="Grand Duke Boris Vladimirovich of Russia">Grand Duke Boris Vladimirovich of Russia</a> gained considerable notoriety by drinking champagne from the satin slipper of one of the chorus girls during a 1902 trip to Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel">Sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The success of the play led Baum to write <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i> after four years of demand for a sequel to the novel. He dedicated the book to Montgomery and Stone, and made the roles of the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman prominent, with the roles of Dorothy and the Cowardly Lion reduced to a reminiscence. After the team balked at leaving <i>Wizard</i> for a sequel, Baum wrote the stage musical, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Woggle-Bug_(musical)" title="The Woggle-Bug (musical)">The Woggle-Bug</a></i>, eliminating the Tin Woodman, replacing the Scarecrow with Regent Sir Richard Spud, replacing Glinda with Maetta from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magical_Monarch_of_Mo" title="The Magical Monarch of Mo">The Magical Monarch of Mo</a></i> and renaming the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_City" title="Emerald City">Emerald City</a> the "City of Jewels," though Oz is mentioned several times. The first appearance of the title character was moved from halfway through the novel to the opening scene, and his mentor, Professor Knowitall, name shortened to Professor Knowitt, was raised to the level of romantic lead with a girlfriend named Prissy Pring, a Captain in General <a href="/wiki/Jinjur" title="Jinjur">Jinjur</a>'s Army of Revolt. Jack Pumpkinhead and The Woggle-Bug became a comic team analogous to the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman. The play was performed at the <a href="/wiki/Garrick_Theater_(Chicago)" title="Garrick Theater (Chicago)">Garrick Theater</a> in Chicago and opened to reviews panning Baum's script and praising the score by <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Chapin" title="Frederic Chapin">Frederic Chapin</a>. No songs were interpolated (although two were derived from an earlier source and erroneously credited to Baum), but the general consensus was that the play was a cash-in or rip-off of <i>The Wizard of Oz</i> rather than a sequel.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-World_War_II_revivals">Post-World War II revivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-World War II revivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The musical was performed in a concert version in New York City in May 1982 by the New Amsterdam Theatre Company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was revived in <a href="/wiki/Tarpon_Springs,_Florida" title="Tarpon Springs, Florida">Tarpon Springs, Florida</a> by the New Century Opera Company in 1998 and 2006, and the company recorded the Baum/Tietjens music on CD with piano accompaniment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Hungry_Tiger_Press" title="Hungry Tiger Press">Hungry Tiger Press</a> published a two-CD set of vintage recordings related to the musical in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Canton Comic Opera Company, a community theatre company in <a href="/wiki/Canton,_Ohio" title="Canton, Ohio">Canton, Ohio</a>, performed a "restored" version in 2010. They believe that their production was the first in over 80 years with full orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Show_tour">Show tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Show tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The show toured from 1903 to 1909. It ran on Broadway from January to October 1903, and again from March 1904 to October 1905. It was released for stock and regional shows in 1911.</p>
