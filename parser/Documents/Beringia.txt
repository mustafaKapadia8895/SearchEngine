<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Beringia</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Beringia</b> is defined today as the land and maritime area bounded on the west by the <a href="/wiki/Lena_River" title="Lena River">Lena River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>; on the east by the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>; on the north by 72 degrees north latitude in the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Sea" title="Chukchi Sea">Chukchi Sea</a>; and on the south by the tip of the <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shared_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-shared-1">[1]</a></sup> It includes the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Sea" title="Chukchi Sea">Chukchi Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Peninsula" title="Chukchi Peninsula">Chukchi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka Peninsulas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>The area includes land lying on the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a> and Siberian land east of the <a href="/wiki/Chersky_Range" title="Chersky Range">Chersky Range</a>. Historically, it formed a <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridge</a> that was up to 1,000 kilometres (620 miles) wide at its greatest extent and which covered an area as large as <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> together,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> totaling approximately 1,600,000 square kilometres (620,000 square miles). Today, the only land that is visible from the central part of the Bering land bridge are the <a href="/wiki/Diomede_Islands" title="Diomede Islands">Diomede Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pribilof_Islands" title="Pribilof Islands">Pribilof Islands</a> of St. Paul and St. George, <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_Island" title="St. Lawrence Island">St. Lawrence Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/King_Island_(Alaska)" title="King Island (Alaska)">King Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shared_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-shared-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>Beringia</i> was coined by the Swedish botanist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hult%C3%A9n" title="Eric Hultén">Eric Hultén</a> in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoffeckerElias2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoffeckerElias2007-3">[3]</a></sup> During the ice ages, Beringia, like most of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and all of <a href="/wiki/North_China" title="North China">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a>, was not <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciated</a> because <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">snowfall was very light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voous1973_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voous1973-4">[4]</a></sup> It was a grassland <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>, including the land bridge, that stretched for hundreds of kilometres into the continents on either side.</p>
<p>It is believed that a small human population of at most a few thousand arrived in Beringia from eastern Siberia during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> before expanding into the <a href="/wiki/Settlement_of_the_Americas" title="Settlement of the Americas">settlement of the Americas</a> sometime after 16,500 years ago. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Glacial_Maximum" title="Late Glacial Maximum">Late Glacial Maximum</a> as the American glaciers blocking the way southward melted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goebel_2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goebel_2008-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fagundes_2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fagundes_2008-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tamm_2007_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tamm_2007-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but before the bridge was covered by the sea about 11,000 years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated60_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated60-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jakobsson2017_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-jakobsson2017-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before European colonization, Beringia was inhabited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yupik peoples</a> on both sides of the straits. This culture remains in the region today along with others. In 2012, the governments of Russia and the United States announced a plan to formally establish "a transboundary area of shared Beringian heritage". Among other things this agreement would establish close ties between the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Land_Bridge_National_Preserve" title="Bering Land Bridge National Preserve">Bering Land Bridge National Preserve</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Krusenstern_National_Monument" title="Cape Krusenstern National Monument">Cape Krusenstern National Monument</a> in the United States and the planned <a href="/wiki/Beringia_National_Park" title="Beringia National Park">Beringia National Park</a> in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The remains of <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> mammals that had been discovered on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleutians" title="Aleutians">Aleutians</a> and islands in the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a> at the close of the nineteenth century indicated that a past land connection might lie beneath the shallow waters between <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Chukotka Autonomous Okrug">Chukotka</a>. The underlying mechanism was first thought to be tectonics, but by 1930 changes in the icemass balance, leading to global sea-level fluctuations, was viewed as the cause of the Bering Land Bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffecker2016_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoffecker2016-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hult%C3%A9n" title="Eric Hultén">Eric Hultén</a> proposed that around the Aleutians and the Bering Strait region were tundra plants that had originally dispersed from a now-submerged plain between Alaska and Chukotka, which he named Beringia after <a href="/wiki/Vitus_Bering" title="Vitus Bering">Vitus Bering</a> who had sailed into the strait in 1728.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffecker2016_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-hoffecker2016-14">[14]</a></sup> The American arctic geologist David Hopkins redefined Beringia to include portions of Alaska and Northeast Asia. Beringia was later regarded as extending from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verkhoyansk_Mountains" title="Verkhoyansk Mountains">Verkhoyansk Mountains</a> in the west to the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> in the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffecker2016_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-hoffecker2016-14">[14]</a></sup> The distribution of plants in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Erythranthe" title="Erythranthe">Erythranthe</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinus" title="Pinus">Pinus</a></i> are good examples of this as genera members are found in Asia and the Americas with a high degree of similarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sinoalba_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sinoalba-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brubaker_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-brubaker-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Pleistocene epoch, global cooling led periodically to the expansion of glaciers and lowering of sea levels. This created land connections in various regions around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Today, the average water depth of the Bering Strait is 40–50 m (130–160 ft), therefore the land bridge opened when the sea level dropped more than 50 m (160 ft) below the current level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A reconstruction of the sea-level history of the region indicated that a seaway existed from <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a><span style="white-space:nowrap;">  135,000</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">  70,000</span> BP, a land bridge from <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">  70,000</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">  60,000</span> BP, intermittent connection from c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">  60,000</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">  30,000</span> BP, a land bridge from c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">  30,000</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">  11,000</span> BP, followed by a Holocene sea-level rise that reopened the strait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">Post-glacial rebound</a> has continued to raise some sections of coast.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, enough of the earth's water became frozen in the great <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> covering <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to cause a drop in <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea levels</a>. For thousands of years the sea floors of many <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> shallow seas were exposed, including those of the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Sea" title="Chukchi Sea">Chukchi Sea</a> to the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a> to the south. Other <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridges</a> around the world have emerged and disappeared in the same way. Around 14,000 years ago, mainland <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> was linked to both <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> became an extension of continental <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> via the dry beds of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, and the dry bed of the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> linked <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beringian_refugium">Beringian refugium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beringia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beringian refugium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="688" data-file-width="1024" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png/300px-Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png/450px-Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png/600px-Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lgm_ccsm4_beringia_annual_precipitation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Beringia precipitation 22,000 years ago</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_steppe" title="Mammoth steppe">Mammoth steppe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asa_Gray#The_.22Asa_Gray_disjunction.22" title="Asa Gray">Asa_Gray § The "Asa Gray disjunction"</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, commonly referred to as the "Ice Age", spanned 125,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc2007_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc2007-23">[23]</a></sup>–14,500<span class="nowrap"> </span>YBP<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark2009_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-clark2009-24">[24]</a></sup> and was the most recent <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial period</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">current ice age</a>, which occurred during the last years of the Pleistocene era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc2007_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc2007-23">[23]</a></sup> The Ice Age reached its peak during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> began advancing from 33,000<span class="nowrap"> </span>YBP and reached their maximum limits 26,500<span class="nowrap"> </span>YBP. Deglaciation commenced in the Northern Hemisphere approximately 19,000<span class="nowrap"> </span>YBP and in Antarctica approximately 14,500 years<span class="nowrap"> </span>YBP, which is consistent with evidence that glacial meltwater was the primary source for an abrupt rise in sea level 14,500<span class="nowrap"> </span>YBP<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark2009_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-clark2009-24">[24]</a></sup> and the bridge was finally inundated around 11,000 YBP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jakobsson2017_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-jakobsson2017-11">[11]</a></sup> The fossil evidence from many continents points to the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_extinction_event" title="Quaternary extinction event">extinction</a> of large animals, termed <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene_megafauna" title="Pleistocene megafauna">Pleistocene megafauna</a>, near the end of the last glaciation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elias2016_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-elias2016-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Ice Age a vast, cold and dry <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_steppe" title="Mammoth steppe">Mammoth steppe</a> stretched from the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_in_the_Arctic_Ocean" title="List of islands in the Arctic Ocean">arctic islands</a> southwards to China, and from Spain eastwards across Eurasia and over the Bering land bridge into Alaska and the Yukon where it was blocked by the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_glaciation" title="Wisconsin glaciation">Wisconsin glaciation</a>. The land bridge existed because sea-levels were lower because more of the planet's water than today was locked up in glaciers. Therefore, the flora and fauna of Beringia were more related to those of Eurasia rather than North America. Beringia received more moisture and intermittent maritime cloud cover from the north Pacific Ocean than the rest of the Mammoth steppe, including the dry environments on either side of it. This moisture supported a shrub-tundra habitat that provided a <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">ecological refugium</a> for plants and animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elias2008_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-elias2008-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guthrie2001_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-guthrie2001-27">[27]</a></sup> In East Beringia 35,000 YBP, the northern arctic areas experienced temperatures 1.5 C degrees warmer than today but the southern sub-Arctic regions were 2 C degrees cooler. During the LGM 22,000 YBP the average summer temperature was 3–5 C degrees cooler than today, with variations of 2.9 C degrees cooler on the <a href="/wiki/Seward_Peninsula" title="Seward Peninsula">Seward Peninsula</a> to 7.5 C cooler in the Yukon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elias2007_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-elias2007-28">[28]</a></sup> In the driest and coldest periods of the Late Pleistocene, and possibly during the entire Pleistocene, moisture occurred along a north-south gradient with the south receiving the most cloud cover and moisture due to the air-flow from the North Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guthrie2001_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-guthrie2001-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Late Pleistocene, Beringia was a mosaic of biological communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffecker2007_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoffecker2007-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elias2008_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-elias2008-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brigham2004_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-brigham2004-30">[30]</a></sup> Commencing from <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 57,000</span> BP (<a href="/wiki/Marine_isotope_stage" title="Marine isotope stage">MIS</a> 3), steppe–tundra vegetation dominated large parts of Beringia with a rich diversity of grasses and herbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffecker2007_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-hoffecker2007-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elias2008_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-elias2008-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sher2005_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-sher2005-31">[31]</a></sup> There were patches of shrub tundra with isolated refugia of larch (<i>Larix</i>) and spruce (<i>Picea</i>) forests with birch (<i>Betula</i>) and alder (<i>Alnus</i>) trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffecker2007_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-hoffecker2007-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brigham2004_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-brigham2004-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sher2005_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-sher2005-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It has been proposed that the largest and most diverse megafaunal community residing in Beringia at this time could only have been sustained in a highly diverse and productive environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Analysis at Chukotka on the Siberian edge of the land bridge indicated that from c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 57,000</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 15,000</span> BP (MIS 3 to MIS 2) the environment was wetter and colder than the steppe–tundra to the east and west, with warming in parts of Beringia from c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 15,000</span> BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> These changes provided the most likely explanation for mammal migrations after c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 15,000</span> BP, as the warming provided increased forage for browsers and mixed feeders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Beringia did not block the movement of most dry steppe-adapted large species such as saiga antelope, woolly mammoth, and caballid horses. However, from the west the woolly rhino went no further east than the Anadyr River, and from the east North American camels, the American kiang-like equids, the short-faced bear, bonnet-horned muskoxen, and badger did not travel west. At the beginning of the Holocene, some <a href="/wiki/Mesic_habitat" title="Mesic habitat">mesic habitat</a>-adapted species left the refugium and spread westward into what had become tundra-vegetated northern Asia and eastward into northern North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guthrie2001_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-guthrie2001-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The latest emergence of the land bridge was <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 70,000</span> years ago. However, from c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 24,000</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 13,000</span> BP the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurentide_ice_sheet" title="Laurentide ice sheet">Laurentide ice sheet</a> fused with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cordilleran_ice_sheet" title="Cordilleran ice sheet">Cordilleran ice sheet</a>, which blocked gene flow between Beringia (and Eurasia) and continental North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The Yukon corridor opened between the receding ice sheets c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 13,000</span> BP, and this once again allowed gene flow between Eurasia and continental North America until the land bridge was finally closed by rising sea levels c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 10,000</span> BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> During the Holocene, many mesic-adapted species left the refugium and spread eastward and westward, while at the same time the forest-adapted species spread with the forests up from the south. The arid adapted species were reduced to minor habitats or became extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guthrie2001_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-guthrie2001-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beringia constantly transformed its <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> as the changing climate affected the environment, determining which plants and animals were able to survive. The land mass could be a barrier as well as a bridge: during colder periods, glaciers advanced and precipitation levels dropped. During warmer intervals, clouds, rain and snow altered <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> and drainage patterns. <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossil</a> remains show that <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a> once grew beyond their northernmost range today, indicating that there were periods when the climate was warmer and wetter. The environmental conditions were not homogenous in Beringia. Recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotope</a> studies of <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">woolly mammoth</a> bone <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> demonstrate that western Beringia (<a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>) was colder and drier than eastern Beringia (<a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>), which was more ecologically diverse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SzpakP3_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-SzpakP3-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">Mastodons</a>, which depended on shrubs for food, were uncommon in the open dry <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> landscape characteristic of Beringia during the colder periods. In this tundra, <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoths</a> flourished instead.</p>
<p>The extinct pine species <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_matthewsii" title="Pinus matthewsii">Pinus matthewsii</a></i> has been described from Pliocene sediments in the Yukon areas of the refugium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKown2002_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKown2002-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_habitation">Human habitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beringia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Human habitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Settlement_of_the_Americas" title="Settlement of the Americas">Settlement of the Americas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="884" data-file-width="708" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg/220px-Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg/330px-Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg/440px-Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_gene_flow_in_and_out_of_Beringia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Genetic settlement of Beringia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Bering land bridge is a postulated route of <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">human migration</a> to the Americas from Asia about 20,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> An open corridor through the ice-covered North American Arctic was too barren to support human migrations before around 12,600 BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> A study has indicated that the genetic imprints of only 70 of all the individuals who settled and traveled the land bridge into North America are visible in modern descendants. This genetic bottleneck finding is an example of the <a href="/wiki/Founder_effect" title="Founder effect">founder effect</a> and does not imply that only 70 individuals crossed into North America at the time; rather, the genetic material of these individuals became amplified in North America following isolation from other Asian populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Seagoing coastal settlers may also have crossed much earlier, but there is no <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> on this point, and the coastal sites that would offer further information now lie submerged in up to a hundred metres of water offshore. Land animals migrated through Beringia as well, introducing to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> species that had evolved in Asia, like <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Proboscidea" title="Proboscidea">proboscideans</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_lion" title="American lion">American lions</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> into now-extinct <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> North American species. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Equidae" title="Equidae">equids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camelid" title="Camelid">camelids</a> that had evolved in North America (and later became extinct there) migrated into Asia as well at this time.</p>
<p>A study published in 2007 suggests that the Bering land bridge migration occurred 12,000 BP, that every human who migrated across the land bridge came from Eastern Siberia, and that every indigenous person directly descends from that same group of Eastern Siberian migrants. The authors note that a "[u]nique genetic variant widespread in natives across both continents suggests that the first humans in the Americas came in a single migration or multiple waves from a single source, not in waves of migrations from different sources".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_connections">Previous connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beringia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Previous connections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">Biogeographical</a> evidence demonstrates previous connections between North America and Asia. Similar <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaur</a> fossils occur both in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. For instance the dinosaur <i><a href="/wiki/Saurolophus" title="Saurolophus">Saurolophus</a></i> was found in both Mongolia and western North America. Relatives of <i><a href="/wiki/Troodon" title="Troodon">Troodon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Triceratops" title="Triceratops">Triceratops</a></i>, and even <i><a href="/wiki/Tyrannosaurus" title="Tyrannosaurus">Tyrannosaurus</a> rex</i> all came from Asia.</p>
<p>Fossils in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> demonstrate a migration of Asian mammals into North America around 55 million years ago. By 20 million years ago, evidence in North America shows a further interchange of mammalian species. Some, like the ancient <a href="/wiki/Saber-toothed_cat" title="Saber-toothed cat">saber-toothed cats</a>, have a recurring geographical range: Europe, Africa, Asia, and North America. The only way they could reach the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> was by the Bering land bridge. Had this bridge not existed at that time, the fauna of the world would be very different.</p>
<p>Researchers have started to use molecular <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetics</a> to trace the history of faunal exchange and diversification, through the genetic history of parasites and pathogens of North American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ungulates" title="Ungulates">ungulates</a>. An international Beringian Coevolution Project is collaborating to provide material to assess the pattern and timing of faunal exchange and the potential impact of past climatic events on differentiation.</p>
