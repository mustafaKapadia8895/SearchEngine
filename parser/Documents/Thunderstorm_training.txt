<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Training (meteorology)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <b>training</b> denotes repeated areas of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, typically associated with <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>, that move over the same region in a relatively short period of time. Training thunderstorms are capable of producing excessive <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a> totals, often causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_flooding" title="Flash flooding">flash flooding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-glossary-1">[1]</a></sup> The name <i>training</i> is derived from how a <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Railroad_car" title="Railroad car">cars</a> travel along a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_tracks" title="Rail tracks">track</a> (moving along a single path), without the track moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Training_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Showers and thunderstorms along thunderstorm trains usually develop in one area of stationary <a href="/wiki/Convective_instability" title="Convective instability">instability</a>, and are advanced along a single path by prevailing winds. Additional showers and storms can also develop when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">gust front</a> from a storm collides with warmer air outside of the storm. The same process repeats in the new storms, until overall conditions in the surrounding atmosphere become too stable for support of thunderstorm activity. Showers and storms can also develop along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationary_fronts" title="Stationary fronts">stationary fronts</a>, and winds move them down the front. The showers that often accompany thunderstorms are usually thunderstorms that are not completely developed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Training_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A series of storms continually moving over the same area, dumping heavy rains, can cause flash flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossary_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-glossary-1">[1]</a></sup> Each storm usually produces heavy rain, and after a significant amount of rain falls from the storms which have moved over the same area, flooding occurs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunderstorm_training">Thunderstorm training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Training_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thunderstorm training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><b>Thunderstorm training</b></i> is used to refer specifically to training occurring with thunderstorms. It forms when storms tend to backbuild. This type of training can quickly cause <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a>, especially if the thunderstorms are strong.</p>
