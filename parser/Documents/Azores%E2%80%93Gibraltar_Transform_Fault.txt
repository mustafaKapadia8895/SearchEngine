<p>The <b>Azores–Gibraltar Transform Fault</b> (<b>AGFZ</b>), also called a <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault zone</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Fracture_zone" title="Fracture zone">fracture zone</a>, is a major seismic fault in the Central <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> west of the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a>. It is the product of the complex interaction between the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Plate" title="Iberian Plate">Iberian</a> plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rich-etal_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-etal-1">[1]</a></sup> The AGFZ produced the large-magnitude <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">1755 Lisbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/1969_Portugal_earthquake" title="1969 Portugal earthquake">1969 Horseshoe</a> earthquakes and, consequently, a number of large tsunamis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartLori-intro_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MartLori-intro-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geologic_setting">Geologic setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azores%E2%80%93Gibraltar_Transform_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geologic setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forming the Atlantic segment of the boundary between the African and Eurasian plates, the AGFZ is largely dominated by compressional forces between these converging (3.8 to 5.6 mm/a (0.0047 to 0.0070 in/Ms)) plates, but it is subject of a dynamic tectonic regime also involving extension, <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">trancurrent faulting</a>, and compression. The <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> in the area is directly related to the <a href="/wiki/Opening_of_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="Opening of the North Atlantic Ocean">opening of the North Atlantic Ocean</a> and one of the oldest preserved on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartLori-intro_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MartLori-intro-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The western end of the AGFZ, the <a href="/wiki/Azores_Triple_Junction" title="Azores Triple Junction">Azores Triple Junction</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> (MAR), is where the North American, African, and Eurasian plates meet. Spreading in the MAR is faster south of the AGFZ than north of it, which results in a trancurrent movement along the AGFZ at about 4 mm/a (0.0050 in/Ms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rich-etal_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-etal-1">[1]</a></sup> The eastern segment of the fault is complex and characterised by a series of seamounts and ridges separating the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tores_Abyssal_Plain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tores Abyssal Plain (page does not exist)">Tores</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Horseshoe_Abyssal_Plain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Horseshoe Abyssal Plain (page does not exist)">Horseshoe</a> abyssal plains. The active compressional deformation in this segment is an extremely rare example of compression between two oceanic litospheres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rich-etal_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-etal-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plate_tectonics">Plate tectonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azores%E2%80%93Gibraltar_Transform_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plate tectonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Atlantic Ocean is surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Passive_margin" title="Passive margin">passive margins</a> with the exception of three subduction zones: the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles_Volcanic_Arc" title="Lesser Antilles Volcanic Arc">Lesser Antilles Arc</a> in the Caribbean, the <a href="/wiki/Scotia_Arc" title="Scotia Arc">Scotia Arc</a> in the South Atlantic, and the <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar_Arc" title="Gibraltar Arc">Gibraltar Arc</a> in the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. The Gibraltar Arc is propagating westward into the Atlantic over an east-dipping oceanic slab (one of the remainders of the <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Ocean</a>). This subduction/<a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">back-arc basin</a> system is developing in front of the Alboran Block (under the <a href="/wiki/Alboran_Sea" title="Alboran Sea">Alboran Sea</a>) at a rate faster than that of the Africa-Iberia convergence. Consequently, this area is a rare case of a passive margin slowly being transformed into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_margin" title="Active margin">active margin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duarte-intro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duarte-intro-3">[3]</a></sup> The extension of this subduction system, known as the "allochthonous unit of the Gulf of Cadiz" (AUGZ), marks the continuing propagation of the <a href="/wiki/Alpide_belt" title="Alpide belt">Alpide belt</a> into the Atlantic along the AGFZ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the context of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilson_Cycle" title="Wilson Cycle">Wilson Cycle</a>, this suggests that the beginning of the closure of the Atlantic is taking place in front of the three Atlantic subduction zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duarte-intro_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Duarte-intro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
