<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mesoscale convective vortex</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

A mesoscale convective vortex near <a href="/wiki/Tsushima_Island" title="Tsushima Island">Tsushima Island</a>, revealing a distinct <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature briefly.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>mesoscale convective vortex (MCV)</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure center</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesolow" title="Mesolow">mesolow</a>) within an <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_system" title="Mesoscale convective system">mesoscale convective system</a> (MCS) that pulls winds into a circling pattern, or vortex. With a core only 30 to 60 mi (48 to 97 km) wide and 1 to 3 mi (1.6 to 4.8 km) deep, an MCV is often overlooked in standard <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface observations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSL_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSL-1">[1]</a></sup> They have been most often been detected on <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">radar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellite</a>, particularly with the higher resolution and sensitivity of <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">WSR-88D</a>, but with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Mesonet" title="Mesonet">mesonets</a>, these <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> features can also be detected in <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface analysis</a>. Yet an MCV can take on a life of its own, persisting for more than 12 hours after its parent MCS has dissipated. This orphaned MCV will sometimes then become the seed of the next thunderstorm outbreak. Their remnants will often lead to an "agitated area" of increased cumulus activity that can eventually become an area of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> formation and associated low-level <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">boundaries</a> left behind can themselves cause <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">convergence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> that can increase the level of organization and intensity of any storms that do form. An MCV that moves into tropical waters, such as the Gulf of Mexico, can serve as the nucleus for a tropical storm or hurricane. MCVs, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesovortex" title="Mesovortex">mesovortices</a>, often cause an intensification of convective <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a> winds and can lead to <a href="/wiki/Tornadogenesis" title="Tornadogenesis">tornadogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSL_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSSL-1">[1]</a></sup> One form of MCV is the "comma head" of a <a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">line echo wave pattern</a> (LEWP).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="May_2009_Mid-Mississippi_Valley_MCV">May 2009 Mid-Mississippi Valley MCV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_vortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: May 2009 Mid-Mississippi Valley MCV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/May_2009_Southern_Midwest_derecho" title="May 2009 Southern Midwest derecho">May 2009 Southern Midwest derecho</a></div>
<p>On Friday, May 8, 2009, a major MCV controversially dubbed an "inland hurricane" by local media moved through southern Missouri, southern Illinois, western Kentucky, and southwestern Indiana, killing at least 6 and injuring dozens more. Damage estimates were in the hundreds of millions. Top speeds of 106 mph (171 km/h) were reported in <a href="/wiki/Carbondale,_Illinois" title="Carbondale, Illinois">Carbondale, Illinois</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
