<p>The <b>Gulf menhaden</b> (<i>Brevoortia patronus</i>) is a small marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_feeding" title="Filter feeding">filter-feeding</a> fish belonging to the family <a href="/wiki/Clupeidae" title="Clupeidae">Clupeidae</a>. The range of Gulf menhaden encompasses the entirety of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> nearshore waters, with the exception of the extreme eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatan" title="Yucatan">Yucatan</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> Evidence from morphology <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahlberg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dahlberg-3">[3]</a></sup> and DNA analyses <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> suggest that the Gulf menhaden is the Gulf of Mexico complement to the Atlantic menhaden (<i>Brevoortia tyrannus</i>). Both species support large commercial reduction fisheries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with Gulf menhaden supporting the second largest fishery, by weight, in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pritchard_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pritchard-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_distribution">Range and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Range and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gulf menhaden occurs throughout the Gulf of Mexico, but its distribution is patchy. The center of distribution of the species appears to be the northwest/northcentral Gulf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> particularly in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> where populations are very large and numerous. In the southern Gulf of Mexico the range of Gulf menhaden overlaps that of the closely related finescale menhaden (<i>Brevoortia gunteri</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and there is evidence for resource partitioning (a process whereby closely related species occurring in close proximity results in subtle differences in ecological niches) between these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castillo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-8">[8]</a></sup> In the eastern Gulf, the range of Gulf menhaden overlaps that of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowfin_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yellowfin menhaden (page does not exist)">yellowfin menhaden</a> (<i>Brevoortia smithi</i>), and hybridization between these species has been demonstrated using morphological <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dahlberg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dahlberg-3">[3]</a></sup> and DNA evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AndersonAndKarel_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AndersonAndKarel-9">[9]</a></sup> Gulf menhaden also may have a presence on the southern Atlantic coast of Florida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AndersonAndKarel_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-AndersonAndKarel-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> although this finding is based primarily upon DNA evidence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_biology">General biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gulf menhaden are commonly 8 inches in length but can reach 12 inches. Gulf menhaden are a dull silver with a greenish back. Like their Atlantic counterpart, Gulf menhaden have a prominent black spot found behind the gill cover followed by a row of smaller spots <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gulf menhaden are filter feeders, meaning that they collect food by filtering water through modifications of the branchial apparatus (gill or <a href="/wiki/Branchial_arch" title="Branchial arch">branchial arches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gill_raker" title="Gill raker">gill rakers</a>). Like <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_menhaden" title="Atlantic menhaden">Atlantic menhaden</a>, Gulf menhaden’s diet depends on the size of their gill rakers, which change as menhaden age. Larval gulf menhaden feed primary on zooplankton because the rakers are not well developed. The juveniles, which generally correspond to when they are under the age of 1, Gulf menhaden feed more on <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>. As they age and their gill rakers fully developed, menhaden shift their diet to primarily consume <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menhaden.gsmfc.org_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-menhaden.gsmfc.org-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spawning occurs offshore in winter (October–March).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> Eggs and larvae are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> and are carried into estuarine nursery areas via prevailing currents. As a result, migration at this stage can be lengthy, and populations of Gulf menhaden throughout the Gulf of Mexico are generally thought to comprise a single genetic stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_fishery">Commercial fishery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial fishery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gulf menhaden fishery is one of the largest in the United States. In 2013, the fishery supported four of the nation’s top ten ports by volume of landings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Gulf menhaden are harvested primarily for fish meal and fish oil based products. A much smaller number of menhaden are caught for use as bait. In addition to being one of the largest fisheries in the US, the Gulf menhaden fishery has also been recognized internationally for its sustainability.</p>
<p>The fishery’s sustainability has also been certified by independent organizations. <a href="/wiki/Friend_of_the_Sea" title="Friend of the Sea">Friend of the Sea</a>, an international seafood sustainability certification program, has recognized both the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_menhaden" title="Atlantic menhaden">Atlantic menhaden</a> and Gulf menhaden fisheries as sustainable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This is both due to the healthy status of the stock as well as the fishery’s low levels of bycatch, which it achieves with the use of purse seine nets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gulf menhaden are managed by an interstate compact called the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_States_Marine_Fisheries_Commission" title="Gulf States Marine Fisheries Commission">Gulf States Marine Fisheries Commission</a> (GSMFC). According to the most recent 2013 stock assessment by the GSMFC, Gulf menhaden are “neither overfished nor experiencing overfishing.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> According to the GSMFC, “the Gulf menhaden fishery is probably the most closely monitored and managed fishery in the Gulf of Mexico.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menhaden.gsmfc.org_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-menhaden.gsmfc.org-12">[12]</a></sup> An example of the fishery’s monitoring is the Gulf of Mexico purse seine fishery’s participation in NOAA’s Southeast Fisheries Observer Program since 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Specifically, the Menhaden Advisory Committee (MAC) is the GSMFC subcommittee that oversees menhaden management.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dead_zones">Dead zones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_menhaden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dead zones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the GSMFC, “Menhaden do not have the capacity to reduce unwanted phytoplankton blooms that arise from manmade sources, primarily because they eat mostly zooplankton. In addition, menhaden excrete large amounts of ammonia (a nitrogenous product), contributing to an already high nitrogen load.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menhaden.gsmfc.org_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-menhaden.gsmfc.org-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition, the commercial menhaden fishery only targets adult menhaden, which consume zooplankton, not juvenile menhaden, which do consume phytoplankton <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
