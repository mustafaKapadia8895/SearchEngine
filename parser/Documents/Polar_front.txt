<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Polar front</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png"><img alt="AtmosphCirc2.png" data-file-height="507" data-file-width="576" height="352" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/400px-AtmosphCirc2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png 1.5x" width="400"/></a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, the <b>polar front</b> is the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation#Polar_cell" title="Atmospheric circulation">polar cell</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation#Ferrel_cell" title="Atmospheric circulation">Ferrel cell</a> around the 60Â° latitude in each <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hemisphere" title="wikt:hemisphere">hemisphere</a>. At this boundary a sharp <a href="/wiki/Temperature_gradient" title="Temperature gradient">gradient in temperature</a> occurs between these two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_masses" title="Air masses">air masses</a>, each at very different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperatures" title="Temperatures">temperatures</a>.</p>
<p>The polar front arises as a result of cold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar</a> air meeting warm <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> air. It is a <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary front</a> as the air masses are not moving against each other. Off the coast of eastern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, there is a sharp <a href="/wiki/Temperature_gradient" title="Temperature gradient">temperature gradient</a> between the snow-covered land and the warm offshore currents.</p>
<p>The polar front theory says that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-latitude_cyclone" title="Mid-latitude cyclone">mid-latitude cyclones</a> form on boundaries between warm and cold air. In winter, the polar front shifts towards the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>, whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_systems" title="High pressure systems">high pressure systems</a> dominate more in the <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>.</p>
