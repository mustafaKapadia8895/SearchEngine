<p><b>Gioachino Antonio Rossini</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[dʒoaˈkiːno anˈtɔːnjo rosˈsiːni]</a></span>; 29 February 1792 – 13 November 1868) was an Italian <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a> who wrote 39 <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, as well as some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_music" title="Sacred music">sacred music</a>, songs, <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber music</a> and piano pieces.</p>
<p>A precocious composer of operas, he made his full debut at the age of eighteen (with <i><a href="/wiki/La_cambiale_di_matrimonio" title="La cambiale di matrimonio">La cambiale di matrimonio</a></i>). His best-known operas include the Italian comedies <i>Il barbiere di Siviglia</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Barber_of_Seville" title="The Barber of Seville">The Barber of Seville</a></i>), <i>L'italiana in Algeri</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/L%27italiana_in_Algeri" title="L'italiana in Algeri">The Italian Girl in Algiers</a></i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/La_Cenerentola" title="La Cenerentola">La Cenerentola</a></i> (<i>Cinderella</i>). He also wrote a string of serious operas in Italian, including works such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tancredi" title="Tancredi">Tancredi</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Otello_(Rossini)" title="Otello (Rossini)">Otello</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Semiramide" title="Semiramide">Semiramide</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Opera_semiseria" title="Opera semiseria">semi-serious opera</a> <i>La Gazza Ladra</i> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Thieving_Magpie" title="The Thieving Magpie">The Thieving Magpie</a></i>) has one of Rossini's most celebrated <a href="/wiki/Overture" title="Overture">overtures</a>. After moving to Paris in 1824, he eventually started to write in French. His last opera, the epic <i><a href="/wiki/William_Tell_(opera)" title="William Tell (opera)">Guillaume Tell</a></i> (<i>William Tell</i>), replete with <a href="/wiki/William_Tell_Overture" title="William Tell Overture">its iconic overture</a>, helped usher in <a href="/wiki/Grand_opera" title="Grand opera">grand opera</a> in France.</p>
<p>After composing thirty-nine significant operas in nineteen years Rossini retired from the theatre in 1829. Later, he was affected by both physical and mental illnesses and for decades wrote relatively little apart from <a href="/wiki/Stabat_Mater_(Rossini)" title="Stabat Mater (Rossini)">a setting of the <i>Stabat Mater</i></a>. A return to Paris from Italy in 1855 was followed by better health and the provision of exclusive musical and culinary soirées. During these, he presented <a href="/wiki/Salon_music" title="Salon music">salon music</a> in the form of songs, piano pieces and small chamber ensembles that he called <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ch%C3%A9s_de_vieillesse" title="Péchés de vieillesse">"Sins of Old Age"</a>. He considered the last of these "Sins" to be the unusually scored <i><a href="/wiki/Petite_messe_solennelle" title="Petite messe solennelle">Petite messe solennelle</a></i> (Little solemn mass) that he wrote in 1863.</p>
<p>Rossini had been the most popular opera composer in history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fisher_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-3">[3]</a></sup> and he was one of the most renowned public figures of his time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A rapid and prolific composer, he was quoted as joking, "Give me the laundress' bill and I will even set that to music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A tendency for inspired, song-like melodies is evident throughout his scores, earning him the nickname "The Italian <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGorlin2013119_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGorlin2013119-6">[6]</a></sup> Use of an exciting buildup of orchestral sound over a <a href="/wiki/Ostinato" title="Ostinato">repeated phrase</a>—commonly known as a "Rossini <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crescendo" title="Crescendo">crescendo</a>"—also prompted the nickname "Signor Crescendo".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1132" data-file-width="865" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg/170px-Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg/255px-Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg/340px-Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pesaro-Gioacchino_Rossini.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Portrait of Rossini as a young man</div>
</div>

<p>Gioachino Antonio Rossini was born into a family of musicians in <a href="/wiki/Pesaro" title="Pesaro">Pesaro</a>, a town on the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic</a> coast of Italy that was then part of the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a>. His father, Giuseppe, was a horn player and inspector of slaughterhouses. His mother, Anna, was a <a href="/wiki/Singing" title="Singing">singer</a> and a baker's daughter.</p>
<p>Rossini's father was sympathetic to the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> and welcomed <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s troops when they arrived in northern Italy. When <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> restored the old regime, Rossini's father was sent to prison in 1799, where he remained until June 1800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Rossini's mother took him to <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>, making a living as leading singer at various theatres of the <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a> region. Her husband would ultimately join her in Bologna. During this time, Rossini was frequently left in the care of his aging grandmother, who had difficulty supervising the boy.</p>
<p>He remained at Bologna in the care of a pork butcher while his father played the horn in the orchestras of the theatres at which his wife sang. The boy had three years of instruction in the playing of the <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> from Giuseppe Prinetti, originally from <a href="/wiki/Novara" title="Novara">Novara</a>, who played the scale with two fingers only; Prinetti also owned a business selling beer and had a propensity to fall asleep while standing. These qualities made him a subject for ridicule in the eyes of the young Rossini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>He was eventually taken from Prinetti and apprenticed to a blacksmith. In Angelo Tesei, he found a congenial music master, and learned to sight-read, play accompaniments on the <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> and sing well enough to take solo parts in the church when he was ten years of age. Important products of this period are six <i>sonate a quattro</i>, or string sonatas, composed in three days, unusually scored for two violins, cello and double bass. The original scores, dating from 1804 when the composer was twelve, were found in the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> in Washington D.C. Often transcribed for string orchestra, these sonatas reveal the young composer's affinity for <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>, already showing signs of operatic tendencies, punctuated by frequent rhythmic changes and dominated by clear, songlike melodies.</p>
<p>In 1805, he appeared at the theatre of the Commune in <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_Paer" title="Ferdinando Paer">Ferdinando Paer</a>'s <i>Camilla</i>, his only public appearance as a singer. He was also a capable horn player, treading in the footsteps of his father. Around this time, he composed individual numbers to a <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> by Vincenza Mombelli called <i><a href="/wiki/Demetrio_e_Polibio" title="Demetrio e Polibio">Demetrio e Polibio</a></i>, which was handed to the boy in pieces. Though it was Rossini's first opera, written when he was thirteen or fourteen, the work was not staged until the composer was twenty years old, premiering as his sixth official opera.</p>
<p>In 1806, Rossini became a <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> student under Cavedagni at the Conservatorio di Bologna. The following year he was admitted to the counterpoint class of Padre <a href="/wiki/Stanislao_Mattei" title="Stanislao Mattei">Stanislao Mattei</a> (1750–1825). He learned to play the cello with ease, but the pedantic severity of Mattei's views on counterpoint served only to drive the young composer's views toward a freer school of composition. His insight into orchestral resources is generally ascribed not to the strict compositional rules that he learned from Mattei, but to knowledge gained independently while scoring the quartets and symphonies of Haydn and Mozart. At Bologna, he was known as "il Tedeschino" ("the Little German") on account of his devotion to Mozart.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_as_a_composer">Career as a composer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career as a composer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Early_years:_Demetrio_e_Polibio_(1812)_to_Torvaldo_e_Dorliska_(1815)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years:_Demetrio_e_Polibio_.281812.29_to_Torvaldo_e_Dorliska_.281815.29">Early years: <i>Demetrio e Polibio</i> (1812) to <i>Torvaldo e Dorliska</i> (1815)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early years: Demetrio e Polibio (1812) to Torvaldo e Dorliska (1815)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through the friendly interposition of the Marquis Cavalli, his first opera, <i><a href="/wiki/La_cambiale_di_matrimonio" title="La cambiale di matrimonio">La cambiale di matrimonio</a></i> (<i>The Marriage Contract</i>), was produced at Venice when he was a youth of 18 years. Two years before this he had already received the prize at the Conservatorio of Bologna for his cantata <i>Il pianto d'Armonia sulla morte d'Orfeo</i>. Between 1810 and 1813 at Bologna, Rome, Venice and Milan, Rossini produced operas of varying success, most notably <i><a href="/wiki/La_pietra_del_paragone" title="La pietra del paragone">La pietra del paragone</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Il_signor_Bruschino" title="Il signor Bruschino">Il signor Bruschino</a></i>, with its brilliant and unique overture. In 1813, <i><a href="/wiki/Tancredi" title="Tancredi">Tancredi</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/L%27italiana_in_Algeri" title="L'italiana in Algeri">L'italiana in Algeri</a></i> were even bigger successes, and catapulted the 20-year-old composer to international fame.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> for <i>Tancredi</i> was an arrangement by <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Rossi" title="Gaetano Rossi">Gaetano Rossi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>'s tragedy <i>Tancrède</i>. Traces of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_Paer" title="Ferdinando Paer">Ferdinando Paer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Paisiello" title="Giovanni Paisiello">Giovanni Paisiello</a> were undeniably present in fragments of the music. But any critical feeling on the part of the public was drowned by appreciation of such melodies as "<i>Di tanti palpiti... Mi rivedrai, ti rivedrò</i>", which became so popular that the Italians would sing it in crowds at the law courts until called upon by the judge to desist.</p>

<p>By the age of 21, Rossini had established himself as the idol of the Italian opera public. He continued to write operas for Venice and Milan during the next few years, but their reception was tame and in some cases unsatisfactory after the success of <i>Tancredi.</i> In 1815 he retired to his home in Bologna, where <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Barbaia" title="Domenico Barbaia">Domenico Barbaia</a>, the impresario of the <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> theatre, contracted an agreement that made him musical director of the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_di_San_Carlo" title="Teatro di San Carlo">Teatro di San Carlo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_del_Fondo" title="Teatro del Fondo">Teatro del Fondo</a> at Naples. He would compose one opera a year for each. His payment was to be 200 ducats per month; he was also to receive a share from the gambling tables set in the theatre's "ridotto", amounting to about 1000 ducats per annum. This was an extraordinarily lucrative arrangement for any professional musician at that time.</p>
<p>He visited the Naples conservatory, and, although less than four years senior to <a href="/wiki/Saverio_Mercadante" title="Saverio Mercadante">Mercadante</a>, he said to the Director <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Antonio_Zingarelli" title="Niccolò Antonio Zingarelli">Niccolò Zingarelli</a>, "My compliments Maestro—your young pupil Mercadante begins where we finish."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RCAFluteConcertos_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-RCAFluteConcertos-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some older composers in Naples, notably Zingarelli and Paisiello, were inclined to intrigue against the success of the youthful composer, but all hostility was rendered futile by the enthusiasm that greeted the court performance of his <i><a href="/wiki/Elisabetta,_regina_d%27Inghilterra" title="Elisabetta, regina d'Inghilterra">Elisabetta, regina d'Inghilterra</a></i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Colbran" title="Isabella Colbran">Isabella Colbran</a>, who subsequently became the composer's wife, took a leading part. The libretto of this opera by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Schmidt" title="Giovanni Schmidt">Giovanni Schmidt</a> was in many of its incidents an anticipation of those presented to the world a few years later in Sir <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Kenilworth_(novel)" title="Kenilworth (novel)">Kenilworth</a></i>. The opera was the first in which Rossini wrote out the ornaments of the arias instead of leaving them to the fancy of the singers, and also the first in which the <a href="/wiki/Recitative#Secco" title="Recitative">recitativo <i>secco</i></a> was replaced by a recitative accompanied by a <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartet</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Resounding_success_of_The_Barber_of_Seville_(1816)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Resounding_success_of_The_Barber_of_Seville_.281816.29">Resounding success of <i>The Barber of Seville</i> (1816)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Resounding success of The Barber of Seville (1816)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Barber_of_Seville" title="The Barber of Seville">The Barber of Seville</a></div>
<p>Rossini's most famous opera was produced on 20 February 1816, at the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Argentina" title="Teatro Argentina">Teatro Argentina</a> in Rome. The libretto, a version of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Beaumarchais" title="Pierre Beaumarchais">Pierre Beaumarchais</a>' stage play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Barber_of_Seville_(play)" title="The Barber of Seville (play)">Le Barbier de Séville</a></i>, was newly written by <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Sterbini" title="Cesare Sterbini">Cesare Sterbini</a> and not the same as that already used by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Paisiello" title="Giovanni Paisiello">Giovanni Paisiello</a> in his own <i>Barbiere</i>, an opera which had enjoyed European popularity for more than a quarter of a century. Much is made of how quickly Rossini's opera was written, scholarship generally agreeing upon two or three weeks. Later in life, Rossini claimed to have written the opera in only twelve days. It was a colossal failure when it premiered as <i>Almaviva</i>; Paisiello's admirers were extremely indignant, sabotaging the production by whistling and shouting during the entire first act. However, not long after the second performance, the opera became so successful that the fame of Paisiello's opera was transferred to Rossini's, to which the title <i>The Barber of Seville</i> passed as an inalienable heritage.</p>

<p>Later in 1822, a 30-year-old Rossini succeeded in meeting <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>, who was then aged 51, deaf, cantankerous and in failing health. Communicating in writing, Beethoven noted: "Ah, Rossini. So you're the composer of <i>The Barber of Seville</i>. I congratulate you. It will be played as long as Italian opera exists. Never try to write anything else but <a href="/wiki/Opera_buffa" title="Opera buffa">opera buffa</a>; any other style would do violence to your nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fisher_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Between 1815 and 1823 Rossini produced 20 operas. Of these, <i><a href="/wiki/Otello_(Rossini)" title="Otello (Rossini)">Otello</a></i> formed the climax to his reform of serious opera (<i><a href="/wiki/Opera_seria" title="Opera seria">opera seria</a></i>), and offers a suggestive contrast with the treatment of the same subject at a similar point of artistic development by the composer <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Giuseppe Verdi</a>. In Rossini's time, the tragic ending was so distasteful to the public of Rome that it was necessary to invent a happy conclusion to <i>Otello</i>.</p>
<p>Conditions of stage production in 1817 are illustrated by Rossini's acceptance of the subject of <a href="/wiki/Cinderella" title="Cinderella">Cinderella</a> for a libretto only on the condition that the supernatural element should be omitted. The opera <i><a href="/wiki/La_Cenerentola" title="La Cenerentola">La Cenerentola</a></i> was as successful as <i>Barbiere</i>. The absence of a similar precaution in construction of his <i><a href="/wiki/Mos%C3%A8_in_Egitto" title="Mosè in Egitto">Mosè in Egitto</a></i> led to disaster in the scene depicting the passage of the Israelites through the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, when the defects in stage contrivance always raised a laugh, so that the composer was at length compelled to introduce the chorus "Dal tuo stellato soglio" to divert attention from the dividing waves.</p>
<p>In 1822, four years after the production of this work, Rossini married the renowned opera singer <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Colbran" title="Isabella Colbran">Isabella Colbran</a>. In the same year, he moved from Italy to Vienna, where his operas were the rage of the audiences. He directed his <i>Cenerentola</i> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, where <i><a href="/wiki/Zelmira" title="Zelmira">Zelmira</a></i> was also performed. After this he returned to Bologna, but an invitation from Metternich to the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Verona" title="Congress of Verona">Congress of Verona</a> to "assist in the general re-establishment of harmony" was too tempting to refuse, and he arrived at the Congress in time for its opening on 20 October 1822. Here he made friends with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Ren%C3%A9_de_Chateaubriand" title="François-René de Chateaubriand">Chateaubriand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Lieven" title="Dorothea Lieven">Dorothea Lieven</a>. The opera <a href="/wiki/Semiramide" title="Semiramide">Semiramide</a> was first performed at <a href="/wiki/La_Fenice" title="La Fenice">La Fenice</a> in Venice on 3 February 1823. It was Rossini's last Italian opera.</p>
<p>In 1823, at the suggestion of the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Theatre" title="Her Majesty's Theatre">King's Theatre</a>, London, he came to Britain, being much fêted on his way through Paris. He was given a generous welcome, which included an introduction to King <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV</a> and the receipt of £7000 (£570000 today) after a residence of five months. The next year, he became musical director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2tre-Italien_(1801%E2%80%931878)" title="Théâtre-Italien (1801–1878)">Théâtre des Italiens</a> in Paris at a salary of £800 (£63000 today) per annum. Rossini's popularity in Paris was so great that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_X" title="Charles X">Charles X</a> gave him a contract to write five new operas a year, and at the expiration of the contract, he was to receive a generous pension for life.</p>
<h3><span id="Composing_for_Paris:_Il_viaggio_a_Reims_(1825)_to_Guillaume_Tell_(1829)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Composing_for_Paris:_Il_viaggio_a_Reims_.281825.29_to_Guillaume_Tell_.281829.29">Composing for Paris: <i>Il viaggio a Reims</i> (1825) to <i>Guillaume Tell</i> (1829)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Composing for Paris: Il viaggio a Reims (1825) to Guillaume Tell (1829)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During his Paris years, Rossini created the comic operas <i><a href="/wiki/Le_comte_Ory" title="Le comte Ory">Le comte Ory</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/William_Tell_(opera)" title="William Tell (opera)">Guillaume Tell</a></i> (<i>William Tell</i>). The production of the latter in 1829 brought his career as a writer of opera to a close. <a href="/wiki/Fabio_Luisi" title="Fabio Luisi">Fabio Luisi</a> said that Rossini planned for <i>Guillaume Tell</i> to be his last opera even as he composed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He was thirty-eight years old and had already composed thirty-eight operas. <i>Guillaume Tell</i> was a political epic adapted from <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Schiller</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/William_Tell_(play)" title="William Tell (play)">Wilhelm Tell</a></i> (1804) about <a href="/wiki/William_Tell" title="William Tell">the 13th-century Swiss patriot</a> who rallied his country against the Austrians. The libretto was by <a href="/wiki/Victor-Joseph_%C3%89tienne_de_Jouy" title="Victor-Joseph Étienne de Jouy">Étienne Jouy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Bis" title="Hippolyte Bis">Hippolyte Bis</a>, but their version was revised by <a href="/wiki/Armand_Marrast" title="Armand Marrast">Armand Marrast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The music is remarkable for its freedom from the conventions discovered and utilized by Rossini in his earlier works, and marks a transitional stage in the history of opera, <a href="/wiki/William_Tell_Overture" title="William Tell Overture">the overture</a> serving as a model for romantic overtures throughout the 19th century. Though an excellent opera, it is rarely heard uncut today, as the original score runs more than four hours in performance. The overture is one of the most famous and frequently recorded works in the classical repertoire.</p>

<p>In 1829 he returned to Bologna. His mother had died in 1827, and he was anxious to be with his father. Arrangements for his subsequent return to Paris on a new agreement were temporarily upset by the abdication of <a href="/wiki/Charles_X_of_France" title="Charles X of France">Charles X</a> and the <a href="/wiki/July_Revolution" title="July Revolution">July Revolution</a> of 1830. Rossini, who had been considering the subject of <a href="/wiki/Faust" title="Faust">Faust</a> for a new opera, did return, however, to Paris in November of that year.</p>
<p>Six movements of his <i><a href="/wiki/Stabat_Mater_(Rossini)" title="Stabat Mater (Rossini)">Stabat Mater</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> were written in 1832 by Rossini himself and the other six by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Tadolini" title="Giovanni Tadolini">Giovanni Tadolini</a>, a good musician who was asked by Rossini to complete the work. However, Rossini composed the rest of the score in 1841. The success of the work bears comparison with his achievements in opera, but his comparative silence during the period from 1832 to his death in 1868 makes his biography appear almost like the narrative of two lives—the life of swift triumph and the long life of seclusion, of which biographers give us pictures in stories of the composer's cynical wit, his speculations in fish mongering,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> his mask of humility and indifference.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Paris:_the_later_years">In Paris: the later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In Paris: the later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nadar_-_(Gioacchino_Rossini)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4399" data-file-width="3156" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Nadar_-_%28Gioacchino_Rossini%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/150px-Nadar_-_%28Gioacchino_Rossini%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Nadar_-_%28Gioacchino_Rossini%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/225px-Nadar_-_%28Gioacchino_Rossini%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Nadar_-_%28Gioacchino_Rossini%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/300px-Nadar_-_%28Gioacchino_Rossini%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nadar_-_(Gioacchino_Rossini)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gioachino Rossini (photo by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Nadar" title="Félix Nadar">Félix Nadar</a>, c. 1856)</div>

</div>
<p>His first wife died in 1845, and on 16 August 1846, he married <a href="/wiki/Olympe_P%C3%A9lissier" title="Olympe Pélissier">Olympe Pélissier</a>, who had sat for <a href="/wiki/Horace_Vernet" title="Horace Vernet">Vernet</a> for his picture of <i>Judith and Holofernes</i>. Political disturbances compelled Rossini to leave Bologna in 1848. After living for a time in Florence, he settled in Paris in 1855, where he hosted many artistic and literary figures in his apartment at 2 <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_la_Chauss%C3%A9e-d%27Antin" title="Rue de la Chaussée-d'Antin">Rue de la Chaussée-d'Antin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> among whom the forty years younger Belgian mattauphone virtuoso Edmond Michotte,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> considering him as his <i>quasi figlio</i>.</p>
<p>Rossini had been a well-known <a href="/wiki/Gourmand" title="Gourmand">gourmand</a> and an excellent amateur chef his entire life, but he indulged these two passions fully once he retired from composing, and today there are a number of dishes with the appendage "alla Rossini" to their names that were created either by or specifically for him. Probably the most famous of these is <a href="/wiki/Tournedos_Rossini" title="Tournedos Rossini">tournedos Rossini</a>, still served by many restaurants today.</p>
<p>In the meantime, after years of various physical and mental illnesses, he had slowly returned to music, composing obscure little works intended for private performance. These included his <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ch%C3%A9s_de_vieillesse" title="Péchés de vieillesse">Péchés de vieillesse</a> ("Sins of Old Age"), which are grouped into 14 volumes, mostly for solo piano, occasionally for voice and various chamber ensembles. Often whimsical, these pieces display Rossini's natural ease of composition and gift for melody, showing obvious influences of Beethoven and <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Chopin</a>, with many flashes of the composer's long buried desire for serious, academic composition. They also underpin the fact that Rossini himself was an outstanding pianist whose playing attracted high praise from people such as <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigismond_Thalberg" title="Sigismond Thalberg">Sigismond Thalberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Di%C3%A9mer" title="Louis Diémer">Louis Diémer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>He died at the age of 76 from <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> at his country house at <a href="/wiki/Passy" title="Passy">Passy</a> on Friday, 13 November 1868. He was buried in <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_Lachaise_Cemetery" title="Père Lachaise Cemetery">Père Lachaise Cemetery</a> in Paris, France. In 1887, his remains were moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Santa_Croce,_Florence" title="Basilica of Santa Croce, Florence">Basilica of Santa Croce, Florence</a>, at the request of the Italian government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Herbert Weinstock's 1968 biography,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the composer's estate was valued at 2.5 million francs upon his death in 1868, the equivalent of about 1.4 million US dollars. According to one contemporary account, at the time of Rossini's death, his estate yielded revenues of 150,000 francs per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Apart from some individual legacies in favour of his wife and relatives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Rossini willed his entire estate to the Comune of Pesaro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The inheritance was invested to establish a <span lang="it">Liceo Musicale</span> (Conservatory) in the town. In 1940, the <span lang="it">Liceo</span> was put under state control and turned into the <a href="/wiki/Conservatorio_Statale_di_Musica_%22Gioachino_Rossini%22" title='Conservatorio Statale di Musica "Gioachino Rossini"'>Conservatorio Statale di Musica "Gioachino Rossini"</a>. The corporate body which managed Rossini's inheritance assumed the name Fondazione G. Rossini. The aims of the institution, which is still active, are to support the conservatory and promote the figure, the memory, and the works of Rossini. The institution has been a major sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/Rossini_Opera_Festival" title="Rossini Opera Festival">Rossini Opera Festival</a> since its beginning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rossini's estate funded the <a href="/wiki/Prix_Rossini" title="Prix Rossini">Prix Rossini</a>, a prize awarded to young French composers and librettists. The prize began to be awarded in 1878 on the death of his widow and is given by the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_Beaux-Arts" title="Académie des Beaux-Arts">Académie des Beaux-Arts</a>. Prize-winning works were produced by the Société des Concerts, <a href="/wiki/Institut_de_France" title="Institut de France">Institut de France</a>, from 1885 to 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The bequest sought to reward composers of music which emphasized melody, which Rossini wrote "today is neglected" ("<span lang="it">melodia, oggi si trascurata</span>"). The prize for librettists was to be given to writers who observed "the laws of morality, which the modern writers completely ignore" ("<span lang="it">osservando le leggi della morale di cui i moderni scrittori piu non tengono verun conto</span>"). The prizes were exclusively for French composers and librettists ("<span lang="it">exclusivamente per I Francesi</span>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_tributes">Honors and tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Honors and tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Rossini was a foreign associate of the institute, Grand Officer of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Legion of Honour</a> and recipient of innumerable orders.</p>
<p>Immediately after Rossini's death, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Giuseppe Verdi</a> proposed to <a href="/wiki/Classical_music_written_in_collaboration" title="Classical music written in collaboration">collaborate</a> with twelve other Italian composers on a <i><a href="/wiki/Messa_per_Rossini" title="Messa per Rossini">Requiem for Rossini</a></i>, to be performed on the first anniversary of Rossini's death, conducted by <a href="/wiki/Angelo_Mariani_(conductor)" title="Angelo Mariani (conductor)">Angelo Mariani</a>. The music was written, but the performance was abandoned shortly before its scheduled premiere. Verdi re-used the "Libera me, Domine" he had written for the Rossini Requiem in his 1872 <i><a href="/wiki/Requiem_(Verdi)" title="Requiem (Verdi)">Requiem for Manzoni</a></i>. In 1989 the conductor <a href="/wiki/Helmuth_Rilling" title="Helmuth Rilling">Helmuth Rilling</a> recorded the original <i>Requiem for Rossini</i> in its world premiere.</p>
<p>In 1900, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Cassioli" title="Giuseppe Cassioli">Giuseppe Cassioli</a> created a monument to Rossini in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Santa_Croce,_Florence" title="Basilica of Santa Croce, Florence">Basilica of Santa Croce, Florence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edmond_Michotte_fund">Edmond Michotte fund</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Edmond Michotte fund">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Library of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Conservatory_of_Brussels" title="Royal Conservatory of Brussels">Royal Conservatory of Brussels</a> possesses an important collection of scores, documents and objects from Rossini, assembled in the <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Michotte_fund" title="Edmond Michotte fund">Edmond Michotte fund</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rossiniana">Rossiniana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rossiniana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mauro_Giuliani" title="Mauro Giuliani">Mauro Giuliani</a> (who died in 1829) wrote six sets of variations for guitar on themes by Rossini, Opp. 119–124 (c. 1820–1828). Each set was called "Rossiniana", and collectively they are called "Rossiniane". This was the first known tribute by one composer to another using a title with the ending <a href="/wiki/-ana" title="-ana">-ana</a>.</p>
<p>In 1925, <a href="/wiki/Ottorino_Respighi" title="Ottorino Respighi">Ottorino Respighi</a> orchestrated four pieces from <i><a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ch%C3%A9s_de_vieillesse" title="Péchés de vieillesse">Péchés de vieillesse</a></i> as the suite <i><a href="/wiki/Rossiniana" title="Rossiniana">Rossiniana</a></i> (he had earlier used pieces from the same collection as the basis of his ballet <i><a href="/wiki/La_Boutique_fantasque" title="La Boutique fantasque">La Boutique fantasque</a></i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gioachino_Rossini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <i>Oxford History of Western Music</i>, "Rossini's fame surpassed that of any previous composer, and so, for a long time, did the popularity of his works. Audiences took to his music as if to an intoxicating drug—or, to put it more decorously, to champagne, with which Rossini's bubbly music was constantly compared."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_History_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_History-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rossini took existing operatic genres and forms and perfected them in his own style. Through his own work, as well as through that of his followers and imitators, Rossini's style dominated Italian opera throughout the first half of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_History_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_History-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his compositions, Rossini plagiarized freely from himself, a common practice among deadline-pressed opera composers of the time. Few of his operas are without such admixtures, frankly introduced in the form of arias or overtures. For example, in <i>Il Barbiere</i> there is an aria for the Count (often omitted) "Cessa di più resistere", which Rossini used (with minor changes) in the <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantata</a> <i>Le Nozze di Teti e di Peleo</i> and in <i><a href="/wiki/La_Cenerentola" title="La Cenerentola">La Cenerentola</a></i> (the cabaletta for Angelina's rondo is almost unchanged). Moreover, four of his best known overtures (<i><a href="/wiki/La_cambiale_di_matrimonio" title="La cambiale di matrimonio">La cambiale di matrimonio</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tancredi" title="Tancredi">Tancredi</a></i>, <i>La Cenerentola</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Barber_of_Seville" title="The Barber of Seville">The Barber of Seville</a></i>) share operas apart from those with which they are most famously associated.</p>
<p>A characteristic mannerism in Rossini's orchestral scoring is a long, steady building of sound over an <a href="/wiki/Ostinato" title="Ostinato">ostinato</a> figure, known as a "Rossini <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crescendo" title="Crescendo">crescendo</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> creating "tempests in teapots by beginning in a whisper and rising to a flashing, glittering storm,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> which earned him the nickname of "Signor Crescendo".</p>
<p>A few of Rossini's operas remained popular throughout his lifetime and continuously since his death; others were resurrected from semi-obscurity in the last half of the 20th century, during the so-called "Rossini Renaissance".</p>
<p>Rossini himself correctly predicted that his <i>Barber of Seville</i> would continue to find favor with posterity, telling a friend:</p>
<dl>
<dd>One thing I believe I can assure you: that of my works, the second act of <i>Guglielmo Tell</i>, the third act of <i>Otello</i>, and all of <i>il Barbiere di Siviglia</i> will certainly endure. ("<span lang="it">Ma di una cosa credo potervi assicurare: che di mio rimarrà di certo il secondo atto del Guglielmo Tell, il terzo atto dell'Otello, e tutto il barbiere di Siviglia</span>")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
