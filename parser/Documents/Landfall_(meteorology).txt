<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Landfall</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Landfall</b> is the event of a storm moving over land after being over water. When a <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a> makes landfall it becomes a regular <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>, which can then cause damage inland. When a fair weather waterspout makes landfall it usually dissipates quickly as it loses the inflow of warm air into the <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortex</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclone">Tropical cyclone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> is classified as making landfall when the center of the storm moves across the coast; in strong tropical cyclones this is when the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> moves over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This is where most of the damage occurs within a mature tropical cyclone, such as a typhoon or hurricane, as most of the damaging aspects of these systems are concentrated near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a>. Such effects include the peaking of the <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, the core of strong winds coming ashore, and heavy flooding rains. These coupled with high surf can cause major beach <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. In low-lying areas, the storm surge can stay inland for a long time and mix with chemicals already in the area to create a toxic mess.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> When a tropical cyclone makes landfall, the eye closes in upon itself due to the weakening process, which causes surf to decrease. <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">Maximum sustained winds</a> will naturally decrease as the cyclone moves inland due to frictional differences between water and land with the free atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Landfall is distinct from a <i>direct hit</i>. A direct hit is where the core of high winds (or eyewall) comes onshore but the center of the storm may stay offshore. The effects of this are quite similar to a landfall, as this term is used when the radius of maximum wind within a tropical cyclone moves ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These effects are high surf, heavy rains that may cause flooding, water buildup along the coast with minor storm surge, coastal beach erosion, high winds, and possibly severe thunderstorms with tornadoes around the periphery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornado_or_waterspout">Tornado or waterspout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tornado or waterspout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornadic" title="Tornadic">tornadic</a> <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a> makes landfall it is reclassified as a tornado,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which can subsequently cause damage to areas inland of the coast. When a fair weather waterspout makes landfall it usually dissipates quickly due to friction and a reduction in the amount of warm air supplied to the funnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
