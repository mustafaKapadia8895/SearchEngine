<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Atmospheric sounding</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>An <b>atmospheric sounding</b> is a measurement of vertical distribution of physical properties of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> column such as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a> (thus deriving <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>), liquid water content, <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> concentration, pollution, and other properties. Such measurements are performed in a variety of ways including <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> and <a href="/wiki/In_situ#Earth_and_atmospheric_sciences" title="In situ">in situ</a> observations.</p>
<p>The most common in situ sounding is a <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a>, which usually is a <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a>, but can also be a <a href="/wiki/Rocketsonde" title="Rocketsonde">rocketsonde</a>.</p>
<p>Remote sensing soundings generally use passive <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometers</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>airborne instruments</li>
<li>surface stations</li>
<li>Earth-observing satellite instruments such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Infrared_Sounder" title="Atmospheric Infrared Sounder">AIRS</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Microwave_Sounding_Unit" title="Advanced Microwave Sounding Unit">AMSU</a></li>
<li>observation of atmospheres on different planets, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_climate_sounder" title="Mars climate sounder">Mars climate sounder</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter">Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter</a></li>
</ul>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_methods">Direct methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_sounding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Direct methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sensors that measure atmospheric constituents directly, such as thermometers, barometers, and humidity sensors, can be sent aloft on balloons, rockets or <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsondes</a>. They can also be carried on the outer hulls of ships and aircraft or even mounted on towers. In this case, all that is needed to capture the measurements are storage devices and/or <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponders</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_methods">Indirect methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_sounding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Indirect methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The more challenging case involves sensors, primarily satellite-mounted, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiometers" title="Radiometers">radiometers</a>, optical sensors, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">lidar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceilometer" title="Ceilometer">ceilometer</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodar" title="Sodar">sodar</a> since these cannot measure the quantity of interest, such as temperature, pressure, humidity etc., directly. By understanding emission and absorption processes, we can figure out what the instrument is looking at between the layers of atmosphere. While this type of instrument can also be operated from ground stations or vehicles—optical methods can also be used inside in situ instruments—satellite instruments are particularly important because of their extensive, regular coverage. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Microwave_Sounding_Unit" title="Advanced Microwave Sounding Unit">AMSU</a> instruments on three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> and two <a href="/wiki/EUMETSAT" title="EUMETSAT">EUMETSAT</a> satellites, for instance, can sample the entire globe at better than one degree resolution in less than a day.</p>
<p>We can distinguish between two broad classes of sensor: <i>active</i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, that have their own source, and <i>passive</i> that only detect what is already there. There can be a variety of sources for a passive instrument, including scattered radiation, light emitted directly from the sun, moon or stars—both more appropriate in the visual or ultra-violet range—as well light emitted from warm objects, which is more appropriate in the microwave and infrared.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viewing_geometry">Viewing geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_sounding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Viewing geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A limb sounder looks at the edge of the atmosphere where it is visible above the Earth. It does this in one of two ways: either it tracks the sun, moon, a star, or another transmitting satellite through the limb as the source gets <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultated</a> behind the Earth, or it looks towards empty space, collecting radiation that is scattered from one of these sources. In contrast, a <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a>-looking atmospheric sounder looks down through the atmosphere at the surface. The <a href="/wiki/SCIAMACHY" title="SCIAMACHY">SCIAMACHY</a> instrument operates in all three of these modes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_inverse_problem">Atmospheric inverse problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_sounding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atmospheric inverse problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_solution">Methods of solution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_sounding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Methods of solution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If the problem is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">linear</a> we can use some type of matrix inverse method—often the problem is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ill-posed" title="Ill-posed">ill-posed</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_instability" title="Numerical instability">unstable</a> so we will need to <a href="/wiki/Regularization_(mathematics)" title="Regularization (mathematics)">regularize</a> it: good, simple methods include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_equation" title="Normal equation">normal equation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singular_value_decomposition" title="Singular value decomposition">singular value decomposition</a>. If the problem is weakly nonlinear, an iterative method such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton-Raphson" title="Newton-Raphson">Newton-Raphson</a> may be appropriate.</p>
<p>Sometimes the physics is too complicated to model accurately or the forward model too slow to be used effectively in the inverse method. In this case, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical" title="Statistical">statistical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a> methods such as <a href="/wiki/Linear_regression" title="Linear regression">linear regression</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_networks" title="Neural networks">neural networks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistical_classification" title="Statistical classification">statistical classification</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kernel_estimation" title="Kernel estimation">kernel estimation</a>, etc. can be used to form an inverse model based on a collection of ordered pairs of samples mapping the state space to the measurement space, that is, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mo fence="false" stretchy="false">{</mo>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mover>
<mi>x</mi>
<mo stretchy="false">→<!-- → --></mo>
</mover>
</mrow>
</mrow>
<mo>:</mo>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mover>
<mi>y</mi>
<mo stretchy="false">→<!-- → --></mo>
</mover>
</mrow>
</mrow>
<mo fence="false" stretchy="false">}</mo>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \lbrace {\vec {x}}:{\vec {y}}\rbrace }</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\lbrace {\vec  x}:{\vec  y}\rbrace " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83926ea1335a1cd75699e320644b3f0b985cc2b9" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:6.903ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>. These can be generated either from models—e.g. state vectors from dynamical models and measurement vectors from radiative transfer or similar forward models—or from direct, empirical measurement. Other times when a statistical method might be more appropriate include highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonlinear" title="Nonlinear">nonlinear</a> problems.</p>
