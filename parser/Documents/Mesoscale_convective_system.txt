<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mesoscale convective system</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DangerousShelfCloud.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Arcus_cloud" title="Arcus cloud">shelf cloud</a> such as this one can be a sign that a <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squall</a> is imminent</div>
</div>

<p>A <b>mesoscale convective system</b> (<b>MCS</b>) is a complex of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a> that becomes organized on a scale larger than the individual thunderstorms but smaller than <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, and normally persists for several hours or more. A mesoscale convective system's overall cloud and precipitation pattern may be round or linear in shape, and include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_system" title="Weather system">weather systems</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake-effect snow</a> events, <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_Complex" title="Mesoscale Convective Complex">Mesoscale Convective Complexes</a> (MCCs), and generally form near <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather fronts</a>. The type that forms during the warm season over land has been noted across <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, with a maximum in activity noted during the late afternoon and evening hours.</p>
<p>Forms of MCS that develop within the tropics use either the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon troughs</a> as a focus for their development, generally within the warm season between <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall" title="Fall">fall</a>. One exception is that of <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake-effect snow</a> bands, which form due to cold air moving across relatively warm bodies of water, and occurs from fall through spring. Polar lows are a second special class of MCS which form at high latitudes during the cold season. Once the parent MCS dies, later thunderstorm development can occur in connection with its remnant <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_vortex" title="Mesoscale convective vortex">mesoscale convective vortex</a> (MCV). Mesoscale convective systems are important to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_rainfall_climatology" title="United States rainfall climatology">United States rainfall climatology</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> since they bring the region about half of their annual warm season rainfall.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">Mesoscale</a> convective systems are thunderstorm regions which may be round or linear in shape, on the order of 100 kilometres (62 mi) or more across in one direction but smaller than extratropical cyclones,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and include systems such as tropical cyclones, squall lines, and <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_complex" title="Mesoscale convective complex">Mesoscale Convective Complexes</a> (MCCs), among others. MCS is a more generalized term which includes systems that do not satisfy the stricter size, shape, or duration criteria of an MCC. They tend to form near <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather fronts</a> and move into areas of 1000-500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">mb</a> thickness diffluence, which are areas where the low to mid level temperature gradient broadens, which generally steers the thunderstorm clusters into the warm sector of <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, or equatorward of <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm fronts</a>. They can also form along any convergent zones within the tropics. Their formation has been noted worldwide, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mei-Yu_front" title="Mei-Yu front">Mei-Yu front</a> in the far East to the deep tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Mesoscale convective systems are important to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_rainfall_climatology" title="United States rainfall climatology">United States rainfall climatology</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> since they bring the region about half of their annual warm season rainfall.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunderstorm_types_and_levels_of_organization">Thunderstorm types and levels of organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thunderstorm types and levels of organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorm</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="777" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png/220px-CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png/330px-CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png/440px-CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CAPE_vs_SHEAR.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Conditions favorable for thunderstorm types and complexes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are four main types of thunderstorms: single-cell, multi-cell, squall line (also called multi-cell line) and supercell. Which type forms depends on the instability and relative wind conditions at different layers of the atmosphere ("<a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>"). Single-cell thunderstorms form in environments of low vertical wind shear and last only 20–30 minutes. Organized thunderstorms and thunderstorm clusters/lines can have longer life cycles as they form in environments of sufficient moisture, significant vertical wind shear (normally greater than 25 knots (13 m/s) in the lowest 6 kilometres (3.7 mi) of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), which aids the development of stronger updrafts as well as various forms of severe weather. The supercell is the strongest of the thunderstorms, most commonly associated with large hail, high winds, and tornado formation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Precipitable_water" title="Precipitable water">Precipitable water</a> values of greater than 31.8 millimetres (1.25 in) favor the development of organized thunderstorm complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Those with heavy rainfall normally have precipitable water values greater than 36.9 millimetres (1.45 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> normally greater than 25 knots (13 m/s),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Upstream values of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability#CAPE_and_CIN" title="Atmospheric instability">CAPE</a> of greater than 800 J/kg are usually required for the development of organized convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscale_Convective_Complex">Mesoscale Convective Complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mesoscale Convective Complex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_Complex" title="Mesoscale Convective Complex">Mesoscale Convective Complex</a></div>
<p>A mesoscale convective complex (MCC) is a unique kind of mesoscale convective system which is defined by characteristics observed in infrared <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a>. Their area of cold cloud tops exceeds 100,000 square kilometres (39,000 sq mi) with temperature less than or equal to −32 °C (−26 °F); and an area of cloud top of 50,000 square kilometres (19,000 sq mi) with temperature less than or equal to −52 °C (−62 °F). Size definitions must be met for six hours or greater. Its maximum extent is defined as when the cloud shield, or the overall cloud formation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> reaches its maximum area. Its <a href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics)" title="Eccentricity (mathematics)">eccentricity</a> (minor axis/major axis) is greater than or equal to 0.7 at maximum extent, so they are fairly round. They are long-lived, <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a> in formation as they tend to form overnight, and commonly contain heavy rainfall, wind, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">Squall line</a></div>
<p>A squall line is an elongated line of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms#Severe_thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">severe thunderstorms</a> that can form along and/or ahead of a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the early 20th century, the term was used as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OU_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-OU-12">[12]</a></sup> The squall line contains heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, frequent <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, strong straight line <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterspouts" title="Waterspouts">waterspouts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">Severe weather</a>, in form of strong straight-line winds can be expected in areas where the squall line itself is in the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a>, within the portion of the line which bows out the most.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornadoes</a> can be found along waves within a <a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">line echo wave pattern</a>, or LEWP, where mesoscale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure areas</a> are present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some bow echoes which develop within the summer season are known as <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a>, and they move quite fast through large sections of territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-16">[16]</a></sup> On the back edge of the rain shield associated with mature squall lines, a <a href="/wiki/Wake_low" title="Wake low">wake low</a> can form, which is a mesoscale low pressure area that forms behind the mesoscale high pressure system normally present under the rain canopy, which are sometimes associated with a <a href="/wiki/Heat_burst" title="Heat burst">heat burst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Another term that may be used in association with squall line and bow echoes is <b>quasi-linear convective systems (QLCSs)</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclone">Tropical cyclone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2004" data-file-width="3032" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/250px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/375px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/500px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclone_Catarina" title="Cyclone Catarina">Cyclone Catarina</a>, a rare <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South Atlantic tropical cyclone</a> viewed from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> on March 26, 2004</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></div>
<p>A tropical cyclone is a fairly symmetric <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm system</a> characterized by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_system" title="Low pressure system">low pressure</a> center and numerous thunderstorms that produce strong winds and <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> rain. A tropical cyclone feeds on the heat released when moist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> rises, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> contained in the moist air. It is fueled by a different heat mechanism than other cyclonic windstorms such as <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor'easter">nor'easters</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_windstorm" title="European windstorm">European windstorms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a>, leading to their classification as "warm core" storm systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "tropical" refers to both the geographic origin of these systems, which form often in <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> regions of the globe, and their formation in <a href="/wiki/Air_mass#Classification_and_notation" title="Air mass">Maritime Tropical air masses</a>. The term "cyclone" refers to such storms' cyclonic nature, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clockwise_and_counterclockwise" title="Clockwise and counterclockwise">counterclockwise</a> rotation in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and clockwise rotation in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. Depending on their location and strength, tropical cyclones are referred to by other names, such as hurricane, typhoon, tropical storm, cyclonic storm, tropical depression, or simply as a cyclone. Generally speaking, a tropical cyclone is referred to as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> (from the name of the ancient Central American deity of wind, <a href="/wiki/Huracan" title="Huracan">Huracan</a>) in the Atlantic and eastern Pacific oceans, a typhoon across the northwest Pacific ocean, and a cyclone across in the southern hemisphere and Indian ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_glossary_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_glossary-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tropical cyclones can produce extremely powerful winds and torrential rain, as well as high waves and damaging <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfo_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-21">[21]</a></sup> They develop over large bodies of warm water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-22">[22]</a></sup> and lose their strength if they move over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This is the reason coastal regions can receive significant damage from a tropical cyclone, while inland regions are relatively safe from the strong winds. Heavy rains, however, can produce significant flooding inland, and storm surges can produce extensive coastal flooding up to 40 kilometres (25 mi) from the coastline. Although their effects on human populations can be devastating, tropical cyclones can also relieve <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005_EPac_outlook_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005_EPac_outlook-24">[24]</a></sup> They also carry heat and energy away from the tropics and transport it toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a>, which makes them an important part of the global <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a> mechanism. As a result, tropical cyclones help to maintain equilibrium in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, and to maintain a relatively stable and warm.</p>
<p>Many tropical cyclones <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">develop</a> when the atmospheric conditions around a weak disturbance in the atmosphere are favorable. Others form when <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_system#Types" title="Mesoscale convective system">other types of cyclones</a> acquire tropical characteristics. Tropical systems are then moved by steering winds in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>; if the conditions remain favorable, the tropical disturbance intensifies, and can even develop an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>. On the other end of the spectrum, if the conditions around the system deteriorate or the tropical cyclone makes landfall, the system weakens and eventually dissipates. A tropical cyclone can become extratropical as it moves toward higher latitudes if its energy source changes from heat released by condensation to differences in temperature between air masses;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-19">[19]</a></sup> From an operational standpoint, a tropical cyclone is usually not considered to become a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> during its extratropical transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PadgetDecember2000_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-PadgetDecember2000-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake-effect_snow">Lake-effect snow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lake-effect snow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="775" data-file-width="820" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif/250px-October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif/375px-October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif/500px-October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lake-effect precipitation coming off <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a>, as seen by <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">NEXRAD</a> radar, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Storm_%22Aphid%22" title='Lake Storm "Aphid"'>October 12–13, 2006</a></div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">Lake-effect snow</a></div>
<p>Lake-effect snow is produced in the winter in the shape of one or more elongated bands when cold winds move across long expanses of warmer lake water, providing energy and picking up <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> which freezes and is deposited on the <a href="/wiki/Lee_shore" title="Lee shore">lee shores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The same effect over bodies of salt water is called <b>ocean effect snow</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <b>sea effect snow</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> or even <b>bay effect snow</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The effect is enhanced when the moving air mass is uplifted by the <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic</a> effect of higher elevations on the downwind shores. This uplifting can produce narrow, but very intense bands of precipitation, which is deposited at a rate of many inches of snow per hour and often brings copious snowfall totals. The areas affected by lake-effect snow are called <a href="/wiki/Snowbelt" title="Snowbelt">snowbelts</a>. This effect occurs in many locations throughout the world, but is best known in the populated areas of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greglake_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greglake-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>If the air temperature is not low enough to keep the precipitation frozen, it falls as lake-effect rain. In order for lake-effect rain or snow to form, the air moving across the lake must be significantly cooler than the surface air (which is likely to be near the temperature of the water surface). Specifically, the air temperature at the altitude where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pressure" title="Air pressure">air pressure</a> is 850 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> (or 1.5 kilometres (0.93 mi) altitude) should be 13 °C (24 °F) lower than the temperature of the air at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greglake_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greglake-30">[30]</a></sup> Lake-effect occurring when the air at 850 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> is 25 °C (45 °F) colder than the water temperature can produce <a href="/wiki/Thundersnow" title="Thundersnow">thundersnow</a>, snow showers accompanied by lightning and <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> (due to the larger amount of energy available from the increased instability).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_low">Polar low</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Polar low">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar low</a> is a small-scale, symmetric, short-lived atmospheric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure system</a> (depression) that is found over the ocean areas poleward of the main <a href="/wiki/Polar_front" title="Polar front">polar front</a> in both the Northern and Southern Hemispheres. The systems usually have a horizontal length scale of less than 1,000 kilometres (620 mi) and exist for no more than a couple of days. They are part of the larger class of <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> weather systems. Polar lows can be difficult to detect using conventional weather reports and are a hazard to high-latitude operations, such as shipping and gas and oil platforms. Polar lows have been referred to by many other terms, such as polar mesoscale vortex, Arctic hurricane, Arctic low, and cold air depression. Today the term is usually reserved for the more vigorous systems that have near-surface winds of at least 17 metres per second (38 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polar_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-polar-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Where_they_form">Where they form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Where they form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Plains_of_the_United_States">Great Plains of the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Great Plains of the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bow_echo_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="294" data-file-width="542" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bow_echo_diagram.svg/250px-Bow_echo_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bow_echo_diagram.svg/375px-Bow_echo_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bow_echo_diagram.svg/500px-Bow_echo_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bow_echo_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The time period in the Plains where thunderstorm areas are most prevalent ranges between May and September. Mesoscale convective systems develop over the region during this time frame, with a bulk of the activity occurring between 6 and 9 p.m. local time. Mesoscale convective systems bring 30 to 70 percent of the annual warm season rainfall to the Plains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A subset of these systems known as mesoscale convective complexes lead to up to 10% of the annual rainfall across the Plains and Midwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Squall lines account for 30% of the large thunderstorm complexes which move through the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While most form over the continent, some MCSs form during the second half of August and September over the western <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. MCS triggering over Europe is strongly tied to mountain ranges. On average, a European MCS moves east-northeast, forming near 3 p.m. local <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">solar time</a>, lasts 5.5 hours, dissipating near 9 p.m. LST. Around 20% of the MCSs over Europe do not form during maximum heating. Their average maximum extent is around 9,000 square kilometres (3,500 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropics">Tropics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tropics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mesoscale convective systems, which can evolve into tropical cyclones, form along areas such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> or easterly waves which progress westward along monsoon troughs and the Intertropical Convergence Zone in regions of ample low level moisture, convergent surface winds, and divergent winds aloft. This typically occurs north of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic</a> and eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_ocean" title="Pacific ocean">Pacific oceans</a>, as well as across the northwest and southwest Pacific oceans, from <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> eastward into <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_ocean" title="Indian ocean">Indian ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and from southeast <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> into the southern Atlantic ocean. It is also noted on occasion in the southeast Pacific ocean mild to cool <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">ENSO</a> years, outside of El Niño.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> More intense systems form over land than water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lee_of_warm_water_bodies_in_the_winter">Lee of warm water bodies in the winter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lee of warm water bodies in the winter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the cases of Lake-effect snow and polar lows, the convective systems form over warm water bodies when cold air sweeps over their surface and leads to an increase in moisture and significant vertical motion. This vertical motion leads to the development of showers and thunderstorms in areas of cyclonic flow on the backside of <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greglake_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greglake-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polar_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-polar-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Their_remnants">Their remnants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_convective_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Their remnants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_vortex" title="Mesoscale convective vortex">Mesoscale convective vortex</a></div>
<p>A mesoscale convective vortex--(MCV)--is a mid-level low-pressure center within an MCS that pulls winds into a circling pattern, or vortex. Once the parent MCS dies, this vortex can persist and lead to future convective development. With a core only 30 miles (48 km) to 60 miles (97 km) and up to 8 kilometres (5.0 mi) deep,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> an MCV can occasionally spawn a mesoscale surface low pressure area which appears on mesoscale <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface weather analyses</a>. But an MCV can take on a life of its own, persisting for up to several days after its parent MCS has dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The orphaned MCV will sometimes then become the seed of the next thunderstorm outbreak. An MCV that moves into tropical waters, such as the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, can serve as the nucleus for a tropical storm or hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
