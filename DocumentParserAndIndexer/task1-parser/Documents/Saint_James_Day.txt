<p>The naval <b>St James' Day Battle</b> (also known as the <b>St James' Day Fight</b>), the <b>Battle of the <a href="/wiki/North_Foreland" title="North Foreland">North Foreland</a></b> and the <b>Battle of <a href="/wiki/Orford_Ness" title="Orford Ness">Orfordness</a></b>) took place on 25 July 1666 — <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_James_the_Great" title="Saint James the Great">St James</a>' day in the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> then in use in England (4 August 1666 in the Gregorian calendar), during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Second Anglo-Dutch War">Second Anglo-Dutch War</a> and was fought between fleets of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, commanded jointly by <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine" title="Prince Rupert of the Rhine">Prince Rupert of the Rhine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Monck" title="George Monck">George Monck</a>, 1st <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Albemarle" title="Duke of Albemarle">Duke of Albemarle</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">United Provinces</a> commanded by Lieutenant-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Michiel_de_Ruyter" title="Michiel de Ruyter">Michiel de Ruyter</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, the battle is known as the <i>Two Days' Battle</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._James%27s_Day_Battle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the Dutch had inflicted considerable damage on the English fleet in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_Days_Battle" title="Four Days Battle">Four Days Battle</a>, the leading Dutch politician, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Pensionary" title="Grand Pensionary">Grand Pensionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Johan_de_Witt" title="Johan de Witt">Johan de Witt</a>, ordered Lieutenant-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Michiel_de_Ruyter" title="Michiel de Ruyter">Michiel de Ruyter</a> to carry out a plan that had been prepared for over a year: to land in the <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway</a> to destroy the English fleet while it was being repaired in the <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_Kent" title="Chatham, Kent">Chatham</a> dockyards. For this purpose, ten <a href="/wiki/Fluyt" title="Fluyt">fluyt</a> ships carried 2,700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marines_(military)" title="Marines (military)">marines</a> of the newly created <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Marine_Corps" title="Dutch Marine Corps">Dutch Marine Corps</a>, one of the first in history to be specialised in amphibious landings. Also, De Ruyter was to combine his fleet with the French one.</p>
<p>The French, however, failed to show up and bad weather prevented the landing. De Ruyter had to limit his actions to a blockade of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">Thames</a>. On 1 August, he observed that the English fleet was leaving port, earlier than expected. Then, a storm drove the Dutch fleet back to the Flemish coast. On 3 August, De Ruyter again crossed the North Sea, leaving behind the troop ships.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._James%27s_Day_Battle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_day">First day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._James%27s_Day_Battle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early morning of 25 July, the Dutch fleet of 88 ships discovered the English fleet of 89 ships near North Foreland, sailing to the north, and pursued it from the southeast in a leeward position, as the wind blew from the northwest. Suddenly, the wind turned to the northeast. The commander of the English fleet, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine" title="Prince Rupert of the Rhine">Prince Rupert of the Rhine</a>, then turned sharply east to regain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_gauge" title="Weather gauge">weather gauge</a> and De Ruyter followed to keep it. This proved to be a fatal manoeuvre for the Dutch. They now sailed right into the core of a high-pressure area. The Dutch van, commanded by Lieutenant-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Johan_Evertsen" title="Johan Evertsen">Johan Evertsen</a>, lost all speed and couldn't maintain a line of battle. This awkward situation lasted for hours; then, again, a soft breeze began to blow from the northeast. Immediately, the English van, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Thomas_Allin,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Thomas Allin, 1st Baronet">Thomas Allin</a>, and part of the centre formed a line of battle and sailed right to the Dutch van, still in disarray and basically defenceless. Ship after ship of the Frisian fleet was mauled by the combined firepower of the English line. Vice-Admiral <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Coenders&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rudolf Coenders (page does not exist)">Rudolf Coenders</a> was killed. Lieutenant-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Tjerk_Hiddes_de_Vries" title="Tjerk Hiddes de Vries">Tjerk Hiddes de Vries</a> had an arm and a leg shot off, yet still tried to bring cohesion to his forces, but to no avail. Unable to reach them with his centre, the horrified De Ruyter saw the Frisian ships drifting to the south, now no more than floating wrecks full of dead, the moans of the dying clearly audible above the other sounds of battle.</p>
<p>Now Rupert combined his full van and centre to deliver the coup-de-grâce to the Dutch centre. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Monck" title="George Monck">George Monck</a>, accompanying Rupert, predicted that De Ruyter would give two broadsides and run, but the latter put up a furious fight on the Dutch flagship <i><a href="/wiki/Dutch_ship_De_Zeven_Provinci%C3%ABn_(1665)" title="Dutch ship De Zeven Provinciën (1665)">De Zeven Provinciën</a></i>. He withstood a combined attack by <a href="/wiki/HMS_Sovereign_of_the_Seas" title="HMS Sovereign of the Seas"><i>Sovereign of the Seas</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_Charles_(1655)" title="HMS Royal Charles (1655)"><i>Royal Charles</i></a> and forced Rupert to leave the damaged <i>Royal Charles</i> for <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_James_(1658)" title="HMS Royal James (1658)"><i>Royal James</i></a>. This way, De Ruyter managed to cover the retreat of the Dutch van.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Lieutenant-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Cornelis_Tromp" title="Cornelis Tromp">Cornelis Tromp</a>, commanding the Dutch rear, had seen the sad events evolve from a great distance. Annoyed by the lack of competence shown, he decided to give the correct example. He turned sharply to the west, crossed the line of the English rear, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Smith_(Royal_Navy_officer)" title="Jeremiah Smith (Royal Navy officer)">Jeremiah Smith</a>, separating it from the rest of the English fleet and then, having the weather gauge, kept attacking it doggedly until at last the English were routed and fled to the west. He pursued well into the night, destroying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Resolution_(1654)" title="HMS Resolution (1654)"><i>Resolution</i></a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fireship" title="Fireship">fireship</a>. After Tromp thrice shot the entire crew from its rigging, Smith's flagship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Loyal_London_(1666)" title="HMS Loyal London (1666)"><i>Loyal London</i></a> had to be towed home. The vice commander of the English rear was <a href="/wiki/Edward_Spragge" title="Edward Spragge">Edward Spragge</a>, who felt so humiliated by the course of events that he became a personal enemy of Tromp, dying himself while trying to kill his foe in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Texel" title="Battle of Texel">Battle of Texel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_day">Second day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._James%27s_Day_Battle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the morning of 26 July, Tromp broke off pursuit, well-pleased with his first real victory as a squadron commander. During the night, a ship had brought him the message that De Ruyter had likewise been victorious, so Tromp was in a euphoric mood. That abruptly changed upon the discovery of the drifting flagship of the dying <a href="/wiki/Tjerk_Hiddes_de_Vries" title="Tjerk Hiddes de Vries">Tjerk Hiddes de Vries</a>. Suddenly he feared that his ship was now the only remnant of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Empire" title="Dutch Empire">Dutch</a> fleet and that he was in mortal peril. Behind him, those ships of the English rear still operational had again turned to the east. In front, the other enemy squadrons surely awaited him. On the horizon, only <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_England" title="Flag of England">English flags</a> were to be seen. Manoeuvring wildly, Tromp, drinking a lot of gin to restore his nerve, dodged any attempt to trap him and brought his squadron safely home in the port of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flushing,_Netherlands" title="Flushing, Netherlands">Flushing</a> on the morning of 26 July. There, to great mutual relief, he discovered the rest of the Dutch fleet.</p>
<p>It took Tromp six hours to gather enough courage to face De Ruyter. It was obvious to him that he should never have allowed himself to get completely separated from the main force. Indeed De Ruyter, not being his usual charitable self, immediately blamed him for the defeat and ordered Tromp and his subcommanders <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Sweers" title="Isaac Sweers">Isaac Sweers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willem_van_der_Zaan" title="Willem van der Zaan">Willem van der Zaan</a> from his sight, and told them to never again set foot on <i>De Zeven Provinciën</i>. The commander of the Dutch fleet still hadn't mentally recovered from the events of the previous day.</p>

<p>On the morning of 5 August, after a short summer's night, De Ruyter discovered that his position had become hopeless. Lieutenant-Admiral Johan Evertsen had died after losing a leg, De Ruyter's force was now reduced to about forty ships, crowding together and most of these were inoperational, being survivors of the van. Some fifteen good ships had apparently deserted during the night. A strong gale from the east prevented an easy retreat to the continental coast, and to the west the British van and centre (about fifty ships) surrounded him in a half-circle, safely bombarding him from a leeward position.</p>
<p>De Ruyter was desperate. When his second-in-command of the centre, Lieutenant-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Aert_Jansse_van_Nes" title="Aert Jansse van Nes">Aert Jansse van Nes</a> visited him for a council of war, he exclaimed: "With seven or eight against the mass!" He then sagged, mumbling: "What's wrong with us? I wish I were dead." His close personal friend Van Nes tried to cheer him up, joking: "Me too. But you never die when you want to!" No sooner had both men left the cabin than the table they had been sitting at was smashed by a cannonball.</p>
<p>The English, however, had their own problems. The strong gale prevented them from closing with the Dutch. They tried to use fire ships, but these, too, had trouble reaching the enemy. Only the sloop <i>Fan-Fan</i>, Rupert's personal pleasure yacht, rowed to the Dutch flagship <i>De Zeven Provinciën</i> to harass it with its two little guns, much to the hilarious laughter of the English crews.</p>
<p>When his ship had again warded off an attack by a fire ship (the <i>Land of Promise</i>) and Tromp still didn't show up, for <a href="/wiki/Michiel_de_Ruyter" title="Michiel de Ruyter">De Ruyter</a> tension became unbearable. He sought death, exposing himself deliberately on the deck. When he failed to be hit, he exclaimed: "Oh, God, how unfortunate I am! Amongst so many thousands of cannonballs, is there not one that would take me?" His son-in-law, Captain of the Marines Johann de Witte, heard him and said: "Father, what desperate words! If you merely want to die, let us then turn, sail in the midst of our enemies and fight ourselves to death!". This brave but foolish proposal brought the Admiral back to his senses, for he discovered that he wasn't so desperate and answered: "You don't know what you are talking about! If I did that, all would be lost. But if I can bring myself and these ships safely home, we'll finish the job later."</p>
<p>Then the wind, that had brought so much misfortune to the Dutch, saved them by turning to the west. They formed a line of battle and brought their fleet to safety through the Flemish shoals, Vice-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Adriaen_Banckert" title="Adriaen Banckert">Adriaen Banckert</a> of the Zealandic fleet covering the retreat of all damaged ships with the operational vessels, the number of the latter slowly growing as it turned out that only very few ships had actually deserted in the night; most had merely drifted away, and now, one after the other, they rejoined the battle.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._James%27s_Day_Battle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The battle was a clear English victory, but the separate clash of the two years was a victory for Tromp. Dutch casualties were enormous, estimated immediately after the battle of about 5,000 men, compared with 300 English killed; later, more precise information showed that only about 1,200 of them had been killed or seriously wounded. However, the Dutch lost only two ships: De Ruyter had been successful at saving almost the complete van, only <i>Sneek</i> and <i>Tholen</i> struck their flag, and they could quickly repair the damage. The twin disasters of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_London" title="Great Plague of London">Great Plague of London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a>, however, combined with his financial mismanagement, left <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> without the funds to continue the war. In fact, he had had only enough reserves for this one last battle. The Dutch soon recovered; within a month, they again took sea, but only a minor skirmish resulted. During this later fight, De Ruyter inhaled a burning fuse filament that burnt a <a href="/wiki/Fistula" title="Fistula">fistula</a> in his throat; he would recover just in time to inflict a severe blow on the English navy in the <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_the_Medway" title="Raid on the Medway">Raid on the Medway</a> in 1667, when, at last, he could carry out the plan he was prevented from executing in 1666.</p>
<p>During the weeks that the Dutch fleet was in repair, Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Holmes_(admiral)" title="Robert Holmes (admiral)">Robert Holmes</a>, aided by the Dutch traitor Laurens van Heemskerck, penetrated the <a href="/wiki/Vlie" title="Vlie">Vlie</a> estuary, burnt a fleet of 150 merchants (<a href="/wiki/Holmes%27s_Bonfire" title="Holmes's Bonfire">Holmes's Bonfire</a>) and sacked the town of Ter Schelling (the present <a href="/wiki/West-Terschelling" title="West-Terschelling">West-Terschelling</a>) on the Frisian island of <a href="/wiki/Terschelling" title="Terschelling">Terschelling</a>. <i>Fan-Fan</i> was again present.</p>
<p>In the Republic, the defeat also had a far-reaching political effect. Tromp was the champion of the Orangist party; now that he was accused of severe negligence, the country split over this issue. To defend himself, Tromp let his brother-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Johan_Kievit" title="Johan Kievit">Johan Kievit</a>, publish an account of his conduct. Shortly afterward, Kievit was discovered to have planned a coup, secretly negotiating a peace treaty with the English king. He fled to England and was condemned to death in absentia; Tromp's family was fined and he himself forbidden to serve in the fleet. In November 1669, a supporter of Tromp tried to stab De Ruyter in the entrance hall of his house. Only in 1672 would Tromp have his revenge, when Johan de Witt was murdered; some claim Tromp has had a hand. The new ruler, <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III of Orange</a>, succeeded, with great difficulty, in reconciling De Ruyter with Tromp in 1673.</p>
