<p><b>Hurricane Alma</b> was a rare June major hurricane in the <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1966 Atlantic hurricane season">1966 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. It was the earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> in the calendar year in fifteen years, as well as the earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_U.S." title="Continental U.S.">continental U.S.</a> hurricane strike since 1825. Alma developed on June 4 over Central America, and while moving through Honduras, it dropped heavy rainfall that killed at least 73 people in the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael,_Lempira" title="San Rafael, Lempira">San Rafael</a>. Offshore northern Honduras, the system produced heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Swan_Islands,_Honduras" title="Swan Islands, Honduras">Swan Island</a>. Alma moved northeastward and intensified into a hurricane on June 6. It crossed western Cuba, causing heavy crop damage and water shortages. Alma destroyed over 1,000 houses, and damage was estimated around $200 million (1966 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>). The storm killed 12 people in the country.</p>
<p>After crossing Cuba, Alma intensified further to reach winds of 125 mph (201 km/h) in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. The hurricane passed west of <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West, Florida</a>, causing a power outage and flooding. Alma dropped heavy rainfall and produced winds across most of Florida, which damaged crops and caused scattered power outages. The hurricane weakened before moving ashore near <a href="/wiki/Apalachee_Bay" title="Apalachee Bay">Apalachee Bay</a>. Damage in Florida was estimated at $10 million, and there were six deaths in the state.</p>
<p>Alma crossed southeastern <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> as a tropical storm, damaging a few houses and causing light damage. The storm re-intensified into a hurricane over the western Atlantic Ocean, and its outer rainbands dropped heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>. Alma encountered colder water temperatures and its intensity decreased to being an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> on June 13. Its remnants dissipated a day later over Massachusetts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Alma_(1966)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alma_1966_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="794" data-file-width="1024" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Alma_1966_track.png/220px-Alma_1966_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Alma_1966_track.png/330px-Alma_1966_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Alma_1966_track.png/440px-Alma_1966_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alma_1966_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>

<p>During June 1966, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low atmospheric pressure</a> stretched across the western <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and Gulf of Mexico—a pattern that is conducive for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>. During the first few days of the month, a dissipating <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> extended from the southeastern United States into the western Caribbean. A mid-level <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Eye_and_center" title="Tropical cyclone">circulation</a> developed along the trough near <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Gracias_a_Dios" title="Cabo Gracias a Dios">Cabo Gracias a Dios</a> on June 3. By the next day, the circulation had reached the surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> which marked the development of a tropical depression over eastern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. On June 5, the depression crossed Honduras and emerged into the western Caribbean, moving north-northeastward through an area of warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>. It had a good cyclonic circulation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a>, and moisture content, but most significantly, a passing trough to the north provided the depression with favorable <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> On June 6, the depression rapidly intensified into Tropical Storm and later Hurricane Alma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> This made it the earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">hurricane in the basin</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Able_(1951)" title="Hurricane Able (1951)">Hurricane Able</a> in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ld_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ld-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After attaining hurricane status, Alma continued moving slowly to the north-northeast, although its intensification rate slowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> While over the western Caribbean, Alma was a large hurricane with gale-force winds covering about 200,000 sq mi (520,000 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-4">[4]</a></sup> The hurricane accelerated on June 8, quickly moving across <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isla de la Juventud</a> and later mainland <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> with winds of around 100 mph (160 km/h). Alma did not weaken over land and entered the Gulf of Mexico, where it turned northwestward and passed between Key West and the <a href="/wiki/Dry_Tortugas" title="Dry Tortugas">Dry Tortugas</a>. Late on June 8, a station in the Dry Tortugas reported winds of 125 mph (201 km/h); this would be Alma's peak intensity. The hurricane began weakening as it paralleled the west coast of Florida, at which point a developing upper-level low over the northern Gulf of Mexico began to influence its motion. While the hurricane moved northward through the Gulf of Mexico, it had a large, ragged <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> that reached 75 mi (121 km) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> Initially, forecasters anticipated Alma would cross central Florida, although the hurricane turned to the north-northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verne_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-verne-5">[5]</a></sup> Cool water temperatures contributed to weakening, and Alma made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near Apalachee Bay with winds of 90 mph (140 km/h) on June 9. This marked the earliest United States hurricane landfall since a hurricane in May and June of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1820%E2%80%931829_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons#1825_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1820–1829 Atlantic hurricane seasons">1825</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>While turning northeastward over northern Florida, Alma quickly weakened to a tropical storm. It moved across Georgia and emerged into the Atlantic near <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah</a> late on June 10. Its circulation remained near the coast, although by late on June 11, Alma had moved far enough offshore that it was able to re-intensify into a hurricane. It developed a large eye and maintained hurricane intensity for about 18 hours. Afterward, Alma turned to the north into an area of drier air and colder waters, and again weakened to a tropical storm on June 12. The next day, Alma transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> to the east of Maryland. The remnants continued to the northeast and dissipated on June 14 over Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In Cuba, evacuations were ordered for <a href="/wiki/Nueva_Gerona" title="Nueva Gerona">Nueva Gerona</a> on Isla de la Juventud, and for 113,754 people on the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sjnp_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sjnp-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-spok-7">[7]</a></sup> Cattle were moved to a safe location for the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sjnp_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-sjnp-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/President_of_Cuba" title="President of Cuba">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> declared a state of emergency in four western <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Cuba" title="Provinces of Cuba">provinces</a> due to the threat to the local sugar industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before Alma struck Florida, officials issued <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warnings</a> from the Florida Keys through <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">Apalachicola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> A hurricane watch and gale warning were issued for the state's eastern coastline, extending to Savannah, Georgia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim14_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim14-9">[9]</a></sup> In Key West, residents boarded up houses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> and about 1,000 people fled to the region's 11 shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/MIM-23_Hawk" title="MIM-23 Hawk">MIM-23 Hawk</a> antiaircraft missiles in the city were moved to higher ground, having been in place since the 1962 <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ld_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ld-3">[3]</a></sup> Further north, officials ordered the evacuation of <a href="/wiki/Captiva_Island" title="Captiva Island">Captiva Island</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanibel_Island" title="Sanibel Island">Sanibel Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> The causeways to Sanibel Island were closed, isolating residents who had not evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-day-12">[12]</a></sup> Ten emergency shelters were opened in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers,_Florida" title="Fort Myers, Florida">Fort Myers</a> and two in <a href="/wiki/Naples,_Florida" title="Naples, Florida">Naples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> Overall, about 100,000 people were advised to seek shelter, though many of them chose to stay in their homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-day-12">[12]</a></sup> All <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II" title="McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II">Phantom jet fighters</a> at <a href="/wiki/MacDill_Air_Force_Base" title="MacDill Air Force Base">MacDill Air Force Base</a> in Tampa were transported to <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Air_Force_Base" title="Columbus Air Force Base">Columbus Air Force Base</a> in Mississippi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> while aircraft were also evacuated from <a href="/wiki/Homestead_Air_Reserve_Base" title="Homestead Air Reserve Base">Homestead Air Reserve Base</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-11">[11]</a></sup> Because Alma was initially forecast to cross the central portion of the state, residents along the Florida panhandle were not adequately prepared for the hurricane. Just hours before the storm moved ashore, officials ordered evacuations for residents in low-lying areas around Apalachicola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verne_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-verne-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>One week before a scheduled hurricane preparedness drill at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>, Alma presented the facility with an actual storm threat. A full-scale version of a <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket was rolled back to the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">Vehicle Assembly Building</a> on June 8 in less than 11 hours, which was within the anticipated time for such a move and before the expected arrival of 60 mph (97 km/h) wind gusts. Two other rockets were also removed from their pads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Alma_(1966)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During its formative stages, Alma produced heavy rainfall for several days across Central America, primarily in Nicaragua and Honduras. Overnight on June 5, the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael,_Lempira" title="San Rafael, Lempira">San Rafael, Honduras</a> recorded about 30 in (760 mm) of precipitation, possibly in relation to a localized <a href="/wiki/Cloudburst" title="Cloudburst">cloudburst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> The extreme rainfall flooded the entire city and left most of it covered with about 3 ft (1 m) of mud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-4">[4]</a></sup> Most of San Rafael was destroyed, and there were at 73 deaths in the city;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> one news report indicated that 75 people died in the city. Many rivers in Honduras became swollen at the height of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc-14">[14]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Swan_Islands,_Honduras" title="Swan Islands, Honduras">Swan Island</a>, offshore northern Honduras, the storm dropped 14.28 in (363 mm) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-15">[15]</a></sup> There was little damage on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-miami-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Before affecting Cuba, Alma spawned a tornado in <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Brac" title="Cayman Brac">Cayman Brac</a> that destroyed a few houses and power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim14_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-prelim14-9">[9]</a></sup> On Isla de la Juventud, the combination of high winds and rains destroyed dairy facilities, chicken farms, and large areas of fruit crops. The storm also destroyed a large radio tower on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup> When Alma struck Cuba, it produced high tides in southern <a href="/wiki/La_Habana_Province" title="La Habana Province">La Habana Province</a> that destroyed many fishing boats and wharves. Several towns along the coastline were flooded during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fort_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fort-17">[17]</a></sup> The hurricane crossed western Cuba while strengthening. The <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a> Institute of Meteorology recorded winds of 110 mph (180 km/h) and a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 980 mbar (29 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> The strong winds knocked down trees and power lines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> including in the capital city of Havana where hundreds of trees fell. The city had power outages and damage to its water systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fort_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-fort-17">[17]</a></sup> More than 1,000 houses were damaged in Havana. Many of them were poorly constructed due to lack of government funding to repair the gradually deteriorating structures in the tropical climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup> High winds sank a boat in <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo_Province" title="Pinar del Río Province">Pinar del Río Province</a>; one of the occupants drowned, and the other two swam to shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alma caused significant crop damage in western Cuba;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> it destroyed 13,400 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a> (5,400 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a>) of bananas and 18,090 acres (7,320 hectares) of corn fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-spok-7">[7]</a></sup> There were also heavy losses to fruit trees and to the sugar industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-spok-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> after the storm destroyed sugar mills and the rail lines that carried the cane to the mills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup> The storm destroyed over 100 tobacco drying barns, including their contents. The destruction of the tobacco represented a $30 million loss for the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup> High winds also destroyed 96 tobacco storage facilities, although the tobacco was removed beforehand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fort_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-fort-17">[17]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCines" title="Güines">Güines</a> near Havana, Alma destroyed food crops, which further exacerbated Cuba's existing food shortages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup> Alma's passage also resulted in water shortages, prompting the government to urge residents to use less water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-11">[11]</a></sup> There was heavy structural damage across western Cuba, including in Havana, <a href="/wiki/Matanzas" title="Matanzas">Matanzas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey" title="Camagüey">Camagüey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Pinar del Río">Pinar del Río</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Villa_Clara_Province" title="Villa Clara Province">Las Villas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup> The hurricane destroyed 1,083 houses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-spok-7">[7]</a></sup> and overall damage was estimated at $200 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> Alma killed 12 people in the country and injured 78 others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-spok-7">[7]</a></sup> In the storm's aftermath, Cuban officials sent about 100,000 people from Havana to the cities around the capital to repair storm damage. Such forced labor was a complaint about the rule of Fidel Castro, after the food and supply shortages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-mn-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Alma affected the entire state of Florida with strong winds and rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-tol-18">[18]</a></sup> While the hurricane passed east of the Dry Tortugas, a station recorded winds of 125 mph (201 km/h), which were the highest winds on land. The station also recorded a pressure of 970 mbar (29 inHg), which was the lowest pressure recorded on land. Wind gusts reached 70 mph (110 km/h) in Key West,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> causing a power outage. Rising tides flooded streets in the city,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-spt-10">[10]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_Road_A1A" title="Florida State Road A1A">A1A</a> which sustained damage to an adjacent seawall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-11">[11]</a></sup> Damage in the lower Florida Keys was estimated at around $300,000. In southwestern Florida, the hurricane heavily damaged the mango crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> High rainfall totals occurred across the state, peaking at 9.94 in (252 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Dania_Beach,_Florida" title="Dania Beach, Florida">Dania Beach</a> near Miami.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-15">[15]</a></sup> There were eight tornadoes or funnel clouds in the state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> including one in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Dade County</a> that damaged a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Along the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Pinellas_County,_Florida" title="Pinellas County, Florida">Pinellas County</a>, the storm damaged the grapefruit crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> High winds, including a gust of 93 mph (150 km/h), caused power outages in the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Area" title="Tampa Bay Area">Tampa Bay Area</a>. A wind gust knocked a car off of a bridge between Tampa and St. Petersburg, although the occupants were able to escape safely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-day-12">[12]</a></sup> As it paralleled the coastline, Alma produced a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_tide" title="Storm tide">storm tide</a> of 10 ft (3.0 m) in <a href="/wiki/New_Port_Richey,_Florida" title="New Port Richey, Florida">New Port Richey</a>. The high tides caused significant flooding in <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Key,_Florida" title="Cedar Key, Florida">Cedar Key</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> In northern Florida where Alma moved ashore, wind gusts reached 100 mph (160 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Crawfordville,_Florida" title="Crawfordville, Florida">Crawfordville</a>; the high winds damaged the local tobacco crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> There were six deaths, of which two were electrocutions, two were drownings, and two were from heart attacks related to the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> One of the deaths was a man who had a heart attack while preparing to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-11">[11]</a></sup> Damage throughout Florida was estimated at around $10 million. This was less than other storms of similar intensity, due to the sparse population of the affected area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further north, Alma produced 44 mph (71 km/h) wind gusts and 2.18 in (55 mm) in Savannah, Georgia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-21"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> High winds damaged a few houses and downed trees near <a href="/wiki/Albany,_Georgia" title="Albany, Georgia">Albany, Georgia</a>. The winds overturned a bus at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Gordon" title="Fort Gordon">Fort Gordon</a>, injuring 11 soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-tol-18">[18]</a></sup> The storm caused about $50,000 in damage in the state. Effects were minor in South Carolina, although heavy rainfall totaled 7.80 in (198 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>. Rainfall and increased storm tides extended into Virginia and New Jersey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-22"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
