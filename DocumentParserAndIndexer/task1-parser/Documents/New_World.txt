<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">New World</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>New World</b> is one of the names used for the majority of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> (including nearby islands such as those of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Caribbean_islands" title="List of Caribbean islands">Caribbean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>).</p>
<p>The term originated in the early 16th century after <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">Europeans made landfall</a> in what would later be called the Americas in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_discovery" title="Age of discovery">age of discovery</a>, expanding the geographical horizon of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_geography" title="Greco-Roman geography">classical geographers</a>, who had thought of the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a> as consisting of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, collectively now referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> (a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">Afro-Eurasia</a>).</p>
<p>The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Florence" title="Republic of Florence">Florentine</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a>. The Americas were also referred to as the "fourth part of the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.H.Davidson_1997_p.417_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.H.Davidson_1997_p.417-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms "<a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>" vs. "New World" are meaningful in historical context and for the purpose of distinguishing the world's major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecozones" title="Ecozones">ecozones</a>, and to classify plant and animal species that originated therein.</p>
<p>One can speak of the "New World" in a <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">historical</a> context, e.g., when discussing the voyages of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Spanish conquest of Yucatán">Spanish conquest of Yucatán</a> and other events of the <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">colonial</a> period. For lack of alternatives, the term is also still useful to those discussing issues that concern the Americas and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Island#Oceanic_islands" title="Island">oceanic islands</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clipperton_Island" title="Clipperton Island">Clipperton Island</a>, collectively.</p>
<p>The term "New World" is used in a <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> context, when one speaks of Old World (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palearctic_ecozone" title="Palearctic ecozone">Palearctic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afrotropic_ecozone" title="Afrotropic ecozone">Afrotropic</a>) and New World species (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nearctic_ecozone" title="Nearctic ecozone">Nearctic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropic_ecozone" title="Neotropic ecozone">Neotropic</a>). Biological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_classification" title="Biological classification">taxonomists</a> often attach the "New World" label to groups of species that are found exclusively in the Americas, to distinguish them from their counterparts in the "Old World" (Europe, Africa and Asia), e.g. <a href="/wiki/New_World_monkey" title="New World monkey">New World monkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_World_vulture" title="New World vulture">New World vultures</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_World_warbler" title="New World warbler">New World warblers</a>.</p>
<p>The label is also often used in agriculture. Africa, Asia, and Europe share a common agricultural history stemming from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a>, and the same domesticated plants and animals spread through these three continents thousands of years ago, making them largely indistinct and useful to classify together as "Old World". Common Old World crops (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oats" title="Oats">oats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>), and domesticated animals (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>) did not exist in the Americas until they were introduced by post-<a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">Columbian contact</a> in the 1490s (see "<a href="/wiki/Columbian_Exchange" title="Columbian Exchange">Columbian Exchange</a>"). Conversely, many common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crops" title="Crops">crops</a> were originally domesticated in the Americas before they spread worldwide after Columbian contact, and are still often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/New_World_crops" title="New World crops">New World crops</a>"; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_bean" title="Common bean">common beans</a> (<i>phaseolus</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maiz" title="Maiz">maiz</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squash_(plant)" title="Squash (plant)">squash</a> – the "<a href="/wiki/Three_Sisters_(agriculture)" title="Three Sisters (agriculture)">three sisters</a>" – as well as the <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomato</a>, and wide varieties of <a href="/wiki/Capsicum" title="Capsicum">capsicum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bell_pepper" title="Bell pepper">bell pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili pepper</a>, etc.), and the <a href="/wiki/Turkey_(bird)" title="Turkey (bird)">turkey</a> were originally domesticated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> peoples in <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, while agriculturalists in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andean" title="Andean">Andean</a> region of South America brought forth the <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a> and domesticated animals like the <a href="/wiki/Alpaca" title="Alpaca">alpaca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pig" title="Guinea pig">guinea pig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llama</a>. Other famous New World crops include the <a href="/wiki/Cashew" title="Cashew">cashew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">sunflower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a>, and fruits like the <a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">guava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papaya" title="Papaya">papaya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a>. There are rare instances of overlap, e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Calabash" title="Calabash">calabash</a> (bottle-gourd), <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yam_(vegetable)" title="Yam (vegetable)">yam</a>, and the dog, are believed to have been domesticated separately in both the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old</a> and New World, their early forms possibly brought along by <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a> from Asia during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine_terminology" title="Wine terminology">wine terminology</a>, "New World" has a different definition. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_World_wines" title="New World wines">New World wines</a>" include not only North American and South American wines, but also those from South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, and all other locations outside the traditional wine-growing regions of Europe, North Africa and the Near East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wine_Folly_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wine_Folly-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_term">Origin of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stradanus_America.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="337" data-file-width="480" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stradanus_America.jpg/220px-Stradanus_America.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stradanus_America.jpg/330px-Stradanus_America.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stradanus_America.jpg/440px-Stradanus_America.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stradanus_America.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Allegory of the New World: <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> awakens the sleeping America</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The term "New World" ("Mundus Novus") was first coined by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Florence" title="Republic of Florence">Florentine</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a>, in a letter written to his friend and former patron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici_(1463%E2%80%931503)" title="Lorenzo de' Medici (1463–1503)">Lorenzo di Pier Francesco de' Medici</a> in the Spring of 1503, and published (in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>) in 1503–04 under the title <i>Mundus Novus</i>. Vespucci's letter contains arguably the first explicit articulation in print of the hypothesis that the lands discovered by European navigators to the west were not the edges of Asia, as asserted by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, but rather an entirely different continent, a "New World".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.H.Davidson_1997_p.417_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-M.H.Davidson_1997_p.417-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vespucci first approached this realization in June 1502, during a famous chance meeting between two different expeditions at the watering stop of "Bezeguiche" (the Bay of <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>) – his own outgoing expedition, on its way to chart the coast of newly discovered <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and the vanguard ships of the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Cabral,_1500)" title="2nd Portuguese India Armada (Cabral, 1500)">Second Portuguese India armada</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral" title="Pedro Álvares Cabral">Pedro Álvares Cabral</a>, returning home from India. Having already visited the Americas in prior years, Vespucci probably found it difficult to reconcile what he had already seen in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, with what the returning sailors told him of the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>. Vespucci wrote a preliminary letter to Lorenzo, while anchored at Bezeguiche, which he sent back with the Portuguese fleet – at this point only expressing a certain puzzlement about his conversations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Vespucci was finally convinced when he proceeded on his mapping expedition through 1501–02, covering the huge stretch of coast of eastern Brazil. After returning from Brazil, in the Spring of 1503, Amerigo Vespucci composed the <i>Mundus Novus</i> letter in Lisbon to Lorenzo in Florence, with its famous opening paragraph:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In passed days I wrote very fully to you of my return from new countries, which have been found and explored with the ships, at the cost and by the command of this Most Serene King of Portugal; and it is lawful to call it a new world, because none of these countries were known to our ancestors and all who hear about them they will be entirely new. For the opinion of the ancients was, that the greater part of the world beyond the equinoctial line to the south was not land, but only sea, which they have called the Atlantic; and even if they have affirmed that any continent is there, they have given many reasons for denying it is inhabited. But this opinion is false, and entirely opposed to the truth. My last voyage has proved it, for I have found a continent in that southern part; full of animals and more populous than our Europe, or Asia, or Africa, and even more temperate and pleasant than any other region known to us.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Vespucci's letter was a publishing sensation in Europe, immediately (and repeatedly) reprinted in several other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_usage">Prior usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prior usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Amerigo Vespucci is usually credited for coming up with the term "New World" (<i>Mundus Novus</i>) for the Americas in his 1503 letter, certainly giving it its popular cachet, similar terms had nonetheless been used and applied before him.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Alvise_Cadamosto" title="Alvise Cadamosto">Alvise Cadamosto</a> had used the term "un altro mundo" ("another world") to refer to <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>, which he explored in 1455 and 1456 on behalf of the Portuguese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, this was merely a literary flourish, not a suggestion of a new "fourth" part of the world. Cadamosto was quite aware sub-Saharan Africa was firmly part of the African continent.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a>-born Spanish chronicler <a href="/wiki/Peter_Martyr_d%27Anghiera" title="Peter Martyr d'Anghiera">Peter Martyr d'Anghiera</a> often shares credit with Vespucci for designating the Americas as a new world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Peter Martyr used the term <i>Orbe Novo</i> (literally, "New Globe", but often translated as "New World") in the title of his history of the discovery of the Americas as a whole, which began to appear in 1511 (cosmologically, "orbis" as used here refers to the whole hemisphere, while "mundus" refers to the land within it).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Peter Martyr had been writing and circulating private letters commenting on Columbus's discoveries since 1493 and, from the start, doubted Columbus's claims to have reached East Asia ("the Indies"), and consequently came up with alternative names to refer to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Only a few weeks after Columbus's return from his first voyage, Peter Martyr wrote letters referring to Columbus's discovered lands as the "western antipodes" ("antipodibus occiduis", letter of May 14, 1493),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the "new hemisphere of the earth" ("novo terrarum hemisphaerio", September 13, 1493),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and in a letter dated November 1, 1493, refers to Columbus as the "discoverer of the new globe" ("Colonus ille novi orbis repertor").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A year later (October 20, 1494), Peter Martyr again refers to the marvels of the New Globe ("Novo Orbe") and the "Western hemisphere."("ab occidente hemisphero").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> touched the continent of South America in his 1498 <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus#Third_voyage" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">third voyage</a>. In his own 1499 letter to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> of Spain, reporting the results of his third voyage, Columbus relates how the massive waters of the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> delta rushing into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Paria" title="Gulf of Paria">Gulf of Paria</a> implied that a previously unknown continent must lie behind it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, bowing to the classical tripartite division of the world, Columbus discards that hypothesis and proposes instead that the South American landmass is not a "fourth" continent, but rather the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_paradise" title="Terrestrial paradise">terrestrial paradise</a> of Biblical tradition, not a previously unknown "new" part of the world, but a land already "known" (but location undiscovered) by <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In another letter (to the nurse of Prince John, written 1500), Columbus refers to having reached a "new heavens and world" ("nuevo cielo e mundo")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and that he had placed "another world" ("otro mundo") under the dominion of the Kings of Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acceptance">Acceptance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acceptance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Vespucci passage above applied the "New World" label to merely the continental landmass of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> At the time, most of the continent of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> was not yet discovered, and Vespucci's comments did not eliminate the possibility that the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">Antilles</a> discovered earlier by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> might still be the eastern edges of Asia, as Columbus continued to insist down to his dying day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A critical step in the transition was the conference of navigators (<i>Junta de Navegantes</i>) assembled by the Spanish monarchs at <a href="/wiki/Toro,_Zamora" title="Toro, Zamora">Toro</a> in 1505, and continued at <a href="/wiki/Burgos" title="Burgos">Burgos</a> in 1508, to digest all existing information about the Indies, come to an agreement on what had really been discovered, and set out the future goals of Spanish exploration. Amerigo Vespucci attended both conferences, and seems to have had an outsized influence on them – Vespucci ended up being appointed the first <i>piloto mayor</i>, the chief of navigation of Spain, at Burgos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Although the proceedings of the Toro-Burgos conferences are missing, it is almost certain that Vespucci articulated his recent "New World" thesis to his fellow navigators there. It was during these conferences when Spanish officials seem to have finally accepted that the Antilles and the known stretch of Central America were definitely <i>not</i> the Indies they had originally sought, and Columbus had insisted they were, and set out the new goal for Spanish explorers: to find a sea passage or <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">strait</a> through the Americas which would permit them to sail to Asia proper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In English usage the term "New World" was problematic and only accepted relatively late.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartographic_representation">Cartographic representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cartographic representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>While it became generally accepted after Vespucci that Columbus's discoveries were not Asia but a "New World", the geographic relationship between the two continents was still unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-parry-23">[23]</a></sup> That there must be a large ocean between Asia and the Americas was implied by the known existence of vast continuous sea along the coasts of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. Given the size of the Earth as calculated by <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a> this left a large space between Asia and the newly discovered lands.</p>
<p>Even prior to Vespucci, several maps, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Cantino_planisphere" title="Cantino planisphere">Cantino planisphere</a> of 1502 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canerio_map" title="Canerio map">Canerio map</a> of 1504, placed a large open ocean between China on the east side of the map, and the inchoate largely water-surrounded North American and South American discoveries on the western side of map. However, out of uncertainty, they depicted a finger of the Asian land mass stretching across the top to the eastern edge of the map, suggesting it carried over into the western hemisphere (e.g. the Cantino Planisphere denotes <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> as "Punta d'Asia" – "edge of Asia"). Some maps, e.g. the 1506 <a href="/wiki/Contarini%E2%80%93Rosselli_map" title="Contarini–Rosselli map">Contarini–Rosselli map</a> and the 1508 <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Ruysch" title="Johannes Ruysch">Johannes Ruysch</a> map, bowing to Ptolemaic authority and Columbus's assertions, have the northern Asian landmass stretching well into the western hemisphere and merging with known North America (Labrador, Newfoundland, etc.). These maps place the island of Japan near Cuba and leave the South American continent – Vespucci's "New World" proper – detached and floating below by itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-parry-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Waldseem%C3%BCller_map" title="Waldseemüller map">Waldseemüller map</a> of 1507, which accompanied the famous <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmographiae_Introductio" title="Cosmographiae Introductio">Cosmographiae Introductio</a></i> volume (which includes reprints of Vespucci's letters) comes closest to modernity by placing a completely open sea (with no stretching land fingers) between Asia on the eastern side and the New World (being represented two times in the same map in a different way: with and without a sea passage in the middle of what is now named Central America) on the western side – which (on what is now named South America) that same map famously labels simply "<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naming_of_America" title="Naming of America">America</a></i>". However, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Waldseem%C3%BCller" title="Martin Waldseemüller">Martin Waldseemüller</a>'s map of 1516 retreats considerably from his earlier map and back to classical authority, with the Asian land mass merging into North America (which he now calls <i>Terra de Cuba Asie partis</i>), and quietly drops the "America" label from South America, calling it merely <i><a href="/wiki/Terra_incognita" title="Terra incognita">Terra Incognita</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-parry-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The western coast of the New World – the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> – was only discovered in 1513 by <a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Núñez de Balboa">Vasco Núñez de Balboa</a>. But it would take a few more years – <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a>'s voyage of 1519–22 – to determine that the Pacific definitely formed a single large body of water separating Asia from the Americas. It would be several more years before the Pacific Coast of North America was mapped, dispelling lingering doubts. Of course, until the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Straits</a> in the 17th century, there was no absolute confirmation that Asia and North America were not connected, and some European maps of the 16th century still continued to hopefully depict North America connected by a land bridge to Asia (e.g. the 1533 <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Sch%C3%B6ner_globe" title="Johannes Schöner globe">Johannes Schöner globe</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-parry-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1524, the term was used by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">Giovanni da Verrazzano</a> in a record of his voyage that year along the Atlantic coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, land that is now part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
