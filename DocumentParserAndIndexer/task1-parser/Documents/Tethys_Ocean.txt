<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tethys Ocean</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <b>Tethys Ocean</b> (Ancient Greek: Τηθύς), <b>Tethys Sea</b> or <b>Neotethys</b> was an ocean during much of the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> era located between the ancient continents of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a>, before the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> oceans during the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name stems from the mythological Greek Sea Goddess <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(mythology)" title="Tethys (mythology)">Tethys</a>, sister and consort of <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a>, mother of the great rivers, lakes and fountains of the world and of the <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Oceanid</a> sea nymphs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_and_subdivisions">Terminology and subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology and subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eastern part of the Tethys Ocean is sometimes referred to as <i>Eastern Tethys</i>. The western part of the Tethys Ocean is called <i>Tethys Sea</i>, <i>Western Tethys Ocean</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Paratethys" title="Paratethys">Paratethys</a></i> or <i>Alpine Tethys Ocean</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral</a> Seas are thought to be its <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crustal</a> remains, though the Black Sea may in fact be a remnant of the older <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Tethys_Ocean" title="Paleo-Tethys Ocean">Paleo-Tethys Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van-der-Voo-1993_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Van-der-Voo-1993-1">[1]</a></sup> The <i>Western Tethys</i> was not simply a single open ocean. It covered many small plates, <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arcs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microcontinent" title="Microcontinent">microcontinents</a>. Many small <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_basin" title="Oceanic basin">Oceanic basins</a> (<a href="/wiki/Valais_Ocean" title="Valais Ocean">Valais Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piemont-Liguria_Ocean" title="Piemont-Liguria Ocean">Piemont-Liguria Ocean</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meliata_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meliata Ocean (page does not exist)">Meliata Ocean</a>) were separated from each other by continental <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a> on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alboran_plate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alboran plate (page does not exist)">Alboran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Plate" title="Iberian Plate">Iberian</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apulian_Plate" title="Apulian Plate">Apulian</a> plates. The high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eustasy" title="Eustasy">sea level</a> in the Mesozoic era flooded most of these continental domains, forming shallow seas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As theories have improved, scientists have extended the "Tethys" name to refer to 3 similar oceans that preceded it, separating the continental <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a>: in Asia, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleo-Tethys" title="Paleo-Tethys">Paleo-Tethys</a> (Devonian–Triassic), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meso-Tethys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meso-Tethys (page does not exist)">Meso-Tethys</a> (late Early Permian–Late Cretaceous) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ceno-Tethy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ceno-Tethy (page does not exist)">Ceno-Tethy</a> (Late-Triassic–Cenozoic) are recognized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metcalfe_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-metcalfe-2">[2]</a></sup> Neither Tethys oceans should be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Rheic_Ocean" title="Rheic Ocean">Rheic Ocean</a>, which existed to the west of them in the Silurian period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To the north of the Tethys, the then land mass was called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angaraland" title="Angaraland">Angaraland</a> and to the south of it, it was called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gondwanaland" title="Gondwanaland">Gondwanaland</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_theory">Modern theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a> (600 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>) into the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> (360 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>), the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Tethys_Ocean" title="Proto-Tethys Ocean">Proto-Tethys Ocean</a> existed and was situated between <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> to the north and <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> to the south.</p>
<p>From the <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> (440 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>) through the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> periods, the Paleo-Tethys Ocean existed between the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hunic_terranes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hunic terranes (page does not exist)">Hunic terranes</a> and Gondwana. Over a period of 400 million years, continental <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a> intermittently separated from Gondwana in the Southern Hemisphere to migrate northward to form Asia in the Northern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metcalfe_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-metcalfe-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Triassic_period">Triassic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Triassic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:249_global.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="771" data-file-width="766" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/249_global.png/220px-249_global.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/249_global.png/330px-249_global.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/249_global.png/440px-249_global.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:249_global.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plate tectonic reconstruction of the Tethys realm at 249 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>About 250 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> during the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a>, a new ocean began forming in the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Tethys_Ocean" title="Paleo-Tethys Ocean">Paleo-Tethys Ocean</a>. A rift formed along the northern continental shelf of Southern <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> (Gondwana). Over the next 60 million years, that piece of shelf, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cimmerian_Plate" title="Cimmerian Plate">Cimmeria</a>, traveled north, pushing the floor of the Paleo-Tethys Ocean under the eastern end of Northern <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> (i.e.<a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a>). The Tethys Ocean formed between Cimmeria and Gondwana, directly over where the Paleo-Tethys used to be.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_period">Jurassic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jurassic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> Period about 150<abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>, Cimmeria finally collided with Laurasia and stalled, so the ocean floor behind it <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">buckled under</a>, forming the <a href="/wiki/Tethyan_Trench" title="Tethyan Trench">Tethyan Trench</a>. Water levels rose, and the western Tethys shallowly covered significant portions of Europe, forming the first Tethys Sea. Around the same time, Laurasia and Gondwana began <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">drifting apart</a>, opening an extension of the Tethys Sea between them which today is the part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. As North and South America were still attached to the rest of Laurasia and Gondwana, respectively, the Tethys Ocean in its widest extension was part of a continuous oceanic belt running around the Earth between about <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> 30° N and the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> at the time around the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a> ran very differently from the way they do today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late Cretaceous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:100_global.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="736" data-file-width="737" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/100_global.png/220px-100_global.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/100_global.png/330px-100_global.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/100_global.png/440px-100_global.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:100_global.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plate tectonic reconstruction of the Tethys realm at 100 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Between the Jurassic and the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a>, which started about 100 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>, Gondwana began breaking up, pushing Africa and India north across the Tethys and opening up the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. As these land masses crowded in on the Tethys ocean from all sides, to as recently as the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Late Miocene</a> 15 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>, the ocean continued to shrink, becoming the Tethys Seaway or second Tethys Sea. Throughout the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> (66 million to the dawn of the Neogene, 23 million years), global sea levels fell hundreds of meters, and eventually the connections between the Atlantic and the Tethys closed off in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oligocene">Oligocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oligocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> (33.9 million to 23 million before annum), large parts of central and eastern Europe were covered by a northern branch of the Tethys Ocean, called the <a href="/wiki/Paratethys" title="Paratethys">Paratethys</a>. The Paratethys was separated from the Tethys with the formation of the Alps, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpathians" title="Carpathians">Carpathians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinarides" title="Dinarides">Dinarides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taurus_Mountains" title="Taurus Mountains">Taurus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elburz" title="Elburz">Elburz</a> mountains during the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_orogeny" title="Alpine orogeny">Alpine orogeny</a>. During the late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> the Paratethys gradually disappeared, and became an isolated inland sea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, India, Pakistan, Indonesia, and the Indian Ocean cover the area once occupied by the Tethys Ocean, and Turkey, Iraq, and Tibet sit on Cimmeria. What was once the western arm of the Tethys Sea was the ancestor of the present-day <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. Other remnants are the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral</a> Seas via a former inland branch known as the <a href="/wiki/Paratethys" title="Paratethys">Paratethys</a>. Most of the floor of the Tethys Ocean disappeared under Cimmeria and Laurasia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Geologists including <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Suess" title="Eduard Suess">Eduard Suess</a> have found fossils of ocean creatures in rocks in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, indicating that those rocks were once underwater, before the Indian continental shelf began <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">pushing upward</a> as it collided with Cimmeria.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Similar geologic evidence can be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_orogeny" title="Alpine orogeny">Alpine orogeny</a> of Europe, where the movement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_plate" title="African plate">African plate</a> raised the Alps. Greece and the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> also retain many units of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rocks" title="Sedimentary rocks">sedimentary rocks</a> deposited by various stands of the Tethys Ocean.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologist" title="Paleontologist">Paleontologists</a> find the Tethys Ocean particularly important, because much of the world's sea shelves were found around its margins for such an extensive length of time with Marine, marsh-dwelling, and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuarian</a> fossils from these shelves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solnhofen_limestone" title="Solnhofen limestone">Solnhofen limestone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, originally a coastal <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoon</a> mud of the Tethys Ocean, yielded the famous <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeopteryx" title="Archaeopteryx">Archaeopteryx</a></i> fossil.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_theory">Historical theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tethys_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Historical theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In 1885, the Austrian palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Melchior_Neumayr" title="Melchior Neumayr">Melchior Neumayr</a> deduced the existence of the Tethys Ocean from Mesozoic marine sediments and their distribution, calling his concept 'Zentrales Mittelmeer' and described it as a Jurassic seaway, which extended from the Caribbean to the Himalayas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1893, the Austrian geologist <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Suess" title="Eduard Suess">Eduard Suess</a> proposed the theory that an ancient and extinct <a href="/wiki/Inland_sea_(geology)" title="Inland sea (geology)">inland sea</a> had once existed between <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a> and the continents which formed <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana II</a>. He named it the 'Tethys Sea' after the Greek sea goddess Tethys. He provided evidence for his theory using fossil records from the Alps and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He proposed the concept of <i>Tethys</i> in his four-volume work <i>Das Antlitz der Erde</i> (<i>The Face of the Earth</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the following decades during the 20th century "<a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">mobilist</a>" geologists like Uhlig (1911), Diener(1925), and Daque (1926) regarded Tethys as a large trough between two supercontinents which lasted from the late Palaeozoic until continental fragments derived from Gondwana obliterated it. After WW2, Tethys was described as a triangular ocean with a wide eastern end.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From 1920s to the 1960s "fixist" geologists, however, regarded Tethys as a composite trough which evolved through a series of orogenic cycles. They used the terms 'Paleotethys', 'Mesotethys', and 'Neotethys' for the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Caledonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Variscan_orogeny" title="Variscan orogeny">Variscan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alpine_orogeny" title="Alpine orogeny">Alpine</a> orogenies respectively. In the 1970s and 1980s these terms and 'Proto-Tethys', were used in different senses by various authors, but the concept of a single ocean wedging into Pangea from the east, roughly where Suess first proposed it, remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, the theory of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> became established, and it became clear Suess's "sea" had in fact been an ocean. Plate tectonics provided an explanation for the mechanism by which the former ocean disappeared: <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> can <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduct</a> under <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Tethys was considered an oceanic plate by Smith (1971), Dewey, Pitman, Ryan and Bonnin (1973), Laubscher and Bernoulli (1973), Bijou-Duval, Dercourt and Pichon (1977),</p>
