<p><b>Debris</b> or <b>débris</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a>:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is <a href="/wiki/Rubble" title="Rubble">rubble</a>, wreckage, <a href="/wiki/Ruins" title="Ruins">ruins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Litter" title="Litter">litter</a> and discarded <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">garbage/refuse/trash</a>, scattered remains of something destroyed, discarded, or as in geology, large rock fragments left by a melting glacier etc. Depending on context, <i>debris</i> can refer to a number of different things. The first apparent use of the French word in English is in a 1701 description of the army of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine" title="Prince Rupert of the Rhine">Prince Rupert</a> upon its retreat from a battle with the army of Oliver Cromwell, in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disaster">Disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In disaster scenarios, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornados</a> leave behind large pieces of houses and mass destruction overall. This debris also flies around the tornado itself when it is in progress. The tornado's winds capture debris it kicks up in its wind orbit, and spins it inside its vortex. The tornado's wind radius is larger than the funnel itself. tsunamis and hurricanes also bring large amounts of debris, such as Hurricane Katrina in 2005 and Hurricane Sandy in 2012. Earthquakes rock cities to rubble debris.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological">Geological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ObsidianDomeCA.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/ObsidianDomeCA.JPG/220px-ObsidianDomeCA.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/ObsidianDomeCA.JPG/330px-ObsidianDomeCA.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/ObsidianDomeCA.JPG/440px-ObsidianDomeCA.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, debris usually applies to the remains of geological activity including <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> explosions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalanches" title="Avalanches">avalanches</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudslide" title="Mudslide">mudflows</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake_outburst_flood" title="Glacial lake outburst flood">Glacial lake outburst floods</a> (Jökulhlaups) and <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lahars" title="Lahars">lahars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> eruptions. Geological debris sometimes moves in a <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Debris_flow" title="Debris flow">debris flow</a>. When it accumulates at the base of hillsides, it can be called "talus" or "<a href="/wiki/Scree" title="Scree">scree</a>".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, debris called <i>attle</i> usually consists of rock fragments which contain little or no ore.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine">Marine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">marine debris</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marine_debris_on_Hawaiian_coast.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="432" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Marine_debris_on_Hawaiian_coast.jpg/220px-Marine_debris_on_Hawaiian_coast.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Marine_debris_on_Hawaiian_coast.jpg/330px-Marine_debris_on_Hawaiian_coast.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/Marine_debris_on_Hawaiian_coast.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p><i>Marine debris</i> applies to floating garbage such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottles" title="Bottles">bottles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin_can" title="Tin can">cans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Styrofoam" title="Styrofoam">styrofoam</a>, cruise <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a> waste, offshore <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> exploration and production facilities <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> paraphernalia from professional and recreational boaters. Marine debris is also called <a href="/wiki/Litter" title="Litter">litter</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flotsam_and_jetsam" title="Flotsam and jetsam">flotsam and jetsam</a>. Objects that can constitute marine debris include used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tires" title="Tires">tires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottles" title="Bottles">bottles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_waste" title="Medical waste">medical wastes</a>, discarded fishing line and <a href="/wiki/Net_(device)" title="Net (device)">nets</a>, soda cans, and <a href="/wiki/Bilge" title="Bilge">bilge</a> waste solids.</p>
<p>In addition to being unsightly, it can pose a serious threat to marine life, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boats" title="Boats">boats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_swimming" title="Human swimming">swimmers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">divers</a>, and others. For example, each year millions of seabirds, sea <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, and marine <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> become entangled in marine debris, or ingest plastics which they have mistaken for food. As many as 30,000 northern fur seals per year get caught in abandoned fishing nets and either drown or suffocate. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whales" title="Whales">Whales</a> mistake <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> bags for <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds">birds</a> may mistake plastic pellets for fish <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">eggs</a>. At other times, animals accidentally eat the plastic while feeding on natural food.</p>
<p>The largest concentration of marine debris is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch" title="Great Pacific Garbage Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a>.</p>
<p>Marine debris most commonly originates from land-based sources. Various international agencies are currently working to reduce marine debris levels around the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological">Meteorological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meteorological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, debris usually applies to the remains of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> habitation and natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a> after <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> related destruction. This debris is also commonly referred to as <b>storm debris</b>. Storm debris commonly consists of roofing material, downed <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> <a href="/wiki/Branch" title="Branch">limbs</a>, downed <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/signs" title="wikt:signs">signs</a>, downed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_line" title="Power line">power lines</a> and poles, and wind-blown garbage. Storm debris can become a serious problem immediately after a storm, in that it often blocks access to individuals and communities that may require emergency services. This material frequently exists in such large quantities that disposing of it becomes a serious issue for a community. In addition, storm debris is often hazardous by its very nature, since, for example, downed power lines annually account for storm-related deaths.</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debris_ball" title="Debris ball">debris ball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Road_debris" title="Road debris">road debris</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space">Space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a></div>
<p><i>Space debris</i> usually refers to the remains of <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> that have either fallen to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> or are still orbiting Earth. Space debris may also consist of natural components such as chunks of <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>. The problem of space debris has grown as various space programs have left legacies of launches, explosions, repairs, and discards in both low Earth <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> and more remote orbits. These orbiting fragments have reached a great enough proportion to constitute a hazard to future space launches of both <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> and manned vehicles. Various government agencies and international organizations are beginning to track space debris and also research possible solutions to the problem. While many of these items, ranging in size from <a href="/wiki/Nut_(hardware)" title="Nut (hardware)">nuts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Screw" title="Screw">bolts</a> to entire satellites and spacecraft, may fall to Earth, other items located in more remote orbits may stay aloft for centuries. The velocity of some of these pieces of space junk have been clocked in excess of 17,000 miles per hour (27,000 km/h). A piece of space debris falling to Earth leaves a fiery trail, just like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteor</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">debris disk</a> is a circumstellar disk of dust and debris in orbit around a star.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgical">Surgical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surgical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, debris usually refers to biological matter that has accumulated or lodged in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgical_instruments" title="Surgical instruments">surgical instruments</a> and is referred to as <b>surgical debris</b>. The presence of surgical debris can result in cross-infections or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nosocomial_infection" title="Nosocomial infection">nosocomial infections</a> if not removed and the affected surgical instruments or equipment properly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfect" title="Disinfect">disinfected</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War">War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In the aftermath of a war, large areas of the region of conflict are often strewn with <i>war debris</i> in the form of abandoned or destroyed hardware and vehicles, mines, <a href="/wiki/Unexploded_ordnance" title="Unexploded ordnance">unexploded ordnance</a>, bullet casings and other fragments of metal.</p>
<p>Much war debris has the potential to be lethal and continues to kill and maim civilian populations for years after the end of a conflict. The risks from war debris may be sufficiently high to prevent or delay the return of refugees. In addition war debris may contain hazardous chemicals or radioactive components that can contaminate the land or poison civilians who come into contact with it. Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mine_clearance_agencies" title="Mine clearance agencies">Mine clearance agencies</a> are also involved in the clearance of war debris.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_mines" title="Land mines">Land mines</a> in particular are very dangerous as they can remain active for decades after a conflict, which is why they have been banned by international war regulations.</p>
<p>In November 2006 the Protocol on Explosive Remnants of War<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> came into effect with 92 countries subscribing to the treaty that requires the parties involved in a conflict to assist with the removal of unexploded ordnance following the end of hostilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the countries most affected by war debris are <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>.</p>
<p>Similarly <i>military debris</i> may be found in and around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firing_range" title="Firing range">firing ranges</a> and military training areas.</p>
<p>Debris can also be used as <a href="/wiki/Cover_(military)" title="Cover (military)">cover</a> for military purposes, depending on the situation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary">Culinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In South Louisiana's <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_cuisine" title="Louisiana Creole cuisine">Creole</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cajun" title="Cajun">Cajun</a> cultures, debris (pronounced "DAY-bree") refers to chopped organs such as liver, heart, kidneys, tripe, spleen, brain, lungs and pancreas.</p>
