<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Windward and leeward</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Windward</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the direction upwind (toward where the wind is coming from) from the point of reference. <b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Leeward" title="wikt:Leeward">Leeward</a></b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈluːɜ(ɹ)d/</span>) is the direction downwind (or downward) from the point of reference. The side of a ship that is towards the leeward is its <b>lee side</b>. If the vessel is <a href="/wiki/Sailing#Heeling" title="Sailing">heeling</a> under the pressure of the wind, this will be the "lower side". During the age of sail, the term <i>weather</i> was used as a synonym for <i>windward</i> in some contexts, as in the <i><a href="/wiki/Weather_gage" title="Weather gage">weather gage</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical_and_naval">Nautical and naval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windward_and_leeward&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nautical and naval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">Sailing</a></div>
<p>Windward and leeward directions are important factors (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Points_of_sail" title="Points of sail">points of sail</a>) to consider when <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> a <a href="/wiki/Sailing_ship" title="Sailing ship">sailing ship</a>. Other terms with broadly the same meaning are widely used, particularly <i>upwind</i> and <i>downwind</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Royce1993_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Royce1993-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The windward vessel is normally the more maneuverable vessel. For this reason, rule 12 of the <a href="/wiki/International_Regulations_for_Preventing_Collisions_at_Sea" title="International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea">International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea</a> stipulates that the windward vessel gives way to the leeward vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_warfare">Naval warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windward_and_leeward&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naval warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In warfare, a <a href="/wiki/Square_rig" title="Square rig">square rigged</a> warship would often try to enter battle from the windward direction (or "hold the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_gauge" title="Weather gauge">weather gauge</a>"), thus gaining an important tactical advantage over the opposing warship – the warship to windward could choose when to engage and when to withdraw. The opposing warship to leeward could often do little but comply without exposing itself unduly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childs2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Childs2014-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>This was particularly important once <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> was introduced to naval warfare. The ships heeled away from the wind so that the leeward vessel was exposing part of her bottom to shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willis2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_significance">Meteorological significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windward_and_leeward&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meteorological significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Leeward</i> and <i>windward</i> refer respectively to what a <a href="/wiki/Game_stalker" title="Game stalker">game stalker</a> would call downwind and upwind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michell2015_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michell2015-5">[5]</a></sup> The terms are used by seamen in relation to their ships but also in reference to islands in an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> and to the different sides of a single island. In the latter case, the windward side is that side of an island subject to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prevailing_wind" title="Prevailing wind">prevailing wind</a>, and is thus the wetter side (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">orographic precipitation</a>). The leeward side is the side protected by the elevation of the island from the prevailing wind, and is typically the drier side of an island. Thus, leeward or windward siting is an important <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> factor on oceanic islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pidwirny2016_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pidwirny2016-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the case of an archipelago, <i>windward islands</i> are upwind and <i>leeward islands</i> are the downwind ones.</p>
