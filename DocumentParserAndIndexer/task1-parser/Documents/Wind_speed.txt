<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Wind speed</h1>

<p><b>Wind speed</b>, or wind <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">flow velocity</a>, is a fundamental <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> quantity.</p>
<p>Wind speed is caused by air moving from high pressure to low pressure, usually due to changes in temperature. Note that wind direction is usually almost parallel to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">isobars</a> (and not perpendicular as one might expect), due to the rotation of the earth.</p>
<p>Wind speed affects <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and maritime operations, construction projects, growth and <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> rate of many plant species, and countless other implications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">Wind</a> speed is now commonly measured with an <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> but can also be classified using the older <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a> which is based on people's observation of specifically defined wind effects.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_affecting_wind_speed">Factors affecting wind speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_speed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Factors affecting wind speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wind speed is affected by a number of factors and situations, operating on varying scales (from micro to macro scales). These include the pressure gradient, Rossby waves and <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet streams</a>, and local weather conditions. There are also links to be found between wind speed and <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a>, notably with the pressure gradient and terrain conditions.</p>
<p><b>Pressure gradient</b> is a term to describe the difference in air pressure between two points in the atmosphere or on the surface of the Earth. It is vital to wind speed, because the greater the difference in pressure, the faster the wind flows (from the high to low pressure) to balance out the variation. The pressure gradient, when combined with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, also influences <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a>.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby waves</a></b> are strong winds in the upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. These operate on a global scale and move from West to East (hence being known as <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a>). The Rossby waves are themselves a different wind speed from what we experience in the lower <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>.</p>
<p><b>Local weather conditions</b> play a key role in influencing wind speed, as the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a> as freak weather conditions can drastically affect the flow velocity of the wind.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Highest_speed">Highest speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_speed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Highest speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4032" data-file-width="3024" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG/170px-The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG/255px-The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG/340px-The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Big_Wind_Anemometer.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The original anemometer that measured The Big Wind in 1934 at Mount Washington Observatory</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The fastest wind speed not related to <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> ever recorded was during the passage of Tropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Olivia" title="Cyclone Olivia">Cyclone Olivia</a> on 10 April 1996: an <a href="/wiki/Automatic_weather_station" title="Automatic weather station">automatic weather station</a> on <a href="/wiki/Barrow_Island_(Western_Australia)" title="Barrow Island (Western Australia)">Barrow Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, registered a maximum wind gust of 408 km/h (220 kn; 253 mph; 113 m/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The wind gust was evaluated by the WMO Evaluation Panel who found that the anemometer was mechanically sound and the gust was within statistical probability and ratified the measurement in 2010. The anemometer was mounted 10 m above ground level (and thus 64 m above sea level). During the cyclone, several extreme gusts of greater than 300 km/h (160 kn; 83 m/s) were recorded, with a maximum 5-minute mean speed of 176 km/h (95 kn; 110 mph; 49 m/s), the extreme gust factor was in the order of 2.27–2.75 times the mean wind speed. The pattern and scales of the gusts suggest that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesovortex" title="Mesovortex">mesovortex</a> was embedded in the already strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a> of the cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The now second highest surface wind speed ever officially recorded is 372 km/h (231 mph; 103 m/s) at the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Washington_(New_Hampshire)" title="Mount Washington (New Hampshire)">Mount Washington (New Hampshire)</a> Observatory: 6,288 ft (1917 m) above sea level in the US on 12 April 1934, using a <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">heated anemometer</a>. The anemometer, specifically designed for use on Mount Washington was later tested by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Bureau</a> and confirmed to be accurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wind speeds within certain atmospheric phenomena (such as <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>) may greatly exceed these values but have never been accurately measured. Directly measuring these tornadic winds is rarely done as the violent wind would destroy the instruments. Another method of estimating is to use <a href="/wiki/Doppler_on_Wheels" title="Doppler on Wheels">Doppler on Wheels</a> to sense the wind speeds remotely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The figure of 486 km/h (302 mph; 135 m/s) during the <a href="/wiki/1999_Bridge_Creek%E2%80%93Moore_tornado" title="1999 Bridge Creek–Moore tornado">1999 Bridge Creek–Moore tornado</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> on 3 May 1999 is often quoted as the highest-recorded surface wind speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, another figure of 512 kilometres per hour (318 mph) has also been quoted for the same tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldrecordacademy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldrecordacademy-7">[7]</a></sup> Yet another number used by the Center for Severe Weather Research for that measurement is 486 ± 32 km/h (302 ± 20 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, speeds measured by Doppler radar are not considered official records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldrecordacademy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-worldrecordacademy-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_of_structures">Design of structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_speed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Design of structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_engineering" title="Wind engineering">Wind engineering</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG/220px-Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG/330px-Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG/440px-Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anemometer_on_stage_set.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anemometer on an outdoor stage set, to measure wind speed</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wind speed is a common factor in the design of structures and buildings around the world. It is often the governing factor in the required lateral strength of a structure's design.</p>
<p>In the United States, the wind speed used in design is often referred to as a "3-second gust" which is the highest sustained gust over a 3-second period having a probability of being exceeded per year of 1 in 50 (ASCE 7-05). This design wind speed is accepted by most building codes in the United States and often governs the lateral design of buildings and structures.</p>
<p>In Canada, reference wind pressures are used in design and are based on the "mean hourly" wind speed having a probability of being exceeded per year of 1 in 50. The reference wind pressure (q) is calculated in Pascals using the following equation (ref: NBC 2005 Structural Commentaries - Part 4 of Div. B, Comm. I): q=(1/2)pV² where p is the air density in kg/m³ and V is wind speed in m/s.</p>
<p>Historically, wind speeds have been reported with a variety of averaging times (such as fastest mile, 3-second gust, 1-minute and mean hourly) which designers may have to take into account. To convert wind speeds from one averaging time to another, the Durst Curve (Ref: ASCE 7-05 commentary Figure C6-4, ASCE 7-10 C26.5-1) was developed which defines the relation between probable maximum wind speed averaged over t seconds, V<sub>t</sub>, and mean wind speed over one hour V<sub>3600</sub>.</p>
