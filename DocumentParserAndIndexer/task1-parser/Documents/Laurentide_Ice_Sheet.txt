<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Laurentide Ice Sheet</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Laurentide Ice Sheet</b> was a massive <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">sheet of ice</a> that covered millions of square kilometers, including most of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and a large portion of the northern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, multiple times during the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial" title="Glacial">glacial</a> epochs— from 2.588 ± 0.005 million years ago to the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICS2013_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICS2013-1">[1]</a></sup> The last advance covered most of northern North America between c. 95,000 and c. 20,000 years before the present day, and among other geomorphological effects, gouged out the five <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and the hosts of smaller lakes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_shield" title="Canadian shield">Canadian shield</a>. These lakes extend from the eastern <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, through most of northern Canada, and the upper Midwestern United States (<a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>) to the <a href="/wiki/Finger_Lakes" title="Finger Lakes">Finger Lakes</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_George_(New_York)" title="Lake George (New York)">Lake George</a> areas of New York, across the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appalachians" title="Appalachians">Appalachians</a> into and through all of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. At times, the ice sheet's southern margin included the present-day sites of northeastern coastal towns and cities such as <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_New_Hampshire" title="Portsmouth, New Hampshire">Portsmouth, New Hampshire</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, and Great Lakes coastal cities and towns as far south as <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>—and then followed quite precisely the present course of the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> up to the northern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Hills_(Canada)" title="Cypress Hills (Canada)">Cypress Hills</a>, beyond which it merged with the <a href="/wiki/Cordilleran_Ice_Sheet" title="Cordilleran Ice Sheet">Cordilleran Ice Sheet</a>. The ice coverage extended approximately as far south as 38 degrees latitude in the mid-continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dyke_prest_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dyke_prest-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_Ice_Sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pleistocene_north_ice_map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="250" height="367" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Pleistocene_north_ice_map.jpg/220px-Pleistocene_north_ice_map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/Pleistocene_north_ice_map.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pleistocene_north_ice_map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The maximum extent of <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">glacial ice</a> in the north polar area during the Pleistocene period included the vast Laurentide ice sheet in eastern North America.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This ice sheet was the primary feature of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> epoch in North America, commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">ice age</a>. It was up to 2 mi (3.2 km) thick in <a href="/wiki/Nunavik" title="Nunavik">Nunavik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> but much thinner at its edges where <a href="/wiki/Nunatak" title="Nunatak">nunataks</a> were common in hilly areas. It created much of the surface geology of southern Canada and the northern United States, leaving behind glacially scoured valleys, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moraines" title="Moraines">moraines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eskers" title="Eskers">eskers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_till" title="Glacial till">glacial till</a>. It also caused many changes to the shape, size, and drainage of the Great Lakes. As but one of many examples, near the end of the last ice age, <a href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Iroquois" title="Glacial Lake Iroquois">Lake Iroquois</a> extended well beyond the boundaries of present-day <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>, and drained down the Hudson River into the Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its cycles of growth and melting were a decisive influence on global <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> during its existence. This is because it served to divert the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> which would otherwise flow from the relatively warm <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> through <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> to the south. This gave the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">southwestern United States</a>, otherwise a desert, abundant rainfall during ice ages — in extreme contrast to most other parts of the world which became exceedingly dry, though the effect of ice sheets in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> had an analogous effect on the rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, possibly western <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in winter, as well as <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6000" data-file-width="4400" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg/200px-Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg/300px-Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg/400px-Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Barnes Ice Cap, containing remnants of the Laurentide Ice Sheet.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Its melting also caused major disruptions to the global climate cycle, because the huge influx of low-<a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> water into the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murton_Bateman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murton_Bateman-4">[4]</a></sup> is believed to have disrupted the formation of <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>, the very saline, cold, deep water that flows from the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Sea" title="Greenland Sea">Greenland Sea</a>. This interrupted the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, creating the brief <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> cold epoch and a temporary re-advance of the ice sheet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broeckery_Denton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broeckery_Denton-5">[5]</a></sup> which did not retreat from <a href="/wiki/Nunavik" title="Nunavik">Nunavik</a> until 6,500 years ago.</p>
<p>Some people<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have argued that huge influxes of fresh water stopped the feeding of the ice sheet and <a href="/wiki/Starvation_(glaciology)" title="Starvation (glaciology)">starved</a> it, aiding the retreat that had already begun. This is a controversial position because it is known that the North Atlantic was very cold throughout glacial periods and it is likely that the <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> on top of the ice sheet helped sustain it through moist easterly winds which encouraged snow-bearing winds from the south.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Illinoian" title="Pre-Illinoian">Pre-Illinoian Stage</a> the Laurentide Ice Sheet extended even farther south, advancing as far as the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> valleys.</p>
<p>The ultimate collapse of the Laurentide Ice Sheet is also suspected to have influenced European agriculture indirectly through the rise of global sea levels.</p>
<p>Canada's oldest ice is a 20,000-year-old remnant of the Laurentide Ice Sheet called the <a href="/wiki/Barnes_Ice_Cap" title="Barnes Ice Cap">Barnes Ice Cap</a>, located on central <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>.</p>
