<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cumulus cloud</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Cumulus" redirects here. <p><i>Cumulo-</i> means "heap" or "pile" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRH-NOAA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRH-NOAA-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>Cumulus clouds</b> are often described as "puffy", "cotton-like" or "fluffy" in appearance, and have flat bases. Cumulus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds" title="Clouds">clouds</a>, being low-level clouds, are generally less than 2,000 m (6,600 ft) in altitude unless they are the more vertical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">cumulus congestus</a> form. Cumulus clouds may appear by themselves, in lines, or in clusters.</p>
<p>Cumulus clouds are often precursors of other types of cloud, such as <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus</a>, when influenced by weather factors such as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">instability</a>, moisture, and temperature gradient. Normally, cumulus clouds produce little or no precipitation, but they can grow into the precipitation-bearing congestus or cumulonimbus clouds. Cumulus clouds can be formed from water vapor, supercooled water droplets, or ice crystals, depending upon the ambient temperature. They come in many distinct subforms, and generally cool the earth by reflecting the incoming solar radiation. Cumulus clouds are part of the larger category of free-convective cumuliform clouds, which include cumulonimbus clouds. The latter genus-type is sometimes categorized separately as <a href="/wiki/List_of_cloud_types" title="List of cloud types">cumulonimbiform</a> due to its more complex structure that often includes a cirriform or anvil top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LANDSAT_identification_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LANDSAT_identification-2">[2]</a></sup> There are also cumuliform clouds of limited convection that comprise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a> (low-<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%A9tage" title="wikt:étage">étage</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altocumulus" title="Altocumulus">altocumulus</a> (middle-étage) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus" title="Cirrocumulus">cirrocumulus</a>. (high-étage).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These last three genus-types are sometimes classified separately as stratocumuliform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LANDSAT_identification_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LANDSAT_identification-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Cumulus clouds form via <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a> as air warmed by the surface begins to rise. As the air rises, the temperature drops (following the <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>), causing the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> (RH) to rise. If convection reaches a certain level the RH reaches one hundred percent, and the "wet-adiabatic" phase begins. At this point a positive feedback ensues: since the RH is above 100%, water vapor condenses, releasing <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>, warming the air and spurring further convection.</p>
<p>In this phase, water vapor condenses on various nuclei present in the air, forming the cumulus cloud. This creates the characteristic flat-bottomed puffy shape associated with cumulus clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stommel91_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stommel91-5">[5]</a></sup> The height of the cloud (from its bottom to its top) depends on the temperature profile of the atmosphere and the presence of any <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mossop632-634_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mossop632-634-6">[6]</a></sup> During the convection, surrounding air is <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(meteorology)" title="Entrainment (meteorology)">entrained</a> (mixed) with the thermal and the total mass of the ascending air increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langmuir175_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langmuir175-7">[7]</a></sup> Rain forms in a cumulus cloud via a <a href="/wiki/Cloud_physics" title="Cloud physics">process</a> involving two non-discrete stages. The first stage occurs after the droplets coalesce onto the various nuclei. Langmuir writes that surface tension in the water droplets provides a slightly higher pressure on the droplet, raising the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> by a small amount. The increased pressure results in those droplets evaporating and the resulting water vapor condensing on the larger droplets. Due to the extremely small size of the evaporating water droplets, this process becomes largely meaningless after the larger droplets have grown to around 20 to 30 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a>, and the second stage takes over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langmuir175_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langmuir175-7">[7]</a></sup> In the accretion phase, the raindrop begins to fall, and other droplets collide and combine with it to increase the size of the raindrop. Langmuir was able to develop a formula<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formula_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-formula-8">[note 1]</a></sup> which predicted that the droplet radius would grow unboundedly within a discrete time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langmuir177_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langmuir177-9">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The liquid water density within a cumulus cloud has been found to change with height above the cloud base rather than being approximately constant throughout the cloud. At the cloud base, the concentration was 0 grams of liquid water per kilogram of air. As altitude increased, the concentration rapidly increased to the maximum concentration near the middle of the cloud. The maximum concentration was found to be anything up to 1.25 grams of water per kilogram of air. The concentration slowly dropped off as altitude increased to the height of the top of the cloud, where it immediately dropped to zero again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stommel94_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stommel94-10">[9]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Cumulus clouds can form in lines stretching over 480 kilometres (300 mi) long called cloud streets. These cloud streets cover vast areas and may be broken or continuous. They form when <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> causes horizontal circulation in the atmosphere, producing the long, tubular cloud streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weston433_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weston433-11">[10]</a></sup> They generally form during <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">high-pressure systems</a>, such as after a cold front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weston437-438_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weston437-438-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The height at which the cloud forms depends on the amount of moisture in the thermal that forms the cloud. Humid air will generally result in a lower cloud base. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> areas, the base of the cumulus clouds is usually below 550 metres (1,800 ft) above ground level, but it can range up to 2,400 metres (7,900 ft) in altitude. In arid and mountainous areas, the cloud base can be in excess of 6,100 metres (20,000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws-jetstream_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nws-jetstream-13">[12]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="852" data-file-width="1200" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg/220px-Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg/330px-Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg/440px-Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cumulusmediocrissweden.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Some <i>cumulus mediocris</i> clouds</div>
</div>

<p>Cumulus clouds can be composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_crystal" title="Ice crystal">ice crystals</a>, water droplets, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercooled_water" title="Supercooled water">supercooled water</a> droplets, or a mixture of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRH-NOAA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRH-NOAA-1">[1]</a></sup> The water droplets form when water vapor condenses on the nuclei, and they may then coalesce into larger and larger droplets. In temperate regions, the cloud bases studied ranged from 500 to 1,500 metres (1,600 to 4,900 ft) above ground level. These clouds were normally above 25 °C (77 °F), and the concentration of droplets ranged from 23 to 1300 droplets per cubic centimeter (380 to 21,300 droplets per cubic inch). This data was taken from growing isolated cumulus clouds that were not precipitating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner1049_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner1049-14">[13]</a></sup> The droplets were very small, ranging down to around 5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometer_(unit)" title="Micrometer (unit)">micrometers</a> in diameter. Although smaller droplets may have been present, the measurements were not sensitive enough to detect them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner1051_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner1051-15">[14]</a></sup> The smallest droplets were found in the lower portions of the clouds, with the percentage of large droplets (around 20 to 30 micrometers) rising dramatically in the upper regions of the cloud. The droplet size distribution was slightly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bimodal" title="Bimodal">bimodal</a> in nature, with peaks at the small and large droplet sizes and a slight trough in the intermediate size range. The <a href="/wiki/Skewness" title="Skewness">skew</a> was roughly neutral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner1052_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner1052-16">[15]</a></sup> Furthermore, large droplet size is roughly inversely proportional to the droplet concentration per unit volume of air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner1054_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner1054-17">[16]</a></sup> In places, cumulus clouds can have "holes" where there are no water droplets. These can occur when winds tear the cloud and incorporate the environmental air or when strong downdrafts evaporate the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner1056_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner1056-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner1058_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner1058-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subforms">Subforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lowcloudsymbols.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="473" data-file-width="745" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Lowcloudsymbols.gif/220px-Lowcloudsymbols.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Lowcloudsymbols.gif/330px-Lowcloudsymbols.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Lowcloudsymbols.gif/440px-Lowcloudsymbols.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lowcloudsymbols.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Low cloud weather map symbols: Includes low-étage and upward-growing vertical.</div>

<p>Cumulus clouds come in four distinct species, <i>cumulis humilis</i>, <i>mediocris</i>, <i>congestus</i>, and <i>fractus</i>. These species may be arranged into the variety, <i>cumulus radiatus</i>; and may be accompanied by up to seven supplementary features, <i>cumulus pileus</i>, <i>velum</i>, <i>virga</i>, <i>praecipitatio</i>, <i>arcus</i>, <i>pannus</i>, and <i>tuba</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pretor-Pinney17_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pretor-Pinney17-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species <i>Cumulus fractus</i> is ragged in appearance and can form in clear air as a precursor to cumulus humilis and larger cumulus species-types; or it can form in precipitation as the supplementary feature <i>pannus</i> (also called <a href="/wiki/Scud_(cloud)" title="Scud (cloud)">scud</a>) which can also include stratus fractus of bad weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> <i>Cumulus humilis</i> clouds look like puffy, flattened shapes. <i>Cumulus mediocris</i> clouds look similar, except that they have some vertical development. <i>Cumulus congestus</i> clouds have a cauliflower-like structure and tower high into the atmosphere, hence their alternate name "towering cumulus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWC_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-24">[23]</a></sup> The variety <i>Cumulus radiatus</i> forms in radial bands called cloud streets and can comprise any of the four species of cumulus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pretor-Pinney20_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pretor-Pinney20-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1952" data-file-width="2346" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG/220px-Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG/330px-Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG/440px-Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cumulus_congestus_pileus.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Cumulus congestus pileus</i> clouds</div>

</div>
<p>Cumulus supplementary features are most commonly seen with the species congestus. <i>Cumulus virga</i> clouds are cumulus clouds producing <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> (precipitation that evaporates while aloft), and <i>cumulus praecipitatio</i> produce precipitation that reaches the Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunlop77-78_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlop77-78-26">[25]</a></sup> <i>Cumulus pannus</i> comprise shredded clouds that normally appear beneath the parent cumulus cloud during precipitation. <i>Cumulus arcus</i> clouds have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">gust front</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludlum473_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludlum473-27">[26]</a></sup> and <i>cumulus tuba</i> clouds have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funnel_clouds" title="Funnel clouds">funnel clouds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunlop79_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlop79-28">[27]</a></sup> <i>Cumulus pileus</i> clouds refer to cumulus clouds that have grown so rapidly as to force the formation of <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(meteorology)" title="Pileus (meteorology)">pileus</a> over the top of the cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GarretI_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-GarretI-29">[28]</a></sup> <i>Cumulus velum</i> clouds have an ice crystal veil over the growing top of the cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecast">Forecast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forecast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_humilis" title="Cumulus humilis">Cumulus humilis</a> clouds usually indicate fair weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWC_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-24">[23]</a></sup> Cumulus mediocris clouds are similar, except that they have some vertical development, which implies that they can grow into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">cumulus congestus</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus clouds</a>, which can produce heavy rain, lightning, severe winds, hail, and even <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWC_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeatherThompson_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-WeatherThompson-30">[29]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">Cumulus congestus</a> clouds, which appear as towers, will often grow into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus</a> storm clouds. They can produce precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWC_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-24">[23]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glider_aircraft" title="Glider aircraft">Glider</a> pilots often pay close attention to cumulus clouds, as they can be indicators of rising air drafts or <a href="/wiki/Thermal" title="Thermal">thermals</a> underneath that can suck the plane high into the sky—a phenomenon known as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_suck" title="Cloud suck">cloud suck</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pagen-105-108_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pagen-105-108-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_climate">Effects on climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects on climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to reflectivity, clouds cool the earth by around 12 °C (22 °F), an effect largely caused by stratocumulus clouds. However, at the same time, they heat the earth by around 7 °C (13 °F) by reflecting emitted radiation, an effect largely caused by <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus clouds</a>. This averages out to a net loss of 5 °C (9.0 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cloud-heating_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-heating-32">[31]</a></sup> Cumulus clouds, on the other hand, have a variable effect on heating the earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> The more vertical <i>cumulus congestus</i> species and cumulonimbus genus of clouds grow high into the atmosphere, carrying moisture with them, which can lead to the formation of cirrus clouds. The researchers speculated that this might even produce a positive feedback, where the increasing upper atmospheric moisture further warms the earth, resulting in an increasing number of <i>cumulus congestus</i> clouds carrying more moisture into the upper atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature384_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature384-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_other_clouds">Relation to other clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relation to other clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cumulus clouds are a genus of free-convective low-étage cloud along with the related limited-convective cumuliform or stratocumuliform cloud stratocumulus. These clouds form from ground level to 2,000 metres (6,600 ft) at all latitudes. Stratus clouds are also low-étage. In the middle étage are the alto clouds, which consist of the limiited-convective cumuliform or stratocumuliform cloud altocumulus and the stratiform cloud altostratus. Middle-étage clouds form from 2,000 metres (6,600 ft) to 7,000 metres (23,000 ft) in polar areas, 7,000 metres (23,000 ft) in temperate areas, and 7,600 metres (24,900 ft) in tropical areas. The high-étage clouds are all cirriform, one of which, cirrocumulus, is also cumuliform of limited convection or stratocumuliform. The other clouds in this étage are cirrus and cirrostratus. High-étage clouds form 3,000 to 7,600 metres (9,800 to 24,900 ft) in high latitudes, 5,000 to 12,000 metres (16,000 to 39,000 ft) in temperate latitudes, and 6,100 to 18,000 metres (20,000 to 59,100 ft) in low, tropical latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws-jetstream_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-nws-jetstream-13">[12]</a></sup> Cumulonimbus clouds, like cumulus congestus, extend vertically rather than remaining confined to one étage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cumulonimbus-incus_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-cumulonimbus-incus-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cirrocumulus_clouds">Cirrocumulus clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cirrocumulus clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg"><img alt="A large field of cirrocumulus clouds in a blue sky, beginning to merge near the upper left." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/220px-Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/330px-Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/440px-Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A large field of cirrocumulus clouds</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus_cloud" title="Cirrocumulus cloud">Cirrocumulus cloud</a></div>
<p>Cirrocumulus clouds form in patches<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YDN-364_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-YDN-364-36">[35]</a></sup> and cannot cast shadows. They commonly appear in regular, rippling patterns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H.26H-340_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-H.26H-340-37">[36]</a></sup> or in rows of clouds with clear areas between.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cloud-classification_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-classification-38">[37]</a></sup> Cirrocumulus are, like other members of the cumuliform and stratocumuliform categories, formed via <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parungo-251_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-251-39">[38]</a></sup> Significant growth of these patches indicates high-altitude instability and can signal the approach of poorer weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-common-clouds_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-common-clouds-40">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audubon-448_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-448-41">[40]</a></sup> The ice crystals in the bottoms of cirrocumulus clouds tend to be in the form of hexagonal cylinders. They are not solid, but instead tend to have stepped funnels coming in from the ends. Towards the top of the cloud, these crystals have a tendency to clump together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parungo-252_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-252-42">[41]</a></sup> These clouds do not last long, and they tend to change into cirrus because as the water vapor continues to deposit on the ice crystals, they eventually begin to fall, destroying the upward convection. The cloud then dissipates into cirrus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parungo-254_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-254-43">[42]</a></sup> Cirrocumulus clouds come in four species which are common to all three genus-types that have limited-convective or stratocumuliform characteristics: <i>stratiformis</i>, <i>lenticularis</i>, <i>castellanus</i>, and <i>floccus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-common-clouds_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-common-clouds-40">[39]</a></sup> They are <a href="/wiki/Iridescence" title="Iridescence">iridescent</a> when the constituent supercooled water droplets are all about the same size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audubon-448_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-448-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altocumulus_clouds">Altocumulus clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Altocumulus clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Altocumulus_cloud" title="Altocumulus cloud">Altocumulus cloud</a></div>
<p>Altocumulus clouds are a middle-étage cloud that forms from 2,000 metres (6,600 ft) high to 4,000 metres (13,000 ft) in polar areas, 7,000 metres (23,000 ft) in temperate areas, and 7,600 metres (24,900 ft) in tropical areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws-jetstream_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-nws-jetstream-13">[12]</a></sup> They can have precipitation and are commonly composed of a mixture of ice crystals, supercooled water droplets, and water droplets in temperate latitudes. However, the liquid water concentration was almost always significantly greater than the concentration of ice crystals, and the maximum concentration of liquid water tended to be at the top of the cloud while the ice concentrated itself at the bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey2490_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carey2490-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey2491_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carey2491-45">[44]</a></sup> The ice crystals in the base of the altocumulus clouds and in the virga were found to be dendrites or conglomerations of dendrites while needles and plates resided more towards the top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey2491_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carey2491-45">[44]</a></sup> Altocumulus clouds can form via convection or via the forced uplift caused by a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey2494_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carey2494-46">[45]</a></sup> Because Altocumulus is a genus-type of limited convection, it is divided into the same four species as cirrocumulus.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratocumulus_clouds">Stratocumulus clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stratocumulus clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">Stratocumulus cloud</a></div>
<p>A stratocumulus cloud is another type of a cumuliform or stratocumuliform cloud. Like cumulus clouds, they form at low levels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cloud-classification_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-classification-38">[37]</a></sup> and via convection. However, unlike cumulus clouds, their growth is almost completely retarded by a strong <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a>. As a result, they flatten out like stratus clouds, giving them a layered appearance. These clouds are extremely common, covering on average around twenty-three percent of the earth's oceans and twelve percent of the earth's continents. They are less common in tropical areas and commonly form after <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>. Additionally, stratocumulus clouds reflect a large amount of the incoming sunlight, producing a net cooling effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood2374_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood2374-47">[46]</a></sup> Stratocumulus clouds can produce <a href="/wiki/Drizzle" title="Drizzle">drizzle</a>, which stabilizes the cloud by warming it and reducing turbulent mixing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood2398_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood2398-48">[47]</a></sup> Being a cloud of limited convection, stratocumulus is divided into three species; stratiformis, lenticularis, and castellanus, that are common to the higher stratocumuliform genus-types.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cumulonimbus_clouds">Cumulonimbus clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cumulonimbus clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">Cumulonimbus cloud</a></div>
<p>Cumulonimbus clouds are the final form of growing cumulus clouds. They form when <i>cumulus congestus</i> clouds develop a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updraft</a> that propels their tops higher and higher into the atmosphere until they reach the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> at 18,000 metres (59,000 ft) in altitude. Cumulonimbus clouds, commonly called thunderheads, can produce high winds, torrential rain, lightning, gust fronts, <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">funnel clouds</a>, and tornadoes. They commonly have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anvil_cloud" title="Anvil cloud">anvil clouds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWC_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cumulonimbus-incus_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-cumulonimbus-incus-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludlum471_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludlum471-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial">Extraterrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some cumuliform clouds have been discovered on most other planets in the solar system. On <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking_Orbiter" title="Viking Orbiter">Viking Orbiter</a> detected cirrocumulus and stratocumulus clouds forming via convection primarily near the polar icecaps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mars-clouds_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mars-clouds-50">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">Galileo space probe</a> detected massive cumulonimbus clouds near the <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a> on <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jupiter-clouds_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jupiter-clouds-51">[50]</a></sup> Cumuliform clouds have also been detected on <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>. In 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini_spacecraft" title="Cassini spacecraft">Cassini spacecraft</a> determined that cumulus clouds near Saturn's south pole were part of a cyclone over 4,000 kilometres (2,500 mi) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saturn-clouds_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saturn-clouds-52">[51]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_Observatory" title="Keck Observatory">Keck Observatory</a> detected whitish cumulus clouds on <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uranus-clouds_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uranus-clouds-53">[52]</a></sup> Like Uranus, <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> has methane cumulus clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irwin-115_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irwin-115-54">[53]</a></sup> Venus, however, does not appear to have cumulus clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bougher-127-129_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bougher-127-129-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
