<p><b>Car glass</b> includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windscreen" title="Windscreen">windscreens</a>, side and rear windows, and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> panel roofs on a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicle</a>. Side windows can be either fixed or raised and lowered by depressing a button (<a href="/wiki/Power_window" title="Power window">power window</a>) or switch or using a hand-turned <a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">crank</a>. The power <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moonroof" title="Moonroof">moonroof</a>, a transparent, retractable <a href="/wiki/Sunroof" title="Sunroof">sunroof</a>, may be considered as an extension of the power window concept. Some vehicles include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_blind" title="Sun blind">sun blinds</a> for rear and rear side windows. The wind shield of a car is appropriate for safety and protection of debris on the road. The majority of car glass is held in place by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_run_channels" title="Glass run channels">glass run channels</a>, which also serve to contain any fragments of glass if the glass breaks.</p>
<p>Today most cars use <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">laminated glass</a> because it does not shatter into sharp pieces.</p>
