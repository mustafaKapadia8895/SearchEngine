<p><b>The Carolinas</b> are the U.S. states of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, considered collectively. Combining North Carolina's population of 10,042,802 and South Carolina's of 4,896,146, the Carolinas have a population of 14,938,948 as of 2015. If the Carolinas were a single state of the United States, it would be the fifth-<a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._states_and_territories_by_population" title="List of U.S. states and territories by population">most populous state</a>, behind <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. The Carolinas were known as the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Carolina" title="Province of Carolina">Province of Carolina</a> during America's early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonial_America" title="Colonial America">colonial</a> period, from 1663 to 1710. Prior to that, the land was considered part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colony_and_Dominion_of_Virginia" title="Colony and Dominion of Virginia">Colony and Dominion of Virginia</a>, from 1609 to 1663. The province, named <i>Carolina</i> to honor King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a>, was divided into two colonies in 1729, although the actual date is the subject of debate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The territory (together with a part of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>) was declared as Spanish territory by <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de León">Ponce de Leon</a> in 1512, but the first Europeans that paid the territory a visit never cared to name it. Nor was it colonized by any Spaniards; it was largely just proclaimed for a future that never came. But as French settlers arrived in 1562, they were soon thrown out by the Spaniards. However it was during this brief time that the "Carolina" first was named, and the name referred to King <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_France" title="Charles IX of France">Charles IX of France</a>. The territory was thereafter left to native Americans until King <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">The English Restoration</a>, in 1660 he gave all land between the 34th and 36th parallels to eight Englishmen. The territory was named after the English King instead, which however had no impact on the spelling. In 1729 British politicians regretted this gift and redeemed the heirs of the first eight British inhabitants. Now the Carolinas became divided into <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, which both became British colonies. Both the new colonies were among the thirteen first states of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Heath" title="Robert Heath">Robert Heath</a> (1575–1649) was an English judge and politician who was also a member of the English <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_England" title="House of Commons of England">House of Commons</a> from 1621 to 1625. Sir Robert Heath was granted charter over the lands between latitudes 31° and 36° north, from the Atlantic to the Pacific Ocean. Heath's patent required he plant a colony but that was never fully realized.</p>
<p>The 1663 charter granted the Lords Proprietor title to all of the land from the southern border of the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a> at 36 degrees north to 31 degrees north (along the coast of present-day <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>). In 1665, the charter was revised slightly, with the northerly boundary extended to 36 degrees 30 minutes north to include the lands of the <a href="/wiki/Albemarle_Settlements" title="Albemarle Settlements">Albemarle Settlements</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Albemarle_Sound" title="Albemarle Sound">Albemarle Sound</a> who had left the Colony of Virginia. Likewise, the southern boundary was moved south to 29 degrees north, just south of present-day <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">Daytona Beach, Florida</a>, which had the effect of including the existing Spanish settlement at St. Augustine. The charter also granted all the land, between these northerly and southerly bounds, from the Atlantic Ocean, westward to the shores of the Pacific Ocean.</p>

<p>The Charter of 1663 chartered the territory as an English <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_colony" title="Proprietary colony">Proprietary colony</a> assigning rights to eight English Noblemen. These noblemen are known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lords_Proprietors_of_Carolina" title="Lords Proprietors of Carolina">Lords Proprietors of Carolina</a> forming the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Carolina" title="Province of Carolina">Province of Carolina</a>.</p>

<p>Between 1663 and 1729 there were many disagreements relating to defense, governance and the difference between the two differing <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarian</a> styles employed by the inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a> and that practiced by the planters arriving to <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina#Colonial_era_.281670.E2.80.931776.29" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charles Town</a> from the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>.</p>
<p>In 1729 the Province of Carolina was divided when the descendants of seven of the eight Lords Proprietors sold their shares back to the Crown. Only the heirs of Sir George Carteret retained their original rights to what would become the <a href="/wiki/Granville_District" title="Granville District">Granville District</a>. Both the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_North_Carolina" title="Province of North Carolina">Province of North Carolina</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_South_Carolina" title="Province of South Carolina">Province of South Carolina</a> became <a href="/wiki/Crown_colony" title="Crown colony">British Crown Colonies</a> in 1729.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The culture of the Carolinas is a distinct subset of larger <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States" title="Culture of the Southern United States">Southern culture</a>. Notably, the coastal Carolina region was settled by Europeans over a century before the inland regions of the South,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and was influenced by the culture of the Caribbean, especially <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>; many of the early governors during the unified period were Barbadians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Though the two states both form part of <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">the South</a>, there are historically a number of differences in the settlement patterns, political development, and economic growth of the two states. For example, during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, South Carolina was the first Southern state to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secede" title="Secede">secede</a> from the Union,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> while North Carolina was the last state to secede. When NC did secede, it did so largely because North Carolinians refused to give the Federal Government men to fight against their sister state, or allow them to pass through North Carolina to make war on South Carolina. North Carolinians ultimately voted instead in all 100 counties to raise an army, join the Confederacy, and go to war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> During the war, South Carolina was generally one of the strongest supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederacy</a>. The war began in Charleston, South Carolina; where Citadel Cadets fired the opening shots at the Union Ship <i>Star of The West</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> North Carolina was also a key Confederate state, raising and giving more soldiers to The Confederacy than any other southern state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> At Gettysburg, one in four Confederate soldiers was from North Carolina, despite the fact that some North Carolinians (especially in the western part of the state) refused to support the Confederacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> North Carolina's Civil War governor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zebulon_Vance" title="Zebulon Vance">Zebulon Vance</a>, was an outspoken critic of Confederate President <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Davis" title="Jefferson Davis">Jefferson Davis</a> and frequently refused to obey Davis's orders for reinforcements and supplies; Vance insisted the soldiers and supplies would be needed for North Carolina's Confederate effort. However, during the seven day's battles, North Carolina did send large numbers of troops for the general aid of the South as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During the war, the Carolinas were both instrumental in keeping The Confederacy alive, because of their deepwater ports in Wilmington, NC and Charleston, SC. These two cities were key in supplying southern armies with weapons, clothing, and ammunition; and producing food &amp; provisions for southern civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During most of the 20th century, South Carolina was a bastion of the "solid Democratic South" with almost no Republican officeholders, and the state frequently elected politicians who were outspoken supporters of <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation" title="Racial segregation">racial segregation</a>. North Carolina, while mostly Democratic, contained a large Republican minority – the state voted Republican in the presidential election of 1928 and elected several Republican congressmen, governors, and senators from 1868–1928 – and North Carolina was widely known as one of the more progressive Southern states on the issue of segregation and civil rights. In 1947, the journalist <a href="/wiki/John_Gunther" title="John Gunther">John Gunther</a> wrote, "that North Carolina is by a good deal the most progressive Southern state will, I imagine, be agreed to by almost everybody."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunther_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gunther-13">[13]</a></sup> On the other hand, he described South Carolina as "one of the poorest American states, and probably one of the balkiest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunther_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gunther-13">[13]</a></sup> In describing the differences between the two states, Gunther noted that, in 1947, divorce in North Carolina "may be granted simply on the ground of absence of cohabitation; South Carolina is the one American state in which divorce is not possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunther_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gunther-13">[13]</a></sup> North Carolina's nickname for many years was "a vale of humility between two mountains of conceit"; the "mountains" were Virginia and South Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunther_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gunther-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite North Carolina being a swing state, and South Carolina being a red one, they are technically the country's two most politically similar states, according to a comparison of the states along a range of 19 variables performed by the statistician <a href="/wiki/Nate_Silver" title="Nate Silver">Nate Silver</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silver_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silver-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, like much of the South, the Carolinas have been <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, the predominance of certain crops has influenced the regional economy:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Like other [Southern] states, until after World War II North Carolina remained primarily a region of small farms and factories heavily dependent on just a few labor-intensive crops, relying on <a href="/wiki/Sharecropping" title="Sharecropping">sharecropping</a> and tenancy, especially for black laborers. The Carolinas are distinct for their economic dependence on <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> as well as on <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, and for their many small-scale furniture, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textile</a>, and tobacco factories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>These small industries gave the Carolinas, in particular NC, a more significant industrial base than most Southern states, but as increased mechanization in the textiles, apparel, and furniture industries combined with the decline of the tobacco industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> many rural and small urban communities suffered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, during the 1990s, both states began to experience growth in the technological and banking sectors, bringing jobs and population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> These changes, as with earlier industrialization, were more pronounced in North Carolina, and SC has experienced a slower rate of economic growth for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Carolinas have three professional sports teams in the <a href="/wiki/Major_professional_sports_leagues_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Major professional sports leagues in the United States and Canada">Big Four major leagues</a>. Supported by both states, the three teams are all based in NC, two in Charlotte and the third in Raleigh. All of the sports teams are fairly recent additions; the oldest team, the NFL's Panthers, was established in 1995, while the youngest, the Hornets, was added to the NBA in 2004 as the Charlotte Bobcats, although a prior NBA team also named the Hornets played in Charlotte from 1988 before leaving for <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> in 2002. the Bobcats were renamed the Hornets in May 2014, one season after the former New Orleans Hornets decided to rebrand themselves as the <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Pelicans" title="New Orleans Pelicans">Pelicans</a>. Of all the teams, the Hurricanes are the most successful, being the only team with a championship.</p>
<p>The Carolinas are home to a number of NBA superstars, such as <a href="/wiki/Chris_Paul" title="Chris Paul">Chris Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Worthy" title="James Worthy">James Worthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Wall_(basketball)" title="John Wall (basketball)">John Wall</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jordan" title="Michael Jordan">Michael Jordan</a> (from NC) and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Garnett" title="Kevin Garnett">Kevin Garnett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jermaine_O%27Neal" title="Jermaine O'Neal">Jermaine O'Neal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Allen" title="Ray Allen">Ray Allen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Felton" title="Raymond Felton">Raymond Felton</a> (from SC). Six of these players are All Stars, four are NBA champions, and John Wall and James Worthy were the Number 1 draft picks in the <a href="/wiki/2010_NBA_draft" title="2010 NBA draft">2010 NBA draft</a> and <a href="/wiki/1982_NBA_draft" title="1982 NBA draft">1982 NBA draft</a>, respectively. A disproportional number of basketball players come from the Carolinas, on par with such big cities as New York City and Los Angeles. While the Hornets do little to generate buzz in the Carolinas, they are home to three of the most successful collegiate men's basketball teams in the NCAA, the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Tar_Heels_men%27s_basketball" title="North Carolina Tar Heels men's basketball">North Carolina Tarheels</a>, <a href="/wiki/NC_State_Wolfpack_men%27s_basketball" title="NC State Wolfpack men's basketball">North Carolina State Wolfpack</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Duke_Blue_Devils_men%27s_basketball" title="Duke Blue Devils men's basketball">Duke Blue Devils</a>. All three schools are fierce rivals who have combined to win 12 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championships (UNC has 5, Duke has 5, North Carolina State has 2).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_between_the_states">Boundary between the states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Boundary between the states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plotting_the_boundary">Plotting the boundary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Plotting the boundary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the Prefatory Notes to Volume 5 of the <i>Colonial Records of North Carolina</i>, the process of determining the boundary between North and South Carolina began in 1720 "when the purpose to erect a third Province in Carolina, with <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah</a> for its northern boundary"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> began. On 8 January 1730<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carolana_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carolana-22">[22]</a></sup> an agreement between the two colonies said for the border "to begin 30 miles southwest of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_River" title="Cape Fear River">Cape Fear river</a>, and to be run at that parallel distance the whole course of said river;" The next June <a href="/wiki/Robert_Johnson_(governor)" title="Robert Johnson (governor)">Governor Robert Johnson</a> of South Carolina said the border should start 30 miles southwest of the source of the Cape Fear "due west as far as the South Sea", unless the "<a href="/wiki/Waccamaw_River" title="Waccamaw River">Waccamaw river</a> lyes [sic] within 30 miles of the Cape Fear river,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> which would make the Waccamaw the boundary. North Carolina agreed to this until the discovery that the Cape Fear headwaters were very close to Virginia, which would not have "permitted any extension on the part of North Carolina to the westward."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1732, <a href="/wiki/George_Burrington" title="George Burrington">Governor George Burrington</a> of North Carolina stated in <i>Timothy's Southern Gazette</i> that territory north of the Waccamaw was in North Carolina, to which Johnson replied that South Carolina claimed the land. Johnson also said that when the two met before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> in London two years earlier, Burrington had "insisted that the Waccamaw should be the boundary from its mouth to its head,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> while South Carolina agreed the border should be located 30 miles from the <a href="/wiki/River_mouth" title="River mouth">mouth</a>, not the source. Johnson said this was "only a mistake in wording it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Carolinas selected commissioners to survey the line between them. The plan called for the line to run northwest to <a href="/wiki/35th_parallel_north" title="35th parallel north">35 degrees latitude</a>, unless the <a href="/wiki/Pee_Dee_River" title="Pee Dee River">Pee Dee River</a> was reached first, in which case it would run along the Pee Dee to 35 degrees north. Then the line would run west to <a href="/wiki/Catawba,_South_Carolina" title="Catawba, South Carolina">Catawba town</a>, though if the town were north of the line, the line was to run around Catawba to keep it in South Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1735, the surveyors went from the Cape Fear westward thirty miles along the coast. Then they turned northwest and marked the location with stakes. The surveyors agreed to meet again on September 18. However, only the North Carolina team returned at that time, extending the line northwest 70 miles. The South Carolina team arrived in October and only followed the previous line for 40 miles because they had not been paid. A deputy surveyor marked where the Pee Dee crossed the 35th parallel. An extension of the line in 1737 ran 22 miles to a stake in a meadow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> However, the stake placed at the endpoint of the survey was 12 miles too far south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1764, a second extension ran 62 miles westward. In 1772, after making adjustments to keep the <a href="/wiki/Catawba_people" title="Catawba people">Catawba Indians</a> in South Carolina, "extended in a due west course from the confluence of the north and south forks of the <a href="/wiki/Catawba_River" title="Catawba River">Catawba River</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tryon,_North_Carolina" title="Tryon, North Carolina">Tryon Mountain</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> However, this extension was based on the erroneous position of the 1737 stake, removing 422,000 acres from South Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Caldwell" title="Joseph Caldwell">Joseph Caldwell</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a>, found that the line west of the Pee Dee did not run along the 35th parallel, but 12 miles to the south. However, the western part of the line ran far enough to the north to make up for the difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-8"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> West of this point the border was shifted to run north of the 35th parallel so that the total areas of the states would return to what was intended, although the accuracy of this part of the survey was marred by a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anomaly" title="Magnetic anomaly">magnetic anomaly</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>North Carolina did not agree to the line of 1772 until 1813.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roots_21-9"><a href="#cite_note-Roots-21">[21]</a></sup> A 1905 survey determined the border between <a href="/wiki/Scotland_County,_North_Carolina" title="Scotland County, North Carolina">Scotland County, North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marlboro_County,_South_Carolina" title="Marlboro County, South Carolina">Marlboro County, South Carolina</a>. A 1928 survey decided the border between <a href="/wiki/Horry_County,_South_Carolina" title="Horry County, South Carolina">Horry County, South Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_County,_North_Carolina" title="Brunswick County, North Carolina">Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbus_County,_North_Carolina" title="Columbus County, North Carolina">Columbus counties</a> in North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beam_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beam-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid-1990s, <a href="/wiki/Duke_Energy" title="Duke Energy">Duke Energy</a> determined that the border between the Carolinas needed to be re-surveyed, as the company was selling and donating land in the Jocassee Gorge area, which included parts of both states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> Also, with more people living outside cities, the precise boundaries of fire, tax, and school district lines needed to be known. This was especially a problem in the mountains, where people had previously lived in valleys, not on the ridges where the border was. A 15-year plan to re-establish the boundary began, using maps from the 1813–1815 survey and <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> technology. A few stone markers still read, "NC/SC 1815 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but other locations were marked with trees which no longer stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Carolina had recently been involved in a costly legal battle with Georgia over a small number of islands in the <a href="/wiki/Savannah_River" title="Savannah River">Savannah River</a>, and wanted to avoid the expense of a lawsuit regarding the North Carolina border, so the two states agreed in 1993 to cooperate in resurveying the border. The effort included using colonial-era maps to reconstruct the positions of trees making the border that had long since died, and tracking down the original positions of stone markers that had been moved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 18 years and $980,000, it was predicted that the process of determining the border between the Carolinas would be complete in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beam_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beam-24">[24]</a></sup> Financial problems delayed the last survey until October 2012, meaning the results were not expected to be known until Spring 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delay_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delay-26">[26]</a></sup> It was found that a gas station and 30 homes could change states. Lake Wylie Mini Market<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> has been located in South Carolina, along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_321" title="U.S. Route 321">U.S. Route 321</a>, and the move to North Carolina would result in <a href="/wiki/Fuel_taxes_in_the_United_States" title="Fuel taxes in the United States">higher gas taxes</a> and change <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_laws_of_the_United_States" title="Alcohol laws of the United States">laws on beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fireworks#United_States" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The state legislatures involved expect to pass laws alleviating the concerns those changing states would face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delay_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Delay-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Joint Boundary Commission met in February 2014 in <a href="/wiki/Monroe,_North_Carolina" title="Monroe, North Carolina">Monroe, North Carolina</a> to determine what actions still needed to be taken. The persons living in 50 homes that changed states would have to get <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_license" title="Driver's license">driver's licenses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voter_registration" title="Voter registration">register to vote</a> in their new states. Legislative action could allow people to keep utilities, avoid back taxes to the new state, and continue in the same schools. Lake Wylie Minimarket could be <a href="/wiki/Grandfather_clause" title="Grandfather clause">grandfathered</a>, or <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> could change the defined border at the store's location, though the commission intended to avoid such an action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> As of August 2014, the states were expected to pass legislation to mitigate many of the negative impacts to affected landowners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 1, 2016, the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_House_of_Representatives" title="South Carolina House of Representatives">South Carolina House of Representatives</a> passed a bill setting the border. North Carolina's <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Senate" title="North Carolina Senate">Senate</a> also passed a bill, which also had to clear the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_House_of_Representatives" title="North Carolina House of Representatives">House</a>. <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_North_Carolina" title="Governor of North Carolina">North Carolina Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Pat_McCrory" title="Pat McCrory">Pat McCrory</a> signed his state's bill in June. Three families who actually lived in North Carolina had South Carolina addresses, and 16 South Carolina residents had believed they lived in North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCrory_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCrory-36">[36]</a></sup> On December 9, 2016, McCrory announced that he signed a four-page executive order formally defining the border between the two states based on the 20 years of work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCrory_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCrory-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
