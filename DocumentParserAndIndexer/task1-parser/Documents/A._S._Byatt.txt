<p><b>Dame Antonia Susan Duffy</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dame_Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Dame Commander of the Order of the British Empire">DBE</a></span> (<i>née</i> <b>Drabble</b>; born 24 August 1936), known professionally as <b>A. S. Byatt</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><small title="English pronunciation respelling"><i><b>BY</b>-ət</i></small></a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pronunciation_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pronunciation-1">[1]</a></sup> is an English novelist, poet and <a href="/wiki/Booker_Prize" title="Booker Prize">Booker Prize</a> winner. In 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> newspaper named her on its list of the 50 greatest British writers since 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._S._Byatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Byatt was born in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> as Antonia Susan Drabble, the eldest child of John Drabble, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">QC</a>, and Kathleen Bloor, a scholar of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Browning" title="Robert Browning">Browning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup> Her sisters are the novelist <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Drabble" title="Margaret Drabble">Margaret Drabble</a> and the art historian Helen Langdon. Her brother Richard Drabble QC is a <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gruber_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gruber-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result of the bombing of Sheffield during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> the family moved to <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>.</p>
<p>Byatt's upbringing was fairly unhappy as she struggled against her domineering mother. She was educated at two independent boarding schools, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_High_School,_South_Yorkshire" title="Sheffield High School, South Yorkshire">Sheffield High School</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Mount_School,_York" title="The Mount School, York">Mount School</a> in York. She noted in an interview in 2009, "I am not a Quaker, of course, because I'm anti-Christian and the Quakers are a form of Christianity but their religion is wonderful – you simply sat in silence and listened to the nature of things."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>She did not enjoy boarding school, citing her need to be alone and her difficulty in making friends. She went on to <a href="/wiki/Newnham_College,_Cambridge" title="Newnham College, Cambridge">Newnham College, Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Mawr_College" title="Bryn Mawr College">Bryn Mawr College</a> in the United States, and <a href="/wiki/Somerville_College,_Oxford" title="Somerville College, Oxford">Somerville College, Oxford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Byatt lectured in the <a href="/wiki/Birkbeck,_University_of_London#The_School_of_Continuing_Education" title="Birkbeck, University of London">Department of Extra-Mural Studies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> (1962–71),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Central_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="Central School of Art and Design">Central School of Art and Design</a> and from 1972 to 1983 at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>She married Ian Charles Rayner Byatt in 1959 and had a daughter, as well as a son who was killed in a car accident at the age of 11. The marriage was dissolved in 1969. She has two daughters with her second husband Peter John Duffy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Byatt's relationship with her sister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Drabble" title="Margaret Drabble">Margaret Drabble</a> has sometimes been strained due to the presence of autobiographical elements in both their writing. While their relationship is no longer especially close and they do not read each other's books, Drabble describes the situation as "normal sibling rivalry"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and Byatt says it has been "terribly overstated by gossip columnists" and that the sisters "always have liked each other on the bottom line."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_career">Writing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._S._Byatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The story of a young girl growing up in the shadow of a dominant father, Byatt's first novel, <i>The Shadow of the Sun</i>, was published in 1964. Her novel <i>The Game</i> (1967) charts the dynamics between two sisters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup> and the family theme is continued in her quartet <i><a href="/wiki/The_Virgin_in_the_Garden" title="The Virgin in the Garden">The Virgin in the Garden</a></i> (1978), <i>Still Life</i> (1985), <i>Babel Tower</i> (1996), and <i>A Whistling Woman</i> (2002), <i>Still Life</i> winning the PEN/Macmillan Silver Pen Award in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup> Her quartet of novels is inspired by <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence</a>, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow" title="The Rainbow">The Rainbow</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i>.</p>
<p>Describing mid-20th-century Britain, the books follow the life of Frederica Potter, a young intellectual studying at Cambridge at a time when women were heavily outnumbered by men at that university, and then tracing her journey as a divorcée with a young son making a new life in London. Byatt says some of the characters in her fiction represent her "greatest terror which is simple domesticity [...] I had this image of coming out from under and seeing the light for a bit and then being shut in a kitchen, which I think happened to women of my generation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup> Like <i>Babel Tower</i>, <i>A Whistling Woman</i> touches on the utopian and revolutionary dreams of the 1960s. She describes herself as "a naturally pessimistic animal": "I don't believe that human beings are basically good, so I think all utopian movements are doomed to fail, but I am interested in them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>She has written critical studies of Dame <a href="/wiki/Iris_Murdoch" title="Iris Murdoch">Iris Murdoch</a>, who was a friend, mentor and a significant influence on her own writing. In those books and other works, Byatt alludes to, and builds upon, themes from <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victorian_literature" title="Victorian literature">Victorian literature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup> She conceives of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> as an alternative to, rather than an escape from, everyday life, and it is often difficult to tell when the fantastic in her work actually represents the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">psychosis</a>. "In my work", she notes "writing is always so dangerous. It's very destructive. People who write books are destroyers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Possession_(Byatt_novel)" title="Possession (Byatt novel)">Possession</a></i> (1990) parallels the emerging relationship of two contemporary academics with the past of two (fictional) nineteenth century poets whom they are researching. It won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" title="Man Booker Prize">Booker Prize</a> in 1990 and was made into a <a href="/wiki/Possession_(2002_film)" title="Possession (2002 film)">film in 2002</a>. Byatt's novella <i><a href="/wiki/Morpho_Eugenia" title="Morpho Eugenia">Morpho Eugenia</a></i>, included in <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Insects" title="Angels &amp; Insects">Angels &amp; Insects</a></i> (1992), was turned into the successful film with this same title (1995), nominated for an Academy Award. Her novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Children%27s_Book" title="The Children's Book">The Children's Book</a></i> was shortlisted for the 2009 Man Booker Prize<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup> and won the <a href="/wiki/James_Tait_Black_Memorial_Prize" title="James Tait Black Memorial Prize">James Tait Black Memorial Prize</a>.</p>
<p>Also known for her short stories, Byatt has been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Emily_Dickinson" title="Emily Dickinson">Emily Dickinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Browning" title="Robert Browning">Robert Browning</a>, in merging <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">realism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(literature)" title="Naturalism (literature)">naturalism</a> with fantasy. Her story collections include <i>Sugar and Other Stories</i> (1987); <i>The Djinn in the Nightingale's Eye</i> (1994), a collection of fairy tales; <i>Elementals: Stories of Fire and Ice</i> (1998); and <i>Little Black Book of Stories</i> (2003). <i>The Matisse Stories</i> (1993) features three pieces, each describing a painting by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Henri Matisse</a>, each the tale of an initially smaller crisis that shows the long-present unravelling in the protagonists' lives. Her books reflect a continuous interest in <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entomology" title="Entomology">entomology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Darwinism" title="Darwinism">Darwinism</a> among other repeated themes. Byatt has written for media including the British journal <i><a href="/wiki/Prospect_(magazine)" title="Prospect (magazine)">Prospect</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_Literary_Supplement" title="The Times Literary Supplement">The Times Literary Supplement</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCLit_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-BCLit-6">[6]</a></sup> She has been a judge on many literary award panels including the <a href="/wiki/Hawthornden_Prize" title="Hawthornden Prize">Hawthornden Prize</a>, the Booker, <a href="/wiki/David_Higham_Prize_for_Fiction" title="David Higham Prize for Fiction">David Higham Prize for Fiction</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Betty_Trask_Award" title="Betty Trask Award">Betty Trask Award</a>.</p>
<p>On the role of writing in her life, she says: "I think of writing simply in terms of pleasure. It's the most important thing in my life, making things. Much as I love my husband and my children, I love them only because I am the person who makes these things. I, who I am, is the person that has the project of making a thing. Well, that's putting it pompously – but constructing. I do see it in sort of three-dimensional structures. And because that person does that all the time, that person is able to love all these people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
