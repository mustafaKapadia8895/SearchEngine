<p><b>Tropical cyclone warnings and watches</b> are two levels of alert issued by national <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a> bodies to coastal areas threatened by the imminent approach of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> of tropical storm or hurricane intensity. They are notices to the local population and civil authorities to make appropriate preparation for the cyclone, including evacuation of vulnerable areas where necessary. It is important that interests throughout the area of an alert make preparations to protect life and property, and do not disregard it on the strength of the detailed forecast track. Tropical cyclones are not points, and forecasting their track remains an uncertain science.</p>

<p>New tropical cyclone position and forecast information is available at least every twelve hours in the Southern Hemisphere and at least every six hours in the Northern Hemisphere from Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers and Tropical Cyclone Warning Centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_RSMC_list_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_RSMC_list-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCprod_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCprod-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>, the national meteorological and hydrological services of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, the northern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, and the northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140th meridian west</a>, excluding mainland Africa and Europe, all issue tropical storm/hurricane watches and warnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_TCOP-H_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_TCOP-H-6">[6]</a></sup> Tropical storm watches are issued when gale and storm force winds of between 34–63 knots (39–73 mph; 63–118 km/h) are possible, within 48 hours in a specified area in association with a tropical, subtropical or post-tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Glossary_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Glossary-7">[7]</a></sup> These watches are upgraded to tropical storm warnings, when gale and storm force winds become expected to occur somewhere in the warning area within 36 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Glossary_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Glossary-7">[7]</a></sup> Hurricane watches are issued when sustained winds of 64 knots (74 mph; 119 km/h) are possible, within 48 hours in a specified area in association with a tropical, subtropical or post-tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Glossary_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Glossary-7">[7]</a></sup> These warnings are upgraded to hurricane warnings, when hurricane-force winds become expected to occur somewhere in the warning area within 36 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Glossary_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Glossary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because hurricane preparedness activities become difficult once winds reach tropical storm force, the hurricane watch and warnings are issued in advance of the anticipated onset of tropical-storm-force winds, rather than in advance of the anticipated onset of hurricane-force winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Glossary_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Glossary-7">[7]</a></sup> At times a tropical storm warning and a hurricane watch can both be in effect due to uncertainties in the forecast. These watches and warnings are also issued by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> for the Hawaiian Islands and the Weather Forecast Office in <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> for parts of <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> but not for <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a> due to an international agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_Manual_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWS_Manual-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the United States an extreme wind warning is issued by the National Weather Service for any land areas that are expected to be impacted by a major (Category 3 or higher) hurricane and by sustained surface winds greater than or equal to 100 knots (115 mph; 185 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_Manual_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWS_Manual-8">[8]</a></sup> The warning is issued just prior to when the strongest winds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a> are expected to impact an area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nws-9">[9]</a></sup> The warning is to be issued for the smallest area possible, and be valid for times of two hours or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nws-9">[9]</a></sup> It was developed in response to confusion resulting from the landfall of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>. NWS offices in <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Mississippi" title="Jackson, Mississippi">Jackson</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans</a>/<a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge,_Louisiana" title="Baton Rouge, Louisiana">Baton Rouge</a> issued 11 <a href="/wiki/Tornado_warning" title="Tornado warning">tornado warnings</a> for areas that would not experience an actual <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>, but would experience extreme wind speeds commonly associated with tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commerce_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-commerce-10">[10]</a></sup> The extreme wind warning is now expected to be used in these situations.</p>
<p>In 2017, the National Hurricane Center introduced a new system of warnings and watches for <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, which would cover the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Atlantic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coasts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. A storm surge watch would be issued when a life-threatening storm surge, associated with a potential or ongoing tropical, subtropical or post-tropical cyclone, is possible within the next 48 hours. These watches would be upgraded to storm surge warnings when there is a danger of life-threatening storm surge occurring within 36 hours. However, both watches and warnings may be issued earlier than specified if environmental conditions are expected to hamper preparations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, a color coded alert system is used to keep the public informed when a tropical cyclone or possible tropical cyclones poses a threat to the nation. The scale starts with blue at the bottom being minimal danger, then proceeds to a green alert, which means low level danger. A yellow alert signifies moderate danger, followed by an orange alert that means high danger level. The scale tops off with a red alert, the maximum level of danger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>A two-stage warning system was long-established in China for tropical cyclones of tropical storm intensity of above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Nowadays, the use of this system is restricted to coastal waters only. Thus, warnings may be discontinued even if a cyclone is maintaining tropical storm intensity inland. Color-coded alerts (below) may be in effect independently of any two-stage warnings.</p>
<ul>
<li>Warning: Winds may reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Force" title="Beaufort Force">Beaufort Force</a> 8 or rainstorm may occur in 48 hours.</li>
<li>Urgent Warning: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 8 or rainstorm may occur in 24 hours.</li>
</ul>
<p>Guangdong introduced a color-coded tropical cyclone warning system for land use in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>White alert: A tropical cyclone may affect the area in 48 hours.</li>
<li>Green alert: Winds may reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Force" title="Beaufort Force">Beaufort Force</a> 6 in 24 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 6~7 are already blowing.</li>
<li>Yellow alert: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 8 in 12 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 8~9 (gale force) are already blowing.</li>
<li>Red alert: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 10 in 12 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 10~11 (storm force) are already blowing.</li>
<li>Black alert: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 12 in 12 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 12 (hurricane force) are already blowing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Similar systems were developed in <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>.</p>
<p>Later, <a href="/wiki/China_Meteorological_Administration" title="China Meteorological Administration">China Meteorological Administration</a> standardized the system for national use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This set is part of a larger warning system that covers other forms of severe weather conditions, such as extreme temperature, torrential rainfall, drought, etc. <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> maintained a white alert as in the old system.</p>
<ul>
<li>Blue alert: Winds may reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Force" title="Beaufort Force">Beaufort Force</a> 6 in 24 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 6~7 are already blowing.</li>
<li>Yellow alert: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 8 in 24 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 8~9 (gale force) are already blowing.</li>
<li>Orange alert: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 10 in 12 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 10~11 (storm force) are already blowing.</li>
<li>Red alert: Winds may reach Beaufort Force 12 in 6 hours or winds of Beaufort Force 12 (hurricane force) are already blowing.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong_and_Macau">Hong Kong and Macau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hong Kong and Macau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_tropical_cyclone_warning_signals" title="Hong Kong tropical cyclone warning signals">Hong Kong tropical cyclone warning signals</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pearl_River_Delta" title="Pearl River Delta">Pearl River Delta</a> uses a variety of warning systems to inform the public regarding the risks of tropical cyclones to the area. The <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Observatory" title="Hong Kong Observatory">Hong Kong Observatory</a> issues typhoon signals to indicate the existence and effects of a tropical cyclone on <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Macao_Meteorological_and_Geophysical_Bureau" title="Macao Meteorological and Geophysical Bureau">Macao Meteorological and Geophysical Bureau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> uses a similar system. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>The signal system consists of 8 signals in 5 levels numbered non-consecutively for historical reasons. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Each signal has a day signal and a night signal for hoisting, which are still hoisted in Macau but no longer hoisted in Hong Kong. Day signals are also used as signal symbols in both places.</p>
<ul>
<li>Signal No. 1: A tropical cyclone is centred within 800 km of the territory and may affect the territory later.</li>
<li>Signal No. 3: Strong wind with a sustained speed of 41–62 km/h, gusts may exceed 110 km/h.</li>
<li>Signals Nos. 8 NW, 8 SW, 8 NE, 8 SE: Gale or storm force wind with a sustained speed of 63–117 km/h from the northwest, southwest, northeast, southeast quadrants respectively, gusts may exceed 180 km/h.</li>
<li>Signal No. 9: (Hong Kong) Gale or storm force wind is increasing or expected to increase significantly in strength. / (Macau) The centre of a tropical cyclone is approaching and Macau is expected to be severely affected.</li>
<li>Signal No. 10: Hurricane force wind with a sustained speed over 118 km/h, gusts may exceed 220 km/h.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a></div>
<p>The Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) is the government agency responsible for gathering and providing results for the public in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, that are obtained from data based on daily scientific observation and research into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_phenomenon" title="Natural phenomenon">natural phenomena</a> in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcanology" title="Volcanology">volcanology</a>, among other related scientific fields. Its headquarters is located in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.</p>
<p>is also designated one of the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers</a> (RSMC) of the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>. It has the responsibility for <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">tropical cyclone naming</a> and distribution of warnings for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Northwestern Pacific</a> region.</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration</a> (PAGASA) releases tropical cyclone warnings in the form of Public Storm Warning Signals (or just storm signals).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> An area having a storm signal may be under:</p>
<ul>
<li>PSWS #1 - Tropical cyclone winds of 30–61 km/h are expected within the next 36 hours. (Note: If a tropical cyclone forms very close to the area, then a shorter lead time is seen on the warning bulletin.)</li>
<li>PSWS #2 - Tropical cyclone winds of 61–120 km/h are expected within the next 24 hours.</li>
<li>PSWS #3 - Tropical cyclone winds of 121–170 km/h are expected within the next 18 hours.</li>
<li>PSWS #4 - Tropical cyclone winds of 171–220 km/h are expected within 12 hours.</li>
<li>PSWS #5 - Tropical cyclone winds greater than 220 km/h are expected within 12 hours.</li>
</ul>
<p>These storm signals are usually heightened when an area (in the Philippines only) is about to be hit by a tropical cyclone. Thus, as a tropical cyclone gains strength and/or gets closer to an area having a storm signal, it may be heightened to another higher signal in that particular area. Whereas, as a tropical cyclone weakens and/or gets farther away from an area, it may be downgraded to a lower signal or may be lifted (that is, an area will have no storm signal).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Pacific_basin"><span id="Australia_and_the_South_Pacific"></span> South Pacific basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: South Pacific basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Australian <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</a> will issue a cyclone watch for a specified part of Australia, when a tropical cyclone is expected to cause gale-force winds in excess of 62 km/h (40 mph) within 24–48 hours and subsequently make landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advice_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Advice-20">[20]</a></sup> A cyclone warning is subsequently issued for a specified part of Australia when a tropical cyclone, is expected to cause or is causing gale-force winds in excess of 62 km/h (40 mph) within 24 hours and is subsequently expected to make landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advice_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Advice-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">Fiji Meteorological Service</a> <small>(FMS)</small> issues a tropical cyclone alert for the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niue" title="Niue">Niue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokelau" title="Tokelau">Tokelau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>, when a tropical cyclone has a significant probability of causing gale-force winds or stronger winds within 24–48 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_TCOP-T_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_TCOP-T-21">[21]</a></sup> Gale, storm and hurricane-force wind warnings are subsequently issued for the above areas by FMS, when a tropical cyclone is either causing or expected to cause either gale storm or hurricane-force winds within 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_TCOP-T_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_TCOP-T-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Météo-France is responsible for the issuance of tropical cyclone watches and warnings for <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wallis_and_Futuna" title="Wallis and Futuna">Wallis and Futuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_TCOP-T_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_TCOP-T-21">[21]</a></sup> The National Meteorological and Hydrological Services of the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, New Zealand, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timor_Leste" title="Timor Leste">Timor Leste</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a> are responsible for their own watches and warnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_TCOP-T_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_TCOP-T-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Ocean_systems">Indian Ocean systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Indian Ocean systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD/RSMC New Delhi) is responsible for tracking tropical cyclones within the North Indian Ocean. <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9t%C3%A9o-France" title="Météo-France">Météo-France</a> in <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> (MFR/RSMC La Reunion) is responsible for the issuing advisories and tracking of tropical cyclones in the southwest part of the basin, however, the naming of systems is deferred to the Mauritius and Madagascar weather services.</p>

<p>The IMD issues warnings in four stages for the Indian coast.</p>
<ul>
<li>Stage 1: Cyclone watch - Issued 72 hours in advance, it discusses the likelihood of development of a cyclonic disturbance in the north Indian Ocean and the coastal region likely to experience adverse weather.</li>
<li>Stage 2: Cyclone alert - Issued 48 hours in advance of the commencement of adverse weather over the coastal areas.</li>
<li>Stage 3: Cyclone warning - Issued 24 hours in advance of the commencement of adverse weather over the coastal areas. The location of landfall is discussed at this stage.</li>
<li>Stage 4: Landfall outlook - Issued 12 hours in advance of the commencement of adverse weather over the coastal areas. The track of the cyclone after the landfall and the possible impact inland is discussed at this stage.</li>
</ul>
<p>Cyclonic storm conditions mean what winds in excess of 63 km/h (39 mph) are possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_advisories">Military advisories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Military advisories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Cyclone_Condition_of_Readiness">Tropical Cyclone Condition of Readiness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tropical Cyclone Condition of Readiness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> uses a multi-stage system called the Tropical Cyclone Condition of Readiness <small>(TC-CORs)</small> otherwise known as the Hurricane Condition of Readiness <small>(HURCONs)</small>, to prepare bases and evacuate assets and personnel in advance of adverse weather associated with tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCCOR_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCCOR-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>TC-CORs are recommended by weather facilities either on base or by central sites like the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> and are generally related to the timing and potential for destructive sustained windspeeds of above 50 kn (58 mph; 93 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCCOR_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCCOR-23">[23]</a></sup> Recommendations are then considered by base or area commanders along with other subjective factors for setting the TC-CORs like assets, holidays or the bases experience in emergency preparedness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCCOR_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCCOR-23">[23]</a></sup> The bases prefer to set these TC-CORs sequentially, from TC-COR 5 with destructive winds expected within 96 hours, through TC-COR 4, 3, 2 and if needed to a series of four different TC-COR 1 conditions, however depending on the cyclone's movement or location some of these signals can be skipped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCCOR_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCCOR-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> After a system passes and stops affecting the base, the authorities can decide to revert to the seasonal TC-COR or stay in a heightened approach as another tropical cyclone is approaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCCOR_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCCOR-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
