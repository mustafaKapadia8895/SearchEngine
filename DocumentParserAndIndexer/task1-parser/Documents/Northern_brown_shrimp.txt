<p><i><b>Farfantepenaeus aztecus</b></i> is a species of marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penaeid_shrimp" title="Penaeid shrimp">penaeid shrimps</a> found around the east coast of the USA and Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> They are an important commercial species in the USA. The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">FAO</a> refers to them as the <b>northern brown shrimp</b>; other <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common names</a>, used in the USA, are <i>brown shrimp</i>, <i>golden shrimp</i>, <i>red shrimp</i> or <i>redtail shrimp</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishwatch_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishwatch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farfantepenaeus_aztecus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Farfantepenaeus aztecus</i> are found along the USA Atlantic coast from Massachusetts to Texas, and along the Atlantic coast of Mexico from Tamaulipas to Campeche.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> They live at depths of 4–160 metres (13–525 ft), with highest densities at 27–54 m (89–177 ft), on muddy, peat, sandy or clay bottoms, or amongst broken shells. Juveniles are found in marine or estuarine waters, while adults are marine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> This species has now been confirmed to occur in the Mediterranean, probably introduced in ship's ballast water, where it seems to be spreading and may threaten stocks of the native <i><a href="/wiki/Melicertus_kerathurus" title="Melicertus kerathurus">Melicertus kerathurus</a></i> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIR_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIR-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farfantepenaeus_aztecus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Females reach a total length of 236 mm (9.3 in) and males 195 mm (7.7 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishery">Fishery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farfantepenaeus_aztecus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fishery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the United States, 80,000,000 pounds (36,000 t) of <i>F. aztecus</i> were landed in 2010, more than half of which was from the state of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishwatch_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-fishwatch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Farfantepenaeus_aztecus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Farfantepenaeus aztecus</i> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_taxonomy" title="Alpha taxonomy">first described</a> by J. E. Ives in an 1891 paper in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_Academy_of_Natural_Sciences_of_Philadelphia" title="Proceedings of the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia">Proceedings of the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia</a></i>, as a variety of "<i>Penæus brasiliensis</i>" (now <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Farfantepenaeus_brasiliensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Farfantepenaeus brasiliensis (page does not exist)">Farfantepenaeus brasiliensis</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ives_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ives-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, on the Mexico's Gulf coast .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ives_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ives-6">[6]</a></sup> He distinguished the new variety on the basis of the extreme lengths of the antennal flagellum, which is 7–10× longer than the length of the carapace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ives_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ives-6">[6]</a></sup> "<i>P. aztecus</i>" was later treated as a full species, and when he erected the subgenus <i><a href="/wiki/Farfantepenaeus" title="Farfantepenaeus">Farfantepenaeus</a></i>, Rudolf Burukovsky included "<i>P. aztecus</i>" among the species included in that subgenus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buru_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buru-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Farfantepenaeus</i> was later raised to the rank of genus by <a href="/wiki/Isabel_P%C3%A9rez_Farfante" title="Isabel Pérez Farfante">Isabel Pérez Farfante</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Kensley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brian Kensley (page does not exist)">Brian Kensley</a>, giving the species its current name of <i>Farfantepenaeus aztecus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
