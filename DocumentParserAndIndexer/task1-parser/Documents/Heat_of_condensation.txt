<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Enthalpy of vaporization</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>enthalpy of vaporization</b>, (symbol ∆H<sub>vap</sub>) also known as the <b>(latent) heat of vaporization</b> or <b>heat of evaporation</b>, is the amount of energy (<a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>) that must be added to the liquid substance, to transform a quantity of that substance into a gas. The enthalpy of vaporization is a function of the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> at which that transformation takes place.</p>
<p>The enthalpy of vaporization is often quoted for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_boiling_point" title="Normal boiling point">normal boiling temperature</a> of the substance; although tabulated values are usually corrected to 298 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>, that correction is often smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">uncertainty</a> in the measured value.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units">Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy_of_vaporization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Values are usually quoted in <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a> or kJ/mol (molar enthalpy of vaporization), although kJ/kg or J/g (specific heat of vaporization), and older units like <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">kcal</a>/mol, cal/g and <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">Btu</a>/lb are sometimes still used, among others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enthalpy_of_condensation">Enthalpy of condensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy_of_vaporization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Enthalpy of condensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>enthalpy of condensation</b> (or <b>heat of condensation</b>) is by definition equal to the enthalpy of vaporization with the opposite sign: enthalpy changes of vaporization are always positive (heat is absorbed by the substance), whereas enthalpy changes of condensation are always negative (heat is released by the substance).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamic_background">Thermodynamic background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy_of_vaporization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermodynamic background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It is equal to the increased <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> of the vapor phase compared with the liquid phase, plus the work done against ambient pressure. The increase in the internal energy can be viewed as the energy required to overcome the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond#Intermolecular_interactions" title="Chemical bond">intermolecular interactions</a> in the liquid (or solid, in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a>). Hence <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> has a particularly low enthalpy of vaporization, 0.0845 kJ/mol, as the <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a> between helium <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> are particularly weak. On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in liquid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> are held together by relatively strong <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a>, and its enthalpy of vaporization, 40.65 kJ/mol, is more than five times the energy required to heat the same quantity of water from 0 °C to 100 °C (<a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity"><i>c</i><sub>p</sub></a> = 75.3 J K<sup>−1 </sup>mol<sup>−1</sup>). Care must be taken, however, when using enthalpies of vaporization to <i>measure</i> the strength of intermolecular forces, as these forces may persist to an extent in the gas phase (as is the case with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>), and so the calculated value of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_strength" title="Bond strength">bond strength</a> will be too low. This is particularly true of metals, which often form <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalently bonded</a> molecules in the gas phase: in these cases, the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_atomization" title="Enthalpy of atomization">enthalpy of atomization</a> must be used to obtain a true value of the <a href="/wiki/Bond_energy" title="Bond energy">bond energy</a>.</p>

<p>As neither entropy nor <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> vary greatly with temperature, it is normal to use the tabulated standard values without any correction for the difference in temperature from 298 K. A correction must be made if the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> is different from 100 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>, as the entropy of a gas is proportional to its pressure (or, more precisely, to its <a href="/wiki/Fugacity" title="Fugacity">fugacity</a>): the entropies of liquids vary little with pressure, as the <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">compressibility</a> of a liquid is small.</p>
