<p><b>Absolute monarchy</b>, or <b>despotic monarchy</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">monarchy</a> in which one ruler has supreme authority and where that authority is not restricted by any written laws, <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a>, or customs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2009-3">[3]</a></sup> These are often, but not always, <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy" title="Hereditary monarchy">hereditary monarchies</a>. In contrast, in <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>'s authority derives from and is legally bounded or restricted by a <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> or <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some monarchies have weak or symbolic legislatures and other governmental bodies the monarch can alter or dissolve at will. Countries where monarchs still maintain absolute power are: <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qat-abs_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-qat-abs-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oman-abs_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-oman-abs-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Swaziland" title="Swaziland">Swaziland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swazi-abs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-swazi-abs-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaticancity_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-vaticancity-9">[9]</a></sup> and the individual emirates composing the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, which itself is a free association of such monarchies – a <a href="/wiki/Federal_monarchy" title="Federal monarchy">federal monarchy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_examples">Historical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_Europe">Outside Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outside Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">Pharaoh</a> wielded absolute power over the country and was considered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thearch" title="Thearch">living god</a> by his people. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Mesopotamia" title="Ancient Mesopotamia">ancient Mesopotamia</a>, many rulers of <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> were absolute monarchs as well. In ancient and medieval India, rulers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurya" title="Maurya">Maurya</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Satahavana_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Satahavana Empire (page does not exist)">Satahavana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gupta" title="Gupta">Gupta</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalukya" title="Chalukya">Chalukya</a> Empires, as well as other major and minor empires, were considered absolute monarchs. In the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Empire" title="Khmer Empire">Khmer Empire</a>, the kings were called "<a href="/wiki/Devaraja" title="Devaraja">Devaraja</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chakravartin" title="Chakravartin">Chakravartin</a>" (King of the world), and exercised absolute power over the empire and people.</p>
<p>Throughout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_history" title="Chinese history">Chinese history</a>, many <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">emperors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu_Zetian" title="Wu Zetian">one empress</a> wielded absolute power through the <a href="/wiki/Mandate_of_Heaven" title="Mandate of Heaven">Mandate of Heaven</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_America" title="Pre-Columbian America">pre-Columbian America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a> was ruled by a <a href="/wiki/Sapa_Inca" title="Sapa Inca">Sapa Inca</a>, who was considered the son of <a href="/wiki/Inti" title="Inti">Inti</a>, the sun god and absolute ruler over the people and nation. Korea under the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> dynasty was also an absolute monarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout much of European history, the <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a> was the theological justification for absolute monarchy. Many European monarchs, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, claimed supreme autocratic power by divine right, and that their subjects had no rights to limit their power. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James VI</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> (later also James I of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>) and his son <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> of Scotland and England tried to import this principle. Charles I's attempt to enforce <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_polity" title="Episcopal polity">episcopal polity</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> led to rebellion by the <a href="/wiki/Covenanter" title="Covenanter">Covenanters</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Wars" title="Bishops' Wars">Bishops' Wars</a>, then fears that Charles I was attempting to establish absolutist government along European lines was a major cause of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, despite the fact that he did rule this way for 11 years starting in 1629, after dissolving the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a> for a time. By the 19th century, the Divine Right was regarded as an obsolete theory in most countries in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, except in Russia where it was still given credence as the official justification for the Tsar's power until revolution in 1917.</p>
<p>There is a considerable variety of opinion by historians on the extent of absolutism among European monarchs. Some, such as <a href="/wiki/Perry_Anderson" title="Perry Anderson">Perry Anderson</a>, argue that quite a few monarchs achieved levels of absolutist control over their states, while historians such as Roger Mettam dispute the very concept of absolutism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In general, historians who disagree with the appellation of <i>absolutism</i> argue that most monarchs labeled as <i>absolutist</i> exerted no greater power over their subjects than any other <i>non-absolutist</i> rulers, and these historians tend to emphasize the differences between the absolutist <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a> of monarchs and the realities of the effective use of power by these absolute monarchs. Renaissance historian William Bouwsma summed up this contradiction:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Nothing so clearly indicates the limits of royal power as the fact that governments were perennially in financial trouble, unable to tap the wealth of those most able to pay, and likely to stir up a costly revolt whenever they attempted to develop an adequate income.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>William Bouwsma</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy_in_France" title="Absolute monarchy in France">Absolute monarchy in France</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1975" data-file-width="1390" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/220px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/330px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/440px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Louis XIV of France</div>
</div>

<p>A widely held story about <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a> (1638–1715) is that he proclaimed "L'état, c'est moi" ("I am the State!"). What Louis did say was: "The interests of the state come first. When one gives these priority, one labors for one's own good. These advantages to the state redounds to one's glory." Although often criticized for his extravagances, such as the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a>, he reigned over France for a long period, and some historians consider him a successful absolute monarch. More recently, revisionist historians have questioned whether Louis' reign should be considered 'absolute', given the reality of the balance of power between the monarch and the nobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The King of France concentrated in his person legislative, executive, and judicial powers. He was the supreme judicial authority. He could condemn men to death without the right of appeal. It was both his duty to punish offenses and stop them from being committed. From his judicial authority followed his power both to make laws and to annul them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of his steps in creating an absolute monarchy in France was to build the Palace of Versailles, where he lived with many of his nobles and other important people, in order to control and watch over them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span id="Denmark–Norway"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark.E2.80.93Norway">Denmark–Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Denmark–Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Absolutism was underpinned by a written constitution for the first time in Europe in the 1665 <i>Kongeloven ("King's Law")</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denmark-Norway" title="Denmark-Norway">Denmark-Norway</a>, which ordered that the Monarch "shall from this day forth be revered and considered the most perfect and supreme person on the Earth by all his subjects, standing above all human laws and having no judge above his person, neither in spiritual nor temporal matters, except God alone".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This law consequently authorized the king to abolish all other centers of power. Most important was the abolition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rigsraad" title="Rigsraad">Council of the Realm</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prussia">Prussia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prussia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Prussia" title="Brandenburg-Prussia">Brandenburg-Prussia</a>, the concept of absolute monarch took a notable turn from the above with its emphasis on the monarch as the "first servant of the state", but it also echoed many of the important characteristics of Absolutism. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_William_I,_Elector_of_Brandenburg" title="Frederick William I, Elector of Brandenburg">Frederick William</a> (r. 1640–1688), known as the Great Elector, used the uncertainties of the final stages of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to consolidate his territories into the dominant kingdom in northern Germany, whilst increasing his power over his subjects. His actions largely originated the militaristic streak of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hohenzollern" title="Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a>.</p>
<p>In 1653 the Diet of Brandenburg met for the last time and gave Frederick William the power to raise taxes without its consent, a strong indicator of absolutism. Frederick William enjoyed support from the nobles, who enabled the Great Elector to undermine the Diet and other representative assemblies. The leading families saw their future in cooperation with the central government and worked to establish absolutist power.</p>
<p>The most significant indicator of the nobles' success was the establishment of two tax rates – one for the cities and the other for the countryside – to the great advantage of the latter, which the nobles ruled. The nobles served in the upper levels of the elector's army and bureaucracy, but they also won new prosperity for themselves. The support of the Elector enabled the imposition of serfdom and the consolidation of land holdings into vast estates which provided for their wealth.</p>
<p>They became known as <a href="/wiki/Junker" title="Junker">Junkers</a> (from the German for young lord, <i>junger Herr</i>). Frederick William faced resistance from representative assemblies and long-independent cities in his realm. City leaders often revolted at the imposition of Electorate authority. The last notable effort was the uprising of the city of <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> which allied with the Estates General of Prussia to refuse to pay taxes. Frederick William crushed this revolt in 1662, by marching into the city with thousands of troops. A similar approach was used with the towns of Cleves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Until 1905 the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> governed as absolute monarchs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Russia" title="Peter I of Russia">Peter I the Great</a> reduced the power of the <a href="/wiki/Boyar" title="Boyar">Russian nobility</a> and strengthened the central power of the Tsars, establishing a bureaucracy and a <a href="/wiki/Police_state" title="Police state">police state</a>. This tradition of absolutism, known as <a href="/wiki/Tsarist_autocracy" title="Tsarist autocracy">Tsarist autocracy</a>, was expanded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catherine_II_of_Russia" title="Catherine II of Russia">Catherine II the Great</a> and her descendants. Although <a href="/wiki/Alexander_II_of_Russia" title="Alexander II of Russia">Alexander II</a> made some reforms and established an independent judicial system, Russia did not have a representative assembly or a constitution until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905" title="Russian Revolution of 1905">1905 Revolution</a>. However, the concept of absolutism was so ingrained in Russia that the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Constitution_of_1906" title="Russian Constitution of 1906">Russian Constitution of 1906</a> still described the Tsar as an autocrat. Russia became the last <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> country (excluding <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>) to abolish absolutism, and it was the only one to do so as late as the 20th century (the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> drafted its first constitution in 1877).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The form of government instituted in <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Sweden</a> under King <a href="/wiki/Charles_XI_of_Sweden" title="Charles XI of Sweden">Charles XI</a> and passed on to his son, <a href="/wiki/Charles_XII_of_Sweden" title="Charles XII of Sweden">Charles XII</a> is commonly referred to as absolute monarchy; however, the Swedish monarch was never absolute in the sense that he wielded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arbitrary_power" title="Arbitrary power">arbitrary power</a>. The monarch still ruled under the law and could only legislate in agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag_of_the_Estates" title="Riksdag of the Estates">Riksdag of the Estates</a>; rather, the absolutism introduced was the monarch's ability to run the government unfettered by the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_Sweden" title="Privy Council of Sweden">privy council</a>, contrary to earlier practice. The absolute rule of Charles XI was instituted by the crown and the Riksdag in order to carry out the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction_(Sweden)" title="Reduction (Sweden)">Great Reduction</a> which would have been made impossible by the privy council which comprised the high nobility. After the death of Charles XII in 1718, the system of absolute rule was largely blamed for the ruination of the realm in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a>, and the reaction tipped the balance of power to the other extreme end of the spectrum, ushering in the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Liberty" title="Age of Liberty">Age of Liberty</a>. After half a century of largely unrestricted parliamentary rule proved just as ruinous, King <a href="/wiki/Gustav_III_of_Sweden" title="Gustav III of Sweden">Gustav III</a> seized back royal power in the coup d'état of 1772, and later once again abolished the privy council under the <a href="/wiki/Union_and_Security_Act" title="Union and Security Act">Union and Security Act</a> in 1789, which, in turn, was rendered void in 1809 when <a href="/wiki/Gustav_IV_Adolf_of_Sweden" title="Gustav IV Adolf of Sweden">Gustav IV Adolf</a> was deposed in a coup and the <a href="/wiki/Instrument_of_Government_(1809)" title="Instrument of Government (1809)">constitution of 1809</a> was put in its place. The years between 1789 and 1809, then, are also referred to as a period of absolute monarchy.</p>

<p>The popularity of the notion of absolute monarchy declined substantially after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, which promoted theories of government based on <a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty" title="Popular sovereignty">popular sovereignty</a>.</p>
<p>Many nations formerly with absolute monarchies, such as <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, have moved towards <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>, although in some cases the monarch retains tremendous power, to the point that the parliament's influence on political life is negligible.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, the government moved from absolute monarchy to constitutional monarchy following planned parliamentary elections to the <a href="/wiki/Tshogdu" title="Tshogdu">Tshogdu</a> in 2003, and the <a href="/wiki/Bhutanese_National_Assembly_election,_2008" title="Bhutanese National Assembly election, 2008">election of a National Assembly in 2008</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> had several swings between constitutional rule and direct rule related to the <a href="/wiki/Nepalese_Civil_War" title="Nepalese Civil War">Nepalese Civil War</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Nepal_(Maoist)" title="Communist Party of Nepal (Maoist)">Maoist insurgency</a>, and the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Nepalese_royal_massacre" title="Nepalese royal massacre">Nepalese royal massacre</a>, with the Nepalese monarchy being abolished on May 28, 2008. In <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Tonga" title="King of Tonga">King</a> had majority control of the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Tonga" title="Legislative Assembly of Tonga">Legislative Assembly</a> until 2010.</p>
<p>On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a> has moved towards expanding the power of the monarch: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_of_Liechtenstein" title="Prince of Liechtenstein">Prince of Liechtenstein</a> was given expanded powers after a <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein_constitutional_referendum,_2003" title="Liechtenstein constitutional referendum, 2003">referendum</a> amending the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Liechtenstein" title="Constitution of Liechtenstein">Constitution of Liechtenstein</a> in 2003, which led the BBC to describe the prince as an "absolute monarch again".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liechtenstein_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liechtenstein-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kim_dynasty_(North_Korea)" title="Kim dynasty (North Korea)">ruling Kim family</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">Kim Jong-il</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">Kim Jong-un</a>) has been described as a <i>de facto</i> absolute monarchy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> or "hereditary dictatorship".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPRKMonarch_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-DPRKMonarch-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2013, Clause 2 of Article 10 of the new edited Ten Fundamental Principles of the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Korea" title="Workers' Party of Korea">Korean Workers' Party</a> states that the party and revolution must be carried "eternally" by the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Baekdu" title="Mount Baekdu">Baekdu</a> (Kim's) bloodline".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Saudi_Arabia">Saudi Arabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Saudi Arabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Politics of Saudi Arabia">Politics of Saudi Arabia</a></div>
<p>Saudi Arabia is an absolute monarchy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-7">[7]</a></sup> although, according to the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Basic Law of Saudi Arabia">Basic Law of Saudi Arabia</a> adopted by royal decree in 1992, the king must comply with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shari%27a" title="Shari'a">Shari'a</a> (Islamic law) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>. The Qur'an and the corpus of <a href="/wiki/Sunnah" title="Sunnah">Sunnah</a> (traditions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophets_in_Islam" title="Prophets in Islam">Prophet</a> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>) are declared to be the Kingdom's constitution, but no written modern constitution has ever been written for Saudi Arabia, which remains one of two Arab nations where no national elections have ever taken place since its founding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerhard_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerhard-36">[36]</a></sup> the other being Qatar. No political parties or national elections are permitted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-7">[7]</a></sup> and according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a>'s</i> 2010 <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Index" title="Democracy Index">Democracy Index</a>, the Saudi government is the seventh most authoritarian regime from among the 167 countries rated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarship">Scholarship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_monarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scholarship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">Anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethology</a> as well as various other disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> attempt to explain the rise of absolute monarchy ranging from extrapolation generally, to certain <a href="/wiki/The_Eighteenth_Brumaire_of_Louis_Napoleon" title="The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Napoleon">Marxist explanations</a> in terms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a> as the underlying dynamic of human historical development generally and absolute monarchy in particular.</p>
<p>In the 17th century, French legal theorist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Domat" title="Jean Domat">Jean Domat</a> defended the concept of absolute monarchy in works such as <i>"On Social Order and Absolute Monarchy"</i>, citing absolute monarchy as preserving natural <a href="/wiki/Orderliness" title="Orderliness">order</a> as <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> intended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Norbert Elias's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Civilizing_Process" title="The Civilizing Process">The Civilizing Process</a></i> (1939), monarchs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a> could enjoy such great power because of the structure of the societies at that time: more precisely, they could play off against each other two rival classes, namely the rising <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a>, who grew wealthy from commerce and industrial production, and the <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>, who lived off the land and administrative functions.</p>
