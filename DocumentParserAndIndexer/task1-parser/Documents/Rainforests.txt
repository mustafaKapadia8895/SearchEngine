<p><b>Rainforests</b> are <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> characterized by high <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>, with annual rainfall in the case of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a> between 250 and 450 centimetres (98 and 177 in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and definitions varying by region for <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate rainforests</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, alternatively known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">intertropical convergence zone</a>, plays a significant role in creating the climatic conditions necessary for the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s tropical rainforests.</p>
<p>Around 40% to 75% of all biotic <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_(ecology)" title="Indigenous (ecology)">indigenous</a> to the rainforests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variables_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Variables-2">[2]</a></sup> It has been estimated that there may be many millions of species of plants, insects and <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> still undiscovered in tropical rainforests. Tropical rainforests have been called the "jewels of the Earth" and the "<a href="/wiki/Medicine_chest_(idiom)" title="Medicine chest (idiom)">world's largest pharmacy</a>", because over one quarter of natural <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicines</a> have been discovered there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Rainforests are also responsible for 28% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> turnover, sometimes misnamed oxygen production,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> processing it through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and consuming it through respiration.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Undergrowth" title="Undergrowth">undergrowth</a> in some areas of a rainforest can be restricted by poor penetration of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> to ground level. If the <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaf</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a> is destroyed or thinned, the ground beneath is soon colonized by a dense, tangled growth of <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, called a <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungle</a>. The term <i>jungle</i> is also sometimes applied to tropical rainforests generally.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical">Tropical</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">Tropical rainforest</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Koppen_World_Map_Af.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="4231" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koppen_World_Map_Af.png/400px-Koppen_World_Map_Af.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koppen_World_Map_Af.png/600px-Koppen_World_Map_Af.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koppen_World_Map_Af.png/800px-Koppen_World_Map_Af.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Koppen_World_Map_Af.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Worldwide tropical rainforest zones.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Tropical rainforests</b> are characterized by a warm and wet climate with no substantial dry season: typically found within 10 degrees north and south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. Mean monthly temperatures exceed 18 °C (64 °F) during all months of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Average annual rainfall is no less than 168 cm (66 in) and can exceed 1,000 cm (390 in) although it typically lies between 175 cm (69 in) and 200 cm (79 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the world's tropical forests are associated with the location of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, also known as the intertropical convergence zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The broader category of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_moist_forests" title="Tropical moist forests">tropical moist forests</a> are located in the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial zone</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>. Tropical rainforests exist in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Myanmar (Burma)</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congo</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congolian_forests" title="Congolian forests">Congo Rainforest</a>), <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> (e.g. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a>), <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bosaw%C3%A1s_Biosphere_Reserve" title="Bosawás Biosphere Reserve">Bosawás</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>-<a href="/wiki/Pet%C3%A9n_Department" title="Petén Department">El Peten</a>-<a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>-<a href="/wiki/Calakmul_Biosphere_Reserve" title="Calakmul Biosphere Reserve">Calakmul</a>), <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and on many of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_tropical_rainforests" title="Hawaiian tropical rainforests">Hawai<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i</a>). Tropical forests have been called the "Earth's <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>", although it is now known that rainforests contribute little net <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> addition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical forests cover a large part of the globe, but <b>temperate rainforests</b> only occur in few regions around the world. Temperate rainforests are rainforests in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> regions. They occur in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (parts of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> such as the coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Norway" title="Southern Norway">southern Norway</a>, parts of the western <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic</a> coast, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> and coastal areas of the eastern <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a> and coastal <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>), in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Northern_and_southern_China" title="Northern and southern China">southern China</a>, Highlands of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, much of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and on <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin Island</a> and the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East" title="Temperate rainforests of the Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> coast), in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> (southern <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>) and also in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Layers">Layers</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(vegetation)" title="Stratification (vegetation)">Stratification (vegetation)</a></div>
<p>A tropical rainforest typically has a number of layers, each with different plants and animals adapted for life in that particular area. Examples include the emergent, canopy, understorey and <a href="/wiki/Forest_floor" title="Forest floor">forest floor</a> layers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergent_layer">Emergent layer</span></h3>
<p>The <b>emergent layer</b> contains a small number of very large <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> called <b>emergents</b>, which grow above the general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a>, reaching heights of 45–55 m, although on occasion a few species will grow to 70–80 m tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> They need to be able to withstand the hot temperatures and strong winds that occur above the canopy in some areas. <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">Eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> and certain <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a> inhabit this layer.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canopy_layer">Canopy layer</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">Canopy (biology)</a></div>
<p>The <b>canopy</b> layer contains the majority of the largest trees, typically 30 metres (98 ft) to 45 metres (148 ft) tall. The densest areas of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> are found in the forest canopy, a more or less continuous cover of foliage formed by adjacent treetops. The canopy, by some estimates, is home to 50 percent of all plant species. <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">Epiphytic plants</a> attach to <a href="/wiki/Trunk_(botany)" title="Trunk (botany)">trunks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Branch" title="Branch">branches</a>, and obtain water and minerals from rain and debris that collects on the supporting plants. The fauna is similar to that found in the emergent layer, but more diverse. A quarter of all insect species are believed to exist in the rainforest canopy. Scientists have long suspected the richness of the canopy as a habitat, but have only recently developed practical methods of exploring it. As long ago as 1917, <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Beebe" title="William Beebe">William Beebe</a> declared that "another continent of life remains to be discovered, not upon the Earth, but one to two hundred feet above it, extending over thousands of square miles." True exploration of this habitat only began in the 1980s, when scientists developed methods to reach the canopy, such as firing ropes into the trees using <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbows</a>. <a href="/wiki/Canopy_research" title="Canopy research">Exploration of the canopy</a> is still in its infancy, but other methods include the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">balloons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a> to float above the highest branches and the building of cranes and walkways planted on the forest floor. The science of accessing tropical forest canopy using airships or similar aerial platforms is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendronautics" title="Dendronautics">dendronautics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Understory_layer">Understory layer</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">Understory</a></div>
<p>The <b>understory</b> or <b>understorey</b> layer lies between the canopy and the forest floor. It is home to a number of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">jaguars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boa_(genus)" title="Boa (genus)">boa constrictors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a>. The leaves are much larger at this level and insect life is abundant. Many seedlings that will grow to the canopy level are present in the understory. Only about 5% of the sunlight shining on the rainforest canopy reaches the understory. This layer can be called a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrub_layer" title="Shrub layer">shrub layer</a></i>, although the shrub layer may also be considered a separate layer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_floor">Forest floor</span></h3>

<p>The <b>forest floor</b>, the bottom-most layer, receives only 2% of the sunlight. Only plants <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adapted</a> to low light can grow in this region. Away from <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">riverbanks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and clearings, where dense undergrowth is found, the forest floor is relatively clear of vegetation because of the low sunlight penetration. It also contains <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decaying</a> plant and animal matter, which disappears quickly, because the warm, humid conditions promote rapid decay. Many forms of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> growing here help decay the animal and plant waste.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>More than half of the world's species of plants and animals are found in the rainforest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Rainforests support a very broad array of <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. Mammals may include <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">felids</a> and other families. Reptiles include <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chameleon" title="Chameleon">chameleons</a> and other families; while birds include such families as <a href="/wiki/Vanga" title="Vanga">vangidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">Cuculidae</a>. Dozens of families of invertebrates are found in rainforests. <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">Fungi</a> are also very common in rainforest areas as they can feed on the decomposing remains of plants and animals. Many rainforest species are rapidly disappearing due to <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, habitat loss and pollution of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Despite the growth of <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> in a tropical rainforest, <a href="/wiki/Soil_quality" title="Soil quality">soil quality</a> is often quite poor. Rapid <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> decay prevents the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a>. The concentration of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">laterization</a> process gives the <a href="/wiki/Oxisol" title="Oxisol">oxisols</a> a bright red colour and sometimes produces <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposits</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>. Most trees have roots near the surface, because there are insufficient nutrients below the surface; most of the trees' minerals come from the top layer of decomposing leaves and animals. On younger substrates, especially of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> origin, tropical soils may be quite fertile. If rainforest trees are cleared, rain can accumulate on the exposed soil surfaces, creating run-off and beginning a process of soil erosion. Eventually streams and rivers form and flooding becomes possible. In the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a>, the trees are being cut down at a terrible rate and the soil which holds the trees isn't capable of doing anything but holding the trees. Brazilian farmers have tried to farm with the soil with bad results.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_global_climate">Effect on global climate</span></h2>
<p>A natural rainforest emits and absorbs vast quantities of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. On a global scale, long-term fluxes are approximately in balance, so that an undisturbed rainforest would have a small net impact on atmospheric carbon dioxide levels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> though they may have other climatic effects (on <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> formation, for example, by recycling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a>). No rainforest today can be considered to be undisturbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-17">[17]</a></sup> Human-induced deforestation plays a significant role in causing rainforests to release carbon dioxide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhi_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malhi-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trgac_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-trgac-19">[19]</a></sup> as do other factors, whether human-induced or natural, which result in tree death, such as burning and drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Some climate models operating with interactive vegetation predict a large loss of Amazonian rainforest around 2050 due to drought, forest dieback and the subsequent release more carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Five million years from now, the Amazon rainforest may long since have dried and transformed itself into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">savannah</a>, killing itself in the progress (changes such as this may happen even if all human deforestation activity ceases overnight).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wildfuturetv_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-wildfuturetv-22">[22]</a></sup> The descendants of our known animals may adapt to the dry savannah of the former Amazonian rainforest and thrive in the new, warmer temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wildfuturetv_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-wildfuturetv-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical rainforests provide timber as well as animal products such as meat and hides. Rainforests also have value as tourism destinations and for the ecosystem services provided. Many foods originally came from tropical forests, and are still mostly grown on <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> in regions that were formerly primary forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Also, plant-derived medicines are commonly used for fever, fungal infections, burns, gastrointestinal problems, pain, respiratory problems, and wound treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>On January 18, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Nacional_do_%C3%8Dndio" title="Fundação Nacional do Índio">FUNAI</a> reported also that it had confirmed the presence of 67 different <a href="/wiki/Uncontacted_peoples" title="Uncontacted peoples">uncontacted tribes</a> in Brazil, up from 40 in 2005. With this addition, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> has now overtaken the island of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> as the country having the largest number of uncontacted tribes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The province of Irian Jaya or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Papua_(Indonesian_province)" title="West Papua (Indonesian province)">West Papua</a> in the island of New Guinea is home to an estimated 44 uncontacted tribal groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The tribes are in danger because of the deforestation, especially in Brazil.</p>
<p>Central African rainforest is home of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbuti" title="Mbuti">Mbuti</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pygmies" title="Pygmies">pygmies</a>, one of the hunter-gatherer peoples living in equatorial rainforests characterised by their short height (below one and a half metres, or 59 inches, on average). They were the subject of a study by <a href="/wiki/Colin_Turnbull" title="Colin Turnbull">Colin Turnbull</a>, <i>The Forest People</i>, in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Pygmies who live in Southeast Asia are, amongst others, referred to as “<a href="/wiki/Negrito" title="Negrito">Negrito</a>”.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">Tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate rainforests</a> have been subjected to heavy <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> and agricultural clearance throughout the 20th century and the area covered by rainforests around the world is shrinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Biologists have estimated that large numbers of species are being driven to <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> (possibly more than 50,000 a year; at that rate, says <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, a quarter or more of all species on Earth could be exterminated within 50 years)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> due to the removal of habitat with destruction of the rainforests.</p>
<p>Another factor causing the loss of rainforest is expanding urban areas. <a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">Littoral</a> rainforest growing along <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> areas of eastern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> is now rare due to <a href="/wiki/Ribbon_development" title="Ribbon development">ribbon development</a> to accommodate the demand for <a href="/wiki/Seachange_(demography)" title="Seachange (demography)">seachange</a> lifestyles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The forests are being destroyed at a rapid pace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Almost 90% of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>'s rainforest has been destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Since the arrival of humans, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> has lost two thirds of its original rainforest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> At present rates, tropical rainforests in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> would be logged out in 10 years and <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> in 13 to 16 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Rettet_den_Regenwald" title="Rettet den Regenwald">Rainforest Rescue</a>, a main reason for the increasing <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> rate especially in Indonesia is the expansion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_palm" title="Oil palm">oil palm</a> plantations to meet the growing demand for cheap vegetable fats and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>. In Indonesia, <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> is already cultivated on nine million hectares and, together with <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the island nation produces about 85 percent of the world’s palm oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (November 2012)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Several countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> notably <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, have declared their deforestation a national emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Amazon deforestation jumped by 69% in 2008 compared to 2007's twelve months, according to official government data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Deforestation could wipe out or severely damage nearly 60% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a> by 2030, says a 2007 report from <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, a January 30, 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> article stated, "By one estimate, for every <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acre</a> of rain forest cut down each year, more than 50 acres of new forest are growing in the tropics..." The new forest includes secondary forest on former farmland and so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degraded_forest" title="Degraded forest">degraded forest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
