<p>A <b>satellite dish</b> is a dish-shaped type of <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antenna</a> designed to receive or transmit information by <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> to or from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">communication satellite</a>. The term most commonly means a dish used by consumers to receive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite_television" title="Direct-broadcast satellite television">direct-broadcast satellite television</a> from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_broadcast_satellite" title="Direct broadcast satellite">direct broadcast satellite</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle_of_operation">Principle of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principle of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1820" data-file-width="850" height="471" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg/220px-Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg/330px-Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg/440px-Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Parabolic_antenna_types2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Schematics of reflection principles used in parabolic antennas.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic</a> shape of a dish reflects the signal to the dish’s <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focal point</a>. Mounted on brackets at the dish's focal point is a device called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedhorn" title="Feedhorn">feedhorn</a>. This feedhorn is essentially the front-end of a <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguide</a> that gathers the signals at or near the focal point and 'conducts' them to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-noise_block_converter" title="Low-noise block converter">low-noise block downconverter</a> or LNB. The LNB converts the signals from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_waves" title="Electromagnetic waves">electromagnetic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a> to electrical signals and shifts the signals from the downlinked <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C-band</a> and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ku-band" title="Ku-band">K<sub>u</sub>-band</a> to the <a href="/wiki/L_band" title="L band">L-band</a> range. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_broadcast_satellite" title="Direct broadcast satellite">Direct broadcast satellite</a> dishes use an LNBF, which integrates the feedhorn with the LNB. (A new form of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/omnidirectional" title="wiktionary:omnidirectional">omnidirectional</a> satellite antenna, which does not use a directed parabolic dish and can be used on a mobile platform such as a vehicle was announced by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo" title="University of Waterloo">University of Waterloo</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The theoretical gain (<a href="/wiki/Antenna_gain" title="Antenna gain">directive gain</a>) of a dish increases as the frequency increases. The actual gain depends on many factors including surface finish, accuracy of shape, feedhorn matching. A typical value for a consumer type 60 cm satellite dish at 11.75 GHz is 37.50 dB.</p>
<p>With lower frequencies, C-band for example, dish designers have a wider choice of materials. The large size of dish required for lower frequencies led to the dishes being constructed from metal mesh on a metal framework. At higher frequencies, mesh type designs are rarer though some designs have used a solid dish with perforations.</p>
<p>A common misconception is that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-noise_block_converter" title="Low-noise block converter">LNBF</a> (low-noise block/feedhorn), the device at the front of the dish, receives the signal directly from the atmosphere. For instance, one <a href="/wiki/BBC_News_(TV_channel)" title="BBC News (TV channel)">BBC News</a> downlink shows a "red signal" being received by the LNBF directly instead of being beamed to the dish, which because of its parabolic shape will collect the signal into a smaller area and deliver it to the LNBF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern dishes intended for home television use are generally 43 cm (18 in) to 80 cm (31 in) in <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, and are fixed in one position, for Ku-band reception from one orbital position. Prior to the existence of direct broadcast satellite services, home users would generally have a motorised C-band dish of up to 3 m in diameter for reception of channels from different satellites. Overly small dishes can still cause problems, however, including <a href="/wiki/Rain_fade" title="Rain fade">rain fade</a> and interference from adjacent satellites.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the frequencies used by DBS services are 10.7–12.75 GHz on two polarisations H (Horizontal) and V (Vertical). This range is divided into a "low band" with 10.7–11.7 GHz, and a "high band" with 11.7–12.75 GHz. This results in two frequency bands, each with a bandwidth of about 1 GHz, each with two possible polarizations. In the LNB they become down converted to 950–2150 MHz, which is the frequency range allocated for the satellite service on the coaxial cable between LNBF and receiver. Lower frequencies are allocated to <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_TV" title="Terrestrial TV">terrestrial TV</a>, FM radio, etc. Only one of these frequency bands fits on the coaxial cable, so each of these bands needs a separate cable from the LNBF to a switching matrix or the receiver needs to select one of the 4 possibilities at a time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems_design">Systems design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systems design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a single receiver residential installation there is a single <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a> running from the receiver <a href="/wiki/Set-top_box" title="Set-top box">set-top box</a> in the building to the LNB on the dish. The DC electric power for the LNB is provided through the same coaxial cable conductors that carry the signal to the receiver. In addition, control signals are also transmitted from the receiver to the LNB through the cable. The receiver uses different power supply voltages (13 / 18 V) to select antenna <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarization_(antenna)" title="Polarization (antenna)">polarization</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pilot_signal" title="Pilot signal">pilot tones</a> (22 kHz) to instruct the LNB to select one of the two frequency bands. In larger installations each band and polarization is given its own cable, so there are 4 cables from the LNB to a switching matrix, which allows the connection of multiple receivers in a star topology using the same signalling method as in a single receiver installation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Satellite_finder" title="Satellite finder">satellite finder</a> may aid in aiming.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor-driven_dish">Motor-driven dish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Motor-driven dish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A dish that is mounted on a pole and driven by a <a href="/wiki/Stepper_motor" title="Stepper motor">stepper motor</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servo_motor" title="Servo motor">servo</a> can be controlled and rotated to face any satellite position in the sky. Motor-driven dishes are popular with enthusiasts. There are three competing standards: <a href="/wiki/DiSEqC" title="DiSEqC">DiSEqC</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USALS" title="USALS">USALS</a>, and 36 V positioners. Many receivers support all of these standards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-satellite">Multi-satellite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Multi-satellite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antenne-toroidale.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1895" data-file-width="1323" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Antenne-toroidale.jpg/160px-Antenne-toroidale.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Antenne-toroidale.jpg/240px-Antenne-toroidale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Antenne-toroidale.jpg/320px-Antenne-toroidale.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antenne-toroidale.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Special dish for up to 16 satellite positions (K<sub>u</sub>-band).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some designs enable simultaneous reception from multiple different satellite positions without re-positioning the dish. The vertical axis operates as an off-axis concave parabolic concave hyperbolic <a href="/wiki/Cassegrain_reflector" title="Cassegrain reflector">Cassegrain reflector</a>, while the horizontal axis operates as a concave convex Cassegrain. The spot from the main dish wanders across the secondary, which corrects <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism_(optical_systems)" title="Astigmatism (optical systems)">astigmatism</a> by its varying curvature. The elliptic aperture of the primary is designed to fit the deformed illumination by the horns. Due to double spill-over, this makes more sense for a large dish.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VSAT">VSAT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: VSAT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A common type of dish is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_small_aperture_terminal" title="Very small aperture terminal">very small aperture terminal</a> (VSAT). This provides two way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_internet" title="Satellite internet">satellite internet</a> communications for both consumers and private networks for organizations. Today most VSATs operate in <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub> band</a>; <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C band</a> is restricted to less populated regions of the world. There is a move which started in 2005 towards new <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">K<sub>a</sub> band</a> satellites operating at higher frequencies, offering greater performance at lower cost. These antennas vary from 74 to 120 cm (29 to 47 in) in most applications though C-band VSATs may be as large as 4 m (13 ft).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homemade_dishes">Homemade dishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Homemade dishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any metal surface which concentrates a significant fraction of the reflected microwaves at a focus can be used as a dish antenna, at a lower <a href="/wiki/Antenna_gain" title="Antenna gain">gain</a>. This has led to trash can lids, woks, and other items being used as "dishes". Only modern low noise LNBs and the higher transmission power of DTH satellites allows a usable signal to be received from such inefficient DIY antennas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_dish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parabolic antennas referred to as "dish" antennas had been in use long before satellite television. The term "satellite dish" was coined in 1978 during the beginning of the satellite television industry, and came to refer to dish antennas that send and/or receive signals from communications satellites. <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Howard" title="Taylor Howard">Taylor Howard</a> of <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas,_California" title="San Andreas, California">San Andreas, California</a> adapted an ex-military dish in 1976 and became the first person to receive satellite television signals using it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tayhoward_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tayhoward-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first satellite television dishes were built to receive signals on the <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C-band</a> analog, and were very large. The front cover of the 1979 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neiman-Marcus" title="Neiman-Marcus">Neiman-Marcus</a> Christmas catalog featured the first home satellite TV stations on sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uspopcult_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-uspopcult-4">[4]</a></sup> The dishes were nearly 20 feet (6.1 m) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat-5">[5]</a></sup> The satellite dishes of the early 1980s were 10 to 16 feet (3.0 to 4.9 m) in diameter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deseret_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-deseret-6">[6]</a></sup> and made of <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> with an embedded layer of wire mesh or aluminum foil, or solid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baltsun_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-baltsun-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Satellite dishes made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wire_mesh" title="Wire mesh">wire mesh</a> first came out in the early 1980s, and were at first 10 feet (3.0 m) in diameter. As the front-end technology improved and the <a href="/wiki/Noise_figure" title="Noise figure">noise figure</a> of the LNBs fell, the size shrank to 8 feet (2.4 m) a few years later, and continued to get smaller reducing to 6 feet (1.8 m) feet by the late 1980s and 4 feet (1.2 m) by the early 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuscnews_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tuscnews-8">[8]</a></sup> Larger dishes continued to be used, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuscnews_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-tuscnews-8">[8]</a></sup> In December 1988 <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Astra_1A" title="Astra 1A">Astra 1A</a> satellite began transmitting analog television signals on the <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub> band</a> for the European market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2yo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-n2yo-9">[9]</a></sup> This allowed small dishes (90 cm) to be used reliably for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2yo_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-n2yo-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1990s, four large American cable companies founded <a href="/wiki/PrimeStar" title="PrimeStar">PrimeStar</a>, a direct broadcasting company using medium power satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangor_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bangor-10">[10]</a></sup> The relatively strong K<sub>u</sub> band transmissions allowed the use of dishes as small as 90 cm for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangor_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-bangor-10">[10]</a></sup> On 4 March 1996 <a href="/wiki/EchoStar" title="EchoStar">EchoStar</a> introduced Digital Sky Highway (<a href="/wiki/Dish_Network" title="Dish Network">Dish Network</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctu10e_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctu10e-11">[11]</a></sup> This was the first widely used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite_television" title="Direct-broadcast satellite television">direct-broadcast satellite television</a> system and allowed dishes as small as 20 cm to be used. This great decrease of dish size also allowed satellite dishes to be installed on vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Dishes this size are still in use today. Television stations, however, still prefer to transmit their signals on the C-band analog with large dishes due to the fact that C-band signals are less prone to <a href="/wiki/Rain_fade" title="Rain fade">rain fade</a> than K<sub>u</sub> band signals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dishcable_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dishcable-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
