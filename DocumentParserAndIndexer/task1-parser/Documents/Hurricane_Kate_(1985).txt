<p><b>Hurricane Kate</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Huracán Kate</i></span> <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[uɾaˈkan ˈket]</a></span>) was the final in a series of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> to impact the United States during 1985, and the latest in any calendar year to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">strike</a> the country at <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale#Modern_scale" title="Beaufort scale">hurricane intensity</a> on record. The eleventh <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a>, seventh hurricane, and third major hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1985_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1985 Atlantic hurricane season">annual hurricane season</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> Kate originated from the interaction of an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> northeast of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> on November 15. Though the system tracked erratically during the first hours of its existence, the intensification of a region of <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high pressure</a> to the cyclone's north caused Kate to turn westward. A favorable atmospheric pattern allowed the newly developed system to intensify to hurricane intensity on November 16, and further to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Category 2</a> intensity three days later.</p>
<p>Kate made its first landfall on the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> at this intensity prior to emerging as a slightly weaker storm during the evening hours of November 19. Once clear of land, it began to strengthen quickly, becoming a Category 3 and reaching its peak intensity of 120 mph (195 km/h) the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> On November 21, a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> moving across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a> resulted in a north and eventual northeast turn of the cyclone, and Kate came ashore near <a href="/wiki/Mexico_Beach,_Florida" title="Mexico Beach, Florida">Mexico Beach, Florida</a>, as a minimal Category 2 hurricane with winds of 100 mph (160 km/h). Gradual weakening ensued as the cyclone moved along the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_States" title="South Atlantic States">Southeast United States</a> coastline, and Kate transitioned to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_transition#extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical transition">extratropical cyclone</a> on November 23, a day after exiting the coastline of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.</p>
<p>The threat of Hurricane Kate in Cuba prompted the evacuation of 360,000 people. Heavy rainfall in Cuba caused numerous mudslides and flooding, killing 10 people and leading to severe <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Cuba" title="Agriculture in Cuba">agriculture</a> damage. Wind gusts over hurricane intensity resulted in widespread power outages, significant building damage, and major crop damage. Damage totaled roughly $400 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[nb 2]</a></sup> making it the most damaging hurricane to strike the island in many decades. In preparation for the system's arrival, many hurricane <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">watches and warnings</a> were put into effect. Hundreds of thousands of residents were evacuated, and Florida governor <a href="/wiki/Bob_Graham" title="Bob Graham">Bob Graham</a> declared a state of emergency for six counties; this was later cancelled following the relatively minor impacts of Kate. In addition, many shelters were opened.</p>
<p>When Kate struck the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, it became the first hurricane to make landfall in that location since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Eloise" title="Hurricane Eloise">Hurricane Eloise</a> in 1975. Storm surge and flooding rains destroyed much of the <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> industry, causing many people to lose their jobs in the weeks after the storm. Gusts over 100 mph (160 km/h) contributed to downed trees and building damage, while the combination of wind and rain led to downed power poles. Across the remainder of the southeast United States, several inches of rainfall led to flash flooding, damage to roadways, and major tree damage. Overall, Kate resulted in 15 fatalities and $700 million in damage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kate_1985_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Kate_1985_track.png/220px-Kate_1985_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Kate_1985_track.png/330px-Kate_1985_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Kate_1985_track.png/440px-Kate_1985_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kate_1985_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before the formation of Hurricane Kate, a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> was located across the southeastern United States for much of the autumn of 1985; concurrently, a major <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> persisted across the western portion of the country. As a result, weather conditions across the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and western Atlantic Ocean in November were more typical of the pattern in late September, including <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> of 81 °F (27 °C). On November 13, a weak <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[nb 3]</a></sup> began interacting with a trough to the northeast of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. It gradually organized due to the favorable conditions, and on November 15, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> flight into the area indicated the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">development</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>. As gale force winds were already present, the system was immediately declared Tropical Storm Kate, about 240 miles (385 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-6">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>With a ridge to its north, Kate tracked westward after developing, and an <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">upper-level low</a> developed to the southwest of the storm. The combination of the two provided favorable <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a>, allowing Kate to quickly intensify. On November 16, the storm attained hurricane status while moving through the southeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-6">[3]</a></sup> After continued strengthening, Kate made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> at 0600 UTC on November 19 over north-central Cuba with a well-defined <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-7">[4]</a></sup> When it moved ashore, Kate had a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> of 967 mbar (28.6 inHg) and winds of about 110 mph (180 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-7">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-8">[5]</a></sup> The hurricane maintained its well-defined eye while moving across northern Cuba, and about 12 hours after making landfall, it emerged into the southeastern Gulf of Mexico just east of <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>. Over the next 24 hours, Kate re-intensified off the southwest coast of Florida as it passed about 85 mi (135 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West</a>. On November 20, the Hurricane Hunters observed winds as strong as 125 mph (200 km/h), and a <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">buoy</a> recorded a gust of 136 mph (219 km/h);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-7">[4]</a></sup> this was the highest recorded wind gust from a buoy in the Gulf of Mexico until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lili" title="Hurricane Lili">Hurricane Lili</a> in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[6]</a></sup> Based on these observations, it was estimated that Kate attained peak winds of about 120 mph (190 km/h) around 1200 UTC on November 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-8">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Kate maintained peak intensity for about 18 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-8">[5]</a></sup> On November 21, a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> moving through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a> deflected the hurricane to the north and northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-10">[7]</a></sup> The combination of cooler waters and <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> from the front weakened Kate to an intensity of 100 mph (160 km/h) by the time the hurricane struck Crooked Island near <a href="/wiki/Mexico_Beach,_Florida" title="Mexico Beach, Florida">Mexico Beach, Florida</a> late on November 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-10">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> After landfall, Kate continued to the northeast, crossing into Georgia and weakened into a tropical storm. Kate emerged from North Carolina into the Atlantic Ocean late on November 22. Encountering even colder waters and continued shear, the storm weakened further while turning to the east-southeast. On November 23, Kate transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> to the west of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-10">[7]</a></sup> terminating at 1800 UTC that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-8">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 2011, Kate's was considered the second-latest <i>hurricane</i> landfall in the United States, behind only a <a href="/wiki/1925_Florida_tropical_storm" title="1925 Florida tropical storm">cyclone in 1925</a> that struck on December 1; however, a systematic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">reanalysis</a> indicated that the 1925 system was only a tropical storm. In turn, Kate took the record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latest_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-latest-12">[9]</a></sup> With Kate's landfall, the 1985 season had six hurricanes that struck the United States, only one short of the record seven in <a href="/wiki/1886_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1886 Atlantic hurricane season">1886</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By November 18, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warning</a> was in effect for the southeast and central Bahamas and the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mjs_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-mjs-14">[11]</a></sup> Flood warnings were issued for northern Puerto Rico and the Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[12]</a></sup> In preparation for the hurricane's arrival, officials forced 360,000 people to evacuate in north-central Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Kate was moving through the Bahamas, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center (NHC)</a> issued a hurricane warning from <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter</a> to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers,_Florida" title="Fort Myers, Florida">Fort Myers, Florida</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr5_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr5-16">[13]</a></sup> Then-<a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Florida" title="List of Governors of Florida">Governor of Florida</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Graham" title="Bob Graham">Bob Graham</a> declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> for <a href="/wiki/List_of_counties_in_Florida" title="List of counties in Florida">six counties</a> in South Florida. However, it was reversed following the relatively minor effects in the area. Officials recommended evacuation of the Florida Keys, leading to heavy traffic on the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Highway" title="Overseas Highway">Overseas Highway</a> and prompting the <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">Red Cross</a> to open 12 shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi1118_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lodi1118-17">[14]</a></sup> Three shelters were opened in Key West, but only 500 individuals utilized them during the storm. Most residents chose to endure the storm in their homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pp-g_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pp-g-18">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a>, schools were closed and residents of mobile homes were required to leave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi1118_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-lodi1118-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly after the storm reached its peak intensity on November 20, the NHC issued a hurricane watch from <a href="/wiki/Grand_Isle,_Louisiana" title="Grand Isle, Louisiana">Grand Isle, Louisiana</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Key,_Florida" title="Cedar Key, Florida">Cedar Key, Florida</a>. Later that day, a portion of the watch area was upgraded to a warning from <a href="/wiki/Bay_St._Louis,_Mississippi" title="Bay St. Louis, Mississippi">Bay St. Louis, Mississippi</a> to <a href="/wiki/St._Marks,_Florida" title="St. Marks, Florida">St. Marks, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr5_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr5-16">[13]</a></sup> About 20,000 employees on <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil platforms</a> in the Gulf of Mexico were evacuated, many by helicopter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pp-g_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-pp-g-18">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/USS_Lexington_(CV-16)" title="USS Lexington (CV-16)">USS <i>Lexington</i></a> left port from <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Pensacola" title="Naval Air Station Pensacola">Naval Air Station Pensacola</a> to ride out the storm in open waters, and aircraft in the region were flown inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup> About 100,000 people along the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> were told to leave their houses after Governor Bob Graham issued evacuation orders in 13 counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[17]</a></sup> About 2,000 people stayed in 34 shelters in <a href="/wiki/Panama_City,_Florida" title="Panama City, Florida">Panama City</a>. Roads in the region suffered traffic jams from the large volume of evacuees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1121_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap1121-21">[18]</a></sup> Portions of the Florida Gulf Coast had been threatened by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Elena" title="Hurricane Elena">Hurricane Elena</a> earlier in the season, and some evacuees of that storm intended not to leave during Kate due to the poor shelter conditions that they had experienced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[19]</a></sup> Governor Graham activated 300 members of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_National_Guard" title="Florida National Guard">Florida National Guard</a> to prevent looting and to assist in evacuations. One person died from a stress-induced heart attack in <a href="/wiki/Chipley,_Florida" title="Chipley, Florida">Chipley</a> after evacuating. Outside of Florida, about 2,200 people fled Grand Isle, Louisiana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1121_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap1121-21">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Kate moved ashore, the NHC issued gale warnings along the East Coast of the United States from <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine,_Florida" title="St. Augustine, Florida">St. Augustine, Florida</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chincoteague,_Virginia" title="Chincoteague, Virginia">Chincoteague, Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_and_Turks_and_Caicos_Islands">Caribbean and Turks and Caicos Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caribbean and Turks and Caicos Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in its duration, Hurricane Kate sank one boat near Puerto Rico and disabled three others. The crew of five on the sunken boat were rescued after 17 hours. Several homes in northern Puerto Rico were damaged, forcing hundreds to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> Flooding was also reported in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, including around the capital <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi1118_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-lodi1118-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heavy rainfall and winds up to 60 mph (97 km/h) were reported in the Turks and Caicos Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mjs_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-mjs-14">[11]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, heavy precipitation caused mudslides, which in turn blocked 23 major and minor roads and destroyed many bridges, culverts, and drains. Flooding in general caused severe damage to agriculture, especially in <a href="/wiki/Clarendon_Parish,_Jamaica" title="Clarendon Parish, Jamaica">Clarendon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Parish" title="Manchester Parish">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ann_Parish" title="Saint Ann Parish">Saint Ann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Elizabeth_Parish" title="Saint Elizabeth Parish">Saint Elizabeth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trelawny_Parish" title="Trelawny Parish">Trelawny Parishes</a>. Seven fatalities were reported, while the cost to repair damage was approximately $3 million (1985 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jamaica_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jamaica-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Kate moved across northern Cuba, it produced strong winds that peaked at 75 mph (120 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Sagua_La_Grande" title="Sagua La Grande">Sagua La Grande</a>. Wind gusts peaked at 104 mph (167 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Varadero" title="Varadero">Varadero</a>, and winds in the capital of Havana reached 70 mph (110 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-7">[4]</a></sup> In Havana, high winds caused power outages and destroyed buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> Waves of 9 feet (2.7 m) affected the city's waterfront.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup> Outside of Havana, the hurricane damaged sugar mills and much of the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_sugar_economy" title="Cuban sugar economy">sugar cane crop</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> throughout the island, the winds destroyed 3,653 <a href="/wiki/Square_mile" title="Square mile">miles<sup>2</sup></a> (9461 <a href="/wiki/Square_kilometre" title="Square kilometre">km<sup>2</sup></a>) of sugar cane and 34,000 tonnes (37,000 tons) of sugar. The storm also destroyed 141,000 tonnes (139,000 long tons; 155,000 short tons) of bananas and 87,078 tonnes (85,703 long tons; 95,987 short tons) of other fruits and vegetables. Across the island, Kate damaged 88,207 houses and destroyed 4,382 others, affecting 476,891 people. Many public buildings, including schools, were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rw_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-rw-27">[24]</a></sup> Throughout the country, Kate killed 10 people and injured about 50 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> Damage was estimated at $400 million, which was the highest total from all landfalling hurricanes from 1903 to 1998, unadjusted for inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>As Kate passed to the southwest of Key West, the storm produced winds of 47 mph (76 km/h) there, with unofficial wind gusts of 104 mph (167 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-7">[4]</a></sup> Rainfall totals in southwest Florida were generally around 1 in (25 mm),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-29">[26]</a></sup> although Key West reported 2.08 in (53 mm) of precipitation. High winds downed trees and power lines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup> leaving areas between Key West and <a href="/wiki/Big_Pine_Key,_Florida" title="Big Pine Key, Florida">Big Pine Key</a> without power. Electrical outages contributed to a mobile home being destroyed by fire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> and one person died through electrocution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup> Above-normal tides caused minor flooding and erosion along the Florida Keys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> Two people died after their boat capsized in the lower Keys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kate was the first hurricane to make landfall in the Florida Panhandle since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Eloise" title="Hurricane Eloise">Hurricane Eloise</a> in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> In the region, the hurricane dropped heavy rainfall along its path, peaking at 8.32 in (211 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Panama_City,_Florida" title="Panama City, Florida">Panama City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-rain-29">[26]</a></sup> While moving ashore, Kate produced an 11 feet (3.4 m) <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> at Cape San Blas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-10">[7]</a></sup> causing beach and dune erosion in <a href="/wiki/Gulf_County,_Florida" title="Gulf County, Florida">Gulf County</a>. Storm surge flooding left 150 houses uninhabitable in <a href="/wiki/Wakulla_County,_Florida" title="Wakulla County, Florida">Wakulla County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fsbpa_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-fsbpa-31">[28]</a></sup> The hurricane damaged a bridge to <a href="/wiki/St._George_Island_(Florida)" title="St. George Island (Florida)">St. George Island</a> that had been rebuilt after Hurricane Elena, and large portions of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_90_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 90 in Florida">U.S. Routes 90</a> and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_98_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 98 in Florida">98</a> were washed out or damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tally_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-tally-32">[29]</a></sup> Just two months after Elena ravaged the <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola_Bay" title="Apalachicola Bay">Apalachicola Bay</a> shellfish harvesting industry, Hurricane Kate destroyed remaining oyster beds, leaving many oystermen in the area without jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr4_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr4-33">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strong winds buffeted the Florida Panhandle, accompanied by one <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> and several funnel clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcs_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-bcs-11">[8]</a></sup> In Panama City, wind gusts reached 78 mph (126 km/h), damaging two houses, a motel, and a fishing pier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fsbpa_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-fsbpa-31">[28]</a></sup> The winds were strong enough to remove the roof of a two-story federal building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[31]</a></sup> Sustained winds blew 74 mph (119 km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Cape_San_Blas" title="Cape San Blas">Cape San Blas</a>, with gusts up to 108 mph (174 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fsbpa_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-fsbpa-31">[28]</a></sup> Across the area, Kate severely damaged 242 buildings, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Franklin_County,_Florida" title="Franklin County, Florida">Franklin County</a>, where the storm ranked as the most devastating of the late 20th century. The storm compromised about 5.4 mi (8.7 km) of roads in the county,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fsbpa_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-fsbpa-31">[28]</a></sup> and throughout the region many roads were washed out. The intense winds brought down numerous trees, some of them onto adjacent structures. One fallen tree struck a car, killing one person and injuring another. The winds also downed power poles and lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup> About 90 percent of Florida's capital <a href="/wiki/Tallahassee,_Florida" title="Tallahassee, Florida">Tallahassee</a>, or about 80,000 people, lost power, and along the coast from Panama City to Apalachicola, the storm left about 30,000 homes and businesses without electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rob1122_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-rob1122-35">[32]</a></sup> Overall, the hurricane destroyed 325 homes along the panhandle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup> and about 500 buildings were severely damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Light rainfall of around 1 in (25 mm) from the hurricane extended into southeastern <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-rain-29">[26]</a></sup> Rainfall was much heavier in Georgia, peaking at 7.73 in (196 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Bainbridge,_Georgia" title="Bainbridge, Georgia">Bainbridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCSE_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPCSE-38">[35]</a></sup> Portions of southwestern Georgia experienced heavy damage from flash flooding and winds, and several secondary roads were washed out. Gusts of 80 mph (130 km/h) downed thousands of trees, and one fallen tree killed a man west of <a href="/wiki/Thomasville,_Georgia" title="Thomasville, Georgia">Thomasville</a>. The cotton, soybean, and pecan crops suffered heavy losses, estimated at around $50 million. Property and utility damage was also assessed at $50 million, and damage from flash flooding was estimated at $1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup> There were scattered power outages in southern Georgia, affecting fewer than 3,000 customers by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_Power_Company" title="Georgia Power Company">Georgia Power Company</a>'s estimation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rob1122_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-rob1122-35">[32]</a></sup> While moving across southeastern Georgia, Kate produced a 62 mph (100 km/h) wind gust in <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah</a>. The city also reported 1.73 in (44 mm) of rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ga_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ga-39">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Farther northeast, <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a> reported a wind gust of 50 mph (80 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ga_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-ga-39">[36]</a></sup> The highest rainfall total in the state was 6.56 in (167 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_South_Carolina" title="Hampton, South Carolina">Hampton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCSE_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPCSE-38">[35]</a></sup> The rains caused flash flooding that washed out secondary roads and a bridge. The storm knocked tree limbs onto power lines, leaving about 48,000 people without power. In <a href="/wiki/Beaufort,_South_Carolina" title="Beaufort, South Carolina">Beaufort</a>, trees fell onto four cars and a mobile home, and high waves sank a boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-5"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>, the storm dropped 1.99 in (51 mm) of precipitation. Rains across the state caused generally minor flooding, although several cars were swept off roadways. Rising floodwaters prompted the evacuation of a nursing home in <a href="/wiki/Kannapolis,_North_Carolina" title="Kannapolis, North Carolina">Kannapolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_30-6"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-30">[27]</a></sup> Rainfall extended northward into <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-rain-29">[26]</a></sup> Damage throughout the United States was estimated at $300 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an extratropical cyclone, Kate moved north of Bermuda and produced wind gusts of 26 mph (42 km/h) on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-10">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Kate_(1985)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the month after Hurricane Kate struck the island, the government of Cuba issued a request to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations (UN)</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Food_Council" title="World Food Council">World Food Council</a> for international <a href="/wiki/Emergency_management#Response" title="Emergency management">assistance</a>. In response, various UN member nations collectively provided $60,000 for pesticides; $250,000 for herbicides, fungicides, and potato seeds; and $1.381 million in cooking oils and beans to fulfill the dietary needs of over 475,000 people for 60 days. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> also donated about $15 million worth of rice and wheat flour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rw_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-rw-27">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Kate delayed a <a href="/wiki/List_of_mayors_of_Key_West,_Florida" title="List of mayors of Key West, Florida">runoff mayoral election in Key West</a> by two weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[38]</a></sup> Shortly after the storm, the police departments of both <a href="/wiki/Leon_County,_Florida" title="Leon County, Florida">Leon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jackson_County,_Florida" title="Jackson County, Florida">Jackson Counties</a> ordered a nightly curfew. Two disaster relief centers were opened in Franklin County, one in <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">Apalachicola</a> and the other in <a href="/wiki/Eastpoint,_Florida" title="Eastpoint, Florida">Eastpoint</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup> On December 3, 1985, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> declared seven <a href="/wiki/List_of_counties_in_Florida" title="List of counties in Florida">Florida counties</a> as <a href="/wiki/Disaster_area" title="Disaster area">disaster areas</a>, making them eligible to receive federal aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the widespread power outages along the Florida Panhandle, electrical companies enlisted extra workers to repair downed lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curfew_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-curfew-44">[41]</a></sup> Officials had put a curfew in place for Tallahassee due to power outages created by the hurricane, and the curfew was lifted on November 24 after power was gradually restored and roads were cleared of debris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[42]</a></sup> Police officers in the city arrested 20 people for violating curfew or creating unrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curfew_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-curfew-44">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some sections of coastline already suffering from severe erosion lost additional swaths of beach to a 10-foot (3 m) storm surge and strong waves. Many fishermen before and after the storm encountered diminished fish catches after the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tally_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-tally-32">[29]</a></sup></p>
