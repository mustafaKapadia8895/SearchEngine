<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Diaspora</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>diaspora</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> διασπορά, "scattering, dispersion")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-1">[1]</a></sup> is a scattered population whose origin lies within a smaller geographic locale. Diaspora can also refer to the movement of the population from its original homeland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-webster-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ember_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ember-3">[3]</a></sup> Diaspora has come to refer particularly to historical mass dispersions of an involuntary nature, such as the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_diaspora" title="Jewish diaspora">expulsion of Jews from Judea</a> and the fleeing of <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> after the fall of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. Other examples are the African Trans-<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>, the southern <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindus" title="Hindus">Hindus</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Coolie" title="Coolie">coolie</a> trade, the <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> during and after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Irish Famine</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_diaspora" title="Palestinian diaspora">Palestinian diaspora</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ember_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ember-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_exodus_from_Arab_and_Muslim_countries" title="Jewish exodus from Arab and Muslim countries">Jewish exodus from Arab and Muslim countries</a> in the 20th century, the exile and deportation of <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassians</a>, and the emigration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> warriors and their families after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest_of_England" title="Norman Conquest of England">Norman Conquest of England</a>, many of whom found employment in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and bolstered the elite bodyguard of the emperor, the <a href="/wiki/Varangian_Guard" title="Varangian Guard">Varangian Guard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, scholars have distinguished between different kinds of diaspora, based on its causes such as <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>, trade or labor migrations, or by the kind of social coherence within the diaspora community and its ties to the ancestral lands. Some diaspora communities maintain strong political ties with their homeland. Other qualities that may be typical of many diasporas are thoughts of return, relationships with other communities in the diaspora, and lack of full integration into the host country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ember_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ember-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_development_of_the_term">Origins and development of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and development of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is derived from the Greek verb διασπείρω (<i>diaspeirō</i>), "I scatter", "I spread about"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-1">[1]</a></sup> and that from διά (<i>dia</i>), "between, through, across"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-1">[1]</a></sup> + the verb σπείρω (<i>speirō</i>), "I sow, I scatter".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> the term διασπορά (<i>diaspora</i>) hence meant "scattering"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-1">[1]</a></sup> and was inter alia used to refer to citizens of a dominant <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> who emigrated to a conquered land with the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a>, to assimilate the territory into the empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> An example of a diaspora from classical antiquity is the century-long exile of the <a href="/wiki/Messenia_(ancient_region)" title="Messenia (ancient region)">Messenians</a> under <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> rule and the Ageanites as described by Thucydides in his "history of the Peloponnesian wars."</p>
<p>Its use began to develop from this original sense when the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> was translated into Greek;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the first mention of a diaspora created as a result of exile is found in the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-1">[1]</a></sup> first in</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 28:25, in the phrase <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἔσῃ ἐν διασπορᾷ ἐν πάσαις ταῖς βασιλείαις τῆς γῆς</span>, <i>esē en diaspora en pasais tais basileiais tēs gēs</i>, translated to mean "thou shalt be a dispersion in all kingdoms of the earth"</li>
</ul>
<p>and secondly in</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> 146(147).2, in the phrase <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">οἰκοδομῶν Ἰερουσαλὴμ ὁ Kύριος καὶ τὰς διασπορὰς τοῦ Ἰσραὴλ ἐπισυνάξει</span>, <i>oikodomōn Ierousalēm ho <a href="/wiki/Kyrios" title="Kyrios">Kyrios</a> kai tas diasporas tou Israēl episynaxē</i>, translated to mean "The Lord doth build up Jerusalem: he gathereth together the outcasts of Israel".</li>
</ul>
<p>So after the Bible's translation into Greek, the word <i>Diaspora</i> would then have been used to refer to the Northern Kingdom exiled between 740–722 BC from Israel by the Assyrians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> as well as Jews, Benjaminites, and Levites exiled from the Southern Kingdom in 587 BCE by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_exile" title="Babylonian exile">Babylonians</a>, and from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Judea" title="Roman Judea">Roman Judea</a> in 70 CE by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It subsequently came to be used to refer to the historical movements of the dispersed ethnic population of Israel, to the cultural development of that population or to that population itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In English when capitalized and without modifiers (that is simply, <i>the Diaspora</i>), the term refers specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_diaspora" title="Jewish diaspora">Jewish diaspora</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-webster-2">[2]</a></sup> when uncapitalized the word <i>diaspora</i> may be used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugee</a> or <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigrant</a> populations of other origins or ethnicities living "away from an established or ancestral homeland".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-webster-2">[2]</a></sup> The wider application of <i>diaspora</i> evolved from the Assyrian two-way mass deportation policy of conquered populations to deny future territorial claims on their part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary_Online" title="Oxford English Dictionary Online">Oxford English Dictionary Online</a></i>, the first known recorded usage of the word <i>diaspora</i> in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> was in 1876 referring "extensive <i>diaspora</i> work (as it is termed) of evangelizing among the National Protestant Churches on the continent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The term became more widely assimilated into <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> by the mid 1950s, with long-term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expatriates" title="Expatriates">expatriates</a> in significant numbers from other particular countries or regions also being referred to as a diaspora.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An academic field, <a href="/wiki/Diaspora_studies" title="Diaspora studies">diaspora studies</a>, has become established relating to this sense of the word.</p>
<p>In all cases, the term <i>diaspora</i> carries a sense of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_migration" title="Forced migration">displacement</a> the population so described finds itself for whatever reason separated from its national territory, and usually its people have a hope, or at least a desire, to return to their homeland at some point, if the "homeland" still exists in any meaningful sense. Some writers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have noted that diaspora may result in a loss of nostalgia for a single home as people "re-root" in a series of meaningful displacements. In this sense, individuals may have multiple homes throughout their diaspora, with different reasons for maintaining some form of attachment to each. Diasporic cultural development often assumes a different course from that of the population in the original place of settlement. Over time, remotely separated communities tend to vary in culture, traditions, language and other factors. The last vestiges of cultural affiliation in a diaspora is often found in community resistance to <a href="/wiki/Language_change" title="Language change">language change</a> and in maintenance of traditional religious practice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expanding_definition">Expanding definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expanding definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Safran" title="William Safran">William Safran</a> in an article published in 1991<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>, set out six rules to distinguish diasporas from migrant communities. These included criteria that the group maintains a myth or collective memory of their homeland; they regard their ancestral homeland as their true home, to which they will eventually return; being committed to the restoration or maintenance of that homeland; and they relate "personally or vicariously" to the homeland to a point where it shapes their identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20055_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20055-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinar201075_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinar201075-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen20086_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen20086-16">[16]</a></sup> While Safran's definitions were influenced by the idea of the Jewish diaspora, he recognised the expanding use of the term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen20084_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen20084-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rogers_Brubaker" title="Rogers Brubaker">Rogers Brubaker</a> (2005) also notes that use of the term diaspora has been widening. He suggests that one element of this expansion in use "involves the application of the term diaspora to an ever-broadening set of cases: essentially to any and every nameable population category that is to some extent dispersed in space".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20053_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20053-18">[18]</a></sup> Brubaker has used the <a href="/wiki/WorldCat" title="WorldCat">WorldCat</a> database to show that 17 out of the 18 books on diaspora published between 1900 and 1910 were on the Jewish diaspora. The majority of works in the 1960s were also about the Jewish diaspora, but in 2002 only two out of 20 books sampled (out of a total of 253) were about the Jewish case, with a total of eight different diasporas covered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker200514_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker200514-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brubaker outlines the original use of the term diaspora as follows:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Most early discussions of diaspora were firmly rooted in a conceptual 'homeland'; they were concerned with a paradigmatic case, or a small number of core cases. The paradigmatic case was, of course, the Jewish diaspora; some dictionary definitions of diaspora, until recently, did not simply illustrate but defined the word with reference to that case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20052_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20052-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Brubaker argues that the initial expansion of the use of the phrase extended it to other, similar cases, such as the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_diaspora" title="Armenian diaspora">Armenian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_diaspora" title="Greek diaspora">Greek diasporas</a>. More recently, it has been applied to emigrant groups that continue their involvement in their homeland from overseas, such as the category of long-distance nationalists identified by <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Anderson" title="Benedict Anderson">Benedict Anderson</a>. Brubaker notes that (as examples): Albanians, Basques, Hindu Indians, Irish, Japanese, Kashmiri, Koreans, Kurds, Palestinians, and Tamils have been conceptualised as diasporas in this sense. Furthermore, "labour migrants who maintain (to some degree) emotional and social ties with a homeland" have also been described as diasporas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20052_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20052-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In further cases of the use of the term, "the reference to the conceptual homeland – to the 'classical' diasporas – has become more attenuated still, to the point of being lost altogether". Here, Brubaker cites "transethnic and transborder linguistic categories...such as <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">Francophone</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">Anglophone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lusophone" title="Lusophone">Lusophone</a> 'communities'", along with Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Confucian, Huguenot, Muslim and Catholic 'diasporas'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20052.E2.80.933_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker20052.E2.80.933-21">[21]</a></sup> Brubaker notes that, as of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were also academic books or articles on the <a href="/wiki/Dixie" title="Dixie">Dixie</a>, white, liberal, gay, queer and digital diasporas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrubaker200514_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrubaker200514-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some observers have labeled evacuation from <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a> in the wake of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> the <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_diaspora" title="New Orleans diaspora">New Orleans diaspora</a>, since a significant number of evacuees have not been able to return, yet maintain aspirations to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Agnieszka Weinar (2010) notes the widening use of the term, arguing that recently, "a growing body of literature succeeded in reformulating the definition, framing diaspora as almost any <i>population</i> on the move and no longer referring to the specific <i>context</i> of their existence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinar201075_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinar201075-15">[15]</a></sup> It has even been noted that as charismatic Christianity becomes increasingly globalized, many Christians conceive of themselves as a diaspora, and form an imaginary that mimics salient features of ethnic diasporas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Professional communities of individuals no longer in their homeland can also be considered diaspora. For example, science diasporas are communities of scientists who conduct their research away from their homeland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In an article published in 1996, Khachig Tölölyan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> argues that the media have used the term corporate diaspora in a rather arbitrary and inaccurate fashion, for example as applied to “mid-level, mid-career executives who have been forced to find new places at a time of corporate upheaval” (10) The use of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Corporate_diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Corporate diaspora (page does not exist)">corporate diaspora</a> reflects the increasing popularity of the diaspora notion to describe a wide range of phenomena related to contemporary migration, displacement and transnational mobility. While corporate diaspora seems to avoid or contradict connotations of violence, coercion and unnatural uprooting historically associated to the notion of diaspora, its scholarly use may heuristically describe the ways in which corporations function alongside diasporas. In this way, corporate diaspora might foreground the racial histories of diasporic formations without losing sight of the cultural logic of <a href="/wiki/Late_capitalism" title="Late capitalism">late capitalism</a> in which corporations orchestrate the transnational circulation of people, images, ideologies and capital.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="African_diaspora">African diaspora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: African diaspora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/African_diaspora" title="African diaspora">African diaspora</a></div>
<p>One of the largest diaspora of modern times is that of Sub-Saharan Africans, which dates back several centuries. During the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>, 9.4 to 12 million people from <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> survived transportation to arrive in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> as <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This population and their descendants were major influences on the culture of <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> colonies. Prior to the trans-Atlantic slave trade, millions of Africans had moved and settled as merchants, seamen and slaves in different parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. From the 8th through the 19th centuries, an Arab-controlled slave trade dispersed millions of Africans to Asia and the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Black Europe and the African Diaspora</i>, Alexander Weheliye writes a section and clearly explains diaspora this way: "Diaspora offers pathways that retrace laverings of difference in the aftermath of colonialism and slavery, as well as the effects of other forms of migration and displacement. Thus, diaspora enables the desedimentation of the nation from the ‘interior’ by taking into account the groups that fail to comply with the reigning definition of the people as a cohesive political subject due to sharing one culture, one race, one language, one religion, and so on, and from the 'exterior' by drawing attention to the movements that cannot be contained by the nation’s administrative and ideological borders".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Currently, migrant Africans can only enter 13 African countries without advanced visas. In pursuing a unified future, the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> (AU) will allow people to move freely between the 54 countries of the AU under a visa free passport and encourage migrants to return to Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_diasporas">Asian diasporas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Asian diasporas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The earliest known Asian diaspora of note is the Jewish diaspora, the majority of which can be attributed to the Roman conquest, expulsion, and enslavement of the Jewish population of <a href="/wiki/Judea" title="Judea">Judea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and whose descendants became the <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi_Jews" title="Mizrahi Jews">Mizrahim</a> of today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schama2014_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schama2014-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ostrer2012_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ostrer2012-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brenner2010_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brenner2010-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scheindlin1998_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scheindlin1998-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams1840_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams1840-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WhoAreTheJews_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-WhoAreTheJews-42">[42]</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> trace their origins to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, and their presence in Europe is first attested to in the Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenrick_intro_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-kenrick_intro-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chinese_emigration" title="Chinese emigration">Chinese emigration</a> (also known as the Chinese Diaspora; see also <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Overseas Chinese</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> first occurred thousands of years ago. The mass emigration that occurred from the 19th century to 1949 was caused mainly by wars and starvation in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>, as well as political corruption. Most immigrants were illiterate or poorly educated peasants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coolies" title="Coolies">coolies</a> (Chinese: 苦力, literally "hard labor"), who immigrated to developing countries in need of labor, such as the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malaya</a> and other places.</p>
<p>The largest Asian diaspora outside of Southeast Asia is the <a href="/wiki/Non-resident_Indian_and_person_of_Indian_origin" title="Non-resident Indian and person of Indian origin">Indian diaspora</a>. The overseas Indian community, estimated at over 25 million, is spread across many regions in the world, on every continent. It constitutes a diverse, heterogeneous and eclectic global community representing different regions, languages, cultures, and faiths (see <i><a href="/wiki/Desi" title="Desi">Desi</a></i>).</p>
<p>At least three waves of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_of_Nepal" title="People of Nepal">Nepalese diaspora</a> can be identified. The earliest wave dates back to hundreds of years as early marriage and high birthrates propelled Hindu settlement eastward across Nepal, then into <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>. A backlash developed in the 1980s as Bhutan's political elites realized that Bhutanese Buddhists were at risk of becoming a minority in their own country. At least 60,000 ethnic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_of_Nepal" title="People of Nepal">Nepalese</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> have been resettled in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> A second wave was driven by British recruitment of mercenary soldiers beginning around 1815 and resettlement after retirement in the British Isles and southeast Asia. The third wave began in the 1970s as land shortages intensified and the pool of educated labor greatly exceeded job openings in Nepal. Job-related emigration created Nepalese enclaves in India, the wealthier countries of the Middle East, Europe and North America. Current estimates of the number of Nepalese living outside Nepal range well up into the millions.</p>
<p>In Siam, regional power struggles among several kingdoms in the region led to a large diaspora of ethnic Lao between the 1700s–1800s by Siamese rulers to settle large areas of the Siamese kingdom's northeast region, where Lao ethnicity is still a major factor in 2012. During this period, Siam decimated the Lao capital, capturing, torturing and killing the Lao king Anuwongse.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_diasporas">European diasporas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: European diasporas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_diaspora" title="European diaspora">European diaspora</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>European history contains numerous diaspora-like events. In ancient times, the trading and colonising activities of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greeks" title="Ancient Greeks">Greek</a> tribes from the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a> spread people of Greek culture, religion and language around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> basins, establishing Greek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City-states" title="City-states">city-states</a> in <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">southern Italy</a>), northern <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_of_France" title="South of France">south of France</a>, and the Black Sea coasts. Greeks founded more than 400 colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Tyre and Carthage also colonised the Mediterranean.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>'s conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> marked the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, characterized by a new wave of Greek colonization in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, with Greek ruling-classes established in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">southwest Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">northwest India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Subsequent waves of colonization and migration during the Middle Ages added to the older settlements, or created new ones, thus replenishing the <a href="/wiki/Greek_diaspora" title="Greek diaspora">Greek diaspora</a> and making it one of the most long-standing and widespread in the world.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration-Period</a> relocations, which included several phases, are just one set of many in history. The first phase Migration-Period displacement (between CE 300 and 500) included relocation of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>), <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, various other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_people" title="Germanic people">Germanic peoples</a> (<a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Angles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varangian" title="Varangian">Varangians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>), <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a> and numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic tribes</a>. The second phase, between CE 500 and 900, saw <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkic_people" title="Turkic people">Turkic</a>, and other tribes on the move, resettling in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> and gradually leaving it predominantly Slavic, and affecting <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> as the first Turkic tribes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avars_(Carpathians)" title="Avars (Carpathians)">Avars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khazars" title="Khazars">Khazars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pechenegs" title="Pechenegs">Pechenegs</a>), as well as <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgars</a>, and possibly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magyars" title="Magyars">Magyars</a> arrived. The last phase of the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">migrations</a> saw the coming of the Hungarian Magyars and the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Viking</a> expansion out of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> into southern and eastern Europe, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.</p>
<p>Such colonizing migrations cannot be considered indefinitely as diasporas; over very long periods, eventually the migrants assimilate into the settled area so completely that it becomes their new mental homeland. Thus the modern Magyars of Hungary do not feel that they belong in the Western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> that the Hungarian Magyars left 12 centuries ago; and the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Angles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a> do not yearn to reoccupy the plains of Northwest Germany.</p>
<p>In 1492 a Spanish-financed expedition headed by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> arrived in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, after which European exploration and colonization rapidly expanded. Historian <a href="/wiki/James_Axtell" title="James Axtell">James Axtell</a> estimates that 240,000 people left Europe for the Americas in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Emigration continued. In the 19th century alone over 50 million Europeans migrated to North and South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Other Europeans moved to Siberia, Africa, and Australasia.</p>
<p>A specific 19th-century example is the <a href="/wiki/Irish_diaspora" title="Irish diaspora">Irish diaspora</a>, beginning in the mid-19th century and brought about by <i>An Gorta Mór</i> or "the Great Hunger" of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Irish Famine</a>. An estimated 45% to 85% of Ireland's population emigrated to areas including Britain, the United States of America, Canada, Argentina, Australia and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. The size of the Irish diaspora is demonstrated by the number of people around the world who claim Irish ancestry; some sources put the figure at 80 to 100 million.</p>
<p>From the 1860s the <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassian</a> people, originally from Eastern Europe, <a href="/wiki/Circassian_diaspora" title="Circassian diaspora">were dispersed</a> through Anatolia, Australia, the Balkans, the Levant, North America and West Europe, leaving less than 10% of their population in the homeland - parts of historical Circassia (in the modern-day <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> portion of the <a href="/wiki/North_Caucasus" title="North Caucasus">Caucasus</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_diasporas">Internal diasporas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Internal diasporas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, approximately 4.3 million people moved outside their home states in 2010, according to IRS tax exemption data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In a 2011 TEDx presentation, Detroit native Garlin Gilchrist referenced the formation of distinct "Detroit diaspora" communities in Seattle and Washington, D.C.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> while layoffs in the auto industry also led to substantial blue-collar migration from Michigan to Wyoming in the mid 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In response to a statewide exodus of talent, the State of Michigan continues to host "MichAGAIN" career recruiting events in places throughout the United States with significant Michigan diaspora populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Mainland China, millions of migrant workers have sought greater opportunity in the country's booming coastal metropolises, though this trend has slowed with the further development of China's interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Migrant social structures in these Chinese megacities are often based on place of origin, such as a shared hometown or province, and it is common for recruiters and foremen to select entire work crews from the same village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In two separate June 2011 incidents, Sichuanese migrant workers organized violent protests against alleged police misconduct and migrant labor abuse near the southern manufacturing hub of Guangzhou.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">Twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The twentieth century saw huge population movements. Some involved large-scale transfers of people by government action. Some migrations occurred to avoid conflict and warfare. Other diasporas were created as a consequence of political decisions, such as the end of <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_and_the_end_of_colonial_rule">World War II and the end of colonial rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: World War II and the end of colonial rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> unfolded, Nazi Germany deported and killed millions of Jews and many millions of others were likewise enslaved or murdered, including Ukrainians, Russians and other Slavs. Some Jews fled from persecution to unoccupied parts of western Europe and the Americas before borders closed. Later, other eastern European refugees moved west, away from Soviet annexation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a> regimes after World War II. Hundreds of thousands of these anti-Soviet political refugees and Displaced Persons ended up in western Europe, Australia, Canada and the United States of America.</p>
<p>After World War II, the Soviet Union and Communist-controlled <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> expelled millions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Germans" title="Ethnic Germans">ethnic Germans</a>, most of whom were descendants of immigrants who had settled in those areas nearly two centuries before. This was allegedly in retaliation for the German Nazi invasion and their pan-German attempts at annexation. Most of the refugees moved to the West, including western Europe, and with tens of thousands seeking refuge in the United States.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> sent many political activists into exile during <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a>'s military regime from 1936 to his death in 1975.</p>
<p>Following World War II, the creation of the state of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, and a series of uprisings against colonialist rule, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> nations became more hostile in relation to their historic Jewish populations, sepharadim and mizrahiml, of nearly 1 million people. Most of them emigrated, with the majority resettling in Israel.</p>
<p>At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_diaspora" title="Palestinian diaspora">Palestinian diaspora</a> resulted from Israel's creation in 1948, in which 750,000 people were expelled or fled from their homes. The diaspora was enlarged by the effects of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1967_Arab-Israeli_War" title="1967 Arab-Israeli War">1967 Arab–Israeli War</a>. Many Palestinians continue to live in refugee camps maintained by Middle Eastern nations, but others have resettled in the Middle East and other countries.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">1947 Partition</a> resulted in the migration of millions of people between <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. Millions were murdered in the religious violence of the period, with estimates of fatalities up to 2 million people. Thousands of former subjects of the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a> went to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> after India and Pakistan became independent in 1947.</p>
<p>From the late 19th century, and formally from 1910, Japan made Korea a colony. Millions of Chinese fled to western provinces not occupied by Japan (that is, in particular Ssuchuan/Szechwan and Yunnan in the Southwest and Shensi and Kansu in the Northwest) and to Southeast Asia. More than 100,000 Koreans moved across the Amur River into Eastern Russia (then the Soviet Union) away from the Japanese.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cold_War_and_the_formation_of_post-colonial_states">The Cold War and the formation of post-colonial states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Cold War and the formation of post-colonial states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During and after the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>-era, huge populations of refugees migrated from conflict, especially from then-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>.</p>
<p>Upheaval in the Middle East and Central Asia, some of which was related to power struggles between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, created new refugee populations which developed into global diasporas.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, many <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Vietnamese people</a> emigrated to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and later millions to the United States, Australia and Canada after the Cold War-related <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. Later, 30,000 <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <i>colons</i> from <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> were displaced after being expelled by the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Rouge" title="Khmer Rouge">Khmer Rouge</a> regime under <a href="/wiki/Pol_Pot" title="Pol Pot">Pol Pot</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A small, predominantly Muslim ethnic group, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cham_(Asia)" title="Cham (Asia)">Cham people</a> long residing in Cambodia, were nearly eradicated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The mass exodus of Vietnamese people from Vietnam coined the term '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boat_people" title="Boat people">Boat people</a>'.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Southwest_China" title="Southwest China">Southwest China</a>, many <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">Tibetan people</a> emigrated to India, following the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">14th Dalai Lama</a> in 1959 after the failure of his <a href="/wiki/1959_Tibetan_uprising" title="1959 Tibetan uprising">Tibetan uprising</a>. This wave lasted until the 1960s, and another wave followed when Tibet was opened up to trade and tourism in the 1980s. It is estimated that about 200,000 Tibetans live now dispersed worldwide, half of whom in are <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>. In lieu of lost citizenship papers, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Tibetan_Administration" title="Central Tibetan Administration">Central Tibetan Administration</a> offers <a href="/wiki/Green_Book_(Tibetan_document)" title="Green Book (Tibetan document)">Green Book</a> identity documents to Tibetan refugees.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sri_Lankan_Tamils" title="Sri Lankan Tamils">Sri Lankan Tamils</a> have historically migrated to find work, notably during the British colonial period. Since the beginning of the civil war in 1983, more than 800,000 Tamils have been displaced within <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> as local diaspora, and over a half million <a href="/wiki/Tamils" title="Tamils">Tamils</a> living as the <a href="/wiki/Tamil_diaspora" title="Tamil diaspora">Tamil diaspora</a> in destinations such as India, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, the UK and Europe.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghan</a> diaspora resulted from the 1979 invasion by the former Soviet Union; both official and unofficial records<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> indicate that the war displaced over 6 million people, resulting in the creation of the largest refugee population worldwide today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Nov 2009 (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_people" title="Iranian people">Iranians</a> fled the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">Iranian Revolution</a> which culminated in the fall of the <a href="/wiki/1953_Iranian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1953 Iranian coup d'état">USA/British-ensconced</a> <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Pahlavi" title="Mohammad Reza Pahlavi">Shah</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrian</a> diaspora expanded by the Civil War in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, the coming into power of the Islamic republic of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba%27ath" title="Ba'ath">Ba'athist</a> dictatorship in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, and the present-day unrest in Iraq pushed Assyrians on the roads of exile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, a new series of diasporas formed following the end of colonial rule. In some cases as countries became independent, numerous minority descendants of Europeans emigrated; others stayed in the lands which had been family homes for generations. <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> expelled <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Asians_from_Uganda" title="Expulsion of Asians from Uganda">80,000 South Asians in 1972</a> and took over their businesses and properties. The 1990s Civil war in <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> between rival ethnic groups <a href="/wiki/Hutu" title="Hutu">Hutu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tutsi" title="Tutsi">Tutsi</a> turned deadly and produced a mass efflux of refugees.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, following the 1959 <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">Cuban Revolution</a> and the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>, over a million people have left <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was a <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_diaspora" title="Jamaican diaspora">Jamaican diaspora</a> around the start of the 21st century. More than 1 million <a href="/wiki/People_of_the_Dominican_Republic" title="People of the Dominican Republic">Dominicans</a> live abroad a majority living in the US.<cite class="citation journal"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20121208105951/http://www.dominicantoday.com/dr/economy/2011/6/14/39878/Nearly-20-of-all-Dominicans-live-abroad" rel="nofollow">"Nearly 20 Percent of All Dominicans Live Abroad"</a>. Dominican Today. Archived from <a class="external text" href="http://www.dominicantoday.com/dr/economy/2011/6/14/39878/Nearly-20-of-all-Dominicans-live-abroad" rel="nofollow">the original</a> on 8 December 2012</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft.atitle=Nearly+20+Percent+of+All+Dominicans+Live+Abroad&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.dominicantoday.com%2Fdr%2Feconomy%2F2011%2F6%2F14%2F39878%2FNearly-20-of-all-Dominicans-live-abroad&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ADiaspora"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></p>
<p>A million <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombian</a> refugees have left Colombia since 1965 to escape the country's violence and civil wars. In <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, thousands of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentinan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguayan</a> refugees fled to Europe during periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_junta" title="Military junta">military rule</a> in the 1970s and 1980s. In <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_Diaspora" title="Nicaraguan Diaspora">Nicaraguans</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">Salvadorans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemalans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Hondurans</a> fled conflict and poor economic conditions.</p>
<p>Hundreds of thousands of people fled from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rwandan_Genocide" title="Rwandan Genocide">Rwandan Genocide</a> in 1994 into neighboring countries. Thousands of refugees from deteriorating conditions in <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> have gone to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. The long war in <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congo</a>, in which numerous nations have been involved, has also created millions of refugees.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East_conflicts">Middle East conflicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Middle East conflicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, nearly 3 million Iraqis had been displaced as of 2011, with 1.3 million within Iraq and 1.6 million in neighboring countries, mainly Jordan and Syria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Civil_War" title="Syrian Civil War">Syrian Civil War</a> has forced further migration, with at least 4 million displaced as per UN estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Venezuela's_Bolivarian_diaspora"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Venezuela.27s_Bolivarian_diaspora">Venezuela's Bolivarian diaspora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Venezuela's Bolivarian diaspora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_diaspora" title="Bolivarian diaspora">Bolivarian diaspora</a></div>
<p>Following the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a> and the establishment of his <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Revolution" title="Bolivarian Revolution">Bolivarian Revolution</a>, over 1.6 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venezuelan_people" title="Venezuelan people">Venezuelans</a> emigrated from Venezuela in what has been called the <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_diaspora" title="Bolivarian diaspora">Bolivarian diaspora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUdiaspora_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUdiaspora-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEWSWEEKdiaspora_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEWSWEEKdiaspora-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ELIMPdec2014_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-ELIMPdec2014-65">[65]</a></sup> The analysis of a study by the <a href="/wiki/Central_University_of_Venezuela" title="Central University of Venezuela">Central University of Venezuela</a> titled <i>Venezuelan Community Abroad. A New Method of Exile</i> by <i><a href="/wiki/El_Universal_(Caracas)" title="El Universal (Caracas)">El Universal</a></i> states that the Bolivarian diaspora in Venezuela has been caused by the "deterioration of both the economy and the social fabric, rampant crime, uncertainty and lack of hope for a change in leadership in the near future".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUdiaspora_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-EUdiaspora-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diaspora_populations_on_the_Internet">Diaspora populations on the Internet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diaspora populations on the Internet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous web-based news portals and forum sites dedicated to specific diaspora communities, often organized on the basis of an origin characteristic and a current location characteristic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The location-based networking features of mobile applications such as China's <a href="/wiki/WeChat" title="WeChat">WeChat</a> have also created de facto online diaspora communities when used outside of their home markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Now, large companies from the emerging countries are looking at leveraging diaspora communities to enter the more mature market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
