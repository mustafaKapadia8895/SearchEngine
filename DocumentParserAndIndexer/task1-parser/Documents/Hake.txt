<p>The term <b>hake</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span> refers to fish in either of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Family <a href="/wiki/Merlucciidae" title="Merlucciidae">Merlucciidae</a> of northern and southern oceans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithsFishes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SmithsFishes-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Family <a href="/wiki/Phycidae" title="Phycidae">Phycidae</a> (sometimes subfamily Phycinae in family Gadidae) of the northern oceans</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hake">Hake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hake is in the same taxonomic order (<a href="/wiki/Gadiformes" title="Gadiformes">Gadiformes</a>) as <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haddock" title="Haddock">haddock</a>. It is a medium to large fish averaging from 1 to 8 pounds (0.45 to 3.63 kg) in weight, with specimens as large as 60 pounds (27 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theworldwidegourmet1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-theworldwidegourmet1-2">[2]</a></sup> The fish can grow up to 1 metre (3 ft 3 in) in length with a <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">lifespan</a> as long as 14 years. Hake may be found in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> in waters from 200–350 metres (660–1,150 ft) deep. The fish stay in deep sea water during the day and come to shallower depths during the night. An undiscerning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predator</a>, hake feed on their prey found near or on the bottom of the sea. Male and female hake are very similar in appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spawning" title="Spawning">spawning</a>, the hake eggs float on the surface of the sea where the larvae develop. After a certain period of time, the baby hake then migrate to the bottom of the sea, preferring depths of less than 200 metres (660 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A total of 12 hake species are known in the family <a href="/wiki/Merlucciidae" title="Merlucciidae">Merlucciidae</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Argentine_hake" title="Argentine hake">Argentine hake</a> (<i>Merluccius hubbsi</i>), found in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a></li>
<li>Benguela hake (<i>Merluccius poli</i>), found in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a></li>
<li>Deep-water hake (<i><a href="/wiki/Merluccius_paradoxus" title="Merluccius paradoxus">Merluccius paradoxus</a></i>) found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">Southern Atlantic</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_hake" title="European hake">European hake</a> (<i>Merluccius merluccius</i>), found in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Europe" title="Atlantic Europe">Atlantic coast of Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Gayi hake (<i>Merluccius gayi</i>), found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_hake" title="North Pacific hake">North Pacific hake</a> (<i>Merluccius productus</i>), found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Offshore_hake" title="Offshore hake">Offshore hake</a> (<i>Merluccius albidus</i>), found in United States</li>
<li>Panama hake (<i>Merluccius angustimanus</i>), found in <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a></li>
<li>Senegalese hake (<i>Merluccius senegalensis</i>), found in Atlantic coast of western <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a></li>
<li>Shallow-water hake (<i><a href="/wiki/Merluccius_capensis" title="Merluccius capensis">Merluccius capensis</a></i>), found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">Southern Atlantic</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silver_hake" title="Silver hake">Silver hake</a> (<i>Merluccius bilinearis</i>), found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Atlantic" title="Northwest Atlantic">Northwest Atlantic</a></li>
<li>Southern hake (<i>Merluccius australis</i>), found in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Not all hake species are viewed as commercially important, but the deep-water and shallow-water hakes are known to grow rapidly and make up the majority of harvested species.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The highest demand for hake has been in Europe. Hake has been primarily divided into three principal levels—fresh, frozen, and frozen fillet. Fresh hake is mainly supplied by European production and imports. Frozen hake and frozen hake fillet are effectively supplied by imports and European processing companies.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> has the highest consumption of hake in Europe, with a yearly consumption of 6 kg/person. This works out to around half all hake eaten in Europe. Though Spanish consumption of hake and other fish declined in the last decade (second fish consumption in the world after Japan), hake still accounts for about one third of total fish consumption there. Other countries that eat a lot of hake include France, Italy, and Portugal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Spain, fresh hake are mostly purchased by restaurants through retailers. Nonetheless, processed hake products are distributed by hake wholesalers. Fishmongers, public markets and hypermarkets sell hake in various forms: frozen fillet, fillet skin-on, fillet skin-off, etc.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, fish are generally purchased in supermarkets. Due to insufficient European hake, French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesalers" title="Wholesalers">wholesalers</a> purchase fresh hake from external countries such as <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, and then export them to Spain. Fresh hake is mostly exported to Spain.</p>
<p>In Italy, hotels, restaurants, supermarkets, and institutional purchasers purchase much seafood. However, retailers and wholesalers purchase most frozen hake fillets to sell in markets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercially_salable_forms">Commercially salable forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercially salable forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hake is sold as frozen, <a href="/wiki/Fish_fillet" title="Fish fillet">fillets</a> or steaks, fresh, <a href="/wiki/Smoked_fish" title="Smoked fish">smoked</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Salted_fish" title="Salted fish">salted</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buying_criteria">Buying criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Buying criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When buying hake fillet or other hake product, consumers should look for hake with white <a href="/wiki/Flesh" title="Flesh">flesh</a> that is free of signs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Browning_(food_process)" title="Browning (food process)">browning</a>, dryness, or grayness, and a seawater fresh smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theworldwidegourmet1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-theworldwidegourmet1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main catching method of deep-water hake is primarily trawling, and shallow-water hake is mostly caught by inshore trawl and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longlining" title="Longlining">longlining</a>. Hake are mostly found in the Southwest Atlantic (<a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>), Southeast Pacific (<a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>), Southeast Atlantic (<a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>), Southwest Pacific (<a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>), and Mediterranean and Black Sea (<a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>).</p>
<dl>
<dt>Over-exploitation</dt>
</dl>
<p>Due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over-fishing" title="Over-fishing">over-fishing</a>, Argentine hake catches have declined drastically. About 80% of adult hake has apparently disappeared from Argentine waters. Argentine hake is not expected to disappear, but the stock may be so low that it is no longer economic for commercial fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In addition, this adversely affects employment, because many people lose their jobs in the fishing industries. On the other hand, Argentine hake prices are rising due to scarcity. This has reduced exports, which ultimately affects the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globefish1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-globefish1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Chile, seafood exports, especially Chilean hake, have decreased dramatically. Hake export has decreased by almost 19 percent. The main cause of this decline is the February <a href="/wiki/2010_Chile_earthquake" title="2010 Chile earthquake">2010 Chile earthquake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a>. These disasters destroyed most processing plants, especially manufacturing companies that produce fishmeal and frozen fillets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>European hake catches are well below historical levels because of hake <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">depletion</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. However, different factors might have caused this decline, including a too-high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Allowable_Catch" title="Total Allowable Catch">Total Annual Catch</a>, unsustainable fishing, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> problems, juvenile catches, or non-registered catches.</p>
<p>According to the Worldwide Fund, the only Hake species not currently over-fished is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_hake" title="Cape hake">Cape hake</a>, found in Namibia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Namibia is the only country that has increased its hake quota, from 130,000 tonnes in 2009 to 145,000 tonnes in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Furthermore, the Local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Fisheries" title="Ministry of Fisheries">Ministry of Fisheries</a> adheres to very strict rules regarding the catch of hake. For example, the closed seasons for hake lasts approximately two months, September and October, depending on the level of stock. This rule has been applied to ensure the regrowth of the hake population. Supplemental restrictions include forbidding <a href="/wiki/Trawling" title="Trawling">trawling</a> for Hake in less than 200 m depth (to avoid damaging non-target species <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_habitat" title="Natural habitat">habitat</a>)and minimizing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/By-catch" title="By-catch">by-catch</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_introduction_to_non-native_areas">Human introduction to non-native areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human introduction to non-native areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Forrester%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frank Forrester's (page does not exist)">Frank Forrester's</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fishermens%27_Guide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fishermens' Guide (page does not exist)">Fishermens' Guide</a> in 1885 mentions a hake that was transplanted from the coast of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>, United States. It is uncertain which species this was, but the reference is given below:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>This is an Irish salt water fish, similar in appearance to the tom cod. In Galway bay, and other sea inlets of Ireland, the hake is exceedingly abundant, and is taken in great numbers. It is also found in England and France. Since the Irish immigration to America, the hake has followed in the wake of their masters, as it is now found in New York bay, in the waters around Boston, and off Cape Cod. Here it is called the stock fish, and the Bostonians call them poor Johns. It is a singular fact that until within a few years this fish was never seen in America. It does not grow as large here as in Europe, though here they are from ten to eighteen inches [250 to 460 mm] in length. The general color of this fish is a reddish brown, with some golden tints—the sides being of a pink silvery luster.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
