<p>The <b>Agulhas Bank</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>, from Portuguese for <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agulhas" title="Cape Agulhas">Cape Agulhas</a>, <i>Cabo das Agulhas</i>, "Cape of Needles")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a broad, shallow part of the southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> which extends up to 250 km (160 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agulhas" title="Cape Agulhas">Cape Agulhas</a> before falling steeply to the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a>.</p>
<p>It is the ocean region where the warm <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and the cold <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> meet. This convergence leads to treacherous <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> conditions, accounting for numerous wrecked ships in the area over the years. However the meeting of the oceans here also fuels the nutrient cycle for marine life, making it one of the best <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_bank" title="Fishing bank">fishing grounds</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent_and_characteristics">Extent and characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent and characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2549" data-file-width="3000" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png/220px-Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png/330px-Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png/440px-Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bioregions_of_SA_EEZ.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The South African marine bioregions from the 2004 classification. The 2011 ecoregions are slightly different, but the 2004 Agulhas Bioregion (in dark green) is virtually the same as the 2011 Agulhas Ecoregion.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Agulhas Bank stretches approximately 800 km (500 mi) along the African coast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeaAtlas_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SeaAtlas-2">[2]</a></sup> from off <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a> (18°E) to <a href="/wiki/Port_Alfred" title="Port Alfred">Port Alfred</a> (26°E),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanke-etal-2009-intro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanke-etal-2009-intro-3">[3]</a></sup> and up to 250 km (160 mi) from it. The bank is 50 m (160 ft) deep near the coast and reaches 200 m (660 ft) before dropping steeply to 1,000 m (3,300 ft) on its southern edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeaAtlas_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SeaAtlas-2">[2]</a></sup> The shelf spans an area of 116,000 km<sup>2</sup> (45,000 sq mi) with a mean depth slightly over 100 m (330 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whittle-2012-intro_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whittle-2012-intro-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The National Spatial Biodiversity Assessment 2004 recognised 34 biozones nested within 9 bioregions (of which four were offshore). The National Biodiversity Assessment 2011 replaced these ecozones and biozones with the terms ecoregions and ecozones. In 2011, the Agulhas Ecoregion was divided into four distinct ecozones: Agulhas inshore, Agulhas inner shelf, Agulhas outer shelf, and Agulhas shelf edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> 33 different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> habitats types were identified on the Agulhas Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are dozens of warm temperate <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a> along the coast of the Agulhas Ecoregion spanning from 5–30 m (16–98 ft) below sea level. Many rocky sub-tidal reefs are of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeolianite" title="Aeolianite">aeolianite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> origin, but <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstone</a> reefs are also present. The Agulhas reefs are very heterogeneous and include several possible different sub-types. Some of the reefs are within protected areas, but only a few of those protected areas include protection from fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBA-2011-reefs_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBA-2011-reefs-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanography">Oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Agulhas Bank is a natural boundary between <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>, resulting in one of the most turbulent waters of the world oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruijter-etal-2003-p45_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruijter-etal-2003-p45-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agulhas_Current">Agulhas Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Agulhas Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="792" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png/220px-Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png/330px-Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png/440px-Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Eddies of the Agulhas Current meanders past the Agulhas Bank leaking warm and salty water into the South Atlantic before retroflecting back into the Indian Ocean</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Current" title="Agulhas Current">Agulhas Current</a></div>
<p>The Agulhas Current flows south along the African east-coast and along the south-eastern edge of the bank. It then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroflection" title="Retroflection">retroflects</a> back into the Indian Ocean south-west of the bank. This retroflection results in intense <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddy</a> activities such as meanders, eddies, and filaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanke-etal-2009-intro_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanke-etal-2009-intro-3">[3]</a></sup> In upper layer water, the Agulhas rings and eddies move warm and salty water into the large South Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Ocean_gyre" title="Ocean gyre">gyre</a>, which exports it to the tropics. In the lower ocean layers water is transported in the opposite direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruijter-etal-2003-p45_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ruijter-etal-2003-p45-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Upwellings">Upwellings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Upwellings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Cyclonic eddies is another source of edge upwelling west of Port Elisabeth. Plumes of warm surface water migrate onto the bank along its eastern edge, providing subtropical surface water from the Indian Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whittle-2012-intro_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whittle-2012-intro-4">[4]</a></sup> In summer, easterly winds can intermittently drive coastal <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> along the South African south coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whittle-2012-intro_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Whittle-2012-intro-4">[4]</a></sup> The Agulhas Bank is dominated by westerly winds and most of the upwelling on the bank is related to the interaction of the Agulhas Current on the eastern edge, but easterly winds do occur, especially in summer and fall, and can generate local upwelling cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanke-etal-2009-intro_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blanke-etal-2009-intro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the current is diverged away from the coast, dynamic processes draws an onshore <a href="/wiki/Ekman_layer" title="Ekman layer">Ekman layer</a> of cold water from below the warm shelf-edge flow. In spring and summer, at a depth of 100 m (330 ft), a semi-permanent ridge of cold water is present on the eastern and central shelf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whittle-2012-intro_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Whittle-2012-intro-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In summer, there is mixture of subtropical water separated by <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermoclines</a> from cool waters, but there is a considerable seasonal variation. On the shelf, bottom waters exhibit characteristics of the central Indian Ocean in the east and central Atlantic Ocean waters in the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whittle-2012-intro_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Whittle-2012-intro-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agulhas_meanders_and_Natal_pulses">Agulhas meanders and Natal pulses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agulhas meanders and Natal pulses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As the Agulhas Current flows south along the African east coast, it tends to bulge inshore frequently, a deviation from the current's normal path known as Agulhas Current meanders (ACM). These bulges are occasionally (1-7 times per year) followed by a much larger offshore bulge, known as Natal pulses (NP). Natal pulses move along the coast at 20 km (12 mi) per day. An ACM can bulge up to 20 km (12 mi) and a NP up to 120 km (75 mi) from the current's mean position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The AC passes 34 km (21 mi) offshore and an ACM can reach 123 km (76 mi) offshore. When the AC meanders, its width broadens from 88 km (55 mi) to 125 km (78 mi) and its velocity weakens from 208 cm/s (82 in/s) to 136 cm/s (54 in/s). An ACM induces a strong inshore counter-current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large-scale cyclonic meanders known as Natal pulses are formed as the Agulhas Current reaches the continental shelf on the South African east-coast (i.e. the eastern Agulhas Bank off <a href="/wiki/Natal_Province" title="Natal Province">Natal</a>). As these pulses moves along the coast on the Agulhas Bank, they tend to pinch off Agulhas rings from the Agulhas Current. Such a ring shedding can be triggered by a Natal pulse alone, but sometimes meanders on the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Return_Current" title="Agulhas Return Current">Agulhas Return Current</a> merge to contribute to the shedding of an Agulhas ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agulhas_leakage_and_rings">Agulhas leakage and rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Agulhas leakage and rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Agulhas rings are large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticyclonic" title="Anticyclonic">anticyclonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Warm_core_ring" title="Warm core ring">warm core rings</a> of ocean water that are pinched off the Agulhas Current along the eastern edge of the Agulhas Bank from where they move into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">South Atlantic</a>. As the Agulhas Current reaches the east coast of South Africa, large solitary meanders known as Natal pulses form at irregular intervals. 165 days after the appearance of a Natal pulse, an Agulhas ring is formed off Durban. The Agulhas rings are among the largest eddies in the world and play an important role in the Agulhas Leakage, the transport of warm water from the Indian Ocean to the Atlantic Ocean, which affects the global climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The average diameter of the Agulhas rings is 320 km (200 mi), but they can reach 500 km. They extend down to the ocean floor; circulate at 0.3–1.5 m/s (0.98–4.92 ft/s); and move into the South Atlantic at 4–8 km (2.5–5.0 mi)/day. Only half of the Agulhas eddies that leave the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cape Basin (page does not exist)">Cape Basin</a> manage to cross the <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Ridge" title="Walvis Ridge">Walvis Ridge</a> and those that do tend to lose half their energy before reaching the ridge within six months. The Agulhas rings transport an estimated 1-5 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sv</a> (millions m²/s) of water from the Indian Ocean to the South Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Agulhas rings are thought to be of global climatic importance. Their delivery of warm water from the Indian to the Atlantic Ocean can control the rate of <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline overturning</a> of the entire Atlantic. Other factors contribute to various degrees to the inter-ocean exchanges in the region, including filaments from the Agulhas Current and intrusions of water from Antarctica. Cold, cyclonic eddies have been observed in the southwestern Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Based on model simulations, researchers have found that the interaction of the Agulhas Current and the eastern edge of the bank can result in the Agulhas rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The provenance of ocean sediments can be determined by analysing <a href="/wiki/Terrigenous_sediment" title="Terrigenous sediment">terrigenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> isotope ratios in deep ocean cores. Sediments underlying the Agulhas Current and Return Current have significantly higher ratios than surrounding sediments. Analyses of cores in the South Atlantic deposited during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> (LGM, 20 000 years ago), show that the Agulhas leakage (shedding of Agulhas rings) was significantly reduced. It has been hypothesised that the reason for this was that the Agulhas Current was stronger which resulted in a more eastward retroflection and therefore less leakage. However, analyses of such cores south of Africa show that the trajectory of the current was the same during the LGM and that the reduced leakage must be explained by a weaker current. Consequently, it can be predicted that a stronger Agulhas Current will result in its retroflection occurring more eastward and an increased Agulhas leakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benguela_Current">Benguela Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Benguela Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a></div>
<p>Compared to the Agulhas Current, the Benguela Current on the west and south-west coast of Africa is more intense and steadier. Its dynamic southern upwelling system is driven by the prevailing northward winds that produce an intense off-shore <a href="/wiki/Ekman_transport" title="Ekman transport">Ekman transport</a>. Most of this upwelling is concentrated to a few upwelling cells in the southern region: <a href="/wiki/Namaqua_National_Park" title="Namaqua National Park">Namaqua</a> (30°S), <a href="/wiki/Cape_Columbine" title="Cape Columbine">Cape Columbine</a> (32.5°S), and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a> (34°S). The wind is most intense from October to February, and the contrast in sea surface temperature between the open sea and the shelf is most prominent during summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanke-etal-2009-intro_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blanke-etal-2009-intro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coastal upwelling s also common on the western bank, but the more stable atmospheric condition results in larger cold water plumes that sometimes merge to form a continuous upwelling regime along the South African south-west coast. This upwelling zone is the southernmost extension of the Benguela Current Large Maritime Ecosystem. The Agulhas Current regularly flows around the southern tip of the bank and brings warm water to the western bank along the bank's western edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whittle-2012-intro_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Whittle-2012-intro-4">[4]</a></sup> Regularly, the <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> eddies from the east interact with the Benguela upwelling system on the African west coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanke-etal-2009-intro_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Blanke-etal-2009-intro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_water_eddies">Deep water eddies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deep water eddies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Floating south along the South American continental slope, the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water#Spreading_pathways" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">Deep Western Boundary Current</a> (DWBC) carries <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a> (NADW) into the South Atlantic. At about 8°S and at a depth of 2,200–3,500 m (7,200–11,500 ft), the DWBC breaks into anticyclonic eddies during periods of strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridional_overturning_circulation" title="Meridional overturning circulation">meridional overturning circulation</a>. One such NADW eddy was observed in 2003 and the researchers speculated that a deeply penetrating Agulhas ring pinched it off the NADW slope current. Spinning at 20 cm/s (7.9 in/s), these deep-water eddies move around the southern tip of the Agulhas Bank and into the Indian Ocean. Most of the NADW flow (more than 7 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sv</a>) meanders east around the Agulhas Plateau together with the surface Agulhas Return Current, but a smaller portion (3 Sv) continue north along the African east-coast as the Agulhas Undercurrent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Of 89.5 Sv released from the North Atlantic, 3.6 Sv leaves the South Atlantic south of the Agulhas Bank. However, 0.9 Sv recirculate in the basin north of the Walvis Ridge for centuries, of which 50-90% end up flowing south of the Agulhas Bank within 300 years, increasing the net inter-oceanic exchange with 4.1-4.5 Sv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

As Gondwana formed 500 mya, a rift appeared which eventually developed into the Agulhas Sea. This sea filled with sediments that were to become the Cape Supergroup, which subsequently were folded into the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fold_Belt" title="Cape Fold Belt">Cape Fold Belt</a>.</div>

<p>The oldest rock found along the coastline of the Agulhas Bank are <a href="/wiki/Geosyncline" title="Geosyncline">eugeosynclinal</a> sediments of the up to 3 km (1.9 mi) thick Kaaimans group deposited during continental rifting some 900 million years ago (Mya). The proto-South Atlantic closed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saldanian_orogeny" title="Saldanian orogeny">Saldanian orogeny</a> to form part of the supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> (700-600 Mya). The Cape granites were emplaced and the Kaaimans Group rocks were folded and thermally metamorphosed during this period. The formation of the main basin in the Cape Province commenced 570 Mya and lasted for 200 My. The <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a> Group is 4 km (2.5 mi) thick and an erosional unconformity marking its base is composed of both terrestrial and marine sediments. <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">Synclines</a> along the coast of the southern Cape contains sediments from the Bokkveld Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Durrheim-1987_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Durrheim-1987-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fold_Belt" title="Cape Fold Belt">Cape Fold Belt</a> (CFB) rocks and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karoo_Basin" title="Karoo Basin">Karoo Basin</a> were deposited 450 Mya; the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fold_Belt#Cape_Supergroup" title="Cape Fold Belt">Cape Supergroup</a> 450-300 Mya during a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgression_(geology)" title="Transgression (geology)">transgression</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regression_(geology)" title="Regression (geology)">regression</a> cycles. <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_orogeny" title="Pan-African orogeny">Pan-African</a> <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrusts</a> were reactivated 270-215 Mya to form the CFB which was then part of a continuous fold belt that developed during the <a href="/wiki/Gondwanide_orogeny" title="Gondwanide orogeny">Gondwanide orogeny</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_la_Ventana_(mountains)" title="Sierra de la Ventana (mountains)">Sierra de la Ventana</a> (Argentina), <a href="/wiki/Pensacola_Mountains" title="Pensacola Mountains">Pensacola Mountains</a> (East Antarctica), and <a href="/wiki/Ellsworth_Mountains" title="Ellsworth Mountains">Ellsworth Mountains</a> (West Antarctica). In the late <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup" title="Karoo Supergroup">Karoo Supergroup</a> was deposited in the Karoo Basin north of where the CFB is located today, and covering nearly two-thirds of present-day <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parsiegla-etal-2009-p2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parsiegla-etal-2009-p2-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Marine_geology_of_the_Cape_Peninsula_and_False_Bay" title="Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay">Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gondwana_breakup">Gondwana breakup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gondwana breakup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Basaltic lavas were extruded 183 Mya to form the <a href="/wiki/Karoo-Ferrar" title="Karoo-Ferrar">Karoo large igneous province</a>; a volcanism caused by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bouvet_hotspot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bouvet hotspot (page does not exist)">Bouvet hotspot</a> which is linked to the Gondwana break-up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parsiegla-etal-2009-p2_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Parsiegla-etal-2009-p2-20">[20]</a></sup> The Bouvet hotspot was located in or near present-day South Africa from the late Triassic 220 mya and until the Africa-Antarctica breakup 120 mya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Bouvet hotspot track stretches south-east from the African continent, near the South Africa-Mozambique border, and east of the AFFZ down to <a href="/wiki/Bouvet_Island" title="Bouvet Island">Bouvet Island</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bouvet_Triple_Junction" title="Bouvet Triple Junction">Bouvet Triple Junction</a> in the South Atlantic. 100 Mya, the region where the triple junction was located passed over the hotspot, resulting in a continuous eruption that lasted to about 94 Mya and the seafloor spreading that still separates Antarctica, Africa, and South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Agulhas-Falkland Fracture Zone (AFFZ) stretches 1,200 kilometres (750 mi) across the South Atlantic. It is one of the largest and most spectacular <a href="/wiki/Fracture_zone" title="Fracture zone">fracture zones</a> on Earth. It developed during the Early Cretaceous as West <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> (=South America) broke up from Africa. The AFFZ is characterized by a pronounced topographic anomaly, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agulhas Ridge (page does not exist)">Agulhas Ridge</a> (41°S,16°E-43°S,9°E) which rises more than 2 km above the surrounding sea floor. The only equivalent in size are the neighbouring Diaz Ridge and the Falkland Escarpment. The Agulhas Ridge is unique because it was not formed during the continental breakup during the Cretaceous and because it separates oceanic crusts of different age, and not oceanic crust (~14 km thick) from continental crust (25 km thick).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>North of the (AFFZ) is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Outeniqua_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Outeniqua Basin (page does not exist)">Outeniqua Basin</a> which is a complex system of sub-basins separated from each other by faults and basement arches; there are several smaller fault-bounded sub-basins in the north (Bredasdorp, Infanta, Pletmos, Gamtoos, and Algoa) and a distinctively deeper sub-basin in the south (the South Outeniqua Basin.) The sedimentary fill of these basins developed as the northern edge of the Falkland Plateau separated from the South African southern margin during the early <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diaz_Marginal_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diaz Marginal Ridge (page does not exist)">Diaz Marginal Ridge</a> (DMR) separates these basins from the AFFZ. The DMR is buried under 200–250 m (660–820 ft) of sediments and sedimentary rocks and 150–200 m (490–660 ft) of this sedimentary material is undisturbed Cretaceous sediments younger than the oldest Cretaceous sedimentary rocks in the Southern Outeniqua Basin. The DMR must therefore have formed after the initial West Gondwana breakup 130-90 Mya. The DMR probably formed when new, hot oceanic crust slid past old, cold continental crust and the contrast in temperatures induced a thermal uplift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>As West Gondwana drifted away from Africa roughly 125 Myr, the South Atlantic seafloor formed between them and magnetic anomalies north of the AFFZ reflects phase of the seafloor spreading. South of the AFFZ traces can be found of how the Falkland Plateau and the Agulhas Bank moved relative to each other. On a modern map, the Falkland Plateau can still be rotated and fitted into the Natal Valley in the Indian Ocean east of South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Plateau" title="Agulhas Plateau">Agulhas Plateau</a> is located southeast of the shelf, separated from it by the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Passage" title="Agulhas Passage">Agulhas Passage</a> (through which the Agulhas Current flows.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pliocene">Pliocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pliocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the largest known <a href="/wiki/Slump_(geology)" title="Slump (geology)">slumps</a> occurred on the south-eastern edge of the Agulhas Bank in the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> or more recently. Stretching from a depth of 190–700 m (620–2,300 ft), the so-called Agulhas slump is 750 km (470 mi) long, 106 km (66 mi) wide, and has a volume of 20,000 km<sup>3</sup> (4,800 cu mi). It is a composite slump with proximal and distal <a href="/wiki/Allochthon" title="Allochthon">allochthonous</a> sediment masses separated by a large glide plane scar. In the western part, the sediments are dammed by basement ridges, but, in the eastern part, they have spread into the Transkei Basin. A series of slump scarps along the western edge of the shelf are 18–2 Mya, but covered by younger sediments brought there by the Benguela upwelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Marine_geology_of_the_Cape_Peninsula_and_False_Bay" title="Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay">Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_evolution">Human evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Human evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" title="Anatomically modern humans">Anatomically modern humans</a> evolved around 200 <a href="/wiki/Year#Symbols_y_and_yr" title="Year">kya</a>. The genetic diversity in the human lineage is relatively low, which indicate one or several population bottlenecks late in our lineage. It has been estimated that the population was limited to maybe 600 individuals during the <a href="/wiki/Marine_isotope_stage" title="Marine isotope stage">MIS 6</a> glacial stage (195-125 kya), one of the longest cold periods in the Quaternary of Africa. A technological and behavioural revolution that occurred globally about 50 kya led to a cultural complexity which happened in South Africa around 120-70 kya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Floral_Region" title="Cape Floral Region">Cape Floral Region</a> is a thin coastal strip and a botanic hotspot which developed at the confluence of the Benguela Upwelling and Agulhas Current. According to what professor <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Marean" title="Curtis Marean">Curtis Marean</a> call the "Cape Floral Region – South Coast Model" for the origins of modern humans, the early hunter-gatherers survived on <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophyte" title="Geophyte">geophytes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fur_seal" title="Fur seal">fur seal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>, and wash-ups found on the exposed Agulhas Bank. The bank slopes into the sea and a reconstruction of how the coastline has changed over 440 kya shows that the coast during the Pleistocene was located as far as 90 km (56 mi) from the present coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The present South African southern coastal plain (SCP) is still separated from the rest of Africa by the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fold_Belt" title="Cape Fold Belt">Cape Fold Belt</a>. During glacial maxima the sea-level dropped 120 metres (390 ft). This not only left large parts of the Agulhas Bank exposed, which greatly expanded the area of the SCP, but it also reconnected the SCP to the rest of Africa by the shallow water shelves, which broke the isolation of the SCP. Modern humans evolved on the SCP and the fluctuation in sea-levels would have resulted in a significant variation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selective_pressure" title="Selective pressure">selective pressure</a>. No fossil records are known from the now submerged shelf, but a series of key fossil sites along the coastal margin of the present SCP provide earliest traces of anatomically modern humans and the use of marine resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Point" title="Pinnacle Point">Pinnacle Point</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_importance">Commercial importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Commercial importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>South Africa began oil exploration on the Agulhas Bank in the 1980s. Of more than 200 offshore wells in South Africa, most are found on Bredasdorp Basin on the Agulhas Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishery">Fishery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fishery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Agulhas Bank is also significant for fisheries who use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demersal_trawling" title="Demersal trawling">demersal trawling</a>, demersal <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline fishing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trawling#Bottom_versus_midwater_trawling" title="Trawling">midwater trawl fishing</a> on the bank. Squid and small <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_fish" title="Pelagic fish">pelagic fishes</a> are also caught. Before the introduction of the EEZ, foreign fisheries used <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling#Otter_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">roch-hopper gear trawling</a> on the bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the catches are short-lived shelf-zone pelagic species and more long-lived deep-water species. The large populations of sardine and anchovy also present on the shelf follow an annual cycle. Anchovy spawn on the western Agulhas Bank in early summer while the sardines span over a broader season and area — eggs are transported by currents to the nursery area in the <a href="/wiki/St_Helena_Bay" title="St Helena Bay">St Helena Bay</a> on the South African west coast from where juvenile then migrate back to the Agulhas Bank to spawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Africa has a relatively large fishing industry mostly catching pelagic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilchard" title="Pilchard">pilchard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a> and demersal <a href="/wiki/Hake" title="Hake">Hake</a> on the south and western coasts. Though the east coast has fewer commercial fisheries, the large human population along there has resulted in <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> of coastal fish and invertebrate stocks by recreational and subsistence fishers. A small <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> industry produces mussles and oysters offshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffith-etal-p1_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griffith-etal-p1-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several pelagic species are heavily harvested by the commercial fleet: <a href="/wiki/Seine_fishing#Purse_seine" title="Seine fishing">purse-seine</a> fishery is used to catch sardines, anchovies, and round herring; mid-water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawl</a> fishery to catch horse mackerel and chub mackerel; pelagic <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pole_fishing" title="Pole fishing">pole</a> fishery to catch tunas and swordfish; while hook and line are used inshore to catch squid and teleost species, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyrsites_atun" title="Thyrsites atun">snoek</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Atractoscion_aequidens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Atractoscion aequidens (page does not exist)">geelbek</a>. All these species are relatively common and are considered having an important role in the ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grantham-etal-2011_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grantham-etal-2011-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are at least 12,914 marine species in South Africa, but small bodied species are poorly documented and the abyssal zone is almost completely unexplored. Almost a quarter of South Africa's coast line is protected, excluding deeper water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffith-etal-p1_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Griffith-etal-p1-35">[35]</a></sup> A third of the marine species are endemic to South Africa (though poor levels of taxonomic research in adjoining countries probably affects the apparent endemism.) The degree of endemism varies considerably among taxa: <a href="/wiki/Bryozoa" title="Bryozoa">Bryozoa</a> 64%, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">Mollusca</a> 56%, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echinodermata" title="Echinodermata">Echinodermata</a> 3.6%, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porifera" title="Porifera">Porifera</a> 8.8%, <a href="/wiki/Amphipoda" title="Amphipoda">Amphipoda</a> 33%, <a href="/wiki/Isopoda" title="Isopoda">Isopoda</a> 85%, or <a href="/wiki/Cumacea" title="Cumacea">Cumacea</a> 71%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Fisheries are one of the major threats to the biodiversity of the Agulhas Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grantham-etal-2011_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grantham-etal-2011-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crustaceans">Crustaceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Crustaceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">Copepods</a> comprise 90% of the zooplankton carbon on the Agulhas Bank, and are thus an important source of food for pelagic fish and juvenile squids. The population of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Calanus_agulhensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Calanus agulhensis (page does not exist)">Calanus agulhensis</a></i>, a large species that dominates the copepod community in terms of biomass, has a center of distribution on the central Agulhas Bank. Since 1997 the copepod biomass on the central Agulhas Bank has declined significantly while the biomass of pelagic fish has increased significantly. While it is likely that predation has played an important role in the copepod decline, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> (sea surface temperature and Cholorphyll A abundance) is believed to have contributed to a smaller population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishes">Fishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Fishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="1920" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg/220px-Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg/330px-Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg/440px-Great_white_Dyer_island_2010-07.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

A <a href="/wiki/Great_white_shark" title="Great white shark">Great white shark</a> near Dyer Island</div>

<p>The shelf edge along the bank's southern tip is subject to sporadic upwelling. This slope and its surrounding seamounts are the spawning ground for <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Horse_mackerel" title="Horse mackerel">horse mackerel</a>. Eddies help transport water inshore and link the spawning habitat with important nursery areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Eggs and larvae laid by the anchovy are transported via the <a href="/wiki/Good_Hope_Jet" title="Good Hope Jet">Good Hope Jet</a> to Africa's southwestern coast where they mature. Young anchovies then return to the Agulhas Bank to spawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanke-etal-2009-intro_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Blanke-etal-2009-intro-3">[3]</a></sup> Young sardine and anchovy congregate along the west coast between March and September before they migrate to their spawning grounds on the Agulhas Bank. Sardines of intermediate age are present on the western Agulhas Bank between January and April before migrating to KwaZulu-Natal for winter. The spawning on the Agulhas Bank takes place 30–130 km (19–81 mi) offshore from September to February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford-etal-2006-Intro_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-etal-2006-Intro-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sardine_run" title="Sardine run">Sardine run</a></div>
<p>The bank is the spawning area of deep reef fish species, including the threatened endemic <a href="/wiki/Petrus_rupestris" title="Petrus rupestris">red steenbras</a> (<i>Petrus rupestris</i>). Other species have been overexploited, including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daggerhead_seabream&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daggerhead seabream (page does not exist)">daggerhead seabream</a> (<i>Chrysoblephus cristiceps</i>), <a href="/wiki/Black_musselcracker" title="Black musselcracker">black musselcracker</a> (<i>Cymatoceps nasutus</i>), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_kob&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Silver kob (page does not exist)">silver kob</a> (<i>Argyrosomus inodorus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>57 species of <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> have been reported off the western coast of South Africa, of which 21 are <a href="/wiki/Squaliformes" title="Squaliformes">squaloid sharks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_marine_fishes_of_South_Africa" title="List of marine fishes of South Africa">List of marine fishes of South Africa</a></div>

<p>The main food source for <a href="/wiki/African_penguin" title="African penguin">African penguins</a> (<i>Spheniscus demersus</i>) is anchovy and sardine which they forage between <a href="/wiki/Cape_Columbine" title="Cape Columbine">Cape Columbine</a> and the central Agulhas Bank. The birds have colonies on <a href="/wiki/Dassen_Island" title="Dassen Island">Dassen Island</a>, on the South African west coast, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird_Island,_Algoa_Bay" title="Bird Island, Algoa Bay">Bird Island</a>, on the south coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> African penguins breed opportunistically, following the anchovy and sardine: from February to September on the Western Cape but from January to July on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix_Island,_Algoa_Bay" title="St. Croix Island, Algoa Bay">St Croix Island</a> off Eastern Cape. After breeding, the birds forage further offshore: 10–15 km (6.2–9.3 mi) off the western coast and up to 40 km (25 mi) from their colonies off Eastern Cape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford-etal-2006-Intro_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-etal-2006-Intro-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, when Korean and Philippine vessels started longline fishing along the edges of the Agulhas Bank, seabird bycatch became a huge problem. Large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a> were killed — in average 0.6 birds per 1000 hooks, but up to 18 birds per 1000 hooks were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Since 2007, however, more restrictive permit conditions for foreign-flagged fleets and the use of birds scaring lines have decreased the number of killed birds by 85%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fur_seals">Fur seals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Fur seals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_fur_seal" title="Cape fur seal">Cape fur seals</a> are present along the South African coast. Fur seals are protected in South Africa since 1893 although a small number are occasionally culled to protect sea birds. Many seals are caught in fishery nets and boat propellers, but the seals are also regularly accused of stealing fish from the fisheries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Sharks are known to prey on them, but in 2012 a cape fur seal was observed preying on and consuming a mid-sized blue shark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>51 species, or more than 50%, of the recognized species of cetaceans are present in the southern African subregion (between the equator and the Antarctic ice edge), of which 36 have been sighted in South African and Namibian waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>A vulnerable population of fish-eating <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">killer whales</a> are present offshore on the Agulhas Bank. Observations peak in January while few are sighted in April and May. The killer whales move in pods of 1-4 individuals and are mostly sited over the shelf edge off the south-east coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> An analysis of killer whale <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mtDNA</a> has shown that there was a peak inter-oceanic migration events during the <a href="/wiki/Eemian" title="Eemian">Eemian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interglacial_period" title="Interglacial period">interglacial period</a>, 131-114 kya. This peak coincides with a period of maximal Agulhas leakage which promoted a rapid and episodic interchange of killer whale lineages. During this period killer whales and other marine top predators, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_white_shark" title="Great white shark">great white shark</a>, colonised the North Atlantic and Mediterranean by following their prey — <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swordfish" title="Swordfish">swordfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> <a href="/wiki/Commerson%27s_dolphin" title="Commerson's dolphin">Commerson's dolphin</a> — a species with two isolated populations, one along the southern coast of Argentina and the other around the <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen Islands</a> — was sighted on the Agulhas Bank in 2004. It is not known from which population the sighted individual stems. The Kerguelen Islands are located 4,200 km (2,600 mi) and South America 6,300 km (3,900 mi) from the Agulhas Bank, but the west-ward direction of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a> would force the dolphin to swim against the current from the Kerguelen Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fossil <a href="/wiki/Beaked_whale" title="Beaked whale">beaked whales</a> have been recovered by trawling from the seafloor off South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Stranded <a href="/wiki/Pygmy_sperm_whale" title="Pygmy sperm whale">pygmy sperm whales</a> have been recorded on both the east and west coasts of South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
