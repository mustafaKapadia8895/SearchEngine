<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lifted index</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>lifted index</b> (<b>LI</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> difference between the environment Te(p) and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">air parcel</a> lifted <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatically</a> Tp(p) at a given pressure height in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> (lowest layer where most weather occurs) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, usually 500 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mb</a>). The temperature is measured in Celsius. When the value is positive, the atmosphere (at the respective height) is stable and when the value is negative, the atmosphere is <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">unstable</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Determining_LI">Determining LI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lifted_index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Determining LI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>LI can be computed using computer algorithms but can also be determined graphically. To do this, generally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">parcel</a> is lifted from the portion of the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer</a> (PBL) that lies below the morning <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a>. The air here should be about 60 to 65% <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">RH</a>, which is then lifted along the dry adiabat (see also <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a>) to the <a href="/wiki/Lifted_condensation_level" title="Lifted condensation level">lifting condensation level (LCL)</a>, which is the intersection of that curve with the average <a href="/wiki/Mixing_ratio" title="Mixing ratio">mixing ratio</a> in the boundary layer. Once the LCL is found, the parcel is lifted along the moist adiabat to 500 mb. It is then that one finds LI = Te(p) - Tp(p).</p>
<p>LI is generally scaled as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>LI 6 or Greater, Very Stable Conditions</li>
<li>LI Between 1 and 6 : Stable Conditions, Thunderstorms Not Likely</li>
<li>LI Between 0 and -2 : Slightly Unstable, Thunderstorms Possible, With Lifting Mechanism (i.e., cold front, daytime heating, ...)</li>
<li>LI Between -2 and -6 : Unstable, Thunderstorms Likely, Some Severe With Lifting Mechanism</li>
<li>LI Less Than -6: Very Unstable, Severe Thunderstorms Likely With Lifting Mechanism</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_to_thunderstorms">Significance to thunderstorms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lifted_index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Significance to thunderstorms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lifted index can be used in <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> forecasting, however, <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">convective available potential energy</a> (CAPE) is considered by most as a superior measurement of instability and is preferred by many meteorologists for convection forecasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAPE_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAPE-1">[1]</a></sup> However, LI is easier and faster to determine without using a computer, as determining CAPE requires integration from one level to another.</p>
