<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Oceanic plateau</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>An <b>oceanic</b> or <b>submarine plateau</b> is a large, relatively flat elevation that is higher than the surrounding relief with one or more relatively steep sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are 184 oceanic plateaus covering an area of 18,486,600 km<sup>2</sup> (7,137,700 sq mi), or about 5.11% of the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The South Pacific region around Australia and New Zealand contains the greatest number of oceanic plateaus (see map).</p>
<p>Oceanic plateaus produced by <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous provinces</a> are often associated with <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plumes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_island" title="Volcanic island">volcanic islands</a> — such as Iceland, Hawaii, Cape Verde, and Kerguelen. The three largest plateaus, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_large_igneous_province" title="Caribbean large igneous province">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontong_Java_Plateau" title="Ontong Java Plateau">Ontong Java</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mid-Pacific_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mid-Pacific Mountains (page does not exist)">Mid-Pacific Mountains</a>, are located on <a href="/wiki/Swell_(geology)" title="Swell (geology)">thermal swells</a>. Other oceanic plateaus, however, are made of rifted continental crust, for example <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Falkland_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Falkland Plateau (page does not exist)">Falkland Plateau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Rise" title="Lord Howe Rise">Lord Howe Rise</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Plateau" title="Kerguelen Plateau">Kerguelen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau" title="Mascarene Plateau">Seychelles</a>, and Arctic ridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Plateaus formed by large igneous provinces were formed by the equivalent of continental <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalts</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Traps" title="Deccan Traps">Deccan Traps</a> in India and the <a href="/wiki/Snake_River_Plain" title="Snake River Plain">Snake River Plain</a> in the United States.</p>
<p>In contrast to continental flood basalts, most igneous oceanic plateaus erupt through young and thin (6–7 km (3.7–4.3 mi)) <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-mafic" title="Ultra-mafic">ultra-mafic</a> crust and are therefore uncontaminated by felsic crust and representative for their mantle sources. These plateaus often rise 2–3 km (1.2–1.9 mi) above the surrounding ocean floor and are more buoyant than oceanic crust. They therefore tend to withstand subduction, more-so when thick and when reaching subduction zones shortly after their formations. As a consequence, they tend to "dock" to continental margins and be preserved as <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(geology)" title="Accretion (geology)">accreted</a> <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a>. Such terranes are often better preserved than the exposed parts of continental flood basalts and are therefore a better record of large-scale volcanic eruptions throughout Earth's history. This "docking" also means that oceanic plateaus are important contributors to the growth of continental crust. Their formations often had a dramatic impact on global climate, such as the most recent plateaus formed, the three, large, Cretaceous oceanic plateaus in the Pacific and Indian Ocean: Ontong Java, Kerguelen, and Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span id="Role_in_crust–mantle_recycling"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_crust.E2.80.93mantle_recycling">Role in crust–mantle recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role in crust–mantle recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geologists believe that igneous oceanic plateaus may well represent a stage in the development of <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> as they are generally less dense than <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> while still being denser than normal continental crust.</p>
<p>Density differences in crustal material largely arise from different ratios of various elements, especially <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. Continental crust has the highest amount of silicon (such rock is called <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic</a>). Oceanic crust has a smaller amount of silicon (<a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> rock). Igneous oceanic plateaus have a ratio intermediate between continental and oceanic crust, although they are more mafic than felsic.</p>
<p>However, when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">plate</a> carrying oceanic crust <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducts</a> under a plate carrying an igneous oceanic plateau, the volcanism which erupts on the plateau as the oceanic crust heats up on its descent into the mantle erupts material which is more felsic than the material which makes up the plateau. This represents a step toward creating crust which is increasingly continental in character, being less dense and more buoyant. If an igneous oceanic plateau is subducted underneath another one, or under existing continental crust, the eruptions produced thereby produce material that is yet more felsic, and so on through geologic time.</p>
