<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sea ice</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_ice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2823" data-file-width="4251" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Arctic_ice.jpg/300px-Arctic_ice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Arctic_ice.jpg/450px-Arctic_ice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Arctic_ice.jpg/600px-Arctic_ice.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_ice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Broken pieces of Arctic sea ice with a snow cover.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Sea ice</b> arises as <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> freezes. Because <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> is less <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">dense</a> than water, it floats on the ocean's surface (as does <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> ice, which has an even lower density). Sea ice covers about 7% of the Earth’s surface and about 12% of the world’s oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wadhams_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wadhams-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shokr.26Sinha_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shokr.26Sinha-3">[3]</a></sup> Much of the world's sea ice is enclosed within the polar ice packs in the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_ice_pack" title="Arctic ice pack">Arctic ice pack</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_pack" title="Antarctic ice pack">Antarctic ice pack</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>. Polar packs undergo a significant yearly cycling in surface extent, a natural process upon which depends the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_ecology" title="Arctic ecology">Arctic ecology</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_ecology_and_history" title="Arctic sea ice ecology and history">ocean's ecosystems</a>. Due to the action of winds, currents and temperature fluctuations, sea ice is very dynamic, leading to a wide variety of ice types and features. Sea ice may be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a>, which are chunks of <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelves</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> that <a href="/wiki/Ice_calving" title="Ice calving">calve</a> into the ocean. Depending on location, sea ice expanses may also incorporate icebergs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_features_and_dynamics">General features and dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General features and dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="804" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg/300px-Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg/450px-Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg/600px-Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_ice_Drawing_General_features.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hypothetical sea ice dynamics scenario showing some of the most common sea ice features.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sea ice does not simply grow and melt. During its lifespan, it is very dynamic. Due to the combined action of winds, currents, water temperature, and air temperature fluctuations, sea ice expanses typically undergo a significant amount of deformation. Sea ice is classified according to whether or not it is able to drift, and according to its age.</p>
<h3><span id="Fast_ice_versus_drift_(or_pack)_ice"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fast_ice_versus_drift_.28or_pack.29_ice">Fast ice versus drift (or pack) ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fast ice versus drift (or pack) ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sea ice can be classified according to whether or not it is attached (or frozen) to the shoreline (or between <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">shoals</a> or to grounded <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a>). If attached, it is called landfast ice, or more often, <a href="/wiki/Fast_ice" title="Fast ice">fast ice</a> (from <i>fastened</i>). Alternatively, and unlike fast ice, <a href="/wiki/Drift_ice" title="Drift ice">drift ice</a> occurs further offshore in very wide areas, and encompasses ice that is free to move with currents and winds. The physical boundary between fast ice and drift ice is the <i>fast ice boundary</i>. The drift ice zone may be further divided into a <i>shear zone</i>, a <i>marginal ice zone</i> and a <i>central pack</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lepp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lepp-4">[4]</a></sup> Drift ice consists of <i>floes</i>, individual pieces of sea ice 20 metres (66 ft) or more across. There are names for various floe sizes: <i>small</i> – 20 metres (66 ft) to 100 metres (330 ft); <i>medium</i> – 100 metres (330 ft) to 500 metres (1,600 ft); <i>big</i> – 500 metres (1,600 ft) to 2,000 metres (6,600 ft); <i>vast</i> – 2 kilometres (1.2 mi) to 10 kilometres (6.2 mi); and <i>giant</i> – more than 10 kilometres (6.2 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup> The term <i>pack ice</i> is used either as a synonym to <i>drift ice</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup> or to designate drift ice zone in which the floes are densely packed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup> The overall sea ice cover is termed the <i>ice canopy</i> from the perspective of submarine navigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_based_on_age">Classification based on age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification based on age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another classification used by scientists to describe sea ice is based on age, that is, on its development stages. These stages are: <i>new ice</i>, <i>nilas</i>, <i>young ice</i>, <i>first-year</i> and <i>old</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span id="New_ice,_nilas_and_young_ice"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_ice.2C_nilas_and_young_ice">New ice, nilas and young ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: New ice, nilas and young ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3456" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG/220px-Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG/330px-Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG/440px-Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nilas_Sea_Ice.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Nilas in <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Bay" title="Baffin Bay">Baffin Bay</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>New ice</i> is a general term used for recently frozen sea water that does not yet make up solid ice. It may consist of <a href="/wiki/Frazil_ice" title="Frazil ice">frazil ice</a> (plates or spicules of ice suspended in water), <a href="/wiki/Slush" title="Slush">slush</a> (water saturated snow), or <i>shuga</i> (spongy white ice lumps a few centimeters across). Other terms, such as <a href="/wiki/Grease_ice" title="Grease ice">grease ice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pancake_ice" title="Pancake ice">pancake ice</a>, are used for ice crystal accumulations under the action of wind and waves.</p>
<p><i>Nilas</i> designates a sea ice crust up to 10 centimetres (3.9 in) in thickness. It bends without breaking around waves and swells. Nilas can be further subdivided into <i>dark nilas</i> – up to 5 centimetres (2.0 in) in thickness and very dark, and <i>light nilas</i> – over 5 centimetres (2.0 in) in thickness and lighter in color.</p>
<p><i>Young ice</i> is a transition stage between nilas and first-year ice, and ranges in thickness from 10 centimetres (3.9 in) to 30 centimetres (12 in), Young ice can be further subdivided into <i>grey ice</i> – 10 centimetres (3.9 in) to 15 centimetres (5.9 in) in thickness, and <i>grey-white ice</i> – 15 centimetres (5.9 in) to 30 centimetres (12 in) in thickness. Young ice is not as flexible as nilas, but tends to break under wave action. In a compression regime, it will either raft (at the grey ice stage) or ridge (at the grey-white ice stage).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First-year_sea_ice">First-year sea ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First-year sea ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FYvsSYvsMY.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="538" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/FYvsSYvsMY.svg/220px-FYvsSYvsMY.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/FYvsSYvsMY.svg/330px-FYvsSYvsMY.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/FYvsSYvsMY.svg/440px-FYvsSYvsMY.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FYvsSYvsMY.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Distinction between 1st year sea ice (FY), 2nd year (SY), multiyear (MY) and old ice.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>First-year sea ice</i> is ice that is thicker than <i>young ice</i> but has no more than one year growth. In other words, it is ice that grows in the fall and winter (after it has gone through the <i>new ice — nilas — young ice</i> stages and grows further) but does not survive the spring and summer months (it melts away). The thickness of this ice typically ranges from 0.3 metres (0.98 ft) to 2 metres (6.6 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup> First-year ice may be further divided into <i>thin</i> (30 centimetres (0.98 ft) to 70 centimetres (2.3 ft)), <i>medium</i> (70 centimetres (2.3 ft) to 120 centimetres (3.9 ft)) and <i>thick</i> (&gt;120 centimetres (3.9 ft)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_sea_ice">Old sea ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Old sea ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Old sea ice</i> is sea ice that has survived at least one melting season (<i>i.e.</i> one summer). For this reason, this ice is generally thicker than first-year sea ice. Old ice is commonly divided into two types: <i>second-year ice</i>, which has survived one melting season, and <i>multiyear ice</i>, which has survived more than one. (In some sources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>old ice</i> is more than 2-years old.) Multi-year ice is much more common in the Arctic than it is in the Antarctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wadhams2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wadhams2-8">[8]</a></sup> The reason for this is that sea ice in the south drifts into warmer waters where it melts. In the Arctic, much of the sea ice is land-locked.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Driving_forces">Driving forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Driving forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Fast_ice" title="Fast ice">fast ice</a> is relatively stable (because it is attached to the shoreline or the seabed), <a href="/wiki/Drift_ice" title="Drift ice">drift (or pack) ice</a> undergoes relatively complex deformation processes that ultimately give rise to sea ice’s typically wide variety of landscapes. Wind is thought to be the main driving force along with ocean currents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wadhams_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wadhams-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis force</a> and sea ice surface tilt have also been invoked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup> These driving forces induce a state of stress within the drift ice zone. An <a href="/wiki/Drift_ice" title="Drift ice">ice floe</a> converging toward another and pushing against it will generate a state of <i>compression</i> at the boundary between both. The ice cover may also undergo a state of <i>tension</i>, resulting in divergence and fissure opening. If two floes drift sideways past each other while remaining in contact, this will create a state of <i><a href="/wiki/Shear_(geology)" title="Shear (geology)">shear</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deformation">Deformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sea ice deformation results from the interaction between ice floes, as they are driven against each other. The end result may be of three types of features:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup> 1) <a href="/wiki/Finger_rafting" title="Finger rafting">Rafted ice</a>, when one piece is overriding another; 2) <a href="/wiki/Pressure_ridge_(ice)" title="Pressure ridge (ice)">Pressure ridges</a>, a line of broken ice forced downward (to make up the <i>keel</i>) and upward (to make the <i>sail</i>); and 3) <i><a href="/wiki/Hummock" title="Hummock">Hummock</a></i>, an hillock of broken ice that forms an uneven surface. A <i>shear ridge</i> is a pressure ridge that formed under shear – it tends to be more linear than a ridge induced only by compression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup> A <i>new ridge</i> is a recent feature — it is sharp-crested, with its side sloping at an angle exceeding 40 degrees. In contrast, a <i>weathered ridge</i> is one with a rounded crest and with sides sloping at less than 40 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stamukha" title="Stamukha">Stamukhi</a> are yet another type of pile-up but these are grounded and are therefore relatively stationary. They result from the interaction between <a href="/wiki/Fast_ice" title="Fast ice">fast ice</a> and the drifting pack ice.</p>
<p><i>Level ice</i> is sea ice that has not been affected by deformation, and is therefore relatively flat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECGlossary_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-ECGlossary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leads_and_polynyas">Leads and polynyas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Leads and polynyas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lead_(sea_ice)" title="Lead (sea ice)">Leads</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynyas" title="Polynyas">polynyas</a> are areas of open water that occur within sea ice expanses even though air temperatures are below freezing, and provide a direct interaction between the ocean and the atmosphere, which is important for the wildlife. Leads are narrow and linear – they vary in width from meter to km scale. During the winter, the water in leads quickly freezes up. They are also used for navigation purposes – even when refrozen, the ice in leads is thinner, allowing icebreakers access to an easier sail path, and submarines to surface more easily. Polynyas are more uniform in size than leads and are also larger – two types are recognized: 1) <i>Sensible-heat polynyas</i>, caused by the upwelling of warmer water and 2) <i>Latent-heat polynyas</i>, resulting from persistent winds from the coastline.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice_growth_processes" title="Sea ice growth processes">Sea ice growth processes</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seaice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="545" data-file-width="683" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Seaice.jpg/220px-Seaice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Seaice.jpg/330px-Seaice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Seaice.jpg/440px-Seaice.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seaice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Satellite image of sea ice forming near <a href="/wiki/St._Matthew_Island" title="St. Matthew Island">St. Matthew Island</a> in the Bering Sea.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Only the top layer of water needs to cool to the freezing point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Convection of the surface layer involves the top 100 – 150 m, down to the <a href="/wiki/Pycnocline" title="Pycnocline">pycnocline</a> of increased density.</p>
<p>In calm water, the first sea ice to form on the surface is a skim of separate crystals which initially are in the form of tiny discs, floating flat on the surface and of diameter less than 0.3 centimetres (0.12 in). Each disc has its c-axis vertical and grows outwards laterally. At a certain point such a disc shape becomes unstable, and the growing isolated crystals take on a hexagonal, stellar form, with long fragile arms stretching out over the surface. These crystals also have their c-axis vertical. The dendritic arms are very fragile, and soon break off, leaving a mixture of discs and arm fragments. With any kind of turbulence in the water, these fragments break up further into random-shaped small crystals which form a suspension of increasing density in the surface water, an ice type called <a href="/wiki/Frazil_ice" title="Frazil ice">frazil or grease ice</a>. In quiet conditions the frazil crystals soon freeze together to form a continuous thin sheet of young ice; in its early stages, when it is still transparent — that is the ice called <i>nilas</i>. Once nilas has formed, a quite different growth process occurs, in which water freezes on to the bottom of the existing ice sheet, a process called <i>congelation</i> growth. This growth process yields first-year ice.</p>
<p>In rough water, fresh sea ice is formed by the cooling of the ocean as heat is lost into the atmosphere. The uppermost layer of the ocean is <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooled</a> to slightly below the freezing point, at which time tiny ice platelets (frazil ice) form. With time, this process leads to a mushy surface layer, known as <a href="/wiki/Grease_ice" title="Grease ice">grease ice</a>. Frazil ice formation may also be started by <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snowfall</a>, rather than supercooling. Waves and wind then act to compress these ice particles into larger plates, of several meters in diameter, called <a href="/wiki/Pancake_ice" title="Pancake ice">pancake ice</a>. These float on the ocean surface, and collide with one another, forming upturned edges. In time, the pancake ice plates may themselves be rafted over one another or frozen together into a more solid ice cover, known as consolidated pancake ice. Such ice has a very rough appearance on top and bottom.</p>
<p>If sufficient snow falls on sea ice to depress the freeboard below sea level, sea water will flow in and a layer of ice will form of mixed snow/sea water. This is particularly common around <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> scientist <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Wiese" title="Vladimir Wiese">Vladimir Vize</a> (1886–1954) devoted his life to study the Arctic ice pack and developed the <i>Scientific Prediction of Ice Conditions Theory</i>, for which he was widely acclaimed in academic circles. He applied this theory in the field in the <a href="/wiki/Kara_Sea" title="Kara Sea">Kara Sea</a>, which led to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Vize_Island" title="Vize Island">Vize Island</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Yearly_freeze_and_melt_cycle">Yearly freeze and melt cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Yearly freeze and melt cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="516" data-file-width="688" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg/220px-Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg/330px-Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg/440px-Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic-ice-with-labels.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="524" data-file-width="668" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Arctic-ice-with-labels.png/220px-Arctic-ice-with-labels.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Arctic-ice-with-labels.png/330px-Arctic-ice-with-labels.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Arctic-ice-with-labels.png/440px-Arctic-ice-with-labels.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Sea ice freezes and melts due to a combination of factors, including the age of the ice, air temperatures, and solar insolation. During the winter the area of the Arctic Ocean covered by sea ice increases, usually reaching a maximum extent during the month of March. The area covered in sea ice then decreases, reaching its minimum extent in September most years. First-year ice melts more easily than older ice for two reasons: 1) First-year ice is thinner than older ice, since the process of congelation growth has had less time to operate; and 2) first-year ice is less permeable than older ice, so summer meltwater tends to form deeper ponds on the first-year ice surface than on older ice, and deeper ponds mean lower <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and thus greater solar energy capture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring_and_observations">Monitoring and observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Monitoring and observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Measurement_of_sea_ice" title="Measurement of sea ice">Measurement of sea ice</a></div>
<p>Changes in sea ice conditions are best demonstrated by the rate of melting over time. A composite record of Arctic ice demonstrates that the floes’ retreat began around 1900, experiencing more rapid melting beginning within the past 50 years. Satellite study of sea ice began in 1979, and became a much more reliable measure of ice melt and polar climate change. In comparison to the extended record, the sea-ice extent in the polar region by September 2007 was only half the recorded mass that had been estimated to exist within the 1950–1970 period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The volume of ice hit an all-time low in September 2012, when the ice was determined to cover only 24% of the Arctic Ocean, offsetting the previous low of 29% in 2007. Future predictions cast that summer sea ice might disappear altogether as soon as 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During the warmest years, like the winter of 2005–2006, sea ice is observed to reach a winter maximum extent that is smaller than in the years before or after. The summer minimum Arctic ice extent for 2010 was the third lowest over the period of satellite observations of the polar ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modelling">Modelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In order to gain a better understanding about the variability, numerical sea ice models are used to perform <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_analysis" title="Sensitivity analysis">sensitivity studies</a>. The two main ingredients are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_dynamics" title="Ice dynamics">ice dynamics</a> and the thermodynamical properties (see <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice_emissivity_modelling" title="Sea ice emissivity modelling">Sea ice emissivity modelling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice_growth_processes" title="Sea ice growth processes">Sea ice growth processes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice_thickness" title="Sea ice thickness">Sea ice thickness</a>).</p>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">Global Climate Models</a> (GCMs) have sea ice implemented in their numerical simulation scheme in order to capture the <a href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">Ice-albedo feedback</a> correctly. Examples are:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <b>Louvain-la-Neuve Sea Ice Model</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.elic.ucl.ac.be/repomodx/lim/" rel="nofollow">LIM</a>) is a numerical model of sea ice designed for climate studies and operational oceanography developed at <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_catholique_de_Louvain" title="Université catholique de Louvain">Université catholique de Louvain</a> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.uclouvain.be/teclim" rel="nofollow">UCL</a>). It is coupled to the ocean general circulation model OPA (Ocean Parallélisé) and is freely available as a part of the <b>Nucleus for European Modeling of the Ocean</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.nemo-ocean.eu/" rel="nofollow">NEMO</a>).</li>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/MIT_General_Circulation_Model" title="MIT General Circulation Model">MIT General Circulation Model</a></b> (<a class="external text" href="http://mitgcm.org/" rel="nofollow">MITgcm</a>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global circulation model</a> developed at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT) includes a package for sea-ice (<a class="external text" href="http://mitgcm.org/tag/sea-ice-modeling/" rel="nofollow">MITgcm:seaice</a>). The code is freely available there.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/University_Corporation_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="University Corporation for Atmospheric Research">University Corporation for Atmospheric Research</a> (UCAR) develops the <b>Community Sea Ice Model</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.cesm.ucar.edu/models/ice-csim/" rel="nofollow">CSIM</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a> has a project called <b>Los Alamos Sea Ice Model</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://oceans11.lanl.gov/trac/CICE" rel="nofollow">CICE</a>). CICE is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> and designed as a component of GCM, although it provides a standalone mode.</li>
<li>The <b>Finite-Element Sea-Ice Ocean Model</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.awi.de/en/research/research_divisions/climate_science/climate_dynamics/research_projects/coupled_model_development_echam5_fesom/" rel="nofollow">FESOM</a>) developed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener_Institute" title="Alfred Wegener Institute">Alfred Wegener Institute</a> uses an <a href="/wiki/Unstructured_grid" title="Unstructured grid">unstructured grid</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coupled_model_intercomparison_project" title="Coupled model intercomparison project">Coupled model intercomparison project</a> (<a class="external text" href="http://cmip-pcmdi.llnl.gov/" rel="nofollow">CMIP</a>) offers a standard protocol for studying the output of coupled atmosphere-ocean general circulation models. The coupling takes place at the atmosphere-ocean interface where the sea ice may occur.</p>
<p>In addition to global modeling, various <b>regional models</b> deal with sea ice. Regional models are employed for seasonal forecasting experiments and for <a href="/wiki/Process_study" title="Process study">process studies</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sympagic_ecology" title="Sympagic ecology">Sympagic ecology</a></div>
<p>Sea ice is part of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. When sea water freezes, the ice is riddled with brine-filled channels which sustain <a href="/wiki/Sympagic_ecology" title="Sympagic ecology">sympagic organisms</a> such as bacteria, algae, copepods and annelids, which in turn provide food for animals such as krill and specialised fish like the <a href="/wiki/Bald_notothen" title="Bald notothen">Bald notothen</a>, fed upon in turn by larger animals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_penguins" title="Emperor penguins">Emperor penguins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minke_whales" title="Minke whales">Minke whales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A decline of seasonal sea ice puts the survival of Arctic species such as <a href="/wiki/Ringed_seal" title="Ringed seal">ringed seals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a> at risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barber_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barber-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stirling.2C_Lunn_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stirling.2C_Lunn-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stirling.2C_Parkinson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stirling.2C_Parkinson-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_global_warming_and_climate_change">Relationship to global warming and climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Relationship to global warming and climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ponds_on_the_Ocean,_ICESCAPE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ponds_on_the_Ocean%2C_ICESCAPE.jpg/300px-Ponds_on_the_Ocean%2C_ICESCAPE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ponds_on_the_Ocean%2C_ICESCAPE.jpg/450px-Ponds_on_the_Ocean%2C_ICESCAPE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ponds_on_the_Ocean%2C_ICESCAPE.jpg/600px-Ponds_on_the_Ocean%2C_ICESCAPE.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ponds_on_the_Ocean,_ICESCAPE.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
As ice melts, the liquid water collects in depressions on the surface and deepens them, forming these melt ponds in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>. These fresh water ponds are separated from the salty sea below and around it, until breaks in the ice merge the two.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sea ice provides an ecosystem for various polar species, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a>, whose environment is being threatened as global warming causes the ice to melt more as the Earth’s temperature gets warmer. Furthermore, the sea ice itself functions to help keep polar climates cool, since the ice exists in expansive enough amounts to maintain a cold environment. At this, sea ice’s relationship with global warming is cyclical; the ice helps to maintain cool climates, but as the global temperature increases, the ice melts, and is less effective in keeping those climates cold. The bright, shiny surface (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>) of the ice also serves a role in maintaining cooler polar temperatures by reflecting much of the sunlight that hits it back into space. As the sea ice melts, its surface area shrinks, diminishing the size of the reflective surface and therefore causing the earth to absorb more of the sun’s heat. Though the size of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_floes" title="Ice floes">ice floes</a> is affected by the seasons, even a small change in global temperature can greatly affect the amount of sea ice, and due to the shrinking reflective surface that keeps the ocean cool, this sparks a cycle of ice shrinking and temperatures warming. As a result, the polar regions are the most susceptible places to climate change on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, sea ice affects the movement of ocean waters. In the <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezing</a> process, much of the salt in ocean water is squeezed out of the frozen crystal formations, though some remains frozen in the ice. This salt becomes trapped beneath the sea ice, creating a higher concentration of salt in the water beneath ice floes. This concentration of salt contributes to the salinated water’s <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, and this cold, denser water sinks to the bottom of the ocean. This cold water moves along the ocean floor towards the equator, while warmer water on the ocean surface moves in the direction of the poles. This is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">conveyor belt</a> motion", and is a regularly occurring process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSIDC_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-NSIDC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
