<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">La Niña</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

Sea surface skin temperature anomalies in November 2007 showing La Niña conditions</div>

<p><b>La Niña</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[la ˈniɲa]</a></span>) is a coupled ocean-atmosphere phenomenon that is the counterpart of <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> as part of the broader <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> <a href="/wiki/Climate_pattern" title="Climate pattern">climate pattern</a>. The name La Niña originates from <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, meaning "the little girl", analogous to El Niño meaning "the little boy". It has also in the past been called <i>anti-El Niño</i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and El Viejo (meaning "the old man").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> During a period of La Niña, the <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> across the equatorial Eastern Central <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> will be lower than normal by 3 to 5 °C. In the United States, an <i>appearance</i> of La Niña persists for at least five months. It has extensive effects on the weather in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, even affecting the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">Pacific hurricane</a> seasons.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>La Niña is the positive phase of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño Southern Oscillation</a> and is associated with cooler-than-average sea surface temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, each country and island nation has a different threshold for what constitutes a La Niña event, which is tailored to their specific interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2014_EU_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-December_2014_EU-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, the Australian <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</a> looks at the trade winds, SOI, weather models and sea surface temperatures in the Niño 3 and 3.4 regions before declaring that a La Niña event has started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> declares that a La Niña event has started when the average five-month sea surface temperature deviation for the NINO.3 region is more than 0.5 °C (0.90 °F) cooler for 6 consecutive months or longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrences">Occurrences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<center>
<div class="timeline-wrapper"><map id="timeline_540077225a43b1241dd4992d33030466" name="timeline_540077225a43b1241dd4992d33030466"></map><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/timeline/540077225a43b1241dd4992d33030466.png" usemap="#timeline_540077225a43b1241dd4992d33030466"/></div>
<p>A timeline of all La Niña episodes between 1900 and 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</center>
<p>There was a relatively strong La Niña episode during 1988–1989. La Niña also formed in late 1983,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Ni.C3.B1a_and_Winter_Weather_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-La_Ni.C3.B1a_and_Winter_Weather-8">[8]</a></sup> in 1995, and a protracted La Niña event that lasted from mid-1998 through early 2001. This was followed by a neutral period between 2001 and 2002. The La Niña which developed in mid-2007, and lasted until almost 2009, was a moderate one. The strength of La Niña made the <a href="/wiki/2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2008 Atlantic hurricane season">2008 Atlantic hurricane season</a> one of the five most active since 1944; sixteen named storms had winds of at least 39 miles per hour (63 km/h), eight of which became 74-mile-per-hour (119 km/h) or greater hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan10_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan10-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A new La Niña episode developed quite quickly in the eastern and central tropical Pacific in mid-2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup> and lasted until early 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup> It intensified again in mid-2011 and lasted until early 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This La Niña, combined with record-high ocean temperatures in the north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, was a large factor in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%932011_Queensland_floods" title="2010–2011 Queensland floods">2010–2011 Queensland floods</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the quartet of recent heavy snowstorms in North America starting with the <a href="/wiki/December_2010_North_American_blizzard" title="December 2010 North American blizzard">December 2010 North American blizzard</a>. The same La Niña event was also a likely cause of a series of tornadoes of above-average severity that struck the Midwestern and Southern United States in the spring of 2011, and drought conditions in the South Central states including Texas, Oklahoma and Arkansas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Meanwhile, a series of major storms caused extensive flooding in California in December 2010, with seven consecutive days of non-stop rainfall, leading to one of the wettest Decembers in over 120 years of records. This is in contrast to the drier-than-normal conditions typically associated with La Niña in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, on a global scale, La Niña events helped keep the average global temperature below recent trends. As a result, 2011 tied with 1997 for the eleventh-warmest year on record. It was the second-coolest year of the 21st century to date, and tied with the second-warmest year of the 20th century. A relatively strong phase of La Niña opened the year, dissipated in the spring before re-emerging in October and lasted through the end of the year. When compared to previous La Niña years, the 2011 global surface temperature was the warmest observed. The 2011 globally-averaged precipitation over land was the second-wettest year on record, behind 2010. Precipitation varied greatly across the globe. This La Niña contributed to severe drought in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> and to Australia's third-wettest year in its 112-year period of records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>La Niñas occurred in 1904, 1908, 1910, 1916, 1924, 1928, 1938, 1949–51,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> 1954–56, 1964, 1970–72, 1973–76, 1983–85,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Ni.C3.B1a_and_Winter_Weather_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-La_Ni.C3.B1a_and_Winter_Weather-8">[8]</a></sup> 1988–89, 1995–96, 1998–2001, 2007–08, 2010–12 and 2016–17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_on_the_global_climate">Impacts on the global climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impacts on the global climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>La Niña impacts the global climate and disrupts normal weather patterns, which as a result can lead to intense storms in some places and droughts in others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIWA_El_Ni.C3.B1o.2FLa_Ni.C3.B1a_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIWA_El_Ni.C3.B1o.2FLa_Ni.C3.B1a-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_impacts">Regional impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Regional impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Observations of La Niña events since 1950, show that impacts associated with La Niña events depend on what season it is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cascade_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cascade-21">[21]</a></sup> However, while certain events and impacts are expected to occur during events, it is not certain or guaranteed that they will occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cascade_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cascade-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1359" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg/220px-Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg/330px-Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg/440px-Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oxfam_East_Africa_-_A_family_gathers_sticks_and_branches_for_firewood.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Between 50,000 and 100,000 people died during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Horn_of_Africa_drought" title="2011 Horn of Africa drought">2011 Horn of Africa drought</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>La Niña results in wetter-than-normal conditions in Southern Africa from December to February, and drier-than-normal conditions over equatorial East Africa over the same period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During La Niña years, the formation of tropical cyclones, along with the subtropical ridge position, shifts westward across the western Pacific Ocean, which increases the landfall threat to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-China-24">[24]</a></sup> In March 2008, La Niña caused a drop in sea surface temperatures over Southeast Asia by 2 °C. It also caused heavy rains over <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="539" data-file-width="486" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif/256px-La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif/384px-La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif 2x" width="256"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Regional impacts of La Niña.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>La Niña causes mostly the opposite effects of El Niño, above-average <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> across the northern Midwest, the northern Rockies, Northern California, and the Pacific Northwest's southern and eastern regions. Meanwhile, precipitation in the southwestern and southeastern states is below average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This also allows for the development of many stronger-than-average hurricanes in the Atlantic and fewer in the Pacific.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> condition for <a href="/wiki/Tehuantepecer" title="Tehuantepecer">Tehuantepecer</a> winds is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_system" title="High-pressure system">high-pressure system</a> forming in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_de_Oaxaca" title="Sierra Madre de Oaxaca">Sierra Madre</a> of Mexico in the wake of an advancing cold front, which causes winds to accelerate through the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Tehuantepec" title="Isthmus of Tehuantepec">Isthmus of Tehuantepec</a>. Tehuantepecers primarily occur during the cold season months for the region in the wake of cold fronts, between October and February, with a summer maximum in July caused by the westward extension of the Azores-Bermuda high pressure system. Wind magnitude is weaker during La Niña years than <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> years, due to the less frequent cold frontal incursions during La Niña winters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> with its effects can last from a few hours to six days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWL1971_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWL1971-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Canada, La Niña will, in general, cause a cooler, snowier winter, such as the near-record-breaking amounts of snow recorded in La Niña winter of 2007/2008 in Eastern Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cpc.noaa.gov_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-cpc.noaa.gov-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During a time of La Niña, drought plagues the coastal regions of Peru and Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLOBE_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-GLOBE-31">[31]</a></sup> From December to February, northern Brazil is wetter than normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLOBE_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-GLOBE-31">[31]</a></sup> La Niña causes higher than normal rainfall in the central Andes, which in turn causes catastrophic flooding on the Llanos de Mojos of Beni Department, Bolivia. Such flooding is documented from 1853, 1865, 1872, 1873, 1886, 1895, 1896, 1907, 1921, 1928, 1929 and 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversity">Diversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Ni%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enso-index-map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="918" height="66" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Enso-index-map.png/200px-Enso-index-map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Enso-index-map.png/300px-Enso-index-map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Enso-index-map.png/400px-Enso-index-map.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enso-index-map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing Niño3.4 and other index regions</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The traditional La Niña, also called Eastern Pacific (EP) La Niña,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yu2009_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yu2009-33">[33]</a></sup> involves temperature anomalies in the Eastern Pacific. However, in the last two decades, nontraditional La Niña were observed, in which the usual place of the temperature anomaly (Niño 1 and 2) is not affected, but an anomaly arises in the central Pacific (Niño 3.4).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larkin2007_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin2007-34">[34]</a></sup> The phenomenon is called Central Pacific (CP) La Niña,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yu2009_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yu2009-33">[33]</a></sup> "dateline" La Niña (because the anomaly arises near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_dateline" title="International dateline">dateline</a>), or La Niña "Modoki" (Modoki is <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> for "similar, but different").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNM_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNM-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNModoki_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNModoki-36">[36]</a></sup> There are flavors of ENSO additional to EP and CP types and some scientists argue that ENSO exists as a continuum often with hybrid types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The effects of the CP La Niña are different from those of the traditional EP La Niña—e.g., the recently discovered La Niña leads to a rainfall increase over <a href="/wiki/North_West_Australia" title="North West Australia">northwestern Australia</a> and northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray-Darling_basin" title="Murray-Darling basin">Murray-Darling basin</a>, rather than over the east as in a conventional La Niña.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNModoki_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-LNModoki-36">[36]</a></sup> Also, La Niña Modoki increases the frequency of cyclonic storms over <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>, but decreases the occurrence of severe storms in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> overall, with the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> becoming severely non-conductive to tropical cyclone formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_ENSO_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPC_ENSO-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indian_LMN_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indian_LMN-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The recent discovery of ENSO Modoki has some scientists believing it to be linked to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yeh2009_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yeh2009-40">[40]</a></sup> However, comprehensive satellite data go back only to 1979. Generally, there is no scientific consensus on how/if <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> may affect ENSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins10_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins10-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is also a scientific debate on the very existence of this "new" ENSO. A number of studies dispute the reality of this statistical distinction or its increasing occurrence, or both, either arguing the reliable record is too short to detect such a distinction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> finding no distinction or trend using other statistical approaches,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> or that other types should be distinguished, such as standard and extreme ENSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent years when La Niña Modoki events occurred include 1973–74, 1975–76, 1983–84, 1988–89, 1998–99, 2000–01, 2008–09, 2010–11 and 2016–17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNM_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-LNM-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENSO_SA_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ENSO_SA-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modoki_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Modoki-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
