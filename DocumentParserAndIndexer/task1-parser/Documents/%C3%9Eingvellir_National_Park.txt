<p><b>Þingvellir</b> (<small>Icelandic: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Icelandic" title="Help:IPA/Icelandic">[ˈθiŋkˌvɛtlɪr̥]</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:%C3%9Eingvellir_pronunciation.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/31/Is-%C3%9Eingvellir.oga" title="Is-Þingvellir.oga">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), anglicised as <b>Thingvellir</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[Note 1]</a></sup> is a national park in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Bl%C3%A1sk%C3%B3gabygg%C3%B0" title="Bláskógabyggð">Bláskógabyggð</a> in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, about 40 km northeast of Iceland's capital, <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>. Þingvellir is a site of historical, cultural, and geological significance, and is one of the most popular tourist destinations in Iceland. The park lies in a <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> that marks the crest of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> and the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian</a>. To its south lies <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Eingvallavatn" title="Þingvallavatn">Þingvallavatn</a>, the largest natural lake in Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geol_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-geol-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Þingvellir is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">Althing</a>, the national parliament of Iceland, which was established at the site in 930 AD. Sessions were held at the location until 1798.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bj.C3.B6rnsson_1984_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bj.C3.B6rnsson_1984-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Þingvellir National Park</b> (Icelandic: <i>þjóðgarðurinn á Þingvöllum</i>) was founded in 1930, marking the one-thousandth anniversary of the Althing. The park was later expanded to protect the diverse and natural phenomena in the surrounding area, and was designated as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 2004.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Eingvellir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i><span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Þingvellir</span></i> is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> <i><span lang="non" xml:lang="non">Þingvǫllr</span></i>, from <i><span lang="non" xml:lang="non"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%BEing#Old_Norse" title="wiktionary:þing">þing</a></span></i> (“<a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">thing</a>, assembly”) and <i><span lang="non" xml:lang="non"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/v%C7%ABllr#Old_Norse" title="wiktionary:vǫllr">vǫllr</a></span></i> (“field”), meaning <i>assembly fields</i>. Compare the English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thing" title="wiktionary:thing">thing</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weald#English" title="wiktionary:weald">weald</a></i> (“Thingweald”) from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Anglo-Saxon</a> <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%BEing#Old_English" title="wiktionary:þing">þing</a></span></i> and <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weald#Old_English" title="wiktionary:weald">weald</a></span></i>. The site takes its name from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al%C3%BEing" title="Alþing">Alþing</a></i> (<i>Althing</i>), the national parliament of Iceland, which was founded at Þingvellir in 930 and held its sessions there until 1798. A <a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">thing</a> was a form of governing assembly found in <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> societies, and a tradition that endures to this day in one form or another across <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a>.</p>
<p>Although the name <i>Þingvellir</i> is plural, the older form <i>Þingvǫllr</i> is singular, and the modern singular form <i>Þingvöllur</i> can still be heard.</p>
<p>The name is anglicised as <b>Thingvellir</b>, and might appear as <i>Tingvellir</i>, <i>Thingvalla</i> or <i>Tingvalla</i> in other languages. The spelling <i>Pingvellir</i> is incorrect: as the letter “p” should never be used to represent the letter <a href="/wiki/Thorn_(letter)" title="Thorn (letter)">“þ” (thorn)</a>, which is pronounced as "th" is in English.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dingwall" title="Dingwall">Dingwall</a> and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Tingwall_(disambiguation)" title="Tingwall (disambiguation)">Tingwall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thingwall" title="Thingwall">Thingwall</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tingvoll" title="Tingvoll">Tingvoll</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> bear names of the same root and meaning.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Eingvellir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Þingvellir</span> became a national park as a result of legislation passed in 1928 to protect the remains of the parliament site, thus creating the first national park in Iceland. The park was decreed,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...a protected national shrine for all Icelanders, the perpetual property of the Icelandic nation under the preservation of parliament, never to be sold or mortgaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span id="Founding_of_Iceland's_parliament"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_of_Iceland.27s_parliament">Founding of Iceland's parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Eingvellir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding of Iceland's parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Landn%C3%A1mab%C3%B3k" title="Landnámabók">Book of Settlements</a> (<i>Landnámabók</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Settlement_of_Iceland" title="Settlement of Iceland">settlement of Iceland</a> began in AD 874 when the Norwegian chieftain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ing%C3%B3lfur_Arnarson" title="Ingólfur Arnarson">Ingólfur Arnarson</a> became the first permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_people" title="Norwegian people">Norwegian</a> settler on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> Over the next centuries, people of <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> origin settled in Iceland. Early on, district assemblies were formed, but as the population grew, there was a need for a general assembly. The descendants of Ingólfur who dominated the region of southwest Iceland had become the most powerful family in the country, and other chieftains felt a need for a general assembly to limit their power.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmur_Geitsk%C3%B6r" title="Grímur Geitskör">Grímur Geitskör</a> was allotted the role of rallying support and finding a suitable location for the assembly. At about the same time, the owner of Bláskógar (the contemporary name for the Þingvellir region) was found guilty of murder. His land was declared public, and then obligated to be used for assembly proceedings, and the building of temporary dwellings, and the forest to be used for kindling and the <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> of horses. The Þingvellir area was chosen for this reason and for its accessibility to the most populous regions of the north, south and west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> The longest journey a <i>goði</i> (chieftain) had to travel was 17 days, from the easternmost part of the country where mountains and glacial rivers proved bothersome obstacles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bj.C3.B6rnsson_1984_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bj.C3.B6rnsson_1984-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The foundation of the Icelandic parliament is said to be the founding of the nation of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and the first parliamentary proceedings in the summer of 930 laid the ground for a common cultural heritage and national identity. Þingvellir played a central role in the history of the country, and its history runs almost parallel with the history of the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Commonwealth" title="Icelandic Commonwealth">Icelandic Commonwealth</a>.</p>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">Alþingi</a></i> (assembly) at Þingvellir was Iceland's supreme legislative and judicial authority from its establishment in 930 until 1271. The <i><a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6gberg" title="Lögberg">Lögberg</a></i> or Law Rock was the focal point of the Alþingi and a natural platform for holding speeches. The Lawspeaker, elected for three years at a time, presided over the assembly and recited the law of the land. Before the law was written down, he was expected to recite it from memory on the Lögberg over the course of three summers along with the complete assembly procedures every summer. Inauguration and dissolution of the assembly took place at the Lögberg, where rulings made by the Law Council were announced, the calendar was confirmed, legal actions were brought and other announcements made which concerned the entire nation. Anyone attending the assembly was entitled to present his case on important issues from the Lögberg.</p>
<p>The Law Council served as both parliament and supreme court. Laws were passed and approved there, and rulings made on points of law. The Law Council appointed members of the Fifth Court (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">appellate court</a>) and the Lawspeaker, and took part in the election of the bishop. Unlike the Alþingi, the Law Council was a closed body in which only certain people enjoyed full rights: chieftains who held the office of "<a href="/wiki/Gothi" title="Gothi">goði</a>", their "Þingmen" and later also bishops. However, everyone at the assembly was entitled to watch and listen to the Law Council at work.</p>
<p>From the earliest times until the 15th century, the Law Council met at Neðri-Vellir on the east bank of Öxará, but when the river changed its course around 1500, the council was moved to an islet in the river. In 1594, the Law Council was relocated to the foot of the ancient Law Rock, where it remained until the Alþingi was finally transferred from it in 1798.</p>
<p>The Alþingi was Iceland's legislative and chief judicial authority for the duration of the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Commonwealth" title="Icelandic Commonwealth">Commonwealth</a>, until 1271. Executive power was in the hands of the chieftains and parties to individual cases. This proved to be quite an adequate arrangement for as long as the balance of power remained, but flaws emerged when it was disrupted.</p>
<p>In the final decades of the Commonwealth, there were clashes between chieftain families, which resulted in Iceland coming under the Norwegian crown. Executive power was strengthened under this new order, while legislative and judicial authority at first remained in the hands of the Alþingi, but was gradually transferred to the Norwegian and later the Danish rulers, until in 1662, the King of Denmark became the absolute monarch of Iceland.</p>

<p>Þingvellir was the centre of Icelandic culture. Every year during the Commonwealth period, people would flock to Þingvellir from all over the country, sometimes numbering in the thousands. They set up dwellings with walls of turf and rock and temporary roofing and stayed in them for the two weeks of the assembly. Although the duties of the assembly were the main reason for going there, ordinary people gathered at Þingvellir for a wide variety of reasons. Merchants, sword-sharpeners, and tanners would sell their goods and services, entertainers performed, and ale-makers brewed drinks for the assembly guests. News was told from distant parts; games and feasts were held. Young people met to make their plans, no less than leading national figures and experts in law. Itinerant farmhands looked for work and vagrants begged. Þingvellir was a meeting place for everyone in Iceland, laying the foundation for the language and literature that have been a prominent part of people's lives right up to the present day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Þingvellir is notable for its unusual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> environment in a <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a> between the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plates</a> can be clearly seen in the cracks or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_fault" title="Geologic fault">faults</a> which traverse the region, the largest one, Almannagjá, being a veritable canyon. This also causes the often measurable <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the rifts are full of clear water. One, <span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Nikulásargjá</span>, was bridged for the occasion of the visit of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_VIII_of_Denmark" title="Frederick VIII of Denmark">King Frederick VIII of Denmark</a> in 1907. On this occasion, visitors began to throw coins from the bridge into the fissure, a tradition based on European legends.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The bottom has become littered with sparkling coins, and the rift is now better known as <span lang="is" xml:lang="is"><i>Peningagjá</i></span>, or "coin fissure".</p>
<p><span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Þingvellir</span> is situated on the northern shore of <span lang="is" xml:lang="is"><a href="/wiki/%C3%9Eingvallavatn" title="Þingvallavatn">Þingvallavatn</a></span>, the largest natural <a href="/wiki/List_of_lakes_of_Iceland" title="List of lakes of Iceland">lake of Iceland</a>. The river <span lang="is" xml:lang="is"><a href="/wiki/%C3%96xar%C3%A1" title="Öxará">Öxará</a></span> traverses the national park and forms a waterfall at the <span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Almannagjá</span>, called <span lang="is" xml:lang="is"><a href="/wiki/%C3%96xar%C3%A1rfoss" title="Öxarárfoss">Öxarárfoss</a></span>. On the lake's northern shore the <a href="/wiki/Silfra" title="Silfra">Silfra</a> fissure is a popular diving and snorkelling tour location.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Þingvellir</span> was designated a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> based on cultural criteria. It may also qualify on geological criteria in the future, as there has been ongoing discussion of a possible "serial trans-boundary nomination" for the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, which would include other sites in the Atlantic such as <a href="/wiki/Pico_Island" title="Pico Island">Pico Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAR_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAR-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Together with the waterfall <a href="/wiki/Gullfoss" title="Gullfoss">Gullfoss</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">geysers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Haukadalur" title="Haukadalur">Haukadalur</a>, <span lang="is" xml:lang="is">Þingvellir</span> is part of a group of the most famous sights of Iceland, the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Circle_(Iceland)" title="Golden Circle (Iceland)">Golden Circle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Because of its natural environment, Þingvellir has been a subject in the works of a number of Icelandic painters, including <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3hannes_Sveinsson_Kjarval" title="Jóhannes Sveinsson Kjarval">Johannes Sveinsson Kjarval</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%81sgr%C3%ADmur_J%C3%B3nsson" title="Ásgrímur Jónsson">Ásgrímur Jónsson</a>. The National Gallery of Iceland owns more than 150 paintings by Ásgrímur Jónsson that have Þingvellir as their subject. Þingvellir grew popular among artists because not only for its natural environment, but also because it was close to the capital of Iceland, <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a> and thus relatively inexpensive to travel there.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Þingvellir National Park is popular with tourists, and is one of the three key attractions within the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Circle_(Iceland)" title="Golden Circle (Iceland)">Golden Circle</a>. There is a visitor centre, where visitors can obtain interpretation of the history and nature of Þingvellir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-VC-11">[10]</a></sup> There is an information centre near the camping grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IC_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IC-12">[11]</a></sup> There are hiking trails. Scuba diving has also become popular at <a href="/wiki/Silfra" title="Silfra">Silfra Lake</a> as the continental drift between the tectonic plates made it wide enough for divers to enjoy unparalleled visibility<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>

<h4><span id="Things_(assemblies)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Things_.28assemblies.29">Things (assemblies)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Eingvellir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Things (assemblies)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gulating" title="Gulating">Gulating</a>, one of the first <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> legislative assemblies and one of the present-day law courts of <a href="/wiki/Western_Norway" title="Western Norway">western Norway</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jamtam%C3%B3t" title="Jamtamót">Jamtamót</a>, the historical legislative assembly of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4mtland" title="Jämtland">Jämtland</a>.</li>

</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Eingvellir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Thingvalla_Line" title="Thingvalla Line">Thingvalla Line</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> shipping company active between 1879 and 1898, was named after Þingvellir. At its peak, the company had ten ships in its fleet, one of which was named the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=S/S_Thingvalla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="S/S Thingvalla (page does not exist)">S/S Thingvalla</a>, launched in 1873. The company operated four other ships which bore Icelandic names, namely the <i>S/S Geiser</i>, the <i>S/S Island</i> and two vessels named <i>S/S Hekla</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
