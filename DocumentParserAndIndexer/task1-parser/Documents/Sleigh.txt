<p>A <b>sled</b>, <b>sledge</b>, or <b>sleigh</b> is a land vehicle with a smooth underside or possessing a separate body supported by two or more smooth, relatively narrow, longitudinal <b>runners</b> that travels by sliding across a surface. Most commonly sleds are used on snow or <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, however in certain cases they may be used on any surfaces, especially on ones with relatively low <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, such as sand or wet grass. They may be used to transport passengers, cargo, or both. Shades of meaning differentiating the three terms often reflect regional variations depending on historical uses and prevailing climate.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">Britain</a> <i>sledge</i> is the general term, and more common than <i>sled</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Toboggan" title="Toboggan">Toboggan</a></i> is sometimes used synonymously with <i>sledge</i> but more often to refer to a particular type of sledge without runners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Sleigh</i> refers to a moderate to large-sized, usually open-topped vehicle to carry passengers or goods, and typically drawn by horses or dogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In Scandinavia particularly a sleigh may also be drawn by <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeer</a>, as for <a href="/wiki/Father_Christmas" title="Father Christmas">Father Christmas</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> usage <i>sled</i> remains the general term but often implies a smaller device, often for recreational use. <i>Sledge</i> implies a heavier sled used for moving freight or massive objects (<i>syn.</i> "stone boat"). <i>Sleigh</i> refers more specifically than in Britain to a vehicle which is essentially a cold-season alternative to a <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriage</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wagon" title="Wagon">wagon</a> and has seating for passengers; what can be called a dog-sleigh in Britain is known only as a dog-sled in North America.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australia</a>, where there is limited snow, <i>sleigh</i> and <i>sledge</i> are given equal preference in local parlance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mac-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The people of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a> are thought to have used sledges extensively in the construction of their public works, in particular for the transportation of heavy <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisks</a>.</p>
<p>Sleds and sledges were found in the <a href="/wiki/Oseberg_Ship" title="Oseberg Ship">Oseberg "Viking" ship</a> excavation. Sledges were useful not only in winter but can be drawn over wet fields, muddy roads, and even hard ground, if one helps them along by greasing the blades with oil or alternatively wetting them with water; in cold weather the water will freeze to ice and they glide along more smoothly with less effort to pull them. The sledge was also highly prized, because – unlike wheeled vehicles – it was exempt from tolls.</p>
<p>Until the late 19th century, a closed winter sled, or <a href="/wiki/Vozok" title="Vozok">vozok</a>, provided a high-speed means of transport through the snow-covered plains of European <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It was a means of transport preferred by royals, bishops, and boyars of <a href="/wiki/Tsardom_of_Russia" title="Tsardom of Russia">Muscovy</a>. Several royal vozoks of historical importance have been preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Kremlin_Armoury" title="Kremlin Armoury">Kremlin Armoury</a>.</p>
<p>Man-hauled sledges were the traditional means of transport on British exploring expeditions to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> regions in the 19th and early 20th centuries. <a href="/wiki/Dog_sled" title="Dog sled">Dog sleds</a> were used by most others, such as <a href="/wiki/Roald_Amundsen" title="Roald Amundsen">Roald Amundsen</a>. Today some people use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_flying" title="Kite flying">kites</a> to tow exploration sleds in such climes.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sled&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HorseandSleighRide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/HorseandSleighRide.jpg/250px-HorseandSleighRide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/HorseandSleighRide.jpg/375px-HorseandSleighRide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/HorseandSleighRide.jpg/500px-HorseandSleighRide.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>The word <i>sled</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>sledde</i>, which itself has the origins in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Dutch_language" title="Old Dutch language">Old Dutch</a> word <i>slee</i>, meaning "sliding" or "slider". The same word shares common ancestry with both <i>sleigh</i> and <i>sledge</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The word sleigh, on the other hand, is an anglicized form of the modern Dutch word "slee" and was introduced to the English language by Dutch immigrants to North America <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>There are several types of widely used recreational sleds designed for sliding down snowy hills (<a href="/wiki/Sledding" title="Sledding">sledding</a>):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Toboggan" title="Toboggan">Toboggan</a>, an elongated sled without runners, usually made from wood or plastic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Saucer, a round sled curved like a <a href="/wiki/Contact_lens" title="Contact lens">contact lens</a>, also without runners and usually made out of plastic or metal</li>
<li>Steel runner sled or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexible_flyer" title="Flexible flyer">flexible flyer</a>, a steerable wooden sled with thin metal runners<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kicksled" title="Kicksled">Kicksled</a> or spark, a human-powered sled</li>
<li>Inflatable sled or <a href="/wiki/Tubing_(recreation)#Snow" title="Tubing (recreation)">tube</a>, a plastic membrane filled with air to make a very lightweight sled</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Foam" title="Foam">Foam</a> slider, a flat piece of durable foam with handles and a smooth underside</li>
<li>Backcountry sled, a deep, steerable plastic sled to kneel on with pads and a seatbelt, such as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_River_Rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mad River Rocket (page does not exist)">Mad River Rocket</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleds_for_competitive_sledding">Sleds for competitive sledding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sled&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sleds for competitive sledding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few types of sleds are used only for a specific sport:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bobsled" title="Bobsled">Bobsled</a> (British bobsleigh), an aerodynamic composite bodied vehicle on lightweight runners</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Luge" title="Luge">Luge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_(sport)" title="Skeleton (sport)">skeleton</a>, tiny one or two-person sleds with runners<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Various types of sleds are pulled by animals such as <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeer</a>, horses, <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mules</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxen" title="Oxen">oxen</a>, or dogs.</p>

<li>A cutter is a North American type of small horse-drawn sled<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Troika_(driving)" title="Troika (driving)">Troika</a>, a vehicle drawn by three horses, usually a sled, but it may also be a wheeled carriage</li>

<li>In <a href="/wiki/Tractor_pulling" title="Tractor pulling">truck and tractor pulling</a>, an implement pulled behind the machine which uses friction to stop the machine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
