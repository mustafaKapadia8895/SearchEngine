<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Severe thunderstorm warning</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Severe_Thunderstorm_Watch" title="Severe Thunderstorm Watch">Severe Thunderstorm Watch</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>severe thunderstorm warning</b> (<a href="/wiki/Specific_Area_Message_Encoding" title="Specific Area Message Encoding">SAME</a> code: SVR) is an alert issued when trained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_spotters" title="Storm spotters">storm spotters</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> indicate that a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> is producing or will soon produce dangerously large <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> or high winds, capable of causing significant damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warningdefinition_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warningdefinition-1">[1]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, severe thunderstorm warnings don't account for <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, a significant hazard in any thunderstorm, or <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> caused by a thunderstorm's extreme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Urban_and_small_stream_flood_advisory" title="Urban and small stream flood advisory">flood advisory</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_Flood_Warning" title="Flash Flood Warning">Flash Flood Warning</a> is issued in these cases).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warningdefinition_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Warningdefinition-1">[1]</a></sup> A similar warning is issued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_Service_of_Canada" title="Meteorological Service of Canada">Meteorological Service of Canada</a> from their offices in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth,_Nova_Scotia" title="Dartmouth, Nova Scotia">Dartmouth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-2">[2]</a></sup> Skywarn issues the severe thunderstorm warnings for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Also, just as in the United States, lightning doesn't warrant a severe thunderstorm warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, severe thunderstorm warnings are issued by the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</a> for all Australian states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Severe_thunderstorm_warning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS) defines a severe thunderstorm as having large <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> of at least 1 inch (2.5 cm), surface wind speeds of 58 miles per hour (93 km/h) or greater, or both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Prior to January 2010, the hail size for which a thunderstorm would be considered severe was 0.75 inches (1.9 cm); public complacency due to overly frequent issuances of severe thunderstorm warnings and recent studies stating hail didn't produce significant damage on the ground until it reached one inch in diameter caused the upgrade in hail criteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, a severe thunderstorm is defined as having wind gusts of greater than 90 kilometres per hour (56 mph), hail with a diameter of greater than 20 millimetres (0.79 in), rainfall of greater than 50 millimetres (2.0 in) in an hour or greater than 75 millimetres (3.0 in) in three hours, or tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Australia, the Bureau of Meteorology issues severe thunderstorm warnings advising of damaging winds (&gt;90km/h), heavy rainfall, large hail (2cm or larger), and flash flooding. Severe thunderstorm warnings are given as either a broad-based warning, covering expected impact in a weather reporting area, or as a detailed warning, when a thunderstorm is within weather-watch radar range and includes a map depicting any existing thunderstorms and the forecast direction of movement for up to 60 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup> Similar official warnings are also issued for tropical cyclones, severe weather including heatwaves, tsunamis, and bushfires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A severe thunderstorm warning indicates the warned area is in impending danger. Occasionally, severe thunderstorms can and do produce <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> without warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warningsafety_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warningsafety-8">[8]</a></sup> While not all severe thunderstorms produce tornadoes, they can produce serious <a href="/wiki/Downburst#Straight-line_winds" title="Downburst">straight line wind damage</a> as severe as a tornado, which can actually cover a much wider area than a tornado usually does.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Definition-9">[9]</a></sup> If a tornado is detected on radar or is sighted visually, a <a href="/wiki/Tornado_warning" title="Tornado warning">tornado warning</a> will be issued either in replacement of or concurrently to the existing severe thunderstorm warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Upgrade_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Upgrade-10">[10]</a></sup> Generally, but not always, a <a href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm_watch" title="Severe thunderstorm watch">severe thunderstorm watch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">tornado watch</a> will precede a warning.</p>
<p>If a tornado warning is issued, based on Doppler <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a>, it means strong rotation has been detected within a thunderstorm. Usually, if a thunderstorm is producing only weak rotation, it will only yield hazardous weather warranting a severe thunderstorm warning. However, the public will usually be advised this type of rotation has been detected and that the storm in question should be watched closely in the near future for further intensification. In the United States, local <a href="/wiki/List_of_National_Weather_Service_Weather_Forecast_Offices" title="List of National Weather Service Weather Forecast Offices">NWS forecast offices</a>, particularly those in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> or <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern U.S.</a>, sometimes include the wording "Severe thunderstorms can produce tornadoes with no advance warning..." or a similar reference in their severe thunderstorm warnings, usually when there is a tornado threat or especially when a tornado watch is in effect.</p>
<p>Some storms, especially in the Great Plains, may produce massive hailstones the size of baseballs (2.75 inches (7.0 cm)) or larger which may fall fast enough to potentially kill a person by repeated <a href="/wiki/Blunt_trauma" title="Blunt trauma">blunt trauma</a>.</p>
<p>Weather Forecast Offices (WFO) of the National Weather Service outline warnings for tornadoes and severe thunderstorms in <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygonal</a> shapes for their map-based weather hazard products, based on the projected path of a storm at the time of the warning's issuance as estimated by Doppler radar. Warnings were issued on a per-county basis before October 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storm-based_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storm-based-11">[11]</a></sup> and they are now usually delineated on maps in polygon shapes and in text by a sections of counties, although entire <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a> are sometimes included, especially if the total area of the division is small. <a href="/wiki/Storm_Prediction_Center" title="Storm Prediction Center">Storm Prediction Center</a> and other NWS products, as well as severe weather alert displays used by some U.S. <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television stations</a>, highlight severe thunderstorm warnings with a yellow or orange polygon or filled county/<a href="/wiki/Parish_(administrative_division)" title="Parish (administrative division)">parish</a> outlines. Depending on the severity of the storm, some NWS offices may also request activation of the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Alert_System" title="Emergency Alert System">Emergency Alert System</a> at their discretion.</p>
