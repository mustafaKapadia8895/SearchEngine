<p><b>Snappers</b> are a family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perciform" title="Perciform">perciform</a> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <b>Lutjanidae</b>, mainly marine, but with some members inhabiting <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, feeding in fresh water. The family includes about 113 species. Some are important food fish. One of the best known is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_snapper_(fish)" title="Red snapper (fish)">red snapper</a>.</p>
<p>Snappers inhabit tropical and subtropical regions of all oceans. Some snappers grow up to about 1 m (3.3 ft) in length however one specific Snapper, the <a href="/wiki/Cubera_snapper" title="Cubera snapper">Cubera Snapper,</a> grows up to 5 ft in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most are active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivores" title="Carnivores">carnivores</a>, feeding on <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> or other fish,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishes-3">[3]</a></sup> though a few are <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>-feeders. They can be kept in <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a>, but mostly grow too fast to be popular aquarium fish. Most species live at depths reaching 100 m (330 ft) near coral reefs, but some species are found up to 500 m (1,600 ft) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishes_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-fishes-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>As other fish, snappers harbour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a>. A detailed study conducted in <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> has shown that <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a>-associated snappers harbour about 9 species of parasites per fish species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JustineBeveridge2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-JustineBeveridge2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
