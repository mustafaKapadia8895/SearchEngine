<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Simpson_(meteorologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>George Clarke Simpson was born in <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, England, the son of Arthur Simpson, a proprietor of a department store in East Street, and Alice Lambton Clarke.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Simpson_(meteorologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simpson was educated at <a href="/wiki/Derby_School" title="Derby School">Derby School</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Owens_College" title="Owens College">Owens College</a>, Manchester and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of Göttingen">University of Göttingen</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Simpson_(meteorologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1902 he visited Lapland to investigate atmospheric electricity. In 1905 he became the first person to lecture in meteorology at a British university when he was appointed lecturer at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a>. In 1906, he joined the Indian Meteorological Service as an Imperial Meteorologist at their headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Shimla" title="Shimla">Simla</a> and inspected many of the meteorological stations in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>. In 1910, he and his colleague <a href="/wiki/C._S._Wright" title="C. S. Wright">Charles Wright</a> were the meteorologists for <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">Robert Falcon Scott</a>'s Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_Expedition" title="Terra Nova Expedition">Terra Nova Expedition</a>. Given the nickname 'Sunny Jim' by the other expedition members, he constructed one of the continent's first weather stations, conducting balloon experiments to test the atmosphere and determine how altitude affects temperature. Simpson recorded the temperature and wind observations at the base camp at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Evans" title="Cape Evans">Cape Evans</a>. He also held command of this station for several months when Scott and his party left for the journey to the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> in November 1911.</p>
<p>Returning from Antarctica after the death of Scott and the other polar party members in August 1912, Simpson went back to Simla to re-join the 'Indian Meteorogical Services' which had its head office in Kolkata and its branch office in Simla. Whilst in Simla, George Simpson lived at Khud Cottage, today also known as Madan Kunj. It was here at Khud Cottage that Simpson compiled the notes he had made on the Antarctic exploration.</p>

<p>Currently this cottage belongs to Rajeev Madan, and it has been in his family since 1939. Khud Cottage was also the erstwhile residence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Burma" title="Governor of Burma">Governor of Burma</a>, during World War II when Burma was occupied by Japan.</p>
<p>When the First World War broke out in 1914, many officers of the Indian Meteorologist Department were drafted for military service. From March to May 1916, Simpson had to perform army service as the meteorological adviser to the British Expeditionary Force in Mesopotamia, which now forms part of southern Iraq. Later, he was called upon to serve as Assistant Secretary to the Board of Munitions and had to leave Simla again.</p>
<p>In 1920 he was appointed Director of the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Meteorological Office</a>, London. He went on to be its longest serving Director, retiring in 1938. During his tenure as Director, he was engaged in research work in the fields of atmospheric electricity, ionization, radioactivity and solar radiation. He investigated the causes of <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and in 1926 established the Simpson wind force scale, a modification of the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort wind force scale</a> which is the current standard scale used worldwide; still called the Beaufort wind force scale.</p>
<p>Simpson was knighted in 1935.</p>
<p>When the Second World War began in 1939, Dr Simpson, recalled from retirement into active service, was asked to take charge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kew_Observatory" title="Kew Observatory">Kew Observatory</a>. There he continued his research work on the electrical structure of thunderstorms until 1947.</p>
<p>Simpson was awarded honorary doctoral degrees by the universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sydney" title="University of Sydney">Sydney</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>.</p>
<p>Simpson died in 1965 at the age of 86.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Simpson_(meteorologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simpson married Dorothy Jane Stephen, daughter of Cecil Stephen, on 23 September 1914. They had four children: Scott Simpson (1915–1981), Professor of Geology at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Exeter" title="University of Exeter">Exeter University</a>; Arthur Simpson (British scientist specialised in the study of plants and sea animals); Oliver Simpson; and Jean Simpson.</p>
