<p><b>Sir Henry Mainwaring</b> (1587–1653), was an <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> lawyer, soldier, author, seaman and politician who sat in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_England" title="House of Commons of England">House of Commons</a> from 1621 to 1622. He was for a time a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">pirate</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and then a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_officer" title="Naval officer">naval officer</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. He supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavaliers" title="Cavaliers">Royalist</a> cause in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Mainwaring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mainwaring was born in <a href="/wiki/Ightfield" title="Ightfield">Ightfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, second son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Mainwaring_(knight)" title="George Mainwaring (knight)">Sir George Mainwaring</a> and his wife Ann, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/William_More_(died_1600)" title="William More (died 1600)">Sir William More</a> of <a href="/wiki/Loseley_Park" title="Loseley Park">Loseley Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>. His maternal grandfather was Sir William More, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-Admiral_of_Sussex" title="Vice-Admiral of Sussex">Vice-Admiral of Sussex</a>. He graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brasenose_College" title="Brasenose College">Brasenose College</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>, where he was awarded a B.A. in Law, at the age of 15, in 1602. He then served as trial lawyer (admitted in 1604 as a student at <a href="/wiki/Inner_Temple" title="Inner Temple">Inner Temple</a>), soldier (possibly in the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>), sailor, and author (pupil of <a href="/wiki/John_Davies_of_Hereford" title="John Davies of Hereford">John Davies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hereford" title="Hereford">Hereford</a>) before turning to <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="From_pirate-hunter_to_pirate">From pirate-hunter to pirate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Mainwaring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: From pirate-hunter to pirate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1610, at the age of 24, Mainwaring was given a commission from <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Lord High Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> to capture the notorious <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> "arch-pirate" <a href="/wiki/Peter_Easton" title="Peter Easton">Peter Easton</a>, then feared to be hovering around the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a>. This may have been just a convenient excuse for the well-armed <i>Resistance</i>, his small but speedy ship, to become a scourge to the Spanish.</p>
<p>On reaching the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits" title="Straits">Straits</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, Mainwaring announced to his crew his intention of fighting the Spanish anywhere he found them. Turning to piracy was not that out of line for the young valiant in those years.</p>
<p>In 1614 he sailed his fleet to Newfoundland, saying that the region was the best in which to recruit a pirate crew and reprovision his ships. Mainwaring used Easton's old base at <a href="/wiki/Harbour_Grace" title="Harbour Grace">Harbour Grace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, as his pirate base and raided <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> ships.</p>
<p>On 4 June 1614, off the coast of Newfoundland, Mainwaring, in command of eight vessels, plundered the <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>-fishing fleet, stealing provisions and taking away with him carpenters and mariners. In taking seamen, Mainwaring would pick one out of every six. In all, 400 men joined him willingly, while others were <a href="/wiki/Impressment" title="Impressment">perforstmen</a>. Sailing to the coast of Spain, Mainwaring then took a Portuguese ship and plundered her cargo of wine, and he later took a French prize and stole 10,000 dried fish from her hold.</p>
<p>With Mainwaring away from his main base in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Mamora" title="La Mamora">La Mamora</a>, on Atlantic coast of present-day <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, a Spanish fleet under Don <a href="/wiki/Luis_Fajardo_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Luis Fajardo de Córdoba">Luis Fajardo</a>, sailing from <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">Cádiz</a> on 1 August 1614, reduced the town. Mainwaring's relations with the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a> were such that he was able to secure the release of their English prisoners.</p>
<p>So feared was his pirate fleet that Spain offered Mainwaring a pardon and high command in return for his services under the Spanish flag.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pardon_and_service_in_the_Royal_Navy">Pardon and service in the Royal Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Mainwaring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pardon and service in the Royal Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When his pirate activities almost broke the tenuous peace between England, Spain and Portugal, King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I</a> threatened to send a fleet after Mainwaring, to whom he later granted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_pardon" title="Royal pardon">royal pardon</a> in 1616 for having saved the Newfoundland trading fleet near <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>.</p>
<p>Mainwaring wrote a book on piracy (<i>Discourse of Pirates</i>, on the suppression of piracy, 1618), the manuscript of which is in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>. In his book, he explains what causes a desperate man to turn to piracy. He also advises the King against granting pardons to pirates. The King promptly dispatched Mainwaring to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetian_Republic" title="Venetian Republic">Venetian Republic</a> as his representative, over the protests of the Spanish ambassador.</p>
<p>Mainwaring was knighted at <a href="/wiki/Woking" title="Woking">Woking</a> on 20 March 1618.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was commissioned in the Royal Navy. In 1621, he was elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> from <a href="/wiki/Dover_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Dover (UK Parliament constituency)">Dover</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willis-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mainwaring became vice-admiral before leaving the navy in 1639. As a Royalist, he served in the King's cause in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, was exiled to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and died in poverty. He was buried at <a href="/wiki/St_Giles%27_Church,_Camberwell" title="St Giles' Church, Camberwell">St. Giles' Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camberwell" title="Camberwell">Camberwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, on 15 May 1653.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Mainwaring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mainwaring married a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gardiner_(Royalist)" title="Thomas Gardiner (Royalist)">Sir Thomas Gardiner</a> in 1630. She died in 1633. His brothers were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Mainwaring_(courtier)" title="Arthur Mainwaring (courtier)">Sir Arthur Mainwaring</a>, Carver to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Frederick,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Henry Frederick, Prince of Wales">Prince Henry</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mainwaring_(Royalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Mainwaring (Royalist) (page does not exist)">George Mainwaring</a>, the defender of <a href="/wiki/Tong_Castle" title="Tong Castle">Tong Castle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mainwaring" title="Thomas Mainwaring">Sir Thomas Mainwaring</a>, the Recorder of <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mainwaring" title="Mainwaring">Mainwaring</a> family was old and distinguished, probably having arrived in England in the train of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> (1066).</p>
