<p>The <b>1821 Norfolk and Long Island Hurricane</b> was one of four known tropical cyclones that have made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in New York City. Another, even more intense hurricane in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> times (sometime between 1278 and 1438) left evidence that was detected in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_New_Jersey" title="Southern New Jersey">southern New Jersey</a> by <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">paleotempestological</a> research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sediment_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sediment-2">[2]</a></sup> The third was the <a href="/wiki/1893_New_York_hurricane" title="1893 New York hurricane">1893 New York hurricane</a>, and the fourth was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene" title="Hurricane Irene">Hurricane Irene</a> in 2011.</p>
<p>The first of three recorded <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> recorded in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1820-1829_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons" title="1820-1829 Atlantic hurricane seasons">1821 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, the storm that would eventually strike New York was first observed off the southeast United States coast on September 1, with winds estimated in excess of 135 mph (215 km/h). It moved ashore near <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>, and passed near <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a> before moving through the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> just inland. On September 3, the hurricane struck approximately near <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Bay" title="Jamaica Bay">Jamaica Bay</a>, which later became part of New York City, and on September 4 it was observed over <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. This was just 6 years after the destructive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_September_Gale_of_1815" title="Great September Gale of 1815">Great September Gale of 1815</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1821_Norfolk_and_Long_Island_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> was first observed on September 1 off the southeast coast of the United States. Initially, it was believed to be the same storm that struck <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on the same day, though subsequent research indicated there were two separate storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenoweth_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-chenoweth-3">[3]</a></sup> The hurricane tracked by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> while tracking generally northward, and it attained <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane</a> status over the western Atlantic Ocean. As it approached the United States coastline, the hurricane was very intense, with winds estimated at over 135 mph (215 km/h) and potentially as strong as 160 mph (260 km/h), or a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garnet_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-garnet-4">[4]</a></sup> Late on September 2, the hurricane made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a> and later turned to the northeast to cross the <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_Sound" title="Pamlico Sound">Pamlico Sound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hurricane accelerated northeastward, and passed over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach-Norfolk-Newport_News,_VA-NC_MSA" title="Virginia Beach-Norfolk-Newport News, VA-NC MSA">Hampton Roads</a> area early on September 3. After crossing the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>, the cyclone traversed the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a> near the Atlantic coastline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-5">[5]</a></sup> and at around 1500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> passed directly over <a href="/wiki/Cape_Henlopen" title="Cape Henlopen">Cape Henlopen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>; a thirty-minute period of calm was reported. It continued across the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a> and later passed over <a href="/wiki/Cape_May,_New_Jersey" title="Cape May, New Jersey">Cape May, New Jersey</a>, where a fifteen-minute calm was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum1-6">[6]</a></sup> Modern researchers estimate it was a Category 3 or Category 4 hurricane upon striking New Jersey, one of the few hurricanes to <a href="/wiki/List_of_New_Jersey_hurricanes" title="List of New Jersey hurricanes">hit the state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cat3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cat3-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cat4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-cat4-8">[8]</a></sup> Moving ashore at very low tide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sediment_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sediment-2">[2]</a></sup> it paralleled the state's coastline just inland, and after exiting into <a href="/wiki/Lower_New_York_Bay" title="Lower New York Bay">Lower New York Bay</a> the hurricane made landfall on New York City at around 1930 UTC on September 3; this makes it the only major hurricane to directly hit the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ho2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ho2-1">[1]</a></sup> A minimal <a href="/wiki/1893_New_York_hurricane" title="1893 New York hurricane">hurricane in 1893</a> also made landfall on what later became part of New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> One modern researcher estimates the hurricane was moving at a forward speed of 35 mph (55 km/h), and upon moving ashore had a pressure of 965 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ho2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ho2-1">[1]</a></sup> The hurricane continued northeastward through <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, and after entering <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> on September 4 its exact path was unknown;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum3-10">[10]</a></sup> one researcher estimated the cyclone tracked northeastward until losing its identity over southeastern <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-5">[5]</a></sup> while another assessed the storm as passing far to the west of Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cotterly_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cotterly-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on the arrangement of effects in New England, meteorologist <a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Redfield" title="William Charles Redfield">William C. Redfield</a> deduced that the wind field and center of tropical cyclones are circular; previously the winds were believed to be in a straight line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cotterly_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-cotterly-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1821_Norfolk_and_Long_Island_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>The continuous cataracts of rain swept impetuously along, darkening the expanse of vision and apparently confounding the heaven, earth and seas in a general chaos</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><i>The Norfolk Herald</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, a powerful <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> flooded large portions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Island,_North_Carolina" title="Portsmouth Island, North Carolina">Portsmouth Island</a>; residents estimated the island would have been completely under water had the worst of the storm lasted for two more hours. Strong winds occurred across eastern North Carolina, resulting in at least 76 destroyed houses. Numerous people were killed in <a href="/wiki/Currituck,_North_Carolina" title="Currituck, North Carolina">Currituck</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The strongest winds of the hurricane lasted for about an hour in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, after which the storm rapidly abated. Several houses were completely destroyed, with many others receiving moderate to severe damage. The winds destroyed most of the roof of the courthouse, and uprooted trees across the region; fallen tree limbs damaged a stone bridge in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a>. The hurricane produced a strong storm surge along the Virginia coastline, which reached 10 feet (3 m) at <a href="/wiki/Accomack_County,_Virginia" title="Accomack County, Virginia">Pungoteague</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a>. The storm surge, which reached several hundred yards inland, destroyed two bridges and flooded many warehouses along the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_River_(Virginia)" title="Elizabeth River (Virginia)">Elizabeth River</a>. Rough waves grounded the <a href="/wiki/USS_Guerriere_(1814)" title="USS Guerriere (1814)">USS <i>Guerriere</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/USS_Congress_(1799)" title="USS Congress (1799)">USS <i>Congress</i></a>, and also destroyed several schooners and brigs. Along the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_of_Virginia" title="Eastern Shore of Virginia">eastern shore</a>, the storm surge flooded barrier islands along the Atlantic coastline, causing severe crop damage and downing many trees. Several houses were destroyed, and at Pungoteague the impact of the hurricane was described as "unexampled destruction"; five people drowned in <a href="/wiki/Chincoteague,_Virginia" title="Chincoteague, Virginia">Chincoteague</a>. Considered one of the most violent hurricanes on record in the Mid-Atlantic, the hurricane caused $200,000 in damage in Virginia (1821 USD, $3.1 million 2007 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gale force winds affected the Delmarva Peninsula; on <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Maryland" title="List of islands of Maryland">Poplar Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Talbot_County,_Maryland" title="Talbot County, Maryland">Talbot County, Maryland</a>, winds peaked at 1600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> on September 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum1-6">[6]</a></sup> The strongest winds were confined to the Atlantic coastline, with outer <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">rainbands</a> producing heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore, Maryland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum2-12">[12]</a></sup> Fierce winds were observed in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Henlopen" title="Cape Henlopen">Cape Henlopen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, with the strongest gales occurring after the eye passed over the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum1_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon making landfall on <a href="/wiki/Cape_May,_New_Jersey" title="Cape May, New Jersey">Cape May, New Jersey</a>, the cyclone produced a 5 foot (1.5 m) storm surge on the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a> side of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum2-12">[12]</a></sup> Lasting for several hours, the hurricane-force winds were described as "[blowing] with great violence",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum1_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum1-6">[6]</a></sup> causing widespread devastation across the region. In <a href="/wiki/Little_Egg_Harbor" title="Little Egg Harbor">Little Egg Harbor</a>, its passage caused damage to the port. Strong winds reached as far inland as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia, Pennsylvania</a>, where winds of over 40 mph (60 km/h) downed trees and chimneys; in the city, precipitation accrued to 3.92 inches (99.6 mm). Further to the north, the hurricane destroyed a windmill at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bergen_Point,_New_Jersey" title="Bergen Point, New Jersey">Bergen Point, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum2_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum2-12">[12]</a></sup> Though the hurricane struck at low tide, it produced a storm surge of over 29 feet (9 m) along several portions of the New Jersey coastline, causing significant <a href="/wiki/Overwash" title="Overwash">overwash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sediment_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-sediment-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hurricane produced a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of 13 feet (4 m) in only one hour at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a>, a record only broken 191 years later by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manhattan_Island" title="Manhattan Island">Manhattan Island</a> was completely flooded to <a href="/wiki/Canal_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Canal Street (Manhattan)">Canal Street</a>; one hurricane researcher remarked that the storm surge flooding would have been much worse, had the hurricane not struck at low tide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, few deaths were reported in the city, since the flooding affected neighborhoods much less populated than today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The hurricane brought light rainfall as it passed New York City, though strong winds left severe damage across the city. High tides occurred along the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>. Strong waves and winds blew many ships ashore along <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>. One ship sank, killing 17 people. Along Long Island, the winds destroyed several buildings and left crops destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum2_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, the hurricane produced widespread gale force winds, with damage greatest in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum3-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Black_Rock_Harbor_Light" title="Black Rock Harbor Light">Black Rock Harbor Light</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Rock_(Bridgeport)" title="Black Rock (Bridgeport)">Black Rock</a>, Connecticut, was destroyed on September 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum2_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum2-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dentremont_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dentremont-15">[15]</a></sup> Elsewhere in the state, the winds damaged or destroyed churches, houses and small buildings. Moderate crop damage to fruit was reported as well. Strong winds extended into eastern <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, though little damage was reported in the <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ludlum3_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-ludlum3-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
