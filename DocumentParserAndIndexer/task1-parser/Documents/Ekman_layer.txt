<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ekman layer</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

The Ekman layer is the layer in a fluid where the flow is the result of a balance between pressure gradient, Coriolis and turbulent drag forces. In the picture above, the wind blowing North creates a surface stress and a resulting <a href="/wiki/Ekman_spiral" title="Ekman spiral">Ekman spiral</a> is found below it in the column of water.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Ekman layer</b> is the layer in a <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> where there is a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> balance between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">turbulent drag</a>. It was first described by <a href="/wiki/Vagn_Walfrid_Ekman" title="Vagn Walfrid Ekman">Vagn Walfrid Ekman</a>. Ekman layers occur both in the atmosphere and in the ocean.</p>
<p>There are two types of Ekman layers. The first type occurs at the surface of the ocean and is forced by surface winds, which act as a drag on the surface of the ocean. The second type occurs at the bottom of the atmosphere and ocean, where frictional forces are associated with flow over rough surfaces.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ekman developed the theory of the Ekman layer after <a href="/wiki/Fridtjof_Nansen" title="Fridtjof Nansen">Fridtjof Nansen</a> observed that <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">ice</a> drifts at an angle of 20°-40° to the right of the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">prevailing wind</a> direction while on an <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> expedition aboard the <a href="/wiki/Fram" title="Fram">Fram</a>. Nansen asked his colleague, <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Bjerknes" title="Vilhelm Bjerknes">Vilhelm Bjerknes</a> to set one of his students upon study of the problem. Bjerknes tapped Ekman, who presented his results in 1902 as his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctoral_thesis" title="Doctoral thesis">doctoral thesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>There are many regions where an Ekman layer is theoretically plausible; they include the bottom of the atmosphere, near the surface of the earth and ocean, the bottom of the ocean, near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a> and at the top of the ocean, near the air-water interface. Different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_conditions" title="Boundary conditions">boundary conditions</a> are appropriate for each of these different situations. Each of these situations can be accounted for through the boundary conditions applied to the resulting system of ordinary differential equations. The separate cases of top and bottom boundary layers are shown below.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ekman_layer_at_the_bottom_of_the_ocean_and_atmosphere">Ekman layer at the bottom of the ocean and atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ekman layer at the bottom of the ocean and atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The traditional development of Ekman layers bounded below by a surface utilizes two boundary conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/No-slip_condition" title="No-slip condition">no-slip condition</a> at the surface;</li>
<li>The Ekman velocities approaching the geostrophic velocities as <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>z</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle z}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="z" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.099ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span> goes to infinity.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_observations_of_the_Ekman_layer">Experimental observations of the Ekman layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Experimental observations of the Ekman layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is much difficulty associated with observing the Ekman layer for two main reasons: the theory is too simplistic as it assumes a constant eddy viscosity, which Ekman himself anticipated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ekman-3">[3]</a></sup> saying</p>

<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">It is obvious that <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<mo>]</mo>

<p>and because it is difficult to design instruments with great enough sensitivity to observe the velocity profile in the ocean.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_demonstrations">Laboratory demonstrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Laboratory demonstrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bottom Ekman layer can readily be observed in a rotating cylindrical tank of water by dropping in dye and changing the rotation rate slightly.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://paoc.mit.edu/labguide/ekman.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> Surface Ekman layers can also be observed in rotating tanks.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://dennou-k.gaia.h.kyoto-u.ac.jp/library/gfd_exp/exp_e/exp/ek/1/app.htm" rel="nofollow">[2]</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_atmosphere">In the atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In the atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the atmosphere, the Ekman solution generally overstates the magnitude of the horizontal wind field because it does not account for the velocity shear in the <a href="/wiki/Surface_layer" title="Surface layer">surface layer</a>. Splitting the boundary layer into the surface layer and the Ekman layer generally yields more accurate results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holton-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_ocean">In the ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In the ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ekman layer, with its distinguishing feature the Ekman spiral, is rarely observed in the ocean. The Ekman layer near the surface of the ocean extends only about 10 – 20 meters deep,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holton_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holton-4">[4]</a></sup> and instrumentation sensitive enough to observe a velocity profile in such a shallow depth has only been available since around 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vallis_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vallis-2">[2]</a></sup> Also, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_waves" title="Wind waves">wind waves</a> modify the flow near the surface, and make observations close to the surface rather difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Santala_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Santala-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Observations of the Ekman layer have only been possible since the development of robust surface moorings and sensitive current meters. Ekman himself developed a current meter to observe the spiral that bears his name, but was not successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rudnick_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rudnick-6">[6]</a></sup> The Vector Measuring Current Meter <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weller_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weller-7">[7]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustic_Doppler_Current_Profiler" title="Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler">Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler</a> are both used to measure current.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ekman_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first documented observations of an Ekman-like spiral in the ocean were made in the Arctic Ocean from a drifting ice flow in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunkins_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunkins-8">[8]</a></sup> More recent observations include (not an exhaustive list):</p>
<ul>
<li>The 1980 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mixed_Layer_Experiment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mixed Layer Experiment (page does not exist)">Mixed Layer Experiment</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Within the Sargasso Sea during the 1982 Long Term Upper Ocean Study <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Price_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Price-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Within the California Current during the 1993 Eastern Boundary Current experiment <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chereskin_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chereskin-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Within the Drake Passage region of the Southern Ocean <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenn_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenn-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the eastern tropical Pacific, at 2°N, 140°W, using 5 current meters between 5 and 25 meters depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cronin_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-cronin-13">[13]</a></sup> This study noted that the geostrophic shear associated with tropical stability waves modified the Ekman spiral relative to what is expected with horizontally uniform density.</li>
<li>North of the Kerguelan Plateau during the 2008 SOFINE experiment <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roach_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roach-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Common to several of these observations spirals were found to be 'compressed', displaying larger estimates of eddy viscosity when considering the rate of rotation with depth than the eddy viscosity derived from considering the rate of decay of speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Price_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Price-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chereskin_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chereskin-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenn_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lenn-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roach_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roach-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
