<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Daniel Defoe</h1>

<p><b>Daniel Defoe</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; c. 1660 – 24 April 1731),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> born <b>Daniel Foe</b>, was an English trader, writer, journalist, <a href="/wiki/Pamphleteer" title="Pamphleteer">pamphleteer</a>, and spy. He is most famous for his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a></i>, which is second only to the Bible in its number of translations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Defoe is noted for being one of the earliest proponents of the novel, as he helped to popularise the form in Britain with others such as <a href="/wiki/Aphra_Behn" title="Aphra Behn">Aphra Behn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Richardson" title="Samuel Richardson">Samuel Richardson</a>, and is among the founders of the <a href="/wiki/English_novel" title="English novel">English novel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Defoe wrote many political tracts and often was in trouble with the authorities, including prison time. Intellectuals and political leaders paid attention to his fresh ideas and sometimes consulted with him.</p>
<p>Defoe was a prolific and versatile writer, producing more than three hundred works<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> – books, pamphlets, and journals – on diverse topics, including politics, crime, religion, marriage, psychology, and the supernatural. He was also a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Business_journalism" title="Business journalism">business journalism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and economic journalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letters_to_John_Law_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Letters_to_John_Law-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Daniel Foe (his original name) was probably born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fore_Street_(London)" title="Fore Street (London)">Fore Street</a> in the parish of <a href="/wiki/St_Giles-without-Cripplegate" title="St Giles-without-Cripplegate">St. Giles</a> <a href="/wiki/Cripplegate" title="Cripplegate">Cripplegate</a>, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncylLon_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EncylLon-7">[7]</a></sup> Defoe later added the aristocratic-sounding "De" to his name, and on occasion claimed descent from the family of De Beau Faux. His birthdate and birthplace are uncertain, and sources offer dates from 1659 to 1662, with the summer or early autumn of 1660 considered the most likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His father James Foe was a prosperous <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">tallow</a> <a href="/wiki/Chandlery" title="Chandlery">chandler</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Butchers" title="Worshipful Company of Butchers">Worshipful Company of Butchers</a>. In Defoe's early life, he experienced some of the most unusual occurrences in English history: in 1665, 70,000 were killed by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_London" title="Great Plague of London">Great Plague of London</a>, and next year, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a> left standing only Defoe's and two other houses in his neighbourhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-west-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1667, when he was probably about seven, a Dutch fleet sailed up the <a href="/wiki/River_Medway" title="River Medway">Medway</a> via the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and attacked the town of <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_Kent" title="Chatham, Kent">Chatham</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_the_Medway" title="Raid on the Medway">raid on the Medway</a>. His mother Annie had died by the time he was about ten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Defoe was educated at the Rev. James Fisher's boarding school in Pixham Lane in <a href="/wiki/Dorking" title="Dorking">Dorking</a>, Surrey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His parents were <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterian</a> <a href="/wiki/English_Dissenters" title="English Dissenters">dissenters</a>, and around the age of 14, he attended a <a href="/wiki/Dissenting_academies" title="Dissenting academies">dissenting academy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Newington_Green" title="Newington Green">Newington Green</a> in London run by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Morton_(educator)" title="Charles Morton (educator)">Charles Morton</a>, and he is believed to have attended the <a href="/wiki/Newington_Green_Unitarian_Church" title="Newington Green Unitarian Church">Newington Green Unitarian Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> During this period, the English government persecuted those who chose to worship outside the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_career">Business career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Business career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Defoe entered the world of business as a general merchant, dealing at different times in hosiery, general woollen goods, and wine. His ambitions were great and he was able to buy a country estate and a ship (as well as <a href="/wiki/Civet" title="Civet">civets</a> to make perfume), though he was rarely out of debt. He was forced to declare bankruptcy in 1692.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> On 1 January 1684, Defoe married Mary Tuffley at St Botolph, Aldgate. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novak_2001_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Novak_2001-15">[15]</a></sup> She was the daughter of a London merchant, receiving a dowry of £3,700 – a huge amount by the standards of the day. With his debts and political difficulties, the marriage may have been troubled, but it lasted 50 years and produced eight children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1685, Defoe joined the ill-fated <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Rebellion" title="Monmouth Rebellion">Monmouth Rebellion</a> but gained a pardon, by which he escaped the <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Assizes" title="Bloody Assizes">Bloody Assizes</a> of Judge <a href="/wiki/George_Jeffreys,_1st_Baron_Jeffreys" title="George Jeffreys, 1st Baron Jeffreys">George Jeffreys</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mary_II_of_England" title="Mary II of England">Queen Mary</a> and her husband <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> were jointly crowned in 1688, and Defoe became one of William's close allies and a secret agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup> Some of the new policies led to conflict with France, thus damaging prosperous trade relationships for Defoe, who had established himself as a merchant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1692, Defoe was arrested for debts of £700 , though his total debts may have amounted to £17,000. His laments were loud and he always defended unfortunate debtors, but there is evidence that his financial dealings were not always honest<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. He died with little wealth and evidence of lawsuits with the royal treasury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his release, he probably traveled in Europe and Scotland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Backscheider_1989_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Backscheider_1989-16">[16]</a></sup> and it may have been at this time that he traded wine to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">Cadiz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>. By 1695, he was back in England, now formally using the name "Defoe" and serving as a "commissioner of the glass duty", responsible for collecting taxes on bottles. In 1696, he ran a tile and brick factory in what is now <a href="/wiki/Tilbury" title="Tilbury">Tilbury</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Essex" title="History of Essex">Essex</a> and lived in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Chadwell_St_Mary" title="Chadwell St Mary">Chadwell St Mary</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pamphleteering_and_prison">Pamphleteering and prison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pamphleteering and prison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2018" data-file-width="2400" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg/220px-Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg/330px-Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg/440px-Daniel_Defoe_by_James_Charles_Armytage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

</div>
<p>Defoe's first notable publication was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An_essay_upon_projects" title="An essay upon projects">An essay upon projects</a></i>, a series of proposals for social and economic improvement, published in 1697. From 1697 to 1698, he defended the right of King <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Standing_army" title="Standing army">standing army</a> during disarmament, after the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Ryswick" title="Treaty of Ryswick">Treaty of Ryswick</a> (1697) had ended the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War" title="Nine Years' War">Nine Years' War</a> (1688–97). His most successful poem, <i><a href="/wiki/The_True-Born_Englishman" title="The True-Born Englishman">The True-Born Englishman</a></i> (1701), defended the king against the perceived xenophobia of his enemies, satirising the English claim to racial purity. In 1701, Defoe presented the <i>Legion's Memorial</i> to the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" title="Speaker of the House of Commons (United Kingdom)">Speaker of the House of Commons</a>, later his employer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Harley,_1st_Earl_of_Oxford_and_Earl_Mortimer" title="Robert Harley, 1st Earl of Oxford and Earl Mortimer">Robert Harley</a>, flanked by a guard of sixteen gentlemen of quality. It demanded the release of the Kentish petitioners, who had asked Parliament to support the king in an imminent war against France.</p>
<p>The death of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> in 1702 once again created a political upheaval, as the king was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> who immediately began her offensive against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconformism" title="Nonconformism">Nonconformists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup> Defoe was a natural target, and his pamphleteering and political activities resulted in his arrest and placement in a <a href="/wiki/Pillory" title="Pillory">pillory</a> on 31 July 1703, principally on account of his December 1702 pamphlet entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shortest_Way_with_the_Dissenters" title="The Shortest Way with the Dissenters">The Shortest-Way with the Dissenters; Or, Proposals for the Establishment of the Church</a></i>, purporting to argue for their extermination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In it, he ruthlessly satirised both the <a href="/wiki/High_church" title="High church">High church</a> <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tories</a> and those <a href="/wiki/Dissenter" title="Dissenter">Dissenters</a> who hypocritically practised so-called "occasional conformity", such as his <a href="/wiki/Stoke_Newington" title="Stoke Newington">Stoke Newington</a> neighbour Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Abney" title="Thomas Abney">Thomas Abney</a>. It was published anonymously, but the true authorship was quickly discovered and Defoe was arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup> He was charged with seditious libel. Defoe was found guilty after a trial at the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a> in front of the notoriously sadistic judge <a href="/wiki/Salathiel_Lovell" title="Salathiel Lovell">Salathiel Lovell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letters_to_John_Law_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Letters_to_John_Law-6">[6]</a></sup> Lovell sentenced him to a punitive fine, to public humiliation in a pillory, and to an indeterminate length of imprisonment which would only end upon the discharge of the punitive fine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letters_to_John_Law_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Letters_to_John_Law-6">[6]</a></sup> According to legend, the publication of his poem <i>Hymn to the Pillory</i> caused his audience at the pillory to throw flowers instead of the customary harmful and noxious objects and to drink to his health. The truth of this story is questioned by most scholars, although <a href="/wiki/John_Robert_Moore" title="John Robert Moore">John Robert Moore</a> later said that "no man in England but Defoe ever stood in the pillory and later rose to eminence among his fellow men".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-rich-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>"Wherever God erects a house of prayer<br/>
the Devil always builds a chapel there;<br/>
And 't will be found, upon examination,<br/>
the latter has the largest congregation."</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">– Defoe's <i>The True-Born Englishman</i>, 1701</cite></div>
<p>After his three days in the pillory, Defoe went into <a href="/wiki/Newgate_Prison" title="Newgate Prison">Newgate Prison</a>. Robert Harley, 1st <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Oxford_and_Earl_Mortimer" title="Earl of Oxford and Earl Mortimer">Earl of Oxford and Earl Mortimer</a>, brokered his release in exchange for Defoe's co-operation as an intelligence agent for the Tories. In exchange for such co-operation with the rival political side, Harley paid some of Defoe's outstanding debts, improving his financial situation considerably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup> Within a week of his release from prison, Defoe witnessed the <a href="/wiki/Great_Storm_of_1703" title="Great Storm of 1703">Great Storm of 1703</a>, which raged through the night of 26/27 November. It caused severe damage to London and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, uprooted millions of trees, and killed more than 8,000 people, mostly at sea. The event became the subject of Defoe's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Storm_(Daniel_Defoe)" title="The Storm (Daniel Defoe)">The Storm</a></i> (1704), which includes a collection of witness accounts of the tempest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Many regard it as one of the world's first examples of modern journalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In the same year, he set up his <a href="/wiki/Periodical_literature" title="Periodical literature">periodical</a> <i>A Review of the Affairs of France</i> which supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harley_Ministry" title="Harley Ministry">Harley Ministry</a>, chronicling the events of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> (1702–1714). The <i>Review</i> ran three times a week without interruption until 1713. Defoe was amazed that a man as gifted as Harley left vital state papers lying in the open, and warned that he was almost inviting an unscrupulous clerk to commit treason; his warnings were fully justified by the <a href="/wiki/William_Gregg_(clerk_and_spy)" title="William Gregg (clerk and spy)">William Gregg</a> affair. When Harley was ousted from the ministry in 1708, Defoe continued writing the <i>Review</i> to support <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin" title="Sidney Godolphin, 1st Earl of Godolphin">Godolphin</a>, then again to support Harley and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tory_(British_political_party)" title="Tory (British political party)">Tories</a> in the Tory ministry of 1710–1714. The Tories fell from power with the death of <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a>, but Defoe continued doing intelligence work for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whig_(British_political_party)" title="Whig (British political party)">Whig</a> government, writing "Tory" pamphlets that undermined the Tory point of view.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not all of Defoe's pamphlet writing was political. One pamphlet was originally published anonymously, entitled "<i>A True Relation of the Apparition of One Mrs. Veal the Next Day after her Death to One Mrs. Bargrave at Canterbury the 8th of September, 1705</i>." It deals with interaction between the spiritual realm and the physical realm and was most likely written in support of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Drelincourt" title="Charles Drelincourt">Charles Drelincourt</a>'s <i>The Christian Defense against the Fears of Death</i> (1651). It describes Mrs. Bargrave's encounter with her old friend Mrs. Veal after she had died. It is clear from this piece and other writings that the political portion of Defoe's life was by no means his only focus.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Scottish_Union_of_1707">Anglo-Scottish Union of 1707</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anglo-Scottish Union of 1707">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>As many as 545 titles have been ascribed to Defoe, ranging from satirical poems, political and religious pamphlets, and volumes. (<a href="/wiki/P._N._Furbank" title="P. N. Furbank">Furbank</a> and Owens argue for the much smaller number of 276 published items in <i>Critical Bibliography</i> (1998).) His ambitious business ventures saw him bankrupt by 1692, with a wife and seven children to support. In 1703, he published a satirical pamphlet against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Tories" title="High Tories">High Tories</a> and in favour of <a href="/wiki/Toleration" title="Toleration">religious tolerance</a> entitled <i>The Shortest-Way with the Dissenters; Or, Proposals for the Establishment of the Church</i>. This pamphlet was widely misunderstood, as has happened with other ironic writings since, but eventually its author was prosecuted for <a href="/wiki/Seditious_libel" title="Seditious libel">seditious libel</a> and was sentenced to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilloried" title="Pilloried">pilloried</a>, fined 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_(money)" title="Mark (money)">marks</a>, and detained at the Queen's pleasure.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG/220px-Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG/330px-Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG/440px-Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Defoe_1709_The_History_Of_The_Union_Of_Great_Britain.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title page from Daniel Defoe's: <i>The History of the Union of Great Britain</i> dated 1709 and printed in Edinburgh by the Heirs of Anderson</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In despair, he wrote to <a href="/wiki/William_Paterson_(banker)" title="William Paterson (banker)">William Paterson</a>, the London Scot and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a> and part instigator of the <a href="/wiki/Darien_scheme" title="Darien scheme">Darien scheme</a>, who was in the confidence of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Harley,_1st_Earl_of_Oxford_and_Earl_Mortimer" title="Robert Harley, 1st Earl of Oxford and Earl Mortimer">Robert Harley, 1st Earl of Oxford and Earl Mortimer</a>, leading minister and spymaster in the <a href="/wiki/Governance_of_England" title="Governance of England">English Government</a>. Harley accepted Defoe's services and released him in 1703. He immediately published <i>The Review</i>, which appeared weekly, then three times a week, written mostly by himself. This was the main mouthpiece of the English Government promoting the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Act of Union 1707</a>.</p>
<p>In 1709, Defoe authored a rather lengthy book entitled <i>The History of the Union of Great Britain</i>, an Edinburgh publication printed by the Heirs of Anderson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The book was not published anonymously and cites Defoe twice as being its author.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The book attempts to explain the facts leading up to the Act of Union 1707, dating all the way back to 6 December 1604 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">King James</a> was presented with a proposal for unification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This so-called "first draft" for unification took place 100 years before the signing of the 1707 accord, which respectively preceded the commencement of <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> by another ten years.</p>
<p>Defoe began his campaign in <i>The Review</i> and other pamphlets aimed at English opinion, claiming that it would end the threat from the north, gaining for the Treasury an "inexhaustible treasury of men", a valuable new market increasing the power of England. By September 1706, Harley ordered Defoe to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> as a secret agent to do everything possible to help secure acquiescence in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a>. He was conscious of the risk to himself. Thanks to books such as <i>The Letters of Daniel Defoe</i> (edited by G. H. Healey, Oxford 1955), far more is known about his activities than is usual with such agents.</p>
<p>His first reports included vivid descriptions of violent demonstrations against the Union. "A Scots rabble is the worst of its kind", he reported. Years later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Clerk_of_Penicuik" title="John Clerk of Penicuik">John Clerk of Penicuik</a>, a leading Unionist, wrote in his memoirs that it was not known at the time that Defoe had been sent by Godolphin :</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>... to give a faithful account to him from time to time how everything past here. He was therefor a spy among us, but not known to be such, otherways the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mob" title="wiktionary:mob">Mob</a> of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edin.</a> had pull him to pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Defoe was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> who had suffered in England for his convictions, and as such he was accepted as an adviser to the <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_the_Church_of_Scotland" title="General Assembly of the Church of Scotland">General Assembly of the Church of Scotland</a> and committees of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a>. He told Harley that he was "privy to all their folly" but "Perfectly unsuspected as with corresponding with anybody in England". He was then able to influence the proposals that were put to Parliament and reported,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Having had the honour to be always sent for the committee to whom these amendments were referrèd,<br/>
I have had the good fortune to break their measures in two particulars via the bounty on Corn and<br/>
proportion of the Excise.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>For Scotland, he used different arguments, even the opposite of those which he used in England, usually ignoring the English doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">Sovereignty of Parliament</a>, for example, telling the Scots that they could have complete confidence in the guarantees in the Treaty. Some of his pamphlets were purported to be written by Scots, misleading even reputable historians into quoting them as evidence of Scottish opinion of the time. The same is true of a massive history of the Union which Defoe published in 1709 and which some historians still treat as a valuable contemporary source for their own works. Defoe took pains to give his history an air of objectivity by giving some space to arguments against the Union but always having the last word for himself.</p>
<p>He disposed of the main Union opponent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrew_Fletcher_(politician)" title="Andrew Fletcher (politician)">Andrew Fletcher</a> of <a href="/wiki/East_Saltoun_and_West_Saltoun" title="East Saltoun and West Saltoun">Saltoun</a>, by ignoring him. Nor does he account for the deviousness of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Hamilton" title="Duke of Hamilton">Duke of Hamilton</a>, the official leader of the various factions opposed to the Union, who seemingly betrayed his former colleagues when he switched to the Unionist/Government side in the decisive final stages of the debate.</p>
<p>Defoe made no attempt to explain why the same Parliament of Scotland which was so vehement for its independence from 1703 to 1705 became so supine in 1706. He received very little reward from his paymasters and of course no recognition for his services by the government. He made use of his Scottish experience to write his <i>Tour thro' the whole Island of Great Britain</i>, published in 1726, where he admitted that the increase of trade and population in Scotland which he had predicted as a consequence of the Union was "not the case, but rather the contrary".</p>

<p>Defoe's description of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> (Glaschu) as a "Dear Green Place" has often been misquoted as a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a> translation for the town's name. The Gaelic <i>Glas</i> could mean grey or green, while <i>chu</i> means dog or hollow. <i>Glaschu</i> probably means "Green Hollow". The "Dear Green Place", like much of Scotland, was a hotbed of unrest against the Union. The local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_George%27s-Tron_Church" title="St George's-Tron Church">Tron</a> <a href="/wiki/Minister_(Christianity)" title="Minister (Christianity)">minister</a> urged his congregation "to up and anent for the City of God".</p>
<p>The "Dear Green Place" and "City of God" required government troops to put down the rioters tearing up copies of the Treaty at almost every <a href="/wiki/Mercat_cross" title="Mercat cross">mercat cross</a> in Scotland. When Defoe visited in the mid-1720s, he claimed that the hostility towards his party was "because they were English and because of the Union, which they were almost universally exclaimed against"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_writing_and_novels">Late writing and novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late writing and novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The extent and particulars are widely contested concerning Defoe's writing in the period from the Tory fall in 1714 to the publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a></i> in 1719. Defoe comments on the tendency to attribute tracts of uncertain authorship to him in his apologia <i>Appeal to Honour and Justice</i> (1715), a defence of his part in Harley's Tory ministry (1710–14). Other works that anticipate his novelistic career include <i>The Family Instructor</i> (1715), a conduct manual on religious duty; <i>Minutes of the Negotiations of Monsr. Mesnager</i> (1717), in which he impersonates <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Mesnager" title="Nicolas Mesnager">Nicolas Mesnager</a>, the French plenipotentiary who negotiated the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht" title="Treaty of Utrecht">Treaty of Utrecht</a> (1713); and <i>A Continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Letters_Writ_by_a_Turkish_Spy" title="Letters Writ by a Turkish Spy">Letters Writ by a Turkish Spy</a></i> (1718), a satire of European politics and religion, ostensibly written by a <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> in Paris.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>From 1719 to 1724, Defoe published the novels for which he is famous (see below). In the final decade of his life, he also wrote conduct manuals, including <i>Religious Courtship</i> (1722), <i>The Complete English Tradesman</i> (1726) and <i>The New Family Instructor</i> (1727). He published a number of books decrying the breakdown of the social order, such as <i>The Great Law of Subordination Considered</i> (1724) and <i>Everybody's Business is Nobody's Business</i> (1725) and works on the supernatural, like <i><a href="/wiki/The_Political_History_of_the_Devil" title="The Political History of the Devil">The Political History of the Devil</a></i> (1726), <i>A System of Magick</i> (1727) and <i>An Essay on the History and Reality of Apparitions</i> (1727). His works on foreign travel and trade include <i>A General History of Discoveries and Improvements</i> (1727) and <i>Atlas Maritimus and Commercialis</i> (1728). Perhaps his greatest achievement with the novels is the magisterial <i><a href="/wiki/A_tour_thro%27_the_whole_island_of_Great_Britain" title="A tour thro' the whole island of Great Britain">A tour thro' the whole island of Great Britain</a></i> (1724–27), which provided a panoramic survey of British trade on the eve of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Complete_English_Tradesman"><i>The Complete English Tradesman</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Complete English Tradesman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Published in 1726, <i>The Complete English Tradesman</i> is an example of Defoe's political works. He discusses the role of the tradesman in England in comparison to tradesmen internationally, arguing that the British system of trade is far superior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defoe1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-defoe1-25">[25]</a></sup> He also implies that trade is the backbone of the British economy: "estate's a pond, but trade's a spring." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defoe1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-defoe1-25">[25]</a></sup> He praises the practicality of trade not only within the economy but the social stratification as well. Most of the British <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a>, he argues is at one time or another inextricably linked with the institution of trade, either through personal experience, marriage, or genealogy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defoe1_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-defoe1-25">[25]</a></sup> Oftentimes younger members of noble families entered into trade. Marriage to a tradesmen's daughter by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobleman" title="Nobleman">nobleman</a> was also common. Overall Defoe demonstrated a high respect for <b>tradesmen</b>, being one himself.</p>
<p>Not only does Defoe elevate individual British tradesmen to the level of <a href="/wiki/Gentleman" title="Gentleman">gentleman</a>, but he praises the entirety of British trade as a superior system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defoe1_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-defoe1-25">[25]</a></sup> Trade, Defoe argues is a much better catalyst for social and economic change than war. He states that through <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> and trade expansion the British empire is able to "increase commerce at home" through job creation and increased <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defoe1_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-defoe1-25">[25]</a></sup> He states that increased consumption, by laws of supply and demand, increases production and in turn raises wages for the poor therefore lifting part of British society further out of poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defoe1_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-defoe1-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Robinson_Crusoe"><i>Robinson Crusoe</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Robinson Crusoe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Defoe's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a></i> (1719), written when he was in his late fifties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> relates the story of a man's shipwreck on a desert island for thirty years and his subsequent adventures. Usually read as fiction, a coincidence of background geography suggests that this may be non-fiction. In the opening pages of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Farther_Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe" title="The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe">The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe</a></i>, the author describes how Crusoe settled in Bedford, married and produced a family, and that when his wife died, he went off on these further adventures. Bedford is also the place where the brother of "H. F." in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Journal_of_the_Plague_Year" title="A Journal of the Plague Year">A Journal of the Plague Year</a></i> retired to avoid the danger of the plague, so that by implication, if these works were not fiction, Defoe's family met Crusoe in Bedford, from whence the information in these books was gathered. Defoe went to school in Stoke Newington, London, with a friend named Caruso.</p>
<p>The novel has been assumed to be based in part on the story of the Scottish castaway <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Selkirk" title="Alexander Selkirk">Alexander Selkirk</a>, who spent four years stranded in the <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands" title="Juan Fernández Islands">Juan Fernández Islands</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-10">[10]</a></sup> but this is inconsistent with the details of the narrative. The island Selkirk lived on was named Más a Tierra (Closer to Land) at the time and was renamed <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_Island" title="Robinson Crusoe Island">Robinson Crusoe Island</a> in 1966. It has been supposed that Defoe may have also been inspired by the Latin or English translation of a book by the <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Andalusian</a>-Arab Muslim polymath <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Tufail" title="Ibn Tufail">Ibn Tufail</a>, who was known as "Abubacer" in Europe. The Latin edition of the book was entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Hayy_ibn_Yaqdhan" title="Hayy ibn Yaqdhan">Philosophus Autodidactus</a></i> and it was an earlier novel that is also set on a deserted island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amber_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amber-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wainwright_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Captain_Singleton"><i>Captain Singleton</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Captain Singleton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Defoe's next novel was <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Singleton" title="Captain Singleton">Captain Singleton</a></i> (1720), an adventure story whose first half covers a traversal of Africa and whose second half taps into the contemporary fascination with <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a>. It has been commended for its sensitive depiction of the close relationship between the hero and his religious mentor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quaker</a> William Walters.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Memoirs_of_a_Cavalier"><i>Memoirs of a Cavalier</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Memoirs of a Cavalier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Memoirs_of_a_Cavalier" title="Memoirs of a Cavalier">Memoirs of a Cavalier</a></i> (1720) is set during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Journal_of_the_Plague_Year"><i>A Journal of the Plague Year</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: A Journal of the Plague Year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A novel often read as non-fiction, this is an account of the Great Plague of London in 1665. It is undersigned by the initials "H. F.", suggesting the author's uncle Henry Foe as a primary source. It is an historical account of the events based on extensive research, published in 1722.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Bring out your dead!</i> The ceaseless chant of doom echoed through a city of emptied streets and filled grave pits. For this was London in the year of 1665, the Year of the Great Plague ... In 1721, when the Black Death again threatened the European Continent, Daniel Defoe wrote "<a href="/wiki/A_Journal_of_the_Plague_Year" title="A Journal of the Plague Year">A Journal of the Plague Year</a>" to alert an indifferent populace to the horror that was almost upon them. Through the eyes of a saddler who had chosen to remain while multitudes fled, the master realist vividly depicted a plague-stricken city. He re-enacted the terror of a helpless people caught in a tragedy they could not comprehend: the weak preying on the dying, the strong administering to the sick, the sinful orgies of the cynical, the quiet faith of the pious. With dramatic insight he captured for all time the death throes of a great city.</p>
<p>— Back cover of the New American Library version of "<a href="/wiki/A_Journal_of_the_Plague_Year" title="A Journal of the Plague Year">A Journal of the Plague Year</a>"; Signet Classic, 1960</p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonel_Jack"><i>Colonel Jack</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Colonel Jack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Colonel_Jack" title="Colonel Jack">Colonel Jack</a></b></i> (1722) follows an orphaned boy from a life of poverty and crime to colonial prosperity, military and marital imbroglios, and religious conversion, driven by a problematic notion of becoming a "gentleman."</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Moll_Flanders"><i>Moll Flanders</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Defoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Moll Flanders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Also in 1722, Defoe wrote <i><b><a href="/wiki/Moll_Flanders" title="Moll Flanders">Moll Flanders</a></b></i>, another first-person <a href="/wiki/Picaresque_novel" title="Picaresque novel">picaresque novel</a> of the fall and eventual redemption of a lone woman in 17th-century England. The titular heroine appears as a whore, bigamist, and thief, lives in <a href="/wiki/Liberty_of_the_Mint" title="Liberty of the Mint">The Mint</a>, commits adultery and incest, and yet manages to retain the reader's sympathy. Her savvy manipulation of both men and wealth earns her a life of trials but ultimately an ending in reward. <i>Moll Flanders</i> is an important work in the development of the novel, as it challenged the common perception of femininity and gender roles in 17th-century British society.</p>
<p><i><b>Roxana</b></i></p>
<p><i>Moll Flanders</i> and Defoe's final novel, <i><b><a href="/wiki/Roxana:_The_Fortunate_Mistress" title="Roxana: The Fortunate Mistress">Roxana: The Fortunate Mistress</a></b></i> (1724), are examples of the remarkable way in which Defoe seems to inhabit his fictional characters (yet "drawn from life"), not least in that they are women. <i>Roxanna</i> narrates the moral and spiritual decline of a high society courtesan. <i>Roxanna</i> differs from other Defoe works because the main character does not exhibit a conversion experience.</p>

<p>Daniel Defoe died on 24 April 1731, probably while in hiding from his creditors. He often was in debtor's prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The cause of his death was labelled as lethargy, but he probably experienced a stroke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> He was interred in <a href="/wiki/Bunhill_Fields" title="Bunhill Fields">Bunhill Fields</a>, (today Bunhill Fields Burial and Gardens), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borough_of_Islington" title="Borough of Islington">Borough of Islington</a>, London, where a monument was erected to his memory in 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Defoe is known to have used at least 198 pen names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
