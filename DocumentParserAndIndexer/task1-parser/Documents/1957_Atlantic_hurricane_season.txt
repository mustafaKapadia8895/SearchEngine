<p>The <b>1957 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was a generally inactive year for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> in the North Atlantic basin. There were eight tropical storms – two of which went unnamed – and three hurricanes, two of which intensified further to attain major hurricane intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> The season officially began on June 15 and ended on November 15, though the year's first tropical cyclone developed prior to the start of the season on June 8. The final storm dissipated on October 27, well before the official end of the season. The strongest hurricane of the year was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carrie" title="Hurricane Carrie">Carrie</a>, which reached the equivalent of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_4_hurricane" title="Category 4 hurricane">Category 4 hurricane</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a> on two separate occasions in the open Atlantic; Carrie later caused the sinking of the German ship <a href="/wiki/Pamir_(ship)" title="Pamir (ship)"><i>Pamir</i></a> southwest of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, resulting in 80 deaths.</p>
<p>In total, the season resulted in at least 513 fatalities and $152.5 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Audrey" title="Hurricane Audrey">Hurricane Audrey</a> was the season's most destructive and deadly storm, causing 416 deaths and about $150 million in damages. Audrey made landfall just east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabine_Pass,_Texas" title="Sabine Pass, Texas">Sabine Pass, Texas</a>, in the U.S. state of Louisiana as a strong Category 3 hurricane in late June. Three other tropical storms in the year made landfalls along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast of the United States</a>, bringing heavy rains that resulted in widespread flooding across much of the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a>. The highest rainfall total measured associated with a tropical cyclone was 18.39 in (467 mm) in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quarantine,_Louisiana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quarantine, Louisiana (page does not exist)">Quarantine, Louisiana</a>, during Tropical Storm Esther. However, an unofficial reading of 19 in (480 mm) was measured in an unknown location in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> during Tropical Storm One.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rainfall_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rainfall-4">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPC1-5">[3]</a></sup> The year's other tropical systems curved out to sea without causing much impact. After the season, the name Audrey was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_summary">Season summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1957_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Season summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<center>
<div class="timeline-wrapper"><map id="timeline_c8bcbb998940d3801b2f6db88b665d7e" name="timeline_c8bcbb998940d3801b2f6db88b665d7e">
<area alt="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale" coords="602,170,815,190" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" shape="rect" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale"/></map><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/timeline/c8bcbb998940d3801b2f6db88b665d7e.png" usemap="#timeline_c8bcbb998940d3801b2f6db88b665d7e"/></div>
</center>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> officially began on June 15, 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AsWindDies_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AsWindDies-6">[4]</a></sup> It was a below-average season in which eight <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> formed, of which three became hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Of these hurricanes, two became major hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> In an average season, ten tropical cyclones form, of which five become hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Five of the season's eight tropical cyclones developed in the Gulf of Mexico, the most since the <a href="/wiki/1936_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1936 Atlantic hurricane season">1936 season</a>, in which six formed in the gulf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_September_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WC_September-9">[7]</a></sup> The first tropical storm of the season formed on June 8, a week before the official start of the hurricane season. However, the first named storm, Audrey, formed afterwards, on June 25; these two storms were the only to form in June during the season. July featured no Atlantic tropical cyclones. Tropical Storm Bertha was the only system to form during August, developing in the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> Despite warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> (SSTs) in the tropical Atlantic, no storms formed during the month, below the climatological average of one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_August_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-WC_August-10">[8]</a></sup> The low monthly activity was in part due to an unfavorable wind pattern which prevailed across the Atlantic for much of the month. In September, however, conditions for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> were more favorable, with concomitant cyclonic activity. Four storms formed, of which two attained hurricane strength, higher than the mean activity of the last 70 Septembers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_September_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-WC_September-9">[7]</a></sup> By contrast, only one tropical storm formed in October, below the average of two in the month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_October_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WC_October-11">[9]</a></sup> This tropical storm dissipated on October 27, 16 days before the official end of the hurricane season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five tropical cyclones made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> during the hurricane season, including one hurricane. All of these storms made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast of the United States</a>. Property damage that resulted from cyclone-related impacts totaled to $152 million in the United States, with most caused by Hurricane Audrey, which made landfall near the border between <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> 513 deaths were also caused by tropical storms during the season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Briefs_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_Briefs-12">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> with 416 deaths attributable to Hurricane Audrey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deadliest_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deadliest-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season's activity was reflective with an <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 84, which is categorized as being "near normal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACE_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-15">[13]</a></sup> However, this was under the 1950–2000 average of 96.1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSU2009_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSU2009-16">[14]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of hurricanes multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. It is only calculated for full advisories on tropical cyclones with winds exceeding 39 mph (63 km/h), which is tropical storm strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary-17">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>An area of disturbed weather accompanied by low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> was first identified near the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> on June 7. Reports from the following day in the region reported pressures that were indicative of a developing tropical cyclone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> and at 06:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>, the disturbance attained tropical depression strength, the first of the season. The depression quickly developed into a tropical storm later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> Though hurricane reconnaissance flights could not locate a well-defined center, ship observations showed that the tropical storm was moving quickly to the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> Due to its fast forward motion, the storm gained little in organization and made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a href="/wiki/Port_Leon,_Florida" title="Port Leon, Florida">Port Leon, Florida</a>, on June 9 with winds of 50 mph (85 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> However, the system gradually strengthened as it crossed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_peninsula" title="Florida peninsula">Florida peninsula</a>, reentering the North Atlantic later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> On June 10, the storm reached peak winds of 65 mph (100 km/h) prior to becoming an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_storm" title="Extratropical storm">extratropical storm</a>. The post-tropical cyclone then strengthened and moved erratically in open seas before entirely dissipating on June 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the tropical storm's fast passage over land, heavy rainfall was reported,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> officially peaking at 14.95 in (380 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Live_Oak,_Florida" title="Live Oak, Florida">Live Oak, Florida</a>. However, unofficial reports of at least 19 in (480 mm) of rain were collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPC1-5">[3]</a></sup> In Perry, Florida, 100–200 families were evacuated due to the floodwater. Field <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watermelon" title="Watermelon">watermelon</a>, were damaged, with flood damage estimated at $30,000. The storm also spawned ten tornadoes, with nine in northeastern Florida and one on <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jekyll_Island" title="Jekyll Island">Jekyll Island</a>. All of these tornadoes were relatively weak and caused minor damage, totaling to $12,000. At the coast, the tropical storm's strong winds generated waves 2–3 ft (0.61–0.91 m) high, which caused moderate damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Though located south of where the storm made landfall, buildings and roads along the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Area" title="Tampa Bay Area">Tampa Bay Area</a> waterfront were flooded by the waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hard_Storm_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hard_Storm-18">[16]</a></sup> Thee <a href="/wiki/Cabin_cruiser" title="Cabin cruiser">cabin cruiser</a> <i>Kinnebar</i> capsized in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> due to the waves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7M_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-7M-19">[17]</a></sup> and five of the seven crew on board drowned; these were the only deaths associated with the system. Coastal damages due to <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> amounted to $10,000, and overall the storm caused $52,000 in damages across the southeastern United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Audrey" title="Hurricane Audrey">Hurricane Audrey</a></div>
<p>An ill-defined tropical wave was first identified in the Caribbean Sea on June 20, and moved westward into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>. The disturbance slowly strengthened as it developed a low pressure system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audrey_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audrey-20">[18]</a></sup> A nearby trough aided the intensification of the system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_June_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WC_June-21">[19]</a></sup> and it developed into a tropical depression on June 25, while remaining generally stationary in the Bay of Campeche. Situated in an area of favorable upper-air divergence and warm SSTs, the storm quickly organized and strengthened, reaching hurricane strength later that day. Audrey began to accelerate northward due to troughing in the upper-levels in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audrey_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audrey-20">[18]</a></sup> During this time, the hurricane <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a>, attaining Category 3 hurricane strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> On June 27, Audrey reached peak intensity with winds of 145 mph (235 km/h), and made landfall at this intensity near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Sabine_River_(Texas%E2%80%93Louisiana)" title="Sabine River (Texas–Louisiana)">Sabine River</a> later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audrey_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Audrey-20">[18]</a></sup> The hurricane quickly weakened as it moved inland, becoming an extratropical cyclone by June 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> The extratropical remnants of Audrey moved northeastward across the United States, before completely dissipating on June 29 over southern <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audrey_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Audrey-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Audrey caused widespread impacts across a wide swath of the United States and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audrey_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Audrey-20">[18]</a></sup> The storm's worst effects were felt in Louisiana, where the storm caused $120 million in damages. The highest <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> measured with the hurricane was 12.4 ft (3.8 m), reported west of <a href="/wiki/Cameron,_Louisiana" title="Cameron, Louisiana">Cameron, Louisiana</a>. The strong surge inundated much of the coast, killing much of the local wildlife and causing widespread property damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAHurricane_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAHurricane-22">[20]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall also caused flooding, peaking at 10.63 in (270 mm) west of <a href="/wiki/Basile,_Louisiana" title="Basile, Louisiana">Basile, Louisiana</a>. Rainfall was concentrated particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Atchafalaya_Basin" title="Atchafalaya Basin">Atchafalaya Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_Audrey_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPC_Audrey-23">[21]</a></sup> In Texas, effects of the storm were much less severe, but the storm still caused $8 million in damages, primarily as a result of strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TXHurricane_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TXHurricane-24">[22]</a></sup> Further inland, the weakening hurricane spawned tornadoes and caused additional flooding in conjunction with a frontal boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_Audrey_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPC_Audrey-23">[21]</a></sup> The effects of Audrey were felt as far north as Canada, where 15 people died due to the strong winds and heavy rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Audrey_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Audrey-25">[23]</a></sup> In total, the storm caused $152 million in damages and at least 416 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>A weak extratropical low entered the Gulf of Mexico on August 6 and drifted slowly westward. The system slowly organized, developing into a tropical storm on August 8 about 100 mi (160 km) south of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_Delta" title="Mississippi River Delta">Mississippi River Delta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Moving generally northward, Bertha quickly organized;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> the system attained its peak intensity with 65 mph (105 km/h) and an estimated minimum pressure of 998 mbar (hPa; 29.47 inHg) on August 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_August_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-WC_August-10">[8]</a></sup> That same day, Bertha came ashore near Cameron, Louisiana at the same intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> After landfall, the storm moved northward due to a strong high-pressure system and weakened over land,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_August_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-WC_August-10">[8]</a></sup> before degenerating into a remnant low at 1800 UTC on August 11 over <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> The storm's remnants later moved across the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Interior_Highlands" title="U.S. Interior Highlands">U.S. Interior Highlands</a> before dissipating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Gulf of Mexico, 1,350 workers on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Offshore_oil_drilling_platform" title="Offshore oil drilling platform">offshore oil drilling platforms</a> were evacuated in preparation for the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> The oil drilling tender <i>Murmanill No. 1</i> sunk due to the strong waves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oil_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oil-26">[24]</a></sup> though the two people on board at the time were evacuated by helicopter. At the coast, the maximum <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> height measured was 4.7 ft (1.4 m) at the <a href="/wiki/Schooner_Bayou_Control_Structure" title="Schooner Bayou Control Structure">Schooner Bayou Control Structure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> Bertha dropped heavy rainfall primarily as a remnant low across much of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ozarks" title="The Ozarks">The Ozarks</a>. Rainfall peaked at 13.77 in (350 mm) near <a href="/wiki/Damascus,_Arkansas" title="Damascus, Arkansas">Damascus, Arkansas</a>, which set a 24-hour rainfall record for the city and made August 1957 the wettest month on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bertha_Record_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bertha_Record-27">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_Bertha_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPC_Bertha-28">[26]</a></sup> Across <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, the heavy rains triggered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_flooding" title="Flash flooding">flash floods</a> after numerous rivers exceeded <a href="/wiki/Flood_stage" title="Flood stage">flood stage</a>. The flash floods caused property damage in cities adjacent to rivers. Though damage estimates were difficult to accurately obtain, four Arkansas counties reported combined losses of $925,000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> and two deaths were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carrie" title="Hurricane Carrie">Hurricane Carrie</a></div>
<p>Hurricane Carrie formed from an easterly <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> off the western coast of Africa on September 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Moving to the west, the storm gradually intensified, reaching hurricane strength on September 5. Carrie intensified further, before reaching peak intensity on September 8 as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4 hurricane</a> with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 140 mph (225 km/h) in the open Atlantic Ocean. The hurricane curved northward and fluctuated in intensity before recurving to the west and restrengthening, attaining Category 4 intensity for a second time as it neared <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on September 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> However, Carrie passed well north of the island and turned to the northeast towards Europe. Weakening as it reached higher latitudes, the storm transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> on September 23, prior to affecting areas of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its distance away from any major land masses, Carrie caused relatively minor damage along its path. On September 16, the hurricane passed well north of Bermuda, causing minimal damage despite its intensity at the time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-New-29">[27]</a></sup> though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">hurricane reconnaissance flights</a> in the area were postponed due to damage sustained by one of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-50_Superfortress" title="Boeing B-50 Superfortress">aircraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peril_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peril-30">[28]</a></sup> As it was transitioning to an extratropical cyclone southwest of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, the German ship <a href="/wiki/Pamir_(ship)" title="Pamir (ship)">Pamir</a> encountered the storm and capsized on September 21, resulting in the deaths of 80 crew members on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> As an extratropical storm, Carrie brought strong <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> and heavy rain to the British Isles, which claimed three lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Briefs_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-World_Briefs-12">[10]</a></sup> The hurricane's long duration and path in open water also helped it attain a number of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data_2-31">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Debbie">Tropical Storm Debbie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1957_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Debbie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">

<td>40 mph (65 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  1003 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<p>On September 5, a weak easterly wave moved into the Gulf of Mexico from the Caribbean Sea due to the influence of an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> The wave organized and spawned a weak area of circulation which developed into a tropical storm by 0600 UTC on September 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> Moving steadily towards the northeast at roughly 15 mph (25 km/h), Debbie only marginally strengthened due to the presence of cooler air entrainment. Early on September 8, Debbie made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Fort_Walton_Beach,_Florida" title="Fort Walton Beach, Florida">Fort Walton Beach, Florida</a> as a minimal tropical storm with winds of 40 mph (65 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> A minimum barometric pressure of 1005 mbar (hPa; 29.68 inHg) was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-10"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> The tropical storm weakened as it moved over land, degenerating to a tropical depression on September 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-14"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> The system became increasingly diffuse, and later merged with strong weather systems by 0600 UTC later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-11"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Offshore, the tropical storm generated high tides 2.5–4 ft (0.76–1.22 m) above average in <a href="/wiki/Apalachee_Bay" title="Apalachee Bay">Apalachee Bay</a>, located about 150 mi (240 km) east of where Debbie made landfall. The strong surf caused some localized flooding. In <a href="/wiki/St._Marks,_Florida" title="St. Marks, Florida">St. Marks, Florida</a>, a station recorded maximum sustained winds of 40 mph (65 km/h), the highest measured in association with the storm. However, a station in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa, Florida</a> recorded a peak wind gust of 52 mph (84 km/h) in a <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-16"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall caused widespread flooding, though rainfall was mostly beneficial to crops. Rainfall peaked at 11.26 in (286 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Wewahitchka,_Florida" title="Wewahitchka, Florida">Wewahitchka, Florida</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-12"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> while precipitation was reported as far north as <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_Debbie_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPC_Debbie-32">[30]</a></sup> The tropical storm only caused minor damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SlightDebbie_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-SlightDebbie-33">[31]</a></sup> but was indirectly responsible for four deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-17"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Esther">Tropical Storm Esther</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1957_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Esther">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On September 12, a mid-level circulation area formed over <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> and slowly drifted northeastward into the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-13"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> As it entered the Gulf, the system developed thunderstorm activity and an area of low pressure, and as a result the Weather Bureau began initiating advisories on a newly formed tropical depression by 1800 UTC on September 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-18"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-16"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> The depression quickly intensified and attained tropical storm strength by the next day. At the time, Esther had a minimum pressure of 1000 mbar (hPa; 29.53 inHg), as reported by a hurricane reconnaissance flight;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-14"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> this would be the lowest pressure measured associated with Esther.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-17"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> The large tropical storm intensified to peak winds of 65 mph (105 km/h) later that day, and held that intensity before making landfall in southeastern Louisiana on the morning on September 18. Esther weakened over land and later dissipated over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a> by 1200 UTC on September 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-19"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-18"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like Tropical Storm Debbie, which had made landfall just a week prior in the same area, Esther's wind impacts were minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-20"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> The strongest gust measured associated with the storm was 75 mph (121 km/h), as recorded at <a href="/wiki/Pensacola_International_Airport" title="Pensacola International Airport">Pensacola International Airport</a>. Other locations reported similar gusts and strong winds in squalls. Heavy rains persisted in the region well after Esther moved inland, resulting in high rainfall amounts. Rainfall peaked at 18.39 in (467 mm) in Quarantine, Louisiana, near the mouth of the Mississippi River. The heavy rains breached several <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a>, resulting in severe flooding, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Buras,_Louisiana" title="Buras, Louisiana">Buras, Louisiana</a>. Although rainfall was beneficial in many places due to an existing <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pecan" title="Pecan">pecan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a> crops were damaged by the floods. While crop damage amounted to $1 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-15"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> property damage amounted to $1.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-21"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Esther also caused three indirect deaths, all of which were in Louisiana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-16"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Frieda">Hurricane Frieda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1957_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hurricane Frieda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On September 20, a low pressure area developed within the same <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal boundary</a> that had curved Hurricane Carrie towards the Azores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-22"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Rapidly developing, the system was classified as a tropical depression at 1200 UTC later that day, southwest of Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-19"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> Initially, the depression was ill-defined and was slowly moving to the southwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-17"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-20"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> The following day, the attached cold front dissipated, allowing for the system to intensify. The system was subsequently upgraded a tropical storm by September 22,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-21"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> following a reconnaissance flight into the system which reported a minimum pressure of 1001 mbar (hPa; 29.56 inHg). At the time, however, Frieda was a small system with minimal shower activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-23"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Gradually strengthening, Frieda reached an initial peak intensity with winds of 60 mph (95 km/h) later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-22"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> Following the split of an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to the north, Frieda recurved towards the northeast the next day. As it sped to the northeast, a westerly <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_(meteorology)" title="Shortwave (meteorology)">shortwave</a> progressed eastward, enhancing environmental conditions. As a result, the tropical storm began to quickly strengthen. By 1200 UTC on September 25, Frieda attained hurricane strength, and reached peak intensity with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 992 mbar (hPa; 29.30 inHg), as reported by the Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchant_ship" title="Merchant ship">merchant ship</a> <i>Irvingbrook</i>. However, a cold front associated with the shortwave encountered the hurricane, which caused it to transition into an extratropical cyclone on September 26. Frieda's extratropical remnants later passed over <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-24"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> Though Frieda remained far from any land masses, a peak rainfall amount of 3.26 in (83 mm) was measured in Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rainfall_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rainfall-4">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>On October 22, an area of thunderstorm activity developed north of the Lesser Antilles, and barometric pressures fell throughout the region. The following day, a <a href="/wiki/Block_(meteorology)#Cut-off_highs_and_lows" title="Block (meteorology)">cut-off low</a> developed and strengthened along the edge of a trough extending from Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-25"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> This justified the classification of the system as a tropical depression on October 23,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-23"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> 500 mi (800 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-18"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> Moving towards the west, the depression attained tropical storm strength shortly after formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-24"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> A ship reported a minimum pressure of 999 mbar (hPa; 29.50 inHg) in the storm's vicinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-26"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup> The large system gradually strengthened the following day, reaching its peak intensity by 1800 UTC on October 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-25"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[6]</a></sup> A ship reported a minimum pressure of 993 mbar (hPa; 29.33 inHg) early the next day, the lowest pressure measured in association with the tropical storm. At the same time, the tropical storm also recurved towards the northeast. Heading towards more northerly latitudes, a gradual weakening trend began, due to the presence of an extratropical system. The tropical storm was itself absorbed by the same system on October 27. This storm may not have been named operationally due to a lack of discernible tropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-27"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The following names were used for tropical cyclones that reached at least tropical storm intensity in the North Atlantic in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nomenclature_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nomenclature-34">[32]</a></sup> However, two of such storms, the first and last, went unnamed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatological_Data_13-19"><a href="#cite_note-Climatological_Data-13">[11]</a></sup> The name Audrey would later be <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a>, only the seventh name to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retiring_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retiring-35">[33]</a></sup> Storms were named Audrey, Bertha, Carrie, Debbie, Esther, and Frieda for the first time in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Names_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Names-36">[34]</a></sup></p>
