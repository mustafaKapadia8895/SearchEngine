<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Drama (film and television)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Drama_film&amp;redirect=no" title="Drama film">Drama film</a>)</span></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>When used to define a specific <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">genre</a> or <a href="/wiki/Film_genre" title="Film genre">type of film</a> or <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programme</a>, <b>drama</b> is usually qualified with additional terms that specify its particular subgenre, such as "<a href="/wiki/Political_drama" title="Political drama">political drama</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courtroom_drama" title="Courtroom drama">courtroom drama</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_drama" title="Historical drama">historical drama</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Domestic_drama" title="Domestic drama">domestic drama</a>", or "<a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">comedy-drama</a>". These terms tend to indicate a particular <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">setting</a> or subject-matter, or else they qualify the otherwise serious tone of a drama with elements that encourage a broader range of <a href="/wiki/Mood_(literature)" title="Mood (literature)">moods</a>.</p>
<p>All forms of <a href="/wiki/Film_industry" title="Film industry">cinema</a> or <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> that involve <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fictional stories</a> are forms of <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama in the broader sense</a> if their storytelling is achieved by means of <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">actors</a> who <a href="/wiki/Representation_(arts)" title="Representation (arts)">represent</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Mimesis" title="Mimesis">mimesis</a></i>) <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">characters</a>. In this broader sense, drama is a <a href="/wiki/Mode_(literature)" title="Mode (literature)">mode</a> distinct from <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short stories</a>, and narrative <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> or <a href="/wiki/Song" title="Song">songs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elam98_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-elam98-1">[1]</a></sup> In the modern era before the birth of cinema or television, "drama" came to be used within the <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a> as a generic term to describe a type of <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">play</a> that was neither a <a href="/wiki/Comedy_(drama)" title="Comedy (drama)">comedy</a> nor a <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedy</a>. It is this narrower sense that the film and television industries, along with <a href="/wiki/Film_studies" title="Film studies">film studies</a>, adopted. "<a href="/wiki/Radio_drama" title="Radio drama">Radio drama</a>" has been used in both senses—originally transmitted in a live performance, it has also been used to describe the more high-brow and serious end of the dramatic output of <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_drama_in_film_and_television">Types of drama in film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drama_(film_and_television)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of drama in film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crime_drama" title="Crime drama">Crime drama</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Legal_drama" title="Legal drama">legal drama</a></b>: character development based on themes involving criminals, law enforcement and the legal system.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Historical_period_drama" title="Historical period drama"><b>Historical drama</b></a> (epic) (including <b><a href="/wiki/War_film" title="War film">war drama</a></b>): films that focus on dramatic events in history.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">Horror drama</a></b>: a film that focuses on imperiled characters dealing with realistic emotional struggles, often involving dysfunctional family relations, in a horror setting. The film's horror elements often serve as a backdrop to an unraveling dramatic plot.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Docudrama" title="Docudrama">Docudrama</a></b>: the difference between a docudrama and a documentary is that in a documentary it uses real people to describe history or current events; in a docudrama it uses professionally trained actors to play the roles in the current event, that is "dramatized" a bit. Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Docufiction" title="Docufiction">docufiction</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Psychodrama" title="Psychodrama">Psychodrama</a></b>: an action method, often used as a <a href="/wiki/Psychotherapy" title="Psychotherapy">psychotherapy</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">Comedy-drama</a></b>: a film in which there is an equal, or nearly equal, balance of humour and serious content.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Melodrama" title="Melodrama">Melodrama</a></b>: a sub-type of drama films that uses plots that appeal to the heightened emotions of the audience. Melodramatic plots often deal with "crises of human emotion, failed romance or friendship, strained familial situations, tragedy, illness, neuroses, or emotional and physical hardship". Film critics sometimes use the term "pejoratively to connote an unrealistic, pathos-filled, <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">camp</a> tale of romance or domestic situations with stereotypical characters (often including a central female character) that would directly appeal to feminine audiences".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Also called "women's movies", "weepies", tearjerkers, or "chick flicks". If they are targeted to a male audience, then they are called "guy cry" films.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Romance_film" title="Romance film">Romantic drama</a></b>: a sub-type of dramatic film which dwells on the elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_love" title="Romantic love">romantic love</a>.</li>
</ul>
