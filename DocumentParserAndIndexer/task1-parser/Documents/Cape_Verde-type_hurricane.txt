<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cape Verde hurricane</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Verde-type_hurricane&amp;redirect=no" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a>)</span></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about a type of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> categorized by development location. For storms actually affecting the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> archipelago, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_West_Africa_hurricanes" title="List of West Africa hurricanes">List of West Africa hurricanes</a>.</div>

<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a> became the most powerful Atlantic hurricane ever recorded outside of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>Cape Verde hurricane</b>, or <b>Cabo Verde hurricane</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> that originates at low-<a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> in the deep <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that has passed over or near the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands after exiting the coast of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>. The average hurricane season has about two Cape Verde <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>, which are often the largest and most intense storms of the season due to having plenty of warm open ocean over which to develop before encountering land or other factors prompting weakening. A good portion of Cape Verde storms are large, some setting records. Most of the longest-lived <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> in the Atlantic basin are Cape Verde hurricanes. While many move harmlessly out to sea, some move across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_sea" title="Caribbean sea">Caribbean sea</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, becoming damaging storms for <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and occasionally even <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Research projects since the 1970s have been launched to understand the formation of these storms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Verde_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the early 1940s, the term Cape Verde hurricane referred to August and early September storms that formed to the east of the surface plotting charts in use at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn1960_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn1960-1">[1]</a></sup> Cape Verde hurricanes typically develop from tropical waves which form in the African <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a>, then move into the African <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a>. The disturbances move off the western coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storms</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> soon after moving off the coast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-A2-2">[2]</a></sup> within 10 to 15 degrees latitude, or 1,100 kilometres (680 mi) to 1,600 kilometres (990 mi), of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> Islands;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn1960_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn1960-1">[1]</a></sup> this comprises the tropical latitudes east of the <a href="/wiki/40th_meridian_west" title="40th meridian west">40th meridian west</a>. In the years since the phrase's coining, increasing detection has allowed meteorologists to determine that Cape Verde hurricanes have formed as early as July 3 (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bertha_(2008)" title="Hurricane Bertha (2008)">Hurricane Bertha</a> of 2008) or as late as October 2 (<a href="/wiki/1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Gail" title="1953 Atlantic hurricane season">Hurricane Gail</a> of 1953).</p>

<p>A Cape Verde hurricane forms from a tropical depression or tropical wave which passed through or near the Cape Verde islands, and strengthens into a named system (eventually becoming a hurricane at some point) in the mid-Atlantic. The initial track of a Cape Verde storm tends to be generally westward from Cape Verde, with a turn to the north at some stage in the track for most storms lasting more than a few days. Once the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> begins approaching the 40th meridian west, a Cape Verde hurricane can take several basic tracks and from there diverge, become extra-tropical, or dissipate.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<ol>
<li>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joan_1988_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Joan_1988_track.png/150px-Joan_1988_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Joan_1988_track.png/225px-Joan_1988_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Joan_1988_track.png/300px-Joan_1988_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joan_1988_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Joan (1988), on a southern track.</div>
</div>
</div>
It can continue to the west, and if it is far enough south, it will cross the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, possibly affecting <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> to some degree. From there it will often continue westward into <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam" title="Hurricane Joan–Miriam">Hurricane Joan–Miriam</a> took this southerly route in 1988, causing severe flooding in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, making landfall in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, and continuing into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Basin" title="Pacific Basin">Pacific Basin</a>, just as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene%E2%80%93Olivia" title="Hurricane Irene–Olivia">Hurricane Irene–Olivia</a> did in 1971. Rarely, a storm will <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_South_America_tropical_cyclones" title="List of South America tropical cyclones">make landfall in South America</a>.
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
</li>
<li>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emily_2005_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Emily_2005_track.png/150px-Emily_2005_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Emily_2005_track.png/225px-Emily_2005_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Emily_2005_track.png/300px-Emily_2005_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emily_2005_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emily (2005), on a Mexican track.</div>
</div>
</div>
Some storms pass unabated into the Gulf of Mexico. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> of 2004 began with a very southerly route yet it made landfall far to the north in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>. Somewhere in the middle of these extremes, storms such as 1980's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Hurricane Allen</a> track toward the coast of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, affecting areas from the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> to <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. Many of these southerly hurricanes strike <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1915" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1915">Galveston Hurricane of 1915</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a> of 1988. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Janet" title="Hurricane Janet">Hurricane Janet</a> of 1955 will be remembered as one of the deadliest and strongest storms to strike the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico.
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
</li>
<li>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Georges_1998_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Georges_1998_track.png/150px-Georges_1998_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Georges_1998_track.png/225px-Georges_1998_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Georges_1998_track.png/300px-Georges_1998_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Georges_1998_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Georges (1998), on a Caribbean track.</div>
</div>
</div>
If the storm is further north, it may travel past the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>. In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Georges" title="Hurricane Georges">Hurricane Georges</a> took a track of this nature as it continued into <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. Slightly further north, and the storm will track through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> and into <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> in the manner of 1992's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-3">[3]</a></sup> Many of these storms passing through the center of the Caribbean will strike the islands of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_David" title="Hurricane David">Hurricane David</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Frederic" title="Hurricane Frederic">Hurricane Frederic</a> did, both in 1979. Some storms on this track will turn to the west once in the Gulf, and thereby make landfall in Texas, as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1900" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1900">Galveston Hurricane of 1900</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> of 2008 did. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Inez" title="Hurricane Inez">Hurricane Inez</a> of 1966 followed a Caribbean track that took the storm into the Bahamas where it weakened, only to turn sharply west and make a final landfall in Mexico.
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
</li>
<li>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gloria_1985_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="872" data-file-width="1024" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gloria_1985_track.png/150px-Gloria_1985_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gloria_1985_track.png/225px-Gloria_1985_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gloria_1985_track.png/300px-Gloria_1985_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gloria_1985_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gloria (1985) struck the East Coast.</div>
</div>
</div>
A more northerly storm will often have its track affected by <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">the high pressure that typically occurs over the eastern Atlantic in late summer</a>. As these storms pass north of the Antilles, their tracks begin to curve to the north and to the east of the Florida Peninsula, often sending them away from land. A few storms will track northwest, making landfall in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North</a> or <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hurricane Hugo</a> of 1989 and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fran" title="Hurricane Fran">Hurricane Fran</a> of 1996 are typical examples. Occasionally storms following this track can accelerate to the north and strike <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_Hurricane_of_1938" title="New England Hurricane of 1938">New England Hurricane of 1938</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gloria" title="Hurricane Gloria">Hurricane Gloria</a> in 1985 were two such cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-3">[3]</a></sup>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
</li>
<li>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Igor_2010_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1024" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Igor_2010_track.png/150px-Igor_2010_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Igor_2010_track.png/225px-Igor_2010_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Igor_2010_track.png/300px-Igor_2010_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Igor_2010_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Igor (2010) struck Atlantic Canada.</div>
</div>
</div>
When a storm tracks very far north, its potential for landfall is much less. Some of these storms will strike Bermuda before re-curving out to sea, such as the <a href="/wiki/1948_Bermuda%E2%80%93Newfoundland_hurricane" title="1948 Bermuda–Newfoundland hurricane">1948 Bermuda–Newfoundland hurricane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fabian" title="Hurricane Fabian">Hurricane Fabian</a> in 2003. Occasionally, the storm will track west-northwest to strike the Atlantic coast of the U.S., such as the <a href="/wiki/1933_Chesapeake%E2%80%93Potomac_hurricane" title="1933 Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane">1933 Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> of 2003, which struck the mid-Atlantic. Other Cape Verde storms make landfall in Canada, such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Luis" title="Hurricane Luis">Hurricane Luis</a> in 1995 and Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Earl_(2010)" title="Hurricane Earl (2010)">Earl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Igor" title="Hurricane Igor">Igor</a> of 2010. If the storm's track is affected significantly, it will often pass near the Eastern U.S., <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland Island</a> and curve back out to sea, where it becomes extratropical over cooler water. An example of such was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edouard_(1996)" title="Hurricane Edouard (1996)">Hurricane Edouard</a> in 1996. At the extreme, Cape Verde hurricanes can track so far north as to curve all the way to Europe, such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Debbie_(1961)" title="Hurricane Debbie (1961)">Hurricane Debbie</a> of 1961, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isaac_(2000)" title="Hurricane Isaac (2000)">Hurricane Isaac</a> of 2000, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katia_(2011)" title="Hurricane Katia (2011)">Hurricane Katia</a> of 2011, all of which affected <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
</li>
<li>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gaston_2016_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2602" data-file-width="3000" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gaston_2016_track.png/150px-Gaston_2016_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gaston_2016_track.png/225px-Gaston_2016_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gaston_2016_track.png/300px-Gaston_2016_track.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gaston_2016_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gaston (2016) dissipated in the central Atlantic.</div>
</div>
</div>
Sometimes, the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> is displaced from its normal position, which allows storms to recurve quickly by being driven around the east side of an upper trough in the central Atlantic, generally missing land completely as they dissipate. Both <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Danielle_(2004)" title="Hurricane Danielle (2004)">Hurricane Danielle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karl_(2004)" title="Hurricane Karl (2004)">Hurricane Karl</a> were affected like this in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-3">[3]</a></sup> In other cases, storms are steered north very early in their development near Cape Verde, such as the 1989 storms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Erin_(1989)" title="Hurricane Erin (1989)">Hurricane Erin</a> and <a href="/wiki/1989_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Felix" title="1989 Atlantic hurricane season">Hurricane Felix</a>, which both traveled northwest and never threatened land. However, in extremely rare cases, these storms will hit the Cape Verde islands or the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> at a significant intensity. Two notable examples of this are <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fred_(2015)" title="Hurricane Fred (2015)">Hurricane Fred</a>, which hit Cape Verde in 2015, and <a href="/wiki/1893_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Seven" title="1893 Atlantic hurricane season">a hurricane which struck the Azores in 1893</a>; both of which caused considerable destruction.</li>
</ol>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Because most Cape Verde hurricanes take a near-westward path that starts in the eastern Atlantic, they can avoid the two situations that typically end the life of a tropical cyclone: interaction with land, and movement over cool water. Since storms that far out at sea can go over a week without moving over cooler water or near a landmass, Cape Verde hurricanes are some of the longest-lived and farthest-travelling storms. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faith_(1966)" title="Hurricane Faith (1966)">Hurricane Faith</a> of 1966, the farthest-travelling Atlantic hurricane on record,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-3">[3]</a></sup> was a Cape Verde hurricane. It travelled 6,850 miles (11,020 km) and lasted a total of 16 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam" title="Hurricane Joan–Miriam">Hurricane Joan–Miriam</a> of 1988 and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene%E2%80%93Olivia" title="Hurricane Irene–Olivia">Hurricane Irene–Olivia</a> of 1971 lasted longer, both having crossed narrow parts of Central America and regaining cyclonic motion in the Pacific.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Verde_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Twenty nations took part in the GATE research project in 1974, where <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-6" title="Douglas DC-6">Douglas DC-6</a> aircraft examined <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> which spawn Cape Verde hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2006, there was a two-month research project known as NAMMA-06 (short for NASA African Monsoon Multidisciplinary Activities)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which flew <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-8" title="Douglas DC-8">Douglas DC-8s</a> into seedling disturbances in the eastern Atlantic which had the potential to become Cape Verde hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
