<p><i><b>Merluccius paradoxus</b></i>, the <b>deep-water Cape hake</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merluccid_hake" title="Merluccid hake">merluccid hake</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Merluccius" title="Merluccius">Merluccius</a></i>, found in the south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, south of <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>. Its range extends in decreasing abundance around the southern coast of Africa and into the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, but it is at its most plentiful in the cold, nutrient-rich fishing grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, in combination with <i>Merluccius capensis</i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shallow-water_Cape_hake" title="Shallow-water Cape hake">shallow-water Cape hake</a>, it is one of the most important commercial food fishes and locally is generally known as "<a href="/wiki/Stockfish" title="Stockfish">stockfish</a>" (this English name being derived from the <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a> <i>stokvis</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hakes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Very similar to <i>M. merluccius</i> (European hake), it has an average length of 40–60 cm, up to a maximum of about 80 cm. It lives close to the bottom in muddy areas on the continental shelf and slope. It usually is to be found at depths of 200 to 850 m, although most commonly below 400 m. Immature specimens feed on small deep-sea fishes and crustaceans, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphausiacea" title="Euphausiacea">Euphausiacea</a>. Mature hake feed mainly on fish, squid, and crustaceans, especially <a href="/wiki/Mysidacea" title="Mysidacea">Mysidacea</a> and Euphausiacea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hakes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Little information is available on its migratory habits as compared to those of <i>M. capensis</i>, which has been fairly well studied.</p>
<p>The deep-water Cape hake is often fished together with <i>M. capensis</i>, which usually occurs at shallower depths. Most reported commercial catches combine both species, but towards the northwest coast of Southern Africa, in the region of Angola, <i>M. paradoxus</i> does not occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakes_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hakes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
