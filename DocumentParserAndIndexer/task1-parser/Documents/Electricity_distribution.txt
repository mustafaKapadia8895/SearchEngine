<p><b>Electric power distribution</b> is the final stage in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_delivery" title="Power delivery">delivery</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>; it carries electricity from the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission system</a> to individual consumers. Distribution substations connect to the transmission system and lower the transmission voltage to medium <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> ranging between 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilovolt" title="Kilovolt">kV</a> and 35 kV with the use of <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a>. <i>Primary</i> distribution lines carry this medium voltage power to <a href="/wiki/Distribution_transformer" title="Distribution transformer">distribution transformers</a> located near the customer's premises. Distribution transformers again lower the voltage to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilization_voltage" title="Utilization voltage">utilization voltage</a> used by lighting, industrial equipment or household appliances. Often several customers are supplied from one transformer through <i>secondary</i> distribution lines. Commercial and residential customers are connected to the secondary distribution lines through <a href="/wiki/Service_drop" title="Service drop">service drops</a>. Customers demanding a much larger amount of power may be connected directly to the primary distribution level or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtransmission" title="Subtransmission">subtransmission</a> level.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_electric_power_transmission" title="History of electric power transmission">History of electric power transmission</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1861" data-file-width="1246" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png/170px-Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png/255px-Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png/340px-Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The late 1870s and early 1880s saw the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc lamp</a> lighting used outdoors or in large indoor spaces such as this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brush_Electric_Company" title="Brush Electric Company">Brush Electric Company</a> system installed in 1880 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Electric power distribution only became necessary in the 1880s when electricity started being generated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_stations" title="Power stations">power stations</a>. Before that electricity was usually generated where it was used. The first power distribution systems installed in European and US cities were used to supply lighting: <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc lighting</a> running on very high voltage (around 3000 volts) <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> (AC) or <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> (DC), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lamp" title="Incandescent lamp">incandescent lighting</a> running on low voltage (100 volt) direct current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Both were supplanting <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a> systems, with arc lighting taking over large area and street lighting, and incandescent lighting replacing gas for business and residential lighting.</p>
<p>Due to the high voltages used in arc lighting, a single generating station could supply a long string of lights, up to 7-mile (11 km) long circuits. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Each doubling of the voltage would allow the same size cable to transmit the same amount of power four times the distance for a given power loss. Direct current indoor incandescent lighting systems (for example the first Edison <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_Station" title="Pearl Street Station">Pearl Street Station</a> installed in 1882), had difficulty supplying customers more than a mile away due to the low 110 volt system being used throughout the system, from the generators to the final use. The Edison DC system needed thick copper conductor cables, and the generating plants needed to be within about 1.5 miles (2.4 km) of the farthest customer to avoid excessively large and expensive conductors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_the_transformer">Introduction of the transformer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Introduction of the transformer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transmitting electricity a long distance at high voltage and then reducing it to a lower voltage for lighting became a recognized engineering roadblock to electric power distribution with many, not very satisfactory, solutions tested by lighting companies. The mid-1880s saw a breakthrough with the development of functional <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a> that allowed the AC voltage to be "stepped up" to much higher transmission voltages and then dropped down to a lower end user voltage. With much cheaper transmission costs and the greater <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a> of having large generating plants supply whole cities and regions, the use of AC spread rapidly.</p>
<p>In the US the competition between direct current and alternating current took a personal turn in the late 1880s in the form of a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_Currents" title="War of Currents">War of Currents</a>" when <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> started attacking <a href="/wiki/George_Westinghouse" title="George Westinghouse">George Westinghouse</a> and his development of the first US AC transformer systems, pointing out all the deaths caused by high voltage AC systems over the years and claiming any AC system was inherently dangerous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Edison's propaganda campaign was short lived with his company switching over to AC in 1892.</p>
<p>AC became the dominant form of transmission of power with innovations in Europe and the US in <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> designs and the development of engineered <i>universal systems</i> allowing the large number of legacy systems to be connected to large AC grids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_Parke_Hughes_1930.2C_pages_120-121_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas_Parke_Hughes_1930.2C_pages_120-121-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raghu_Garud_2009.2C_page_249_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raghu_Garud_2009.2C_page_249-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first half of the 20th century, in many places the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_industry" title="Electric power industry">electric power industry</a> was <a href="/wiki/Vertical_integration" title="Vertical integration">vertically integrated</a>, meaning that one company did generation, transmission, distribution, metering and billing. Starting in the 1970s and 1980s, nations began the process of <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privatisation" title="Privatisation">privatisation</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Electricity_market" title="Electricity market">electricity markets</a>. The distribution system would remain regulated, but generation, retail, and sometimes transmission systems were transformed into competitive markets.</p>

<p>Electric power begins at a generating station, where the potential difference can be as high as 13,800 volts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parcours_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-parcours-6">[6]</a></sup> AC is usually used. Users of large amounts of DC power such as some <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">railway electrification systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchanges</a> and industrial processes such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> smelting use <a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">rectifiers</a> to derive DC from the public AC supply, or may have their own generation systems. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVDC" title="HVDC">High-voltage DC</a> can be advantageous for isolating alternating-current systems or controlling the quantity of electricity transmitted. For example, <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Québec">Hydro-Québec</a> has a direct-current line which goes from the <a href="/wiki/James_Bay" title="James Bay">James Bay</a> region to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the generating station it goes to the generating station’s switchyard where a step-up <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformer</a> increases the voltage to a level suitable for transmission, from 44kV to 765kV. Once in the transmission system, electricity from each generating station is combined with electricity produced elsewhere. Electricity is consumed as soon as it is produced. It is transmitted at a very high speed, close to the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>.</p>

<p>The transition from transmission to distribution happens in a power <a href="/wiki/Electrical_substation" title="Electrical substation">substation</a>, which has the following functions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">Circuit breakers</a> and switches enable the substation to be disconnected from the transmission grid or for distribution lines to be disconnected.</li>
<li>Transformers step down transmission voltages, 35kV or more, down to primary distribution voltages. These are medium voltage circuits, usually 600-35,000 V.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>From the transformer, power goes to the <a href="/wiki/Busbar" title="Busbar">busbar</a> that can split the distribution power off in multiple directions. The bus distributes power to distribution lines, which fan out to customers.</li>
</ul>
<p>Urban distribution is mainly underground, sometimes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_utility_duct" title="Common utility duct">common utility ducts</a>. Rural distribution is mostly above ground with <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">utility poles</a>, and suburban distribution is a mix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> Closer to the customer, a distribution transformer steps the primary distribution power down to a low-voltage secondary circuit, usually 120/240 volts in the US for residential customers. The power comes to the customer via a <a href="/wiki/Service_drop" title="Service drop">service drop</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Electricity_meter" title="Electricity meter">electricity meter</a>. The final circuit in an urban system may be less than 50 feet, but may be over 300 feet for a rural customer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_distribution">Primary distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Primary distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Primary distribution voltages range from 4 kV to 35 kV phase-to-phase (2.4 kV to 20 kV phase-to-neutral)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eep-pdvl_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-eep-pdvl-10">[10]</a></sup> Only large consumers are fed directly from distribution voltages; most utility customers are connected to a transformer, which reduces the distribution voltage to the low voltage "utilization voltage", "supply voltage" or "mains voltage" used by lighting and interior wiring systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Network_configurations">Network configurations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Network configurations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Distribution networks are divided into two types, radial or network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A radial system is arranged like a tree where each customer has one source of supply. A network system has multiple sources of supply operating in parallel. Spot networks are used for concentrated loads. Radial systems are commonly used in rural or suburban areas.</p>
<p>Radial systems usually include emergency connections where the system can be reconfigured in case of problems, such as a fault or required replacement. This can be done by opening and closing switches. It may be acceptable to close a loop<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag is ambiguous, and needs clarification. (August 2017)">ambiguous</span></a></i>]</sup> for a short time.</p>
<p>Long feeders experience <a href="/wiki/Voltage_drop" title="Voltage drop">voltage drop</a> (<a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">power factor</a> distortion) requiring capacitors to be installed.</p>
<p>Reconfiguration, by exchanging the functional links between the elements of the system, represents one of the most important measures which can improve the operational performance of a distribution system. The problem of optimization through the reconfiguration of a power distribution system, in terms of its definition, is a historical single objective problem with constraints. Since 1975, when Merlin and Back<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> introduced the idea of distribution system reconfiguration for active power loss reduction, until nowadays, a lot of researchers have proposed diverse methods and algorithms to solve the reconfiguration problem as a single objective problem. Some authors have proposed Pareto optimality based approaches (including active power losses and reliability indices as objectives). For this purpose, different artificial intelligence based methods have been used: microgenetic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> branch exchange,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> particle swarm optimization<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and non-dominated sorting genetic algorithm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_services">Rural services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rural services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rural_electrification" title="Rural electrification">Rural electrification</a> systems tend to use higher distribution voltages because of the longer distances covered by distribution lines (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_Electrification_Administration" title="Rural Electrification Administration">Rural Electrification Administration</a>). 7.2, 12.47, 25, and 34.5 kV distribution is common in the United States; 11 kV and 33 kV are common in the UK, Australia and New Zealand; 11 kV and 22 kV are common in South Africa and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eolss_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-eolss-17">[17]</a></sup> Other voltages are occasionally used. Distribution in rural areas may be only single-phase if it is not economical to install three-phase power for relatively few and small customers.</p>
<p>Rural services normally try to minimize the number of poles and wires. <a href="/wiki/Single-wire_earth_return" title="Single-wire earth return">Single-wire earth return</a> (SWER) is the least expensive, with one wire. It uses higher voltages (than urban distribution), which in turn permits use of galvanized steel wire. The strong steel wire allows for less expensive wide pole spacing. In rural areas a pole-mount transformer may serve only one customer. In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan, Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Single-wire_earth_return" title="Single-wire earth return">single-wire earth return</a> systems (SWER) are used to electrify remote rural areas.</p>
<p>Higher voltage or three phase service, at a higher infrastructure and a higher cost, provide increased equipment efficiency and lower energy cost for large agricultural facilities, petroleum pumping facilities, or water plants.</p>
<p>In other rural areas, a "4-wire" system is used but, to reduce the number of conductors, the 4th neutral wire is connected to the ground to use that as a return (an unground <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power#Three-wire_and_four-wire_circuits" title="Three-phase electric power">wye</a> system).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_distribution">Secondary distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Secondary distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Low-voltage_network" title="Low-voltage network">Low-voltage network</a></div>
<p>Electricity is delivered at a frequency of either 50 or 60 Hz, depending on the region. It is delivered to domestic customers as <a href="/wiki/Single-phase_electric_power" title="Single-phase electric power">single-phase electric power</a> In some countries as in Europe a <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">three phase</a> supply may be made available for larger properties. Seen in an <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscope</a>, the domestic power supply in North America would look like a <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine wave</a>, oscillating between -170 volts and 170 volts, giving an effective voltage of 120 volts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">Three-phase</a> power is more efficient in terms of power delivered per cable used, and is more suited to running large electric motors. Some large European appliances may be powered by <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">three-phase</a> power, such as electric stoves and clothes dryers.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Ground_(electricity)" title="Ground (electricity)">ground</a> connection is normally provided for the customer's system as well as for the equipment owned by the utility. The purpose of connecting the customer's system to ground is to limit the voltage that may develop if high voltage conductors fall down onto lower-voltage conductors which are usually mounted lower to the ground, or if a failure occurs within a distribution transformer. <a href="/wiki/Earthing_system" title="Earthing system">Earthing systems</a> can be TT, TN-S, TN-C-S or TN-C.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="220-240_volt_systems">220-240 volt systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 220-240 volt systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most of the world uses 50 Hz 220 or 230 V single phase 400V 3 phase for residential and light industrial services. In this system, the primary distribution network supplies a few substations per area, and the 230 V power from each substation is directly distributed. A live (hot) wire and neutral are connected to the building for each phase of three phase service. Single-phase distribution is used where motor loads are light. In Europe, electricity is normally distributed for industry and domestic use by the three-phase, four wire system. This gives a three-phase voltage of <span class="nowrap">400 volts</span> <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power#Three-wire_and_four-wire_circuits" title="Three-phase electric power">wye</a> service and a single-phase voltage of <span class="nowrap">230 volts</span>. In the UK a typical urban or suburban low-voltage substation would normally be rated between 150 kVA and 1 MVA and supply a whole neighborhood of a few hundred houses. For industrial customers, 3-phase <span class="nowrap">690 / 400 volt</span> is also available, or may be generated locally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Large industrial customers have their own transformer(s) with an input from 11 kV to 220 kV.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="110-120_volt_systems">110-120 volt systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 110-120 volt systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most of the Americas use 60 Hz AC, the 120/240 volt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Split_phase" title="Split phase">split phase</a> system domestically and three phase for larger installations. North American transformers usually power homes at 240 volts, similar to Europe's 230 volts. It is the split-phase that allows use of 120 volts in the home.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_sector_in_Japan" title="Electricity sector in Japan">electricity sector in Japan</a>, the standard frequencies for AC are 50 and 60 Hz. Parts of the country use 50 Hz, while other parts use 60 Hz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">[20]</a></sup> This is a relic of the 1800s. Some local providers in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> imported 50 Hz German equipment, while the local power providers in <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a> brought in 60 Hz generators from the United States. The grids grew until eventually the entire country was wired. Today the frequency is 50 Hz in Eastern Japan (including Tokyo, <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a>, <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku_region" title="Tōhoku region">Tohoku</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a>) and 60 Hertz in Western Japan (including <a href="/wiki/Nagoya" title="Nagoya">Nagoya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shikokuch%C5%AB%C5%8D" title="Shikokuchūō">Shikoku</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kyushu" title="Kyushu">Kyushu</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most household appliances are made to work on either frequency. The problem of incompatibility came into the public eye when the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a> knocked out about a third of the east’s capacity, and power in the west couldn’t be fully shared with the east, since the country does not have a common frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are four <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">high-voltage direct current</a> (HVDC) converter stations that move power across Japan’s AC frequency border. <a href="/wiki/Shin_Shinano" title="Shin Shinano">Shin Shinano</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Back-to-back_connection#Power_transmission" title="Back-to-back connection">back-to-back</a> HVDC facility in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> which forms one of four <a href="/wiki/Frequency_changer" title="Frequency changer">frequency changer</a> stations that link Japan's western and eastern power grids. The other three are at <a href="/wiki/Higashi-Shimizu_Frequency_Converter" title="Higashi-Shimizu Frequency Converter">Higashi-Shimizu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minami-Fukumitsu" title="Minami-Fukumitsu">Minami-Fukumitsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sakuma_Dam#HVDC_frequency_converter" title="Sakuma Dam">Sakuma Dam</a>. Together they can move up to 1.2 GW of power east or west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="240_volt_systems_and_120_volt_outlets">240 volt systems and 120 volt outlets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 240 volt systems and 120 volt outlets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most modern North American homes are wired to receive 240 volts from the transformer, and through the use of <a href="/wiki/Split-phase_electric_power" title="Split-phase electric power">split-phase electrical power</a>, can have both 120 volt receptacles and 240 volt receptacles. The 120 volts is typically used for lighting and most <a href="/wiki/AC_power_plugs_and_sockets" title="AC power plugs and sockets">wall outlets</a>. The 240 volt outlets are usually located to service the oven and stovetop, water heater, and clothes dryer (if they are electric, rather than using natural gas). Sometimes a 240 volt outlet is mounted in the garage for machinery or for charging an <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric car</a>.</p>
