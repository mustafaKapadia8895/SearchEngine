<p>The <b>haddock</b> (<i>Melanogrammus aeglefinus</i>) is a saltwater fish, found in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and associated seas.</p>
<p>The haddock is easily recognized by a black <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> running along its white side (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Pollock" title="Pollock">pollock</a> which has the reverse, i.e., white line on black side) and a distinctive dark blotch above the <a href="/wiki/Fish_anatomy#Types_of_fin" title="Fish anatomy">pectoral fin</a>, often described as a "thumbprint" or even the "Devil's thumbprint" or "St. Peter's mark".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haddock is most commonly found at depths of 40 to 133 m (131 to 436 ft), but has a range as deep as 300 m (980 ft). It thrives in temperatures of 2 to 10 °C (36 to 50 °F). Juveniles prefer shallower waters and larger adults deeper water. Generally, adult haddock do not engage in long migratory behaviour as do the younger fish, but seasonal movements have been known to occur across all ages. Haddock feed primarily on small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, although larger members of the species may occasionally consume fish.</p>
<p>Haddock range in size between 38 and 69 centimeters in length and 0.9 to 1.8 kilograms in weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fisheries_and_Oceans_Canada_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fisheries_and_Oceans_Canada-2">[2]</a></sup> Growth rates of haddock have changed significantly over the past 30 to 40 years. Presently, growth is more rapid, with haddock reaching their adult size much earlier than previously noted. However, the degree to which these younger fish contribute to reproductive success of the population is unknown. Growth rates of haddock, however, had slowed in recent years. Some evidence indicates it may be the result of an exceptionally large year class in 2003. One<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (October 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">Spawning</a> occurs between January and June, peaking during late March and early April. The most important spawning grounds are in the waters off middle <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, near southwest <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bank" title="Georges Bank">Georges Bank</a>. An average-sized female produces approximately 850,000 eggs, and larger females are capable of producing up to 3 million eggs each year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haddock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cod and related species are plagued by parasites. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cod_worm" title="Cod worm">cod worm</a>, <i>Lernaeocera branchialis</i>, starts life as a <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepod</a>, a small, free-swimming crustacean <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larva</a>. The first host used by cod worm is a <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lumpsucker" title="Lumpsucker">lumpsucker</a>, which they capture with grasping hooks at the front of their bodies. They penetrate the lumpsucker with a thin <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/filament" title="wiktionary:filament">filament</a> which they use to suck its blood. The nourished cod worms then mate on the lumpsucker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matthews_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The female worm, with her now fertilized eggs, then finds a cod, or a cod-like fish such as a haddock or <a href="/wiki/Whiting_(fish)" title="Whiting (fish)">whiting</a>. There, the worm clings to the <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> while it metamorphoses into a plump, sinusoidal, wormlike body, with a coiled mass of egg strings at the rear. The front part of the worm's body penetrates the body of the cod until it enters the rear bulb of the host's <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>. There, firmly rooted in the cod's circulatory system, the front part of the parasite develops like the branches of a tree, reaching into the main <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">artery</a>. In this way, the worm extracts nutrients from the cod's blood, remaining safely tucked beneath the cod's gill cover until it releases a new generation of offspring into the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matthews_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piper-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haddock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haddock_fins.tiff"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="717" data-file-width="1704" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Haddock_fins.tiff/lossless-page1-220px-Haddock_fins.tiff.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Haddock_fins.tiff/lossless-page1-330px-Haddock_fins.tiff.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Haddock_fins.tiff/lossless-page1-440px-Haddock_fins.tiff.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Fish_fin" title="Fish fin">Fins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbel_(anatomy)" title="Barbel (anatomy)">barbel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> on a haddock. Haddock have three <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fins</a> and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fins</a>.</div>

<p>Reaching sizes up to 1.1 m (3 ft 7 in), haddock is fished for year-round. Some of the methods used are Danish <a href="/wiki/Seine_fishing" title="Seine fishing">seine nets</a>, trawlers, long lines and fishing nets. The commercial catch of haddock in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> had declined sharply in recent years, but is now recovering, with recruitment rates running around where they historically were from the 1930s to 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Greenpeace International has added the haddock to its seafood red list. "The Greenpeace International seafood red list is a list of fish that are commonly sold in supermarkets around the world, and which have a very high risk of being sourced from unsustainable fisheries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haddock populations on the offshore grounds of <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bank" title="Georges Bank">Georges Bank</a> off <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> have made a remarkable comeback with the adoption of catch shares management program, and are currently harvested at only a fraction of sustainable yields.</p>

<p>Haddock is a very popular food fish, sold fresh, smoked, frozen, dried, or to a small extent canned. Haddock, along with <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plaice" title="Plaice">plaice</a>, is one of the most popular fish used in British <a href="/wiki/Fish_and_chips" title="Fish and chips">fish and chips</a>.</p>
<p>Fresh haddock has clean, white flesh and can be cooked in the same ways as cod. Freshness of a haddock fillet can be determined by how well it holds together, as a fresh one will be firm; also, fillets should be translucent, while older fillets turn chalky (nearly opaque). Young, fresh haddock and cod fillets are often sold as <a href="/wiki/Scrod" title="Scrod">scrod</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>; this refers to the size of the fish which have a variety of sizes, i.e., scrod, markets, and cows. Haddock is the predominant fish of choice in Scotland in a fish supper. It is also the main ingredient of Norwegian fishballs (<i>fiskeboller</i>).</p>
<p>Unlike the related <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, haddock does not <a href="/wiki/Salted_fish" title="Salted fish">salt</a> well and is often preserved by <a href="/wiki/Dried_fish" title="Dried fish">drying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smoked_fish" title="Smoked fish">smoking</a>.</p>
<p>The smoking of haddock was highly refined in <a href="/wiki/Grimsby" title="Grimsby">Grimsby</a>. <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Grimsby_smoked_fish" title="Traditional Grimsby smoked fish">Traditional Grimsby smoked fish</a> (mainly haddock, but sometimes cod) is produced in the traditional smokehouses in Grimsby, which are mostly family-run businesses that have developed their skills over many generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtfs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gtfs-8">[8]</a></sup> Grimsby fish market sources its haddock from the North East Atlantic, principally Iceland, Norway and the Faroe Islands. These fishing grounds are sustainably managed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ice_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ice-9">[9]</a></sup> and have not seen the large scale depreciation in fish stocks seen in EU waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-eu-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>One popular form of haddock is <a href="/wiki/Finnan_haddie" title="Finnan haddie">Finnan haddie</a> which takes its name from the fishing village of <a href="/wiki/Findon,_Aberdeenshire" title="Findon, Aberdeenshire">Finnan or Findon</a> in Scotland, where it was originally cold-smoked over <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>. Finnan haddie is often served <a href="/wiki/Poaching_(cooking)" title="Poaching (cooking)">poached</a> in milk for <a href="/wiki/Breakfast" title="Breakfast">breakfast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town of <a href="/wiki/Arbroath" title="Arbroath">Arbroath</a> on the east coast of Scotland produces the <a href="/wiki/Arbroath_smokie" title="Arbroath smokie">Arbroath smokie</a>. This is a hot-smoked haddock which requires no further cooking before eating.</p>
<p>Smoked haddock naturally has an off-white colour; it is very often <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyed</a> yellow, as are other smoked fish. Smoked haddock is the essential ingredient in the Anglo-Indian dish <i><a href="/wiki/Kedgeree" title="Kedgeree">kedgeree</a></i>, and also in the Scottish dish "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cullen_Skink" title="Cullen Skink">Cullen Skink</a>" (a 'chowder' like soup).</p>
