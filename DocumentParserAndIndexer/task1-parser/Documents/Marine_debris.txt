<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Marine debris</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Marine debris</b>, also known as <b>marine litter</b>, is human-created <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a> that has deliberately or accidentally been released in a <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> or <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">waterway</a>. Floating oceanic debris tends to accumulate at the center of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyre" title="Gyre">gyres</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastlines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> frequently washing aground, when it is known as <i>beach litter</i> or tidewrack. Deliberate disposal of wastes at sea is called <i>ocean dumping</i>. Naturally occurring debris, such as <a href="/wiki/Driftwood" title="Driftwood">driftwood</a>, are also present.</p>
<p>With the increasing use of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>, human influence has become an issue as many types of plastics do not <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegrade</a>. Waterborne plastic poses a serious threat to <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_reptile" title="Marine reptile">marine reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammals</a>, as well as to boats and coasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facts_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Facts-2">[2]</a></sup> Dumping, container spillages, litter washed into storm drains and waterways and wind-blown landfill waste all contribute to this problem.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_debris">Types of debris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of debris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beach_at_Msasani_Bay,_Dar_es_Salaam,_Tanzania.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Beach_at_Msasani_Bay%2C_Dar_es_Salaam%2C_Tanzania.JPG/220px-Beach_at_Msasani_Bay%2C_Dar_es_Salaam%2C_Tanzania.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Beach_at_Msasani_Bay%2C_Dar_es_Salaam%2C_Tanzania.JPG/330px-Beach_at_Msasani_Bay%2C_Dar_es_Salaam%2C_Tanzania.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Beach_at_Msasani_Bay%2C_Dar_es_Salaam%2C_Tanzania.JPG/440px-Beach_at_Msasani_Bay%2C_Dar_es_Salaam%2C_Tanzania.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Researchers classify debris as either land- or ocean-based; in 1991, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> Joint Group of Experts on the Scientific Aspects of Marine Pollution estimated that up to 80% of the pollution was land-based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> More recent studies have found that more than half of plastic debris found on Korean shores is ocean-based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Anthropogeny" title="Anthropogeny">anthropogenic</a> artifacts can become marine debris; <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bag" title="Plastic bag">plastic bags</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">buoys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">rope</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_waste" title="Medical waste">medical waste</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_bottles" title="Glass bottles">glass bottles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_bottles" title="Plastic bottles">plastic bottles</a>, cigarette stubs, <a href="/wiki/Lighter" title="Lighter">cigarette lighters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beverage_can" title="Beverage can">beverage cans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">lost fishing line and nets</a>, and various wastes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_ship_pollution" title="Cruise ship pollution">cruise ships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil rigs</a> are among the items commonly found to have washed ashore. <a href="/wiki/Six_pack_rings" title="Six pack rings">Six pack rings</a>, in particular, are considered emblematic of the problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The US military used ocean dumping for unused weapons and bombs, including ordinary bombs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UXO" title="UXO">UXO</a>, landmines and chemical weapons from at least 1919 until 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wbgh_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wbgh-6">[6]</a></sup> Millions of pounds of ordnance were disposed of in the Gulf of Mexico and off the coasts of at least 16 states, from New Jersey to Hawaii (although these, of course, do not wash up onshore, and the US is not the only country who has practiced this).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-me_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-me-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eighty percent of marine debris is plastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weisman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weisman-8">[8]</a></sup> Plastics accumulate because they typically do not <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegrade</a> as many other substances do. They <a href="/wiki/Photodegradation" title="Photodegradation">photodegrade</a> on exposure to sunlight, although they do so only under dry conditions, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> inhibits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photolysis" title="Photolysis">photolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In a 2014 study using computers models, scientists from the group 5 Gyres, estimate 5.25 trillion pieces of plastic weighing 269,000 tons dispersed in oceans in similar amount in the Northern and Southern Hemispheres, and one-hundredth of them in particles in the scale of a sand-size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ghost_nets">Ghost nets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ghost nets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">Fishing nets</a> left or lost in the ocean by fishermen – <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">ghost nets</a> – can entangle <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dugong" title="Dugong">dugongs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and other creatures. These nets restrict movement, causing starvation, laceration and infection, and, in animals that breathe air, suffocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic">Plastic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plastic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>8.8 million metric tons of plastic waste are dumped in the world's oceans each year. Asia was the leading source of mismanaged plastic waste, with China alone accounting for 2.4 million metric tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plastic waste has reached all the world's oceans. This <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution#Effects_on_animals" title="Plastic pollution">plastic pollution</a> harms an estimated 100,000 sea turtles and marine mammals and 1,000,000 sea creatures each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STRP_Ban_Plastic_Bags_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-STRP_Ban_Plastic_Bags-14">[14]</a></sup> Larger plastics (called "macroplastics") such as <a href="/wiki/Plastic_shopping_bag" title="Plastic shopping bag">plastic shopping bags</a> can clog the digestive tracts of these larger animals when consumed by them<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP-15">[15]</a></sup> and can cause starvation through restricting the movement of food, or by filling the stomach and tricking the animal into thinking it is full. <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">Microplastics</a> on the other hand harm smaller marine life. Pelagic plastic pieces in the center of our ocean’s gyres for example outnumber live marine plankton, and are passed up the food chain to reach all marine life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_et_al.2C_2007_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore_et_al.2C_2007-16">[16]</a></sup> A 1994 study of the <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl_net" title="Trawl net">trawl nets</a> in the North-Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> around the coasts of Spain, France and Italy reported mean concentrations of debris of 1,935 items per square kilometre. Plastic debris accounted for 77%, of which 93% was plastic bags.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plastic_particle_water_pollution" title="Plastic particle water pollution">Nurdles</a>, also known as "mermaids' tears", are plastic pellets, typically under five millimetres in diameter, that are a major component of marine debris. They are a raw material in plastics manufacturing, and enter the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a> when spilled. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_weathering" title="Physical weathering">Weathering</a> produces ever smaller pieces. Nurdles strongly resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_eggs" title="Fish eggs">fish eggs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep-sea_debris">Deep-sea debris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Deep-sea debris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Litter, made from diverse materials that are denser than surface water (such as <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a>, metals and some plastics), have been found to spread over the floor of seas and open oceans, where it can become entangled in <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> and interfere with other <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">sea-floor life</a>, or even become buried under sediment, making <a href="/wiki/Marine_salvage" title="Marine salvage">clean-up</a> extremely difficult, especially due to the wide area of its dispersal compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipwrecks" title="Shipwrecks">shipwrecks</a>. Research performed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MBARI" title="MBARI">MBARI</a> found items including plastic bags <a href="/wiki/Bathyal_zone" title="Bathyal zone">below 2000m depth</a> off the west coast of North America and around Hawaii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBARI_News_Release_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-MBARI_News_Release-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_debris">Sources of debris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sources of debris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Friendly_Floatees.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="523" data-file-width="850" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Friendly_Floatees.png/220px-Friendly_Floatees.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Friendly_Floatees.png/330px-Friendly_Floatees.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Friendly_Floatees.png/440px-Friendly_Floatees.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg/220px-Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg/330px-Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg/440px-Balluta_Bay_Treibgut_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>An estimated 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Containerization" title="Containerization">containers</a> at sea each year are lost by <a href="/wiki/Container_ship" title="Container ship">container ships</a>, usually during storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> One spillage occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> in 1992, when thousands of <a href="/wiki/Friendly_Floatees" title="Friendly Floatees">rubber ducks and other toys</a> (now known as the "Friendly Floatees") went overboard during a storm. The toys have since been found all over the world, providing a better understanding of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>. Similar incidents have happened before, such as when <i><a href="/wiki/Hansa_Carrier" title="Hansa Carrier">Hansa Carrier</a></i> dropped 21 containers (with one notably containing buoyant <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a> shoes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2007, <a href="/wiki/MSC_Napoli" title="MSC Napoli">MSC Napoli</a> beached in the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, dropping hundreds of containers, most of which washed up on the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Coast" title="Jurassic Coast">Jurassic Coast</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Halifax Harbour, Nova Scotia, 52% of items were generated by recreational use of an urban park, 14% from sewage disposal and only 7% from shipping and fishing activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Around four fifths<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Algalita_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Algalita-23">[23]</a></sup> of oceanic debris is from rubbish blown onto the water from <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facts_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Facts-2">[2]</a></sup> In the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Syringe_Tide" title="Syringe Tide">Syringe Tide</a>, medical waste washed ashore in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> after having been blown from <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kills_Landfill" title="Fresh Kills Landfill">Fresh Kills Landfill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On the remote sub-Antarctic island of <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_and_the_South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Georgia and the South Sandwich Islands">South Georgia</a>, fishing-related debris, approximately 80% plastics, are responsible for the entanglement of large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_fur_seal" title="Antarctic fur seal">Antarctic fur seals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marine litter is even found on the floor of the Arctic ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Great Pacific Garbage Patch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_garbage_patch" title="Great Pacific garbage patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="400" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg/220px-North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg/330px-North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1f/North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_Pacific_Subtropical_Convergence_Zone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The currents of the North Pacific Gyre spiral inwards, depositing debris in the convergence zone</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Once waterborne, debris becomes mobile. Flotsam can be blown by the wind, or follow the flow of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>, often ending up in the middle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_gyre" title="Oceanic gyre">oceanic gyres</a> where currents are weakest. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch" title="Great Pacific Garbage Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a> is one such example of this, comprising a vast region of the North <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> rich with anthropogenic wastes. Estimated to be double the size of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, the area contains more than 3 million tons of plastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In fact, patches may be large enough to be picked up by satellites. For example, when the Malaysian Flight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MH370" title="MH370">MH370</a>, disappeared in 2014, satellites were scanning the oceans surface for any sign of it, and instead of finding debris from the plane they came across floating garbage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup> The gyre contains approximately six pounds of plastic for every pound of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The oceans may contain as much as one hundred million <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of plastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Algalita_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Algalita-23">[23]</a></sup> Its estimated that each garbage patch in the ocean have up to one million tons of trash swirling around in them, sometimes extending down to around one hundred feet below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Some items that have been extracted from these garbage patches are: a drum of hazardous chemicals, plastic hangers, tires, cable cords, a ton of tangled netting etc.</p>
<p>Over 40% of oceans are classified as subtropical gyres, a fourth of the planets surface area has become an accumulator of floating plastic debris.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">Islands</a> situated within gyres frequently have coastlines flooded by waste that washes ashore; prime examples are <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">Midway</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midway_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-midway-32">[32]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Clean-up teams around the world patrol beaches to attack this environmental threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midway_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-midway-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_(8080507529).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1575" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_%288080507529%29.jpg/220px-Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_%288080507529%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_%288080507529%29.jpg/330px-Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_%288080507529%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_%288080507529%29.jpg/440px-Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_%288080507529%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Albatross_at_Midway_Atoll_Refuge_(8080507529).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Remains of an <a href="/wiki/Laysan_albatross" title="Laysan albatross">albatross</a> containing ingested flotsam</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many animals that live on or in the sea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feeding" title="Feeding">consume</a> flotsam by mistake, as it often looks similar to their natural prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Bulky plastic debris may become permanently lodged in the digestive tracts of these animals, blocking the passage of food and causing death through starvation or infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Tiny floating plastic particles also resemble <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, which can lead <a href="/wiki/Filter_feeder" title="Filter feeder">filter feeders</a> to consume them and cause them to enter the ocean <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. In samples taken from the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Gyre" title="North Pacific Gyre">North Pacific Gyre</a> in 1999 by the Algalita Marine Research Foundation, the mass of plastic exceeded that of zooplankton by a factor of six.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weisman_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weisman-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlgalitaVid_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlgalitaVid-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_(ghost_net).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="463" data-file-width="700" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_%28ghost_net%29.jpg/220px-Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_%28ghost_net%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_%28ghost_net%29.jpg/330px-Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_%28ghost_net%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_%28ghost_net%29.jpg/440px-Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_%28ghost_net%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turtle_entangled_in_marine_debris_(ghost_net).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_turtle" title="Green turtle">turtle</a> trapped in a <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">ghost net</a>, an abandoned fishing net</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Toxic additives used in plastic manufacturing can <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leach" title="wikt:leach">leach</a> into their surroundings when exposed to water. Waterborne <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> pollutants <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">collect and magnify on the surface</a> of plastic debris,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Algalita_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Algalita-23">[23]</a></sup> thus making plastic more deadly in the ocean than it would be on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weisman_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weisman-8">[8]</a></sup> Hydrophobic contaminants <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulate</a> in fatty tissues, <a href="/wiki/Biomagnification" title="Biomagnification">biomagnifying</a> up the food chain and pressuring <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a> and humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engler_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Engler-37">[37]</a></sup> Some plastic additives disrupt the <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine system</a> when consumed; others can suppress the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a> or decrease reproductive rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlgalitaVid_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-AlgalitaVid-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hydrophobic nature of plastic surfaces stimulates rapid formation of <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilms</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reisser_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reisser-38">[38]</a></sup> which support a wide range of metabolic activities, and drive succession of other micro- and macro-organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Concern among experts has grown since the 2000s that some organisms have <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adapted</a> to live on<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> floating plastic debris, allowing them to disperse with ocean currents and thus potentially become <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in distant ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Research in 2014 in the waters around Australia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reisser_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reisser-38">[38]</a></sup> confirmed a wealth of such colonists, even on tiny flakes, and also found thriving ocean bacteria eating into the plastic to form pits and grooves. These researchers showed that "plastic biodegradation is occurring at the sea surface" through the action of bacteria, and noted that this is congruent with a new body of research on such bacteria. Their finding is also congruent with the other major research undertaken<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> in 2014, which sought to answer the riddle of the overall lack of build up of floating plastic in the oceans, despite ongoing high levels of dumping. Plastics were found as microfibres in core samples drilled from sediments at the bottom of the deep ocean. The cause of such widespread deep sea deposition has yet to be determined.</p>
<p>Not all <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> artifacts placed in the oceans are harmful. Iron and concrete structures typically do little damage to the environment because they generally sink to the bottom and become immobile, and at shallow depths they can even provide scaffolding for <a href="/wiki/Artificial_reef" title="Artificial reef">artificial reefs</a>. Ships and subway cars have been deliberately sunk for that purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Hermit_crab" title="Hermit crab">hermit crabs</a> have been known to use pieces of beach litter as a shell when they cannot find an actual seashell of the size they need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debris_removal">Debris removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Debris removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2130" data-file-width="1927" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg/220px-DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg/330px-DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg/440px-DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DC_WASA_Marine_Debris_Skimmer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skimmer boat used to remove floating debris and trash from the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anacostia_River" title="Anacostia River">Anacostia</a> rivers</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Techniques for collecting and removing marine (or riverine) debris include the use of debris skimmer boats <i>(pictured)</i>. Devices such as these can be used where floating debris presents a danger to navigation. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="US Army Corps of Engineers">US Army Corps of Engineers</a> removes 90 tons of "drifting material" from <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> every month. The Corps has been doing this work since 1942, when a <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">seaplane</a> carrying Admiral <a href="/wiki/Chester_W._Nimitz" title="Chester W. Nimitz">Chester W. Nimitz</a> collided with a piece of floating debris and sank, costing the life of its pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Once debris becomes "beach litter", collection by hand and specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_cleaner" title="Beach cleaner">beach-cleaning machines</a> are used to gather the debris.</p>
<p>Elsewhere, "trash traps" are installed on small rivers to capture waterborne debris before it reaches the sea. For example, <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> operates a number of such traps, known as "trash racks" or "gross pollutant traps" on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torrens_River" title="Torrens River">Torrens River</a>, which flows (during the wet season) into <a href="/wiki/Gulf_St_Vincent" title="Gulf St Vincent">Gulf St Vincent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In lakes or near the coast, manual removal can also be used. <a href="/wiki/Project_AWARE" title="Project AWARE">Project AWARE</a> for example promotes the idea of letting dive clubs clean up litter, for example as a diving exercise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the sea, the removal of artificial debris (i.e. plastics) is still in its infancy. However some projects have been started which used ships with nets (Kaisei and New Horizon) to catch some plastics, primarily for research purposes. Another method to gather artificial litter has been proposed by <a href="/wiki/Boyan_Slat" title="Boyan Slat">Boyan Slat</a>. He suggested using platforms with arms to gather the debris, situated inside the current of gyres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another issue is that removing marine debris from our oceans can potentially cause more harm than good. Cleaning up micro-plastics could also accidentally take out plankton, which are the main lower level food group for the marine food chain and over half of the photosynthesis on earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup> One of the most efficient and cost effective ways to help reduce the amount of plastic entering our oceans is to not participate in using single use plastics, avoid plastic bottled drinks such as water bottles, use reusable shopping bags, and to buy products with reusable packaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once a year there is a diving marine debris removal operation in <a href="/wiki/Scapa_Flow" title="Scapa Flow">Scapa Flow</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkneys</a>, run by <a class="external text" href="http://www.ghostfishing.org/" rel="nofollow">Ghost Fishing UK</a>, funded by <a href="/wiki/World_Animal_Protection" title="World Animal Protection">World Animal Protection</a> and <a class="external text" href="https://www.fatfacefoundation.org/" rel="nofollow">Fat Face Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic-to-fuel_conversion_strategy">Plastic-to-fuel conversion strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Plastic-to-fuel conversion strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Clean_Oceans_Project" title="The Clean Oceans Project">The Clean Oceans Project (TCOP)</a> promotes conversion of the plastic waste into valuable liquid fuels, including <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>, using plastic-to-fuel conversion technology developed by Blest Co. Ltd., a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Environmental_engineering" title="Environmental engineering">environmental engineering</a> company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jimholm_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-jimholm-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> TCOP plans to educate local communities and create a financial <a href="/wiki/Incentive" title="Incentive">incentive</a> for them to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recycle" title="Recycle">recycle</a> plastic, keep their shorelines clean, and minimize plastic waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jimholm_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-jimholm-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laws_and_treaties">Laws and treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Laws and treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ocean dumping is controlled by <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Prevention_of_Marine_Pollution_by_Dumping_of_Wastes_and_Other_Matter" title="Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other Matter">The London Convention</a> (1972) – a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> agreement to control ocean dumping<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">MARPOL 73/78</a> – a convention designed to minimize pollution of the seas, including dumping, oil and exhaust pollution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_law">European law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: European law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1972 and 1974, conventions were held in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> respectively, and resulted in the passing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Marine_Environment_of_the_North-East_Atlantic" title="Convention for the Protection of the Marine Environment of the North-East Atlantic">OSPAR Convention</a>, an international treaty controlling marine pollution in the north-east <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Barcelona_Convention" title="Barcelona Convention">Barcelona Convention</a> protects the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Water_Framework_Directive" title="Water Framework Directive">Water Framework Directive</a> of 2000 is a <a href="/wiki/Directive_(European_Union)" title="Directive (European Union)">European Union directive</a> committing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_State_of_the_European_Union" title="Member State of the European Union">EU member states</a> to free inland and coastal waters from human influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Marine_and_Coastal_Access_Act_2009" title="Marine and Coastal Access Act 2009">Marine and Coastal Access Act 2009</a> is designed to "ensure clean healthy, safe, productive and biologically diverse oceans and seas, by putting in place better systems for delivering sustainable development of marine and coastal environment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_law">United States law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United States law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1972, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> passed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Dumping_Act" title="Ocean Dumping Act">Ocean Dumping Act</a>, giving the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> power to monitor and regulate the dumping of sewage sludge, industrial waste, radioactive waste and biohazardous materials into the nation's territorial waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The Act was amended sixteen years later to include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_waste" title="Medical waste">medical wastes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> It is illegal to dispose of any plastic in US waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facts_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Facts-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Property_law" title="Property law">Property law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_law" title="Admiralty law">admiralty law</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">law of the sea</a> may be of relevance when <a href="/wiki/Lost,_mislaid,_and_abandoned_property" title="Lost, mislaid, and abandoned property">lost, mislaid, and abandoned property</a> is found at sea. <a href="/wiki/Marine_salvage" title="Marine salvage">Salvage law</a> rewards salvors for risking life and property to rescue the property of another from peril. On land the distinction between deliberate and accidental loss led to the concept of a "<a href="/wiki/Treasure_trove" title="Treasure trove">treasure trove</a>". In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, shipwrecked goods should be reported to a <a href="/wiki/Receiver_of_Wreck" title="Receiver of Wreck">Receiver of Wreck</a>, and if identifiable, they should be returned to their rightful owner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activism">Activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_debris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A large number of groups and individuals are active in preventing or educating about marine debris. For example, <a href="/wiki/5_Gyres" title="5 Gyres">5 Gyres</a> is an organization aimed at reducing plastics pollution in the oceans, and was one of two organizations that recently researched the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch" title="Great Pacific Garbage Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a>. <a href="/wiki/Heal_the_Bay" title="Heal the Bay">Heal the Bay</a> is another organization, focusing on protecting <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Bay" title="Santa Monica Bay">Santa Monica Bay</a>, by sponsoring beach cleanup programs along with other activities. <a href="/wiki/Marina_DeBris" title="Marina DeBris">Marina DeBris</a> is an artist focusing most of her recent work on educating people about beach trash. Interactive sites like Adrift<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> demonstrate where marine plastic is carried, over time, on the worlds ocean currents. On April 11, 2013 in order to create awareness, artist <a href="/wiki/Maria_Cristina_Finucci" title="Maria Cristina Finucci">Maria Cristina Finucci</a> founded The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garbage_patch_state" title="Garbage patch state">Garbage patch state</a> at <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> –Paris in front of Director General <a href="/wiki/Irina_Bokova" title="Irina Bokova">Irina Bokova</a> . First of a series of events under the patronage of <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> and of Italian Ministry of the Environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Forty-eight plastics manufacturers from 25 countries, are members of the Global Plastic Associations for solutions on Marine Litter, have made the pledge to help prevent marine debris and to encourage recycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
