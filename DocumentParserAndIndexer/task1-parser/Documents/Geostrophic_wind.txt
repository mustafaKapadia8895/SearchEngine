<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Geostrophic wind</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>geostrophic wind</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>-, -<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>-/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) is the theoretical <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> that would result from an exact balance between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis force</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a> force. This condition is called <b>geostrophic balance</b>. The geostrophic wind is directed <a href="/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)" title="Parallel (geometry)">parallel</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">isobars</a> (lines of constant <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> at a given height). This balance seldom holds exactly in nature. The true wind almost always differs from the geostrophic wind due to other forces such as <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> from the ground. Thus, the actual wind would equal the geostrophic wind only if there were no friction and the isobars were perfectly straight. Despite this, much of the atmosphere outside the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> is close to geostrophic flow much of the time and it is a valuable first approximation. Geostrophic flow in air or water is a zero-frequency <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_waves" title="Inertial waves">inertial wave</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostrophic_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">Air</a> naturally moves from areas of high <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> to areas of low pressure, due to the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a> force. As soon as the air starts to move, however, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis "force"</a> deflects it. The <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deflection" title="wikt:deflection">deflection</a> is to the right in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a>, and to the left in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a>. As the air moves from the high-pressure area, its speed increases, and so does its Coriolis deflection. The deflection increases until the Coriolis and pressure gradient forces are in geostrophic balance: at this point, the air flow is no longer moving from high to low pressure, but instead moves along an <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isobar" title="wikt:isobar">isobar</a>. (Note that this explanation assumes that the atmosphere starts in a geostrophically unbalanced state and describes how such a state would evolve into a balanced flow. In practice, the flow is nearly always balanced.) The geostrophic balance helps to explain why, in the northern hemisphere, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-pressure_system" title="Low-pressure system">low-pressure systems</a> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a></i>) spin counterclockwise and <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure systems</a> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclones</a></i>) spin clockwise, and the opposite in the southern hemisphere.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geostrophic_currents">Geostrophic currents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostrophic_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geostrophic currents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_current" title="Geostrophic current">Geostrophic current</a></div>
<p>Flow of ocean water is also largely geostrophic. Just as multiple weather balloons that measure pressure as a function of height in the atmosphere are used to map the atmospheric pressure field and infer the geostrophic wind, measurements of density as a function of depth in the ocean are used to infer geostrophic currents. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_altimetry" title="Satellite altimetry">Satellite altimeters</a> are also used to measure sea surface height anomaly, which permits a calculation of the geostrophic current at the surface.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_the_geostrophic_approximation">Limitations of the geostrophic approximation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostrophic_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Limitations of the geostrophic approximation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The effect of friction, between the air and the land, breaks the geostrophic balance. Friction slows the flow, lessening the effect of the Coriolis force. As a result, the pressure gradient force has a greater effect and the air still moves from high pressure to low pressure, though with great deflection. This explains why high-pressure system winds radiate out from the center of the system, while low-pressure systems have winds that spiral inwards.</p>
<p>The geostrophic wind neglects <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">frictional</a> effects, which is usually a good <a href="/wiki/Approximation" title="Approximation">approximation</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> instantaneous flow in the midlatitude mid-<a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ageostrophic" title="Ageostrophic">ageostrophic</a> terms are relatively small, they are essential for the time evolution of the flow and in particular are necessary for the growth and decay of storms. <a href="/wiki/Quasi-geostrophic_equations" title="Quasi-geostrophic equations">Quasigeostrophic</a> and Semigeostrophic theory are used to model flows in the atmosphere more widely. These theories allow for divergence to take place and for weather systems to then develop.</p>
