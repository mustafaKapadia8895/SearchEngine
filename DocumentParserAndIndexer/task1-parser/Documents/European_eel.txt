<p>The <b>European eel</b> (<i>Anguilla anguilla</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eel</a>, a snake-like, <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">catadromous</a> fish. They can reach a length of 1.5Â m (4Â ft 11Â in) in exceptional cases, but are normally around 60â80Â cm (2.0â2.6Â ft), and rarely reach more than 1Â m (3Â ft 3Â in). While captive specimens have lived over 85 years, the species' lifespan in the wild has not been determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European eel is a <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> Since the 1970s, the numbers of eels reaching Europe is thought to have declined by around 90% (possibly even 98%). Contributing factors include <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a> such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anguillicola_crassus" title="Anguillicola crassus">Anguillicola crassus</a></i>, barriers to migration such as <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> dams, and natural changes in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">North Atlantic oscillation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_drift" title="North Atlantic drift">North Atlantic drift</a>. Recent work suggests <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> may be a factor in the decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eels have been important sources of food both as adults (including the famous <a href="/wiki/Jellied_eels" title="Jellied eels">jellied eels</a> of <a href="/wiki/East_End_of_London" title="East End of London">East London</a>) and as glass eels. Glass-eel fishing using basket traps has been of significant economic value in many river estuaries on the western seaboard of Europe.</p>
<p>In captivity, European eels can live for very long times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fiskeriverket_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fiskeriverket-6">[6]</a></sup> According to a report in <a href="/wiki/The_Local" title="The Local">The Local</a>, a specimen lived 155 years in the well of a family home in <a href="/wiki/Brantevik" title="Brantevik">Brantevik</a>, a fishing village in southern Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_consumption">Sustainable consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sustainable consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenpeace_International" title="Greenpeace International">Greenpeace International</a> added the European eel to its seafood red list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2010 The Sustainable Eel Group launched the Sustainable Eel Standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_projects">Breeding projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the European eel population has been falling for some time, several projects have been started. In 1997, <a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/InnovatieNetwerk" title="nl:InnovatieNetwerk">Innovatie Netwerk</a> in the Netherlands initiated a project where they attempted to get European eels to breed in captivity by simulating the 6,500Â km (4,000Â mi) journey from Europe to the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Swimming_machine" title="Swimming machine">swimming machine</a> for the fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The first to achieve some success was DTU Aqua, a part of the <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Denmark" title="Technical University of Denmark">Technical University of Denmark</a>. Through a combination of fresh and salt water, as well as hormones, they were able to breed it in captivity in 2006 and make the larvae survive for 4.5 days after hatching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By 2007, DTU Aqua scientists were able to set a new record where the larvae survived for 12 days by feeding the mother eel with a special <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a>-enriched diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At this age the content of the larval <a href="/wiki/Yolk_sac" title="Yolk sac">yolk sac</a> has been used, the mouth and digestive channel have developed, and it requires feeding. Attempts with various substances failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gal3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gal3-14">[14]</a></sup> Deep water sampling of the presumed habitat of larval European eel in the Sargasso Sea was performed by the <a href="/wiki/Galathea_expeditions" title="Galathea expeditions">Galathea</a> 3 expedition in 2006â07, in the hope of revealing the likely feeding preference at the early stage. Their results indicated that they feed on various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planktonic" title="Planktonic">planktonic</a> organisms, but especially microscopic <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gal3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gal3-14">[14]</a></sup> A follow-up expedition was performed by DTU's own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Research_ship" title="Research ship">research ship</a> to the Sargasso Sea region in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>To further the research, the PRO-EEL project, lead by DTU Aqua and involving several research institutes elsewhere in Denmark (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen" title="University of Copenhagen">University of Copenhagen</a> and others), Norway (<a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Fisheries_and_Coastal_Affairs_(Norway)" title="Ministry of Fisheries and Coastal Affairs (Norway)">Norwegian Institute of Fisheries and Food Research</a> and others), the Netherlands (<a href="/wiki/Leiden_University" title="Leiden University">Leiden University</a> and others), Belgium (<a href="/wiki/Ghent_University" title="Ghent University">Ghent University</a>), France (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_National_Center_for_Scientific_Research" title="French National Center for Scientific Research">French National Center for Scientific Research</a> and others), Spain (ICTA at <a href="/wiki/Polytechnic_University_of_Valencia" title="Polytechnic University of Valencia">Polytechnic University of Valencia</a>) and Tunisia (National Institute of Marine Sciences and Technologies), was started in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> By 2014, the eel larvae at their facilities typically survive 20â22 days, but the full <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life cycle</a> has still not been completed in captivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_history">Life history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Life history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eel_life_history" title="Eel life history">Eel life history</a></div>
<p>Much of the European eelâs <a href="/wiki/Eel_life_history" title="Eel life history">life history</a> was a mystery for centuries, as fishermen never caught anything they could identify as a young eel. Unlike many other migrating fish, eels begin their life cycle in the ocean and spend most of their lives in fresh inland water, or brackish coastal water, returning to the ocean to spawn and then die. In the early 1900s, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> researcher <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Schmidt_(biologist)" title="Johannes Schmidt (biologist)">Johannes Schmidt</a> identified the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a> as the most likely spawning grounds for European eels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Leptocephalus" title="Leptocephalus">leptocephali</a>) drift towards Europe in a 300-day migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> When approaching the European coast, the larvae metamorphose into a transparent larval stage called "glass eel", enter estuaries, and many start migrating upstream. After entering their continental habitat, the glass eels metamorphose into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elvers" title="Elvers">elvers</a>, miniature versions of the adult eels. As the eel grows, it becomes known as a "yellow eel" due to the brownish-yellow color of their sides and belly. After 5â20 years in fresh or brackish water, the eels become sexually mature, their eyes grow larger, their flanks become silver, and their bellies white in color. In this stage, the eels are known as "silver eels", and they begin their migration back to the Sargasso Sea to spawn.</p>
