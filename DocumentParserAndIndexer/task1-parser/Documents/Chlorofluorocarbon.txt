<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chlorofluorocarbon</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Chlorofluorocarbons</b> (<b>CFCs</b>) are fully halogenated paraffin hydrocarbons that contain only <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, produced as <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> derivative of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>. They are also commonly known by the <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a> brand name <b><a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a></b>. The most common representative is <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodifluoromethane" title="Dichlorodifluoromethane">dichlorodifluoromethane</a> (R-12 or Freon-12). Many CFCs have been widely used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigerants" title="Refrigerants">refrigerants</a>, propellants (in aerosol applications), and solvents. Because CFCs contribute to <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> in the upper <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, the manufacture of such compounds has been phased out under the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>, and they are being replaced with other products such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbon" title="Hydrofluorocarbon">hydrofluorocarbons</a> (HFCs)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/R-410A" title="R-410A">R-410A</a>) and R-134a.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span id="Structure,_properties_and_production"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure.2C_properties_and_production">Structure, properties and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure, properties and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organofluorine_chemistry" title="Organofluorine chemistry">Organofluorine chemistry</a></div>
<p>As in simpler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a>, carbon in the CFCs bonds with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahedral" title="Tetrahedral">tetrahedral</a> symmetry. Because the fluorine and chlorine atoms differ greatly in size and effective charge from hydrogen and from each other, the methane-derived CFCs deviate from perfect tetrahedral symmetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The physical properties of CFCs and HCFCs are tunable by changes in the number and identity of the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> atoms. In general they are volatile, but less so than their parent alkanes. The decreased volatility is attributed to the molecular polarity induced by the <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a>, which induces intermolecular interactions. Thus, methane boils at −161 °C whereas the fluoromethanes boil between −51.7 (CF<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>) and −128 °C (CF<sub>4</sub>). The CFCs have still higher boiling points because the chloride is even more polarizable than fluoride. Because of their polarity, the CFCs are useful solvents, and their boiling points make them suitable as refrigerants. The CFCs are far less flammable than methane, in part because they contain fewer C-H bonds and in part because, in the case of the chlorides and bromides, the released halides quench the free radicals that sustain flames.</p>
<p>The densities of CFCs are higher than their corresponding alkanes. In general the density of these compounds correlates with the number of chlorides.</p>
<p>CFCs and HCFCs are usually produced by halogen exchange starting from chlorinated methanes and ethanes. Illustrative is the synthesis of chlorodifluoromethane from <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>HCCl<sub>3</sub> + 2 HF → HCF<sub>2</sub>Cl + 2 HCl</dd>
</dl>
<p>The brominated derivatives are generated by free-radical reactions of the chlorofluorocarbons, replacing C-H bonds with C-Br bonds. The production of the <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Halothane" title="Halothane">2-bromo-2-chloro-1,1,1-trifluoroethane</a> ("halothane") is illustrative:</p>
<dl>
<dd>CF<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>Cl + Br<sub>2</sub> → CF<sub>3</sub>CHBrCl + HBr</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most important reaction of the CFCs is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical_reaction" title="Photochemical reaction">photo-induced scission</a> of a C-Cl bond:</p>
<dl>
<dd>CCl<sub>3</sub>F → CCl<sub>2</sub>F<sup><b>.</b></sup> + Cl<sup><b>.</b></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The chlorine atom, written often as Cl<sup><b>.</b></sup>, behaves very differently from the chlorine molecule (Cl<sub>2</sub>). The radical Cl<sup><b>.</b></sup> is long-lived in the upper atmosphere, where it catalyzes the conversion of ozone into O<sub>2</sub>. Ozone absorbs UV-B radiation, so its depletion allows more of this high energy radiation to reach the Earth's surface. <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Bromine</a> atoms are even more efficient catalysts; hence brominated CFCs are also regulated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CFCs and HCFCs are used in a variety of applications because of their low toxicity, reactivity and flammability. Every permutation of fluorine, chlorine and hydrogen based on methane and ethane has been examined and most have been commercialized. Furthermore, many examples are known for higher numbers of carbon as well as related compounds containing bromine. Uses include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigerants" title="Refrigerants">refrigerants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blowing_agent" title="Blowing agent">blowing agents</a>, propellants in medicinal applications and degreasing solvents.</p>
<p>Billions of kilograms of chlorodifluoromethane are produced annually as precursor to <a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoroethylene" title="Tetrafluoroethylene">tetrafluoroethylene</a>, the monomer that is converted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teflon" title="Teflon">Teflon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Classes_of_compounds,_nomenclature"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Classes_of_compounds.2C_nomenclature">Classes of compounds, nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classes of compounds, nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs): when derived from methane and ethane these compounds have the formulae CCl<sub>m</sub>F<sub>4−m</sub> and C<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>m</sub>F<sub>6−m</sub>, where m is nonzero.</li>
<li>Hydro-chlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs): when derived from methane and ethane these compounds have the formula CCl<sub>m</sub>F<sub>n</sub>H<sub>4−m−n</sub> and C<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>x</sub>F<sub>y</sub>H<sub>6−x−y</sub>, where m, n, x, and y are nonzero.</li>
<li>and <a href="/wiki/Bromofluorocarbon" title="Bromofluorocarbon">bromofluorocarbons</a> have formulae similar to the CFCs and HCFCs but also include bromine.</li>
<li>Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs): when derived from methane, ethane, <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>, these compounds have the respective formulae CF<sub>m</sub>H<sub>4−m</sub>, C<sub>2</sub>F<sub>m</sub>H<sub>6−m</sub>, C<sub>3</sub>F<sub>m</sub>H<sub>8−m</sub>, and C<sub>4</sub>F<sub>m</sub>H<sub>10−m</sub>, where m is nonzero.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbering_system">Numbering system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Numbering system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant#Refrigerants_by_class" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant § Refrigerants by class</a></div>
<p>A special numbering system is used for fluorinated alkanes, prefixed with Freon-, R-, CFC- and HCFC-, where the rightmost value indicates the number of fluorine atoms, the next value to the left is the number of hydrogen atoms <i>plus</i> 1, and the next value to the left is the number of carbon atoms <i>less</i> one (zeroes are not stated), and the remaining atoms are <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>.</p>
<p>Freon-12, for example, indicates a methane derivative (only two numbers) containing two fluorine atoms (the second 2) and no hydrogen (1-1=0). It is therefore CCl<sub>2</sub>F<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>Another, easier equation that can be applied to get the correct molecular formula of the CFC/R/Freon class compounds is this to take the numbering and add 90 to it. The resulting value will give the number of carbons as the first numeral, the second numeral gives the number of hydrogen atoms, and the third numeral gives the number of fluorine atoms. The rest of the unaccounted carbon bonds are occupied by chlorine atoms. The value of this equation is always a three figure number. An easy example is that of CFC-12, which gives: 90+12=102 -&gt; 1 carbon, 0 hydrogens, 2 fluorine atoms, and hence 2 chlorine atoms resulting in CCl<sub>2</sub>F<sub>2</sub>. The main advantage of this method of deducing the molecular composition in comparison with the method described in the paragraph above is that it gives the number of carbon atoms of the molecule.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">Carbon tetrachloride</a> (CCl<sub>4</sub>) was used in fire extinguishers and glass "anti-fire grenades" from the late nineteenth century until around the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Experimentation with chloroalkanes for fire suppression on military <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> began at least as early as the 1920s. <i><a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a></i> is a trade name for a group of CFCs which are used primarily as <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>, but also have uses in fire-fighting and as propellants in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosol_can" title="Aerosol can">aerosol cans</a>. Bromomethane is widely used as a fumigant. Dichloromethane is a versatile industrial solvent.</p>
<p>The Belgian scientist <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Swarts" title="Frédéric Swarts">Frédéric Swarts</a> pioneered the synthesis of CFCs in the 1890s. He developed an effective exchange agent to replace chloride in carbon tetrachloride with fluoride to synthesize CFC-11 (CCl<sub>3</sub>F) and CFC-12 (CCl<sub>2</sub>F<sub>2</sub>).</p>
<p>In the late 1920s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Midgley,_Jr." title="Thomas Midgley, Jr.">Thomas Midgley, Jr.</a> improved the process of synthesis and led the effort to use CFC as refrigerant to replace <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (NH<sub>3</sub>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" title="Methyl chloride">chloromethane</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>Cl), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>), which are toxic but were in common use. In searching for a new refrigerant, requirements for the compound were: low <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>, low toxicity, and to be generally non-reactive. In a demonstration for the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a>, Midgley flamboyantly demonstrated all these properties by inhaling a breath of the gas and using it to blow out a candle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNeill_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNeill-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_development_and_use">Commercial development and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial development and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, various chloroalkanes were in standard use in military aircraft, although these early halons suffered from excessive toxicity. Nevertheless, after the war they slowly became more common in civil aviation as well. In the 1960s, fluoroalkanes and bromofluoroalkanes became available and were quickly recognized as being highly effective fire-fighting materials. Much early research with <a href="/wiki/Bromotrifluoromethane" title="Bromotrifluoromethane">Halon 1301</a> was conducted under the auspices of the US Armed Forces, while <a href="/wiki/Bromochlorodifluoromethane" title="Bromochlorodifluoromethane">Halon 1211</a> was, initially, mainly developed in the UK. By the late 1960s they were standard in many applications where water and dry-powder extinguishers posed a threat of damage to the protected property, including computer rooms, telecommunications switches, laboratories, museums and art collections. Beginning with <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warships</a>, in the 1970s, bromofluoroalkanes also progressively came to be associated with rapid knockdown of severe fires in confined spaces with minimal risk to personnel.</p>
<p>By the early 1980s, bromofluoroalkanes were in common use on aircraft, ships, and large vehicles as well as in computer facilities and galleries. However, concern was beginning to be expressed about the impact of chloroalkanes and bromoalkanes on the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer">Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer</a> did not cover bromofluoroalkanes as it was thought, at the time, that emergency discharge of extinguishing systems was too small in volume to produce a significant impact, and too important to human safety for restriction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Since the late 1970s, the use of CFCs has been heavily regulated because of their destructive effects on the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone layer</a>. After the development of his <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture_detector" title="Electron capture detector">electron capture detector</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a> was the first to detect the widespread presence of CFCs in the air, finding a <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a> of 60 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_trillion" title="Parts per trillion">ppt</a> of CFC-11 over <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. In a self-funded research expedition ending in 1973, Lovelock went on to measure CFC-11 in both the Arctic and Antarctic, finding the presence of the gas in each of 50 air samples collected, and concluding that CFCs are not hazardous to the environment. The experiment did however provide the first useful data on the presence of CFCs in the atmosphere. The damage caused by CFCs was discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sherry_Rowland" title="Sherry Rowland">Sherry Rowland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mario_Molina" title="Mario Molina">Mario Molina</a> who, after hearing a lecture on the subject of Lovelock's work, embarked on research resulting in the first publication suggesting the connection in 1974. It turns out that one of CFCs' most attractive features—their low reactivity— is key to their most destructive effects. CFCs' lack of reactivity gives them a lifespan that can exceed 100 years, giving them time to diffuse into the upper <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Once in the stratosphere, the sun's <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation is strong enough to cause the <a href="/wiki/Homolysis_(chemistry)" title="Homolysis (chemistry)">homolytic</a> cleavage of the C-Cl bond.</p>

<p>By 1987, in response to a dramatic seasonal depletion of the ozone layer over <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, diplomats in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> forged a treaty, the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>, which called for drastic reductions in the production of CFCs. On 2 March 1989, 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Community" title="European Community">European Community</a> nations agreed to ban the production of all CFCs by the end of the century. In 1990, diplomats met in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and voted to significantly strengthen the Montreal Protocol by calling for a complete elimination of CFCs by the year 2000. By the year 2010, CFCs should have been completely eliminated from developing countries as well.</p>

<p>Because the only CFCs available to countries adhering to the treaty is from recycling, their prices have increased considerably. A worldwide end to production should also terminate the smuggling of this material. However, there are current CFC smuggling issues, as recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environmental_Programme" title="United Nations Environmental Programme">United Nations Environmental Programme</a> (UNEP) in a 2006 report titled "Illegal Trade in Ozone Depleting Substances". UNEP estimates that between 16,000–38,000 tonnes of CFCs passed through the black market in the mid-1990s. The report estimated between 7,000 and 14,000 tonnes of CFCs are smuggled annually into developing countries. Asian countries are those with the most smuggling; as of 2007, China, India and South Korea were found to account for around 70% of global CFC production,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> South Korea later to ban CFC production in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Possible reasons for continued CFC smuggling were also examined: the report noted that many banned CFC producing products have long lifespans and continue to operate. The cost of replacing the equipment of these items is sometimes cheaper than outfitting them with a more ozone-friendly appliance. Additionally, CFC smuggling is not considered a significant issue, so the perceived penalties for smuggling are low. While the eventual phaseout of CFCs is likely, efforts are being taken to stem these current non-compliance problems.</p>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>, it was realised that deliberate and accidental discharges during system tests and maintenance accounted for substantially larger volumes than emergency discharges, and consequently halons were brought into the treaty, albeit with many exceptions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_gap">Regulatory gap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Regulatory gap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While the production and consumption of CFCs are regulated under the Montreal Protocol, emissions from existing banks of CFCs are not regulated under the agreement. In 2002, there were an estimated 5,791 kilotons of CFCs in existing products such as refrigerators, air conditioners, aerosol cans and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Approximately one-third of these CFCs are projected to be emitted over the next decade if action is not taken, posing a threat to both the ozone layer and the climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A proportion of these CFCs can be safely captured and destroyed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_and_DuPont">Regulation and DuPont</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Regulation and DuPont">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1978 the United States banned the use of CFCs such as Freon in aerosol cans, the beginning of a long series of regulatory actions against their use. The critical DuPont manufacturing patent for Freon ("Process for Fluorinating Halohydrocarbons", U.S. Patent #3258500) was set to expire in 1979. In conjunction with other industrial peers DuPont sponsored efforts such as the "Alliance for Responsible CFC Policy" to question anti-CFC science, but in a turnabout in 1986 DuPont, with new patents in hand, publicly condemned CFCs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ethics_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ethics-14">[14]</a></sup> DuPont representatives appeared before the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> urging that CFCs be banned worldwide and stated that their new HCFCs would meet the worldwide demand for refrigerants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ethics_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ethics-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phasing-out_of_CFCs">Phasing-out of CFCs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Phasing-out of CFCs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Use of certain chloroalkanes as solvents for large scale application, such as dry cleaning, have been phased out, for example, by the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Pollution_Prevention_and_Control" title="Integrated Pollution Prevention and Control">IPPC</a> directive on <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> in 1994 and by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOC) directive of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> in 1997. Permitted chlorofluoroalkane uses are medicinal only.</p>
<p>Bromofluoroalkanes have been largely phased out and the possession of equipment for their use is prohibited in some countries like the Netherlands and Belgium, from 1 January 2004, based on the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> and guidelines of the European Union.</p>
<p>Production of new stocks ceased in most (probably all) countries in 1994.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However many countries still require aircraft to be fitted with halon fire suppression systems because no safe and completely satisfactory alternative has been discovered for this application. There are also a few other, highly specialized uses. These programs recycle halon through "halon banks" coordinated by the Halon Recycling Corporation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> to ensure that discharge to the atmosphere occurs only in a genuine emergency and to conserve remaining stocks.</p>
<p>The interim replacements for CFCs are hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs), which deplete stratospheric ozone, but to a much lesser extent than CFCs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prin_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-prin-16">[16]</a></sup> Ultimately, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haloalkanes#Hydro_fluoro_compounds_.28HFC.29" title="Haloalkanes">hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs)</a> will replace HCFCs. Unlike CFCs and HCFCs, HFCs have an ozone depletion potential (ODP) of 0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> DuPont began producing hydrofluorocarbons as alternatives to Freon in the 1980s. These included Suva refrigerants and Dymel propellants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1930:_Freon.2C_DuPont_Heritage_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-1930:_Freon.2C_DuPont_Heritage-18">[18]</a></sup> Natural refrigerants are climate friendly solutions that are enjoying increasing support from large companies and governments interested in reducing global warming emissions from refrigeration and air conditioning. Hydrofluorocarbons are included in the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> because of their very high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Warming_Potential" title="Global Warming Potential">Global Warming Potential</a> and are facing calls to be regulated under the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (April 2010)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Chlorofluorocarbon#Don.27t_you_mean_Kyoto.3F" title="Talk:Chlorofluorocarbon">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Novel_Tactic_in_Climate_Fight_Gains_Some_Traction.2C_NY_Times_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_Novel_Tactic_in_Climate_Fight_Gains_Some_Traction.2C_NY_Times-19">[19]</a></sup> due to the recognition of halocarbon contributions to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 21 September 2007, approximately 200 countries agreed to accelerate the elimination of hydrochlorofluorocarbons entirely by 2020 in a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> summit. Developing nations were given until 2030. Many nations, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>, who had previously <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">resisted such efforts</a>, agreed with the accelerated phase out schedule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_alternatives_for_CFCs">Development of alternatives for CFCs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Development of alternatives for CFCs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Work on alternatives for chlorofluorocarbons in refrigerants began in the late 1970s after the first warnings of damage to <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> ozone were published.</p>
<p>The hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs) are less stable in the lower atmosphere, enabling them to break down before reaching the ozone layer. Nevertheless, a significant fraction of the HCFCs do break down in the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> and they have contributed to more chlorine buildup there than originally predicted. Later alternatives lacking the chlorine, the hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) have an even shorter lifetimes in the lower atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prin_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-prin-16">[16]</a></sup> One of these compounds, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HFC-134a" title="HFC-134a">HFC-134a</a>, is now used in place of CFC-12 in automobile air conditioners. Hydrocarbon refrigerants (a propane/isobutane blend) are also used extensively in mobile air conditioning systems in Australia, the USA and many other countries, as they have excellent thermodynamic properties and perform particularly well in high ambient temperatures.</p>
<p>One of the natural refrigerants (along with ammonia and carbon dioxide), hydrocarbons have negligible environmental impacts and are also used worldwide in domestic and commercial refrigeration applications, and are becoming available in new split system air conditioners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Various other solvents and methods have replaced the use of CFCs in laboratory analytics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As previously discussed, CFCs were phased out via the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> due to their part in ozone depletion. However, the atmospheric impacts of CFCs are not limited to its role as an active ozone reducer. This anthropogenic compound is also a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, with a much higher potential to enhance the greenhouse effect than CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>Infrared absorption bands trap heat from escaping earth's atmosphere. In the case of CFCs, the strongest of these bands are located in the spectral region 7.8–15.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">µm</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothman_L.S._et_42_alia.2C_2009_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rothman_L.S._et_42_alia.2C_2009-25">[25]</a></sup> – referred to as an atmospheric window due to the relative transparency of the atmosphere within this region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The strength of CFC bands and the unique susceptibility of the atmosphere, at which the compound absorbs and emits radiation, are two factors that contribute to CFCs' "super" greenhouse effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_2009_37.E2.80.9350_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_2009_37.E2.80.9350-27">[27]</a></sup> Another such factor is the low concentration of the compound. Because CO<sub>2</sub> is close to saturation with high concentrations,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title='which property of CO2 is saturated and what concentration is regarded as "high"? (June 2013)'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> it takes more of the substance to enhance the greenhouse effect. Conversely, the low concentration of CFCs allow their effects to increase linearly with mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_2009_37.E2.80.9350_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_2009_37.E2.80.9350-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracer_of_ocean_circulation">Tracer of ocean circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tracer of ocean circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because the time history of CFC concentrations in the atmosphere is relatively well known, they have provided an important constraint on ocean circulation. CFCs dissolve in seawater at the ocean surface and are subsequently transported into the ocean interior. Because CFCs are inert, their concentration in the ocean interior reflects simply the convolution of their atmospheric time evolution and ocean circulation and mixing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CFC_and_SF6_tracer-derived_age_of_ocean_water">CFC and SF<sub>6</sub> tracer-derived age of ocean water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: CFC and SF6 tracer-derived age of ocean water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are anthropogenic compounds that have been released into the atmosphere since the 1930s in various applications such as in air-conditioning, refrigeration, blowing agents in foams, insulations and packing materials, propellants in aerosol cans, and as solvents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PlummerBusenberg_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-PlummerBusenberg-28">[28]</a></sup> The entry of CFCs into the ocean makes them extremely useful as transient tracers to estimate rates and pathways of ocean circulation and mixing processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BullisterWisegraver_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BullisterWisegraver-29">[29]</a></sup> However, due to production restrictions of CFCs in the 1980s, atmospheric concentrations of CFC-11 and CFC-12 has stopped increasing, and the CFC-11 to CFC-12 ratio in the atmosphere have been steadily decreasing, making water dating of water masses more problematic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BullisterWisegraver_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-BullisterWisegraver-29">[29]</a></sup> Incidentally, production and release of sulfur hexafluoride (SF<sub>6</sub>) have rapidly increased in the atmosphere since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BullisterWisegraver_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-BullisterWisegraver-29">[29]</a></sup> Similar to CFCs, SF<sub>6</sub> is also an inert gas and is not affected by oceanic chemical or biological activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Thus, using CFCs in concert with SF<sub>6</sub> as a tracer resolves the water dating issues due to decreased CFC concentrations.</p>
<p>Using CFCs or SF<sub>6</sub> as a tracer of ocean circulation allows for the derivation of rates for ocean processes due to the time-dependent source function. The elapsed time since a subsurface water mass was last in contact with the atmosphere is the tracer-derived age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-31">[31]</a></sup> Estimates of age can be derived based on the partial pressure of an individual compound and the ratio of the partial pressure of CFCs to each other (or SF<sub>6</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_pressure_and_ratio_dating_techniques">Partial pressure and ratio dating techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Partial pressure and ratio dating techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The age of a water parcel can be estimated by the CFC partial pressure (pCFC) age or SF<sub>6</sub> partial pressure (pSF<sub>6</sub>) age. The pCFC age of a water sample is defined as:</p>
<p><big>pCFC = [CFC]/F(T,S</big>)</p>
<p>where [CFC] is the measured CFC concentration (pmol kg<sup>−1</sup>) and F is the solubility of CFC gas in seawater as a function of temperature and salinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WarnerWeiss_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-WarnerWeiss-32">[32]</a></sup> The CFC partial pressure is expressed in units of 10–12 atmospheres or parts-per-trillion (ppt).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinWarner_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-MinWarner-33">[33]</a></sup> The solubility measurements of CFC-11 and CFC-12 have been previously measured by Warner and Weiss<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinWarner_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-MinWarner-33">[33]</a></sup> Additionally, the solubility measurement of CFC-113 was measured by Bu and Warner<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuWarner_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuWarner-34">[34]</a></sup> and SF<sub>6</sub> by Wanninkhof et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and Bullister et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Theses authors mentioned above have expressed the solubility, F, at a total pressure of 1 atm as:</p>
<p><big>ln F = a<sub>1</sub> + a<sub>2</sub>(100/T) + a<sub>3</sub>ln(T/100) + a<sub>4</sub>(T/100)<sup>2</sup> + S[b<sub>1</sub> + b<sub>2</sub>(T/100) + b<sub>3</sub>(T/100)<sup>2</sup>]</big></p>
<p>where F = solubility expressed in either mol l<sup>−1</sup> or mol kg<sup>−1</sup> atm<sup>−1</sup> T = absolute temperature S = salinity in parts per thousand (ppt) a<sub>1</sub>, a<sub>2</sub>, a<sub>3</sub>, b<sub>1</sub>, b<sub>2</sub>, and b<sub>3</sub> are constants to be determined from the least squares fit to the solubility measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuWarner_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-BuWarner-34">[34]</a></sup> This equation is derived from the integrated <a href="/wiki/Van_%27t_Hoff_equation" title="Van 't Hoff equation">Van 't Hoff equation</a> and the logarithmic Setchenow salinity dependence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuWarner_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-BuWarner-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>It can be noted that the solubility of CFCs increase with decreasing temperature at approximately 1% per degree Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the partial pressure of the CFC (or SF<sub>6</sub>) is derived, it is then compared to the atmospheric time histories for CFC-11, CFC-12, or SF<sub>6</sub> in which the pCFC directly corresponds to the year with the same. The difference between the corresponding date and the collection date of the seawater sample is the average age for the water parcel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-31">[31]</a></sup> The age of a parcel of water can also be calculated using the ratio of two CFC partial pressures or the ratio of the SF<sub>6</sub> partial pressure to a CFC partial pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorofluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to their material safety data sheets, CFCs and HCFCs are colorless, volatile, toxic liquids and gases with a faintly sweet ethereal odor. Overexposure at concentrations of 11% or more may cause dizziness, loss of concentration, central nervous system depression and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrhythmia" title="Cardiac arrhythmia">cardiac arrhythmia</a>. Vapors displace air and can cause asphyxiation in confined spaces. Although non-flammable, their combustion products include hydrofluoric acid, and related species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Normal occupational exposure is rated at 0.07% and does not pose any serious health risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
