<p>In modern <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>antimatter</b> is defined as a <a href="/wiki/Material" title="Material">material</a> composed of the <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a> (or "partners") to the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">particles</a> of ordinary <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>.</p>
<p>In theory, a particle and its anti-particle have the same mass as one another, but opposite <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>, and other differences in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_number" title="Quantum number">quantum numbers</a>. For example, a proton has positive charge while an antiproton has negative charge. A collision between any particle and its anti-particle partner is known to lead to their <i>mutual <a href="/wiki/Annihilation" title="Annihilation">annihilation</a></i>, giving rise to various proportions of intense <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a>), <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>, and sometimes less-massive particle–antiparticle pairs.</p>
<p>Annihilation usually results in a release of energy that becomes available for heat or work. The amount of the released energy is usually proportional to the total mass of the collided matter and antimatter, in accord with the <a href="/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence" title="Mass–energy equivalence">mass–energy equivalence</a> equation, <span class="nowrap"><i>E</i> = <i>mc</i><sup>2</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antimatter particles bind with one another to form antimatter, just as ordinary particles bind to form normal matter. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> (the antiparticle of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>) and an <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiproton</a> (the antiparticle of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>) can form an <a href="/wiki/Antihydrogen" title="Antihydrogen">antihydrogen</a> atom. Physical principles indicate that complex antimatter atomic nuclei are possible, as well as anti-atoms corresponding to the known chemical elements.</p>
<p>There is considerable speculation as to why the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a> is composed almost entirely of ordinary matter, as opposed to an equal mixture of matter and antimatter. This <a href="/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry" title="Baryon asymmetry">asymmetry of matter and antimatter</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_universe" title="Visible universe">visible universe</a> is one of the great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsolved_problems_in_physics" title="Unsolved problems in physics">unsolved problems in physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The process by which this inequality between matter and antimatter particles developed is called <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">baryogenesis</a>.</p>
<p>Antimatter in the form of anti-atoms is one of the most difficult materials to produce. Individual antimatter particles, however, are commonly produced by <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a> and in some types of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a>. The nuclei of <a href="#Antihelium">antihelium</a> have been artificially produced with difficulty. These are the most complex anti-nuclei so far observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_definition">Formal definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formal definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Formally, antimatter particles can be defined by their negative <a href="/wiki/Baryon_number" title="Baryon number">baryon number</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lepton_number" title="Lepton number">lepton number</a>, while "normal" (non-antimatter) matter particles have a positive baryon or lepton number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These two classes of particles are the antiparticle partners of one another.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">History of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_matter" title="Negative matter">negative matter</a> appears in past theories of matter that have now been abandoned. Using the once popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vortex_theory_of_gravity" title="Vortex theory of gravity">vortex theory of gravity</a>, the possibility of matter with negative gravity was discussed by <a href="/wiki/William_Mitchinson_Hicks" title="William Mitchinson Hicks">William Hicks</a> in the 1880s. Between the 1880s and the 1890s, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Pearson" title="Karl Pearson">Karl Pearson</a> proposed the existence of "squirts"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and sinks of the flow of <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">aether</a>. The squirts represented normal matter and the sinks represented negative matter. Pearson's theory required a fourth dimension for the aether to flow from and into.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term antimatter was first used by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schuster" title="Arthur Schuster">Arthur Schuster</a> in two rather whimsical letters to <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> in 1898,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in which he coined the term. He hypothesized antiatoms, as well as whole antimatter solar systems, and discussed the possibility of matter and antimatter annihilating each other. Schuster's ideas were not a serious theoretical proposal, merely speculation, and like the previous ideas, differed from the modern concept of antimatter in that it possessed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigravity" title="Antigravity">negative gravity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The modern theory of antimatter began in 1928, with a paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a>. Dirac realised that his <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">relativistic version</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger wave equation</a> for electrons predicted the possibility of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antielectron" title="Antielectron">antielectrons</a>. These were discovered by <a href="/wiki/Carl_David_Anderson" title="Carl David Anderson">Carl D. Anderson</a> in 1932 and named <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of "positive electron"). Although Dirac did not himself use the term antimatter, its use follows on naturally enough from antielectrons, antiprotons, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A complete <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> of antimatter was envisaged by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Janet" title="Charles Janet">Charles Janet</a> in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feynman%E2%80%93Stueckelberg_interpretation" title="Feynman–Stueckelberg interpretation">Feynman–Stueckelberg interpretation</a> states that antimatter and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiparticles" title="Antiparticles">antiparticles</a> are regular particles traveling backward in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notation">Notation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One way to denote an <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a> is by adding a bar over the particle's symbol. For example, the proton and antiproton are denoted as <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>p<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">p</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, respectively. The same rule applies if one were to address a particle by its constituent components. A proton is made up of <a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>u<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>u<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span><a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>d<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a> <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>, so an antiproton must therefore be formed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Up_antiquark" title="Up antiquark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">u</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">u</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Down_antiquark" title="Down antiquark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">d</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiquark" title="Antiquark">antiquarks</a>. Another convention is to distinguish particles by their <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>. Thus, the electron and positron are denoted simply as <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span> respectively. However, to prevent confusion, the two conventions are never mixed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are compelling theoretical reasons to believe that, aside from the fact that antiparticles have different signs on all charges (such as electric charge and spin), matter and antimatter have exactly the same properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This means a particle and its corresponding antiparticle must have identical masses and decay lifetimes (if unstable). It also implies that, for example, a star made up of antimatter (an "antistar") will shine just like an ordinary star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This idea was tested experimentally in 2016 by the <a href="/wiki/Antiproton_Decelerator#ALPHA" title="Antiproton Decelerator">ALPHA</a> experiment, which measured the transition between the two lowest energy states of <a href="/wiki/Antihydrogen" title="Antihydrogen">antihydrogen</a>. The results, which are identical to that of hydrogen, confirmed the validity of quantum mechanics for antimatter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_asymmetry">Origin and asymmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origin and asymmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">Baryogenesis</a></div>
<p>Almost all matter observable from the Earth seems to be made of matter rather than antimatter. If antimatter-dominated regions of space existed, the gamma rays produced in annihilation reactions along the boundary between matter and antimatter regions would be detectable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antiparticles are created everywhere in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> where high-energy particle collisions take place. High-energy <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> impacting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> (or any other matter in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>) produce minute quantities of antiparticles in the resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_jet" title="Particle jet">particle jets</a>, which are immediately annihilated by contact with nearby matter. They may similarly be produced in regions like the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">center</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and other galaxies, where very energetic celestial events occur (principally the interaction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_jet" title="Relativistic jet">relativistic jets</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>). The presence of the resulting antimatter is detectable by the two <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> produced every time <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> annihilate with nearby matter. The <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of the gamma rays indicate that each carries 511 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">keV</a> of energy (i.e., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> multiplied by <i><a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">c</a></i><sup>2</sup>).</p>
<p>Observations by the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/INTEGRAL" title="INTEGRAL">INTEGRAL satellite</a> may explain the origin of a giant antimatter cloud surrounding the galactic center. The observations show that the cloud is asymmetrical and matches the pattern of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_binaries" title="X-ray binaries">X-ray binaries</a> (binary star systems containing black holes or neutron stars), mostly on one side of the galactic center. While the mechanism is not fully understood, it is likely to involve the production of electron–positron pairs, as ordinary matter gains kinetic energy while falling into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_remnant" title="Stellar remnant">stellar remnant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antimatter may exist in relatively large amounts in far-away galaxies due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a> in the primordial time of the universe. Antimatter galaxies, if they exist, are expected to have the same chemistry and <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">absorption and emission spectra</a> as normal-matter galaxies, and their <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical objects</a> would be observationally identical, making them difficult to distinguish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> is trying to determine if such galaxies exist by looking for X-ray and gamma-ray signatures of annihilation events in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colliding_galaxy" title="Colliding galaxy">colliding</a> <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2017, scientists working on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BASE_experiment" title="BASE experiment">BASE experiment</a> at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> reported a measurement of the <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiproton</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> to a precision of 1.5 parts per billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TT-20171025_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TT-20171025-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAT-20171020_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAT-20171020-25">[25]</a></sup> It is consistent with the most precise measurement of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> magnetic moment (also made by BASE in 2014), which supports the hypothesis of <a href="/wiki/CPT_symmetry" title="CPT symmetry">CPT symmetry</a>. It should be noted that this measurement represents the first time that a property of antimatter is known more precisely than the equivalent property in matter.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_production">Natural production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Natural production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">Positron emission</a></div>
<p>Positrons are produced naturally in β<sup>+</sup> decays of naturally occurring radioactive isotopes (for example, <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">potassium-40</a>) and in interactions of gamma quanta (emitted by radioactive nuclei) with matter. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antineutrino" title="Antineutrino">Antineutrinos</a> are another kind of antiparticle created by natural radioactivity (β<sup>−</sup> decay). Many different kinds of antiparticles are also produced by (and contained in) <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. In January 2011, research by the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">American Astronomical Society</a> discovered antimatter (positrons) originating above <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> clouds; positrons are produced in gamma-ray flashes created by electrons accelerated by strong electric fields in the clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Antiprotons have also been found to exist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_Belt" title="Van Allen Belt">Van Allen Belts</a> around the Earth by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_for_Antimatter_Matter_Exploration_and_Light-nuclei_Astrophysics#Results" title="Payload for Antimatter Matter Exploration and Light-nuclei Astrophysics">PAMELA module</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antiparticles are also produced in any environment with a sufficiently high temperature (mean particle energy greater than the <a href="/wiki/Pair_production" title="Pair production">pair production</a> threshold). It is hypothesized that during the period of baryogenesis, when the universe was extremely hot and dense, matter and antimatter were continually produced and annihilated. The presence of remaining matter, and absence of detectable remaining antimatter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> is called <a href="/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry" title="Baryon asymmetry">baryon asymmetry</a>. The exact mechanism which produced this asymmetry during baryogenesis remains an unsolved problem. One of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakharov_conditions" title="Sakharov conditions">necessary conditions</a> for this asymmetry is the <a href="/wiki/CP_violation" title="CP violation">violation of CP symmetry</a>, which has been experimentally observed in the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>.</p>
<p>Recent observations indicate black holes and neutron stars produce vast amounts of positron-electron plasma via the jets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_in_cosmic_rays">Observation in cosmic rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Observation in cosmic rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">Cosmic ray</a></div>
<p>Satellite experiments have found evidence of <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> and a few antiprotons in primary cosmic rays, amounting to less than 1% of the particles in primary cosmic rays. This antimatter cannot all have been created in the Big Bang, but is instead attributed to have been produced by cyclic processes at high energies. For instance, electron-positron pairs may be formed in <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>, as a magnetized neutron star rotation cycle shears electron-positron pairs from the star surface. Therein the antimatter forms a wind which crashes upon the ejecta of the progenitor supernovae. This weathering takes place as "the cold, magnetized relativistic wind launched by the star hits the non-relativistically expanding ejecta, a shock wave system forms in the impact: the outer one propagates in the ejecta, while a reverse shock propagates back towards the star."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The former ejection of matter in the outer shock wave and the latter production of antimatter in the reverse shock wave are steps in a space weather cycle.</p>
<p>Preliminary results from the presently operating <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Magnetic_Spectrometer" title="Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer">Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer</a> (<i>AMS-02</i>) on board the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> show that positrons in the cosmic rays arrive with no directionality, and with energies that range from 10 <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gigaelectron_volt" title="wikt:gigaelectron volt">GeV</a> to 250 GeV. In September, 2014, new results with almost twice as much data were presented in a talk at CERN and published in Physical Review Letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A new measurement of positron fraction up to 500 GeV was reported, showing that positron fraction peaks at a maximum of about 16% of total electron+positron events, around an energy of 275 ± 32 GeV. At higher energies, up to 500 GeV, the ratio of positrons to electrons begins to fall again. The absolute flux of positrons also begins to fall before 500 GeV, but peaks at energies far higher than electron energies, which peak about 10 GeV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> These results on interpretation have been suggested to be due to positron production in annihilation events of massive <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physrevltrs413_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-physrevltrs413-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cosmic ray antiprotons also have a much higher energy than their normal-matter counterparts (protons). They arrive at Earth with a characteristic energy maximum of 2 GeV, indicating their production in a fundamentally different process from cosmic ray protons, which on average have only one-sixth of the energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is no evidence of complex antimatter atomic nuclei, such as <a href="#Antihelium">antihelium</a> nuclei (i.e., anti-alpha particles), in cosmic rays. These are actively being searched for, because the detection of natural antihelium implies the existence of large antimatter structures such as an antistar. A prototype of the <i>AMS-02</i> designated <i>AMS-01</i>, was flown into space aboard the <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery"><i>Discovery</i></a> on <a href="/wiki/STS-91" title="STS-91">STS-91</a> in June 1998. By not detecting any <a href="#Antihelium">antihelium</a> at all, the <i>AMS-01</i> established an upper limit of 1.1×10<sup>−6</sup> for the antihelium to helium <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_production">Artificial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Artificial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positrons">Positrons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Positrons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">Positron</a></div>
<p>Positrons were reported<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> in November 2008 to have been generated by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory</a> in larger numbers than by any previous synthetic process. A <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> drove <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> target's <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>, which caused the incoming electrons to emit <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quanta</a> that decayed into both matter and antimatter. Positrons were detected at a higher rate and in greater density than ever previously detected in a laboratory. Previous experiments made smaller quantities of positrons using lasers and paper-thin targets; however, new simulations showed that short, ultra-intense lasers and millimeter-thick gold are a far more effective source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Antiprotons,_antineutrons,_and_antinuclei"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiprotons.2C_antineutrons.2C_and_antinuclei">Antiprotons, antineutrons, and antinuclei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Antiprotons, antineutrons, and antinuclei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">Antiproton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antineutron" title="Antineutron">Antineutron</a></div>
<p>The existence of the antiproton was experimentally confirmed in 1955 by <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a> <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Segr%C3%A8" title="Emilio Segrè">Emilio Segrè</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owen_Chamberlain" title="Owen Chamberlain">Owen Chamberlain</a>, for which they were awarded the 1959 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> An antiproton consists of two up antiquarks and one down antiquark (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Up_antiquark" title="Up antiquark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">u</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Up_antiquark" title="Up antiquark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">u</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Down_antiquark" title="Down antiquark"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">d</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a>). The properties of the antiproton that have been measured all match the corresponding properties of the proton, with the exception of the antiproton having opposite electric charge and magnetic moment from the proton. Shortly afterwards, in 1956, the antineutron was discovered in proton–proton collisions at the <a href="/wiki/Bevatron" title="Bevatron">Bevatron</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a>) by <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Cork" title="Bruce Cork">Bruce Cork</a> and colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to anti<a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a>, anti-nuclei consisting of multiple bound antiprotons and antineutrons have been created. These are typically produced at energies far too high to form antimatter atoms (with bound positrons in place of electrons). In 1965, a group of researchers led by <a href="/wiki/Antonino_Zichichi" title="Antonino Zichichi">Antonino Zichichi</a> reported production of nuclei of antideuterium at the Proton Synchrotron at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> At roughly the same time, observations of antideuterium nuclei were reported by a group of American physicists at the Alternating Gradient Synchrotron at <a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antihydrogen_atoms">Antihydrogen atoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Antihydrogen atoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Antihydrogen" title="Antihydrogen">Antihydrogen</a></div>
<p>In 1995, <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> announced that it had successfully brought into existence nine hot antihydrogen atoms by implementing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SLAC" title="SLAC">SLAC</a>/<a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermilab</a> concept during the <a href="/wiki/PS210_experiment" title="PS210 experiment">PS210 experiment</a>. The experiment was performed using the <a href="/wiki/Low_Energy_Antiproton_Ring" title="Low Energy Antiproton Ring">Low Energy Antiproton Ring</a> (LEAR), and was led by Walter Oelert and Mario Macri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Fermilab soon confirmed the CERN findings by producing approximately 100 antihydrogen atoms at their facilities. The antihydrogen atoms created during PS210 and subsequent experiments (at both CERN and Fermilab) were extremely energetic and were not well suited to study. To resolve this hurdle, and to gain a better understanding of antihydrogen, two collaborations were formed in the late 1990s, namely, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATHENA" title="ATHENA">ATHENA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATRAP" title="ATRAP">ATRAP</a>.</p>
<p>In 1999, CERN activated the <a href="/wiki/Antiproton_Decelerator" title="Antiproton Decelerator">Antiproton Decelerator</a>, a device capable of decelerating antiprotons from <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990560761770449999♠</span>3500 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">MeV</a></span> to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6987849153538110000♠</span>5.3 MeV</span> — still too "hot" to produce study-effective antihydrogen, but a huge leap forward. In late 2002 the ATHENA project announced that they had created the world's first "cold" antihydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The ATRAP project released similar results very shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The antiprotons used in these experiments were cooled by decelerating them with the Antiproton Decelerator, passing them through a thin sheet of foil, and finally capturing them in a Penning–Malmberg trap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The overall cooling process is workable, but highly inefficient; approximately 25 million antiprotons leave the Antiproton Decelerator and roughly 25,000 make it to the Penning–Malmberg trap, which is about <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">1000</span></span> or 0.1% of the original amount.</p>
<p>The antiprotons are still hot when initially trapped. To cool them further, they are mixed into an electron plasma. The electrons in this plasma cool via cyclotron radiation, and then sympathetically cool the antiprotons via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coulomb_potential" title="Coulomb potential">Coulomb</a> collisions. Eventually, the electrons are removed by the application of short-duration electric fields, leaving the antiprotons with energies less than <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002100000000000000♠</span>100 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">meV</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> While the antiprotons are being cooled in the first trap, a small cloud of positrons is captured from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> in a Surko-style positron accumulator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> This cloud is then recaptured in a second trap near the antiprotons. Manipulations of the trap electrodes then tip the antiprotons into the positron plasma, where some combine with antiprotons to form antihydrogen. This neutral antihydrogen is unaffected by the electric and magnetic fields used to trap the charged positrons and antiprotons, and within a few microseconds the antihydrogen hits the trap walls, where it annihilates. Some hundreds of millions of antihydrogen atoms have been made in this fashion.</p>
<p>In 2005, ATHENA disbanded and some of the former members (along with others) formed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALPHA_Collaboration" title="ALPHA Collaboration">ALPHA Collaboration</a>, which is also based at CERN. The primary goal of these collaborations is the creation of less energetic ("cold") antihydrogen, better suited to study.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2016 a new antiproton decelerator and cooler called ELENA (E Low ENergy Antiproton decelerator) was built. It takes the antiprotons from the antiproton decelerator and cools them to 90 keV which is "cold" enough to study. More than a hundred antiprotons can be captured per second, a huge improvement, but it would still take several thousand years to make a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanogram" title="Nanogram">nanogram</a> of antimatter.</p>
<p>Most of the sought-after high-precision tests of the properties of antihydrogen could only be performed if the antihydrogen were trapped, that is, held in place for a relatively long time. While antihydrogen atoms are electrically neutral, the <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spins</a> of their component particles produce a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a>. These magnetic moments can interact with an inhomogeneous magnetic field; some of the antihydrogen atoms can be attracted to a magnetic minimum. Such a minimum can be created by a combination of mirror and multipole fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Antihydrogen can be trapped in such a magnetic minimum (minimum-B) trap; in November 2010, the ALPHA collaboration announced that they had so trapped 38 antihydrogen atoms for about a sixth of a second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> This was the first time that neutral antimatter had been trapped.</p>
<p>On 26 April 2011, ALPHA announced that they had trapped 309 antihydrogen atoms, some for as long as 1,000 seconds (about 17 minutes). This was longer than neutral antimatter had ever been trapped before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> ALPHA has used these trapped atoms to initiate research into the spectral properties of the antihydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The biggest limiting factor in the large-scale production of antimatter is the availability of antiprotons. Recent data released by CERN states that, when fully operational, their facilities are capable of producing ten million antiprotons per minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Assuming a 100% conversion of antiprotons to antihydrogen, it would take 100 billion years to produce 1 gram or 1 <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of antihydrogen (approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602000000000000♠</span>6.02<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup></span> atoms of anti-hydrogen).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antihelium">Antihelium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Antihelium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antihelium-3 nuclei (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">3<br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">He</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>) were first observed in the 1970s in proton–nucleus collision experiments at the Institute for High Energy Physics by Y. Prockoshkin's group (Protvino near Moscow, USSR)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and later created in nucleus–nucleus collision experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Nucleus–nucleus collisions produce antinuclei through the coalescense of antiprotons and antineutrons created in these reactions. In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/STAR_detector" title="STAR detector">STAR detector</a> reported the observation of artificially created antihelium-4 nuclei (anti-alpha particles) (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">4<br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">He</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>) from such collisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation">Preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antimatter cannot be stored in a container made of ordinary matter because antimatter reacts with any matter it touches, annihilating itself and an equal amount of the container. Antimatter in the form of <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particles</a> can be contained by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> fields, in a device called a <a href="/wiki/Penning_trap" title="Penning trap">Penning trap</a>. This device cannot, however, contain antimatter that consists of uncharged particles, for which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_trap" title="Atomic trap">atomic traps</a> are used. In particular, such a trap may use the <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a> moment (<a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">electric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic</a>) of the trapped particles. At high <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, the matter or antimatter particles can be trapped and cooled with slightly off-resonant laser radiation using a <a href="/wiki/Magneto-optical_trap" title="Magneto-optical trap">magneto-optical trap</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_trap_(atoms)" title="Magnetic trap (atoms)">magnetic trap</a>. Small particles can also be suspended with <a href="/wiki/Optical_tweezers" title="Optical tweezers">optical tweezers</a>, using a highly focused laser beam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> scientists were able to preserve antihydrogen for approximately 17 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">Cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scientists claim that antimatter is the costliest material to make.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1999_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA1999-64">[64]</a></sup> In 2006, Gerald Smith estimated $250 million could produce 10 milligrams of positrons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> (equivalent to $25 billion per gram); in 1999, NASA gave a figure of $62.5 trillion per gram of antihydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1999_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA1999-64">[64]</a></sup> This is because production is difficult (only very few antiprotons are produced in reactions in particle accelerators), and because there is higher demand for other uses of <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>. According to CERN, it has cost a few hundred million <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">Swiss francs</a> to produce about 1 billionth of a gram (the amount used so far for particle/antiparticle collisions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In comparison, to produce the first atomic weapon, the cost of the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> was estimated at $23 billion with inflation during 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several studies funded by the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Institute_for_Advanced_Concepts" title="NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts">NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts</a> are exploring whether it might be possible to use magnetic scoops to collect the antimatter that occurs naturally in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_belt" title="Van Allen belt">Van Allen belt</a> of the Earth, and ultimately, the belts of gas giants, like <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, hopefully at a lower cost per gram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Matter–antimatter reactions have practical applications in medical imaging, such as <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> (PET). In positive <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclide</a> loses surplus positive charge by emitting a positron (in the same event, a proton becomes a neutron, and a <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> is also emitted). Nuclides with surplus positive charge are easily made in a <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">cyclotron</a> and are widely generated for medical use. Antiprotons have also been shown within laboratory experiments to have the potential to treat certain cancers, in a similar method currently used for ion (proton) therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel">Fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Isolated and stored anti-matter could be used as a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> for <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_spaceflight" title="Interplanetary spaceflight">interplanetary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_travel" title="Interstellar travel">interstellar travel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> as part of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimatter_catalyzed_nuclear_pulse_propulsion" title="Antimatter catalyzed nuclear pulse propulsion">antimatter catalyzed nuclear pulse propulsion</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Antimatter_rocket" title="Antimatter rocket">antimatter rocketry</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Redshift_rocket" title="Redshift rocket">redshift rocket</a>. Since the energy density of antimatter is higher than that of conventional fuels, an antimatter-fueled spacecraft would have a higher <a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">thrust-to-weight ratio</a> than a conventional spacecraft.</p>
<p>If matter–antimatter collisions resulted only in <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> emission, the entire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> of the particles would be converted to <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy per unit mass</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7016900000000000000♠</span>9<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>16</sup> J/kg</span>) is about 10 <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">orders of magnitude</a> greater than <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energies</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> and about 3 orders of magnitude greater than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_potential_energy" title="Nuclear potential energy">nuclear potential energy</a> that can be liberated, today, using <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> (about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6989320435297400000♠</span>200 MeV</span> per fission reaction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7013800000000000000♠</span>8<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>13</sup> J/kg</span>), and about 2 orders of magnitude greater than the best possible results expected from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> (about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7014630000000000000♠</span>6.3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>14</sup> J/kg</span> for the <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton–proton chain reaction">proton–proton chain</a>). The reaction of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000100000000000000♠</span>1 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a></span> of antimatter with <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000100000000000000♠</span>1 kg</span> of matter would produce <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7017180000000000000♠</span>1.8<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>17</sup> <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a></span> (180 petajoules) of energy (by the <a href="/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence" title="Mass–energy equivalence">mass–energy equivalence</a> formula, <span class="nowrap"><i>E</i> = <i>mc</i><sup>2</sup></span>), or the rough equivalent of 43 megatons of TNT – slightly less than the yield of the 27,000 kg <a href="/wiki/Tsar_Bomba" title="Tsar Bomba">Tsar Bomba</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear weapon</a> ever detonated.</p>
<p>Not all of that energy can be utilized by any realistic propulsion technology because of the nature of the annihilation products. While electron–positron reactions result in gamma ray photons, these are difficult to direct and use for thrust. In reactions between protons and antiprotons, their energy is converted largely into relativistic neutral and charged <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pions</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pion#Neutral_pion_decays" title="Pion">neutral pions</a> decay almost immediately (with a lifetime of 85 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attoseconds" title="Attoseconds">attoseconds</a>) into high-energy photons, but the <a href="/wiki/Pion#Charged_pion_decays" title="Pion">charged pions</a> decay more slowly (with a lifetime of 26 nanoseconds) and can be <a href="/wiki/Antimatter_rocket#Direct_use_of_reaction_products" title="Antimatter rocket">deflected magnetically to produce thrust</a>.</p>
<p>Charged pions ultimately decay into a combination of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> (carrying about 22% of the energy of the charged pions) and unstable charged <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muons</a> (carrying about 78% of the charged pion energy), with the muons then decaying into a combination of electrons, positrons and neutrinos (cf. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muon_decay" title="Muon decay">muon decay</a>; the neutrinos from this decay carry about 2/3 of the energy of the muons, meaning that from the original charged pions, the total fraction of their energy converted to neutrinos by one route or another would be about <span class="nowrap">0.22 + (2/3)⋅0.78 = 0.74</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons">Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimatter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Antimatter_weapon" title="Antimatter weapon">Antimatter weapon</a></div>
<p>Antimatter has been considered as a trigger mechanism for nuclear weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> A major obstacle is the difficulty of producing antimatter in large enough quantities, and there is no evidence that it will ever be feasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> However, the U.S. Air Force funded studies of the physics of antimatter in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, and began considering its possible use in weapons, not just as a trigger, but as the explosive itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
