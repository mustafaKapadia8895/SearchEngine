<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Shipping Forecast</h1>

<p>The <b>Shipping Forecast</b> is a <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a> broadcast of <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather reports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_weather_forecasting" title="Marine weather forecasting">forecasts</a> for the seas around the coasts of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>. It is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> and broadcast by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_and_Coastguard_Agency" title="Maritime and Coastguard Agency">Maritime and Coastguard Agency</a>. The forecasts sent over the <a href="/wiki/Navtex" title="Navtex">Navtex</a> system use a similar format and the same sea areas. The waters around the British Isles are divided into 31 sea areas, also known as weather areas (see map below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There are four broadcasts per day at the following (<a href="/wiki/Time_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Time in the United Kingdom">UK local</a>) times:</p>
<ul>
<li>0048 – transmitted on <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">LW</a>. Includes weather reports from an extended list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_coastal_weather_stations_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of coastal weather stations of the United Kingdom">coastal stations</a> at 0052 and an <a href="/wiki/Inshore_coastal_areas_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Inshore coastal areas of the United Kingdom">inshore waters</a> forecast at 0055 and concludes with a brief UK weather outlook for the coming day. The broadcast finishes at approximately 0058.</li>
<li>0520 – transmitted on FM and LW. Includes weather reports from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_coastal_weather_stations_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of coastal weather stations of the United Kingdom">coastal stations</a> at 0525, and an inshore waters forecast at 0527.</li>
<li>1201 – normally transmitted on LW only.</li>
<li>1754 – transmitted only on LW on weekdays, as an opt-out from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PM_(Radio_4)" title="PM (Radio 4)"><i>PM</i> programme</a>, but at weekends transmitted on both FM and LW.</li>
</ul>
<p>The unique and distinctive sound of these broadcasts has led to their attracting an audience much wider than that directly interested in maritime weather conditions. In August 2017, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">Radio 4</a> celebrated the service's 150th anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC150_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC150-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1859, the steam clipper <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_(ship)" title="Royal Charter (ship)">Royal Charter</a></i> was wrecked in a strong storm off <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>; 450 people lost their lives. Due to this loss, Vice-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a> introduced a warning service for shipping in February 1861, using telegraph communications. This remained the United Kingdom's Met Office primary responsibility for some time afterwards. In 1911, the Met Office began issuing marine weather forecasts which included gale and storm warnings via radio transmission for areas around Great Britain. This service was discontinued during and following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, between 1914 and June 1921, and again during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> between 1939 and 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, although most ships have onboard technology to provide the Forecast's information, they still use it to check their data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jefferson_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jefferson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Friday 30 May 2014, for the first time in more than 90 years, the BBC failed to broadcast the Shipping Forecast at 0520. Staff at Broadcasting House were reading out the report but it was not transmitted. Listeners instead heard <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>24 August 2017 marked the 150th anniversary of the shipping forecast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC150_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC150-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The areas were already roughly as listed above by 1949. In 1955, meteorologists from countries with <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> coastlines met and recommended that the UK's sea area 'Heligoland' be renamed 'German Bight' to reflect more general usage amongst the nations concerned. They also recommended the divisions of 'Dogger' (with the north-eastern portion to be named 'Fisher') and 'Forties' (with the northern half becoming 'Viking'), and the renaming of 'Iceland' as 'South-east Iceland' for greater clarity. Following international consultation, these changes were introduced in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Met_Office_Shipping_Fact_sheet_8_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Met_Office_Shipping_Fact_sheet_8-6">[6]</a></sup> Around 1983, the Minches sea area was merged with Hebrides.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In August 1984, the areas in the North Sea were again coordinated with those of neighbouring countries, introducing North Utsire and South Utsire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Met_Office_Shipping_Fact_sheet_8_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Met_Office_Shipping_Fact_sheet_8-6">[6]</a></sup> and reducing Viking in size. Finisterre was renamed FitzRoy in 2002, to avoid confusion with the smaller sea area of the same name used in the marine forecasts produced by the French and Spanish meteorological offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzroy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fitzroy-7">[7]</a></sup> Some names still differ; for example, the Dutch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KNMI_(institute)" title="KNMI (institute)">KNMI</a> names the area equivalent to Forties after the <a href="/wiki/Fladen" title="Fladen">Fladen</a> bank, while <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9t%C3%A9o-France" title="Météo-France">Météo-France</a> calls the English Channel sea areas Dover, Wight, Portland, and Plymouth respectively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pas_de_Calais" title="Pas de Calais">Pas de Calais</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_d%27Antifer" title="Cap d'Antifer">Antifer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casquets" title="Casquets">Casquets</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ouessant" title="Ouessant">Ouessant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the forecast, areas are named in a roughly clockwise direction, strictly following the order above. However, a forecast for Trafalgar is found only in the 0048 forecast – other forecasts do, however, report when there are warnings of gales in Trafalgar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_names">Origin of names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin of names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Viking, <a href="/wiki/Long_Forties" title="Long Forties">Forties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dogger_Bank" title="Dogger Bank">Dogger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisher_Bank" title="Fisher Bank">Fisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sole_Bank" title="Sole Bank">Sole</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_Bailey_Bank" title="Bill Bailey Bank">Bailey</a> are named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandbank" title="Sandbank">sandbanks</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cromarty_Firth" title="Cromarty Firth">Cromarty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Forth</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thames_Estuary" title="Thames Estuary">Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">Shannon</a> are named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">estuaries</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Wight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lundy" title="Lundy">Lundy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle" title="Fair Isle">Fair Isle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faeroes" title="Faeroes">Faeroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Portland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>, South-East <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utsire" title="Utsire">Utsire</a> are named after islands.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/German_Bight" title="German Bight">German Bight</a> is an indentation on the Northern European shoreline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jefferson_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jefferson-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> are named after towns.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rockall" title="Rockall">Rockall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fastnet_Rock" title="Fastnet Rock">Fastnet</a> are both named after <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islets</a>.</li>
<li>Malin is named after <a href="/wiki/Malin_Head" title="Malin Head">Malin Head</a>, the northernmost point of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.</li>
<li>Biscay is named after the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>, and Trafalgar after <a href="/wiki/Cape_Trafalgar" title="Cape Trafalgar">Cape Trafalgar</a>.</li>
<li>FitzRoy is named after <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a>, the first professional <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weatherman</a>, captain of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS <i>Beagle</i></a> and founder of the Met Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzroy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-fitzroy-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>The forecast, excluding the header line, has a limit of 350 words—except for the 0048 broadcast, where it is increased to 380 to accommodate Trafalgar's inclusion—and has a very strict format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-format_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-format-9">[9]</a></sup> It begins with "And now the Shipping Forecast, issued by the Met Office on behalf of the Maritime and Coastguard Agency at xxxx today." This format is followed quite strictly, although some <a href="/wiki/Continuity_announcer" title="Continuity announcer">continuity announcers</a> read out the actual date of issue as opposed to the word "today". This is followed by gale warnings (winds of force 8 or more on the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a>), if any (e.g., "There are warnings of gales in Rockall, Malin, Hebrides, Bailey, and Fair Isle"). This sometimes follows the opposite format (e.g., "There are warnings of gales in all areas except Biscay, Trafalgar and FitzRoy").</p>
<p>The <i>General Synopsis</i> follows, giving the position, pressure (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibar" title="Millibar">millibars</a>) and track of pressure areas (e.g., "Low, Rockall, 987, deepening rapidly, expected Fair Isle 964 by 0700 tomorrow"). Each area's forecast is then read out. Several areas may be combined into a single forecast where the conditions are expected to be similar. <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">Wind direction</a> is given first, then strength (on the Beaufort scale), followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, if any, and (usually) lastly visibility.</p>
<p>Change in wind direction is indicated by "veering" (<a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">clockwise</a> change) or "backing" (anti-clockwise change). Winds at or above force 8 are also described by name for emphasis, i.e., Gale 8, Severe Gale 9, Storm 10, Violent Storm 11 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">Hurricane</a> force 12. The word "force" is only officially used when announcing force 12 winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-format_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-format-9">[9]</a></sup> Visibility is given in the format "Good", meaning that the visibility is greater than 5 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> (9.3 km; 5.8 mi); "Moderate", where visibility is between 2 and 5 nmi (3.7 and 9.3 km; 2.3 and 5.8 mi) nautical miles; "Poor", where visibility is between 1,000 metres and two nautical miles and "<a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">Fog</a>", where visibility is less than 1,000 m (3,300 ft). When severe winter cold combines with strong winds and a cold sea, <a href="/wiki/Icing_(nautical)" title="Icing (nautical)">icing</a> can occur, normally only in sea area Southeast Iceland; if expected, icing warnings (light, moderate or severe) are given as the last item of each sea area forecast.</p>

<p>On 10 January 1993, during the <a href="/wiki/Braer_Storm_of_January_1993" title="Braer Storm of January 1993">Braer Storm</a>, a record North Atlantic low pressure of 914 mb was recorded. The shipping forecast was:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>"Rockall, Malin, Hebrides, Bailey. Southwest hurricane force 12 or more."</li>
</ul>
</dd>

<p>With the information provided in the Shipping Forecast it is possible to compile a pressure chart for the coasts of northwestern Europe. Extended shipping forecasts (0520 and 0048) also include weather reports from a list of additional coastal stations and automatic weather logging stations, which are known by their names, such as "Channel Light Vessel Automatic"; these are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_coastal_weather_stations_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of coastal weather stations of the United Kingdom">Coastal Weather Stations</a>. This additional information does not fall within the 350/380 word restriction. (<a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89_Radio_1" title="RTÉ Radio 1">RTÉ Radio 1</a> broadcasts similar coastal reports for <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>).</p>
<p>The extended forecast also includes an <a href="/wiki/Inshore_coastal_areas_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Inshore coastal areas of the United Kingdom">inshore waters</a> forecast.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gale_warnings">Gale warnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gale warnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition, gale warnings are broadcast at other times between programmes and after news. The BBC instruction reads: 'Gale warnings shall be radiated (broadcast) during the next available programme junction and repeated at the end of the following news bulletin'. For example:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That was the news, and now attention all shipping, especially in sea areas German Bight and Humber: The Met Office issued the following gale warning to shipping at 2206 today. German Bight, west or northwest gale 8 to storm 10, imminent. Humber, west gale 8 or severe gale 9, expected soon. That is the end of the gale warning.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>When giving a gale warning the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> will indicate when it expects the gale to occur. "Imminent" means that a gale is expected within 6 hours, "expected soon" that a gale is expected within 6 to 12 hours and "later" in more than 12 hours' time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequencies">Frequencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Frequencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Shipping Forecast is broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> because its <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">longwave</a> signal can be received clearly at sea all around the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> regardless of time of day or radio conditions. For the same reason, the Shipping Forecast was broadcast in the <a href="/wiki/BBC_National_Programme" title="BBC National Programme">BBC National Programme</a> until September 1939, and then after the Second World War on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Light_Programme" title="BBC Light Programme">BBC Light Programme</a> (later <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a>) until November 1978: these services were all broadcast on longwave. When BBC Radio 4 took over the longwave frequency from Radio 2 on 23 November 1978, the Shipping Forecast moved to Radio 4.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_closedown">Before closedown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Before closedown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The last broadcast of the Shipping Forecast at 0048 each day is traditionally preceded by the playing of "<a href="/wiki/Sailing_By" title="Sailing By">Sailing By</a>", a light orchestral piece by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Binge" title="Ronald Binge">Ronald Binge</a>. This is only very rarely omitted, generally when the schedule is running late. Though occasionally played in full, it is common for only a section of the piece to be broadcast; that section being the length required to fill the gap between the previous programme's ending and the start of the forecast at precisely 0048.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> More importantly, "Sailing By" serves as a vital identification tool – it is distinctive and as such assists anyone attempting to tune in. The forecast is then followed by the <a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen" title="God Save the Queen">National anthem</a> and the closedown of the station for the day, with the <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a> taking over the frequencies after the pips of the <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Time_Signal" title="Greenwich Time Signal">Greenwich Time Signal</a> at 0100.</p>
<h2><span id='"Mini"_shipping_forecast,_maritime_safety'></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Mini.22_shipping_forecast.2C_maritime_safety">"Mini" shipping forecast, maritime safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: "Mini" shipping forecast, maritime safety'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Shipping Forecast should not be confused with similar broadcasts given by HM Coastguard to vessels at sea tuned into <a href="/wiki/Marine_VHF_radio" title="Marine VHF radio">Marine VHF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medium_frequency" title="Medium frequency">MF</a> radio frequencies.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HM_Coastguard" title="HM Coastguard">HM Coastguard</a>'s broadcasts can only be heard by vessels or persons using or tuned into marine VHF and MF radio frequencies, whereas the Shipping Forecast can be heard by anyone tuned into <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a>.</p>
<p>The Coastguard's broadcasts follow the same format as the shipping forecast using the same terminology and style, but the information only normally applies to the area sector or region covered by that particular Coastguard Co-ordination Centre (such as the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a>, for instance).</p>
<p>Announcements of pending broadcasts by HMCG is given on marine <a href="/wiki/Channel_16_VHF" title="Channel 16 VHF">Channel 16 VHF</a> and is normally announced along the lines of "All stations. This is Portland Coastguard... Maritime Safety Information will now be Broadcast on Channel 23... Portland Coastguard."</p>
<p>A similar broadcast on MF is initially announced on 2182 kHz, with a further frequency specified, e.g., 1770 kHz. VHF optimum range is approximately 30 nautical miles (NM), effectively line of sight, whereas MF range is much greater at approximately 150 NM, allowing ships in the Atlantic Ocean and North Sea to receive the broadcast.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 18 December 1993, as part of the Arena Radio Night, BBC Radio 4 and BBC 2 collaborated on a simultaneous broadcast so the shipping forecast – read that night by <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Macmillan" title="Laurie Macmillan">Laurie Macmillan</a> – could be seen as well as heard. To date, it is the only time that it has been broadcast on television.</p>
<p>In addition, a limited shipping forecast was included as part of the closing down routines of the former <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> companies for <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westward_Television" title="Westward Television">Westward Television</a> and latterly <a href="/wiki/Television_South_West" title="Television South West">Television South West</a>, until the late 1980s.</p>
<p>A recording of the shipping forecast can be heard during the closing credits of Rick Stein's food heroes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_on_popular_culture">Influences on popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influences on popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Shipping Forecast is immensely popular with the British public; it daily attracts listeners in the hundreds of thousands – far more than actually require it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baffle_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-baffle-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1995, a plan to move the late night broadcast by 12 minutes triggered angry newspaper editorials and debates in the UK Parliament and was ultimately scrapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gloabal_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gloabal-12">[12]</a></sup> Similar outcry greeted the Met Office's decision to rename Finisterre to FitzRoy, but in that case, the decision was carried through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Peter Jefferson, who read the Forecast for 40 years until 2009, says that he received letters from listeners across the UK saying that the 0048 broadcast helped them get to sleep after a long day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jefferson_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jefferson-4">[4]</a></sup> The Controller of BBC Radio 4, Mark Damazer, attempted to explain its popularity:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It scans poetically. It's got a rhythm of its own. It's eccentric, it's unique, it's English. It's slightly mysterious because nobody really knows where these places are. It takes you into a faraway place that you can't really comprehend unless you're one of these people bobbing up and down in the Channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baffle_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-baffle-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Zeb_Soanes" title="Zeb Soanes">Zeb Soanes</a>, a regular Shipping Forecast reader, described it thus:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>To the non-nautical, it is a nightly litany of the sea. It reinforces a sense of being islanders with a proud seafaring past. Whilst the listener is safely tucked-up in their bed, they can imagine small fishing-boats bobbing about at Plymouth or 170ft waves crashing against Rockall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longwave_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-longwave-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another regular reader of the Forecast, <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Clugston" title="Kathy Clugston">Kathy Clugston</a>, described it as "Like a lullaby, almost".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longwave_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-longwave-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Shipping Forecast has inspired a number of songs and poems.</p>
<div>
<ul>
<li>"Mercy" on <a href="/wiki/Wire_(band)" title="Wire (band)">Wire</a>'s 1978 album <i><a href="/wiki/Chairs_Missing" title="Chairs Missing">Chairs Missing</a></i> includes the lyrics:
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>Snow storms forecast imminently in areas<br/>
Dogger, Viking, Moray, Forth, and Orkney</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/This_Is_a_Low" title="This Is a Low">This Is a Low</a>" on <a href="/wiki/Blur_(band)" title="Blur (band)">Blur</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Parklife" title="Parklife">Parklife</a></i> includes the lyrics:
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>On the Tyne, Forth and Cromarty<br/>
There's a low in the high Forties</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>The song also contains references to Biscay, Dogger, Thames ("Hit traffic on the Dogger bank / Up the Thames to find a taxi rank") and Malin Head, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_coastal_weather_stations_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of coastal weather stations of the United Kingdom">coastal stations</a>.</p>
Blur's early tour film, <i><a href="/wiki/Starshaped" title="Starshaped">Starshaped</a></i>, also uses extracts from the Shipping Forecast during the opening and closing credits.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a> used lyrics relating to the Shipping Forecast in their song "<a href="/wiki/Kid_A" title="Kid A">In Limbo</a>" to represent a theme of being lost:
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>Lundy, Fastnet, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a><br/>
I've got a message I can't read</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
This song appears on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Kid_A" title="Kid A">Kid A</a></i>, the vinyl release of which has the names of several of the forecast's sea areas etched into the runoff space.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dry_the_River" title="Dry the River">Dry the River</a> song "New Ceremony" on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Shallow_Bed" title="Shallow Bed">Shallow Bed</a></i> includes lyrics:
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>But after we danced to the shipping forecast<br/>
the words escaped your mouth...</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
</li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012_Olympics_opening_ceremony" title="2012 Olympics opening ceremony">opening ceremony for the 2012 Olympic Games in London</a>, the shipping forecast was played in the opening part of the production with Elgar's <i><a href="/wiki/Enigma_Variations" title="Enigma Variations">Nimrod</a></i> to represent Britain's maritime heritage.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Young_Punx" title="The Young Punx">The Young Punx</a> sampled the shipping forecast as read by BBC presenter <a href="/wiki/Alan_Smith_(radio)" title="Alan Smith (radio)">Alan Smith</a> for their track "Rockall". The shipping forecast forms the entire lyric for the track, both used in its original form (yet rhyming and scanning) e.g. "Tyne, Dogger, German Bight. Humber, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">Thames</a>, Dover, Wight" and also with the words re-edited into new orders to form new meanings and puns such as "expected to, Rock All, by midnight tonight".</li>
<li>Other popular artists who have used <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">samples</a> of the Shipping Forecast include <a href="/wiki/Andy_White_(singer-songwriter)" title="Andy White (singer-songwriter)">Andy White</a> who added the forecast to the track "The Whole Love Story" to create a very nostalgic, cosy and soporific sound, highly evocative of the British Isles; <a href="/wiki/Tears_for_Fears" title="Tears for Fears">Tears for Fears</a>, whose track "<a href="/wiki/Everybody_Wants_to_Rule_the_World#B-side:_Pharaohs" title="Everybody Wants to Rule the World">Pharaohs</a>" (a play on the name of the sea area "Faeroes") is a setting of the forecast to a mixture of mellow music and sound effects; and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dolby" title="Thomas Dolby">Thomas Dolby</a>, who included a shipping forecast read by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s John Marsh on the track "Windpower". "The Good Ship Lifestyle", a track on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Tubthumper" title="Tubthumper">Tubthumper</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Chumbawamba" title="Chumbawamba">Chumbawamba</a>, starts out with a listing of the sea areas — in the wrong order, however.</li>
<li>The British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ" title="DJ">DJ</a> <a href="/wiki/Rob_Overseer" title="Rob Overseer">Rob Overseer</a>'s album <i>Wreckage</i> has a final track entitled "<a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a>", where the Shipping Forecast surrealistically alternates between reporting the weather and the emotional states of an individual.</li>
<li>The band <a href="/wiki/British_Sea_Power" title="British Sea Power">British Sea Power</a> entitled a B-side of their "<a href="/wiki/Please_Stand_Up" title="Please Stand Up">Please Stand Up</a>" single "Gales Warnings in Viking North".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beck" title="Beck">Beck</a> includes a 27-second sample five minutes into the track "The Horrible Fanfare/Landslide/Exoskeleton" on the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Information" title="The Information">The Information</a></i>.</li>
<li>The experimental electronic musician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robin_Storey" title="Robin Storey">Robin Storey</a>, recording under the name <a href="/wiki/Rapoon" title="Rapoon">Rapoon</a>, sampled the shipping forecast for the track "Falling More Slowly" on his 1997 album <i>Easterly 6 or 7</i>, itself named for the Forecast.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Prodigy" title="The Prodigy">The Prodigy</a> sampled a short section of the shipping forecast in their song "Weather Experience" on their album <i><a href="/wiki/Experience_(The_Prodigy_album)" title="Experience (The Prodigy album)">Experience</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Manfred_Mann%27s_Earth_Band" title="Manfred Mann's Earth Band">Manfred Mann's Earth Band</a> extensively used samples of shipping forecasts as a part of the backing track to "Stranded", from their 1980 album, <i>Chance</i>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Stormwatch_(album)" title="Stormwatch (album)">Stormwatch</a></i> features the shipping forecast between verses of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Sea_Oil" title="North Sea Oil">North Sea Oil</a>". It is read by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Wilson_(meteorologist)" title="Francis Wilson (meteorologist)">Francis Wilson</a>, a TV weatherman who also reads the introduction to "Dun Ringill" on the same album.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silly_Wizard" title="Silly Wizard">Silly Wizard</a> includes a snippet of a gale warning from the shipping forecast in the closing instrumental of "The Fishermen's Song", which tells of the loss of a fishing boat in a North Sea storm.</li>
<li><i>Shipping Forecast</i> by the composer <a href="/wiki/Cecilia_McDowall" title="Cecilia McDowall">Cecilia McDowall</a> was commissioned by Portsmouth Festival Choir and conducted by Andrew Cleary. It was first performed in June 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The work combines the poetry of Seán Street, <a href="/wiki/Psalm_107" title="Psalm 107">Psalm 107</a>, and the words of the shipping forecast itself.</li>
<li>There is a three-bell <a href="/wiki/Change_ringing" title="Change ringing">change ringing</a> method named "Shipping Forecast Singles". It was composed by Sam Austin and was rung to a peal in 2004 at St John the Baptist in <a href="/wiki/Middleton,_Warwickshire" title="Middleton, Warwickshire">Middleton, Warwickshire</a>. Other three-bell methods by the same composer are named after various shipping areas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Justin_Sullivan" title="Justin Sullivan">Justin Sullivan</a>, lead vocalist and founding member of <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a>, released a solo album in 2003 called <i>Navigating by the Stars</i>. Featuring a nautical theme, the album samples part of the Shipping Forecast on the track "Ocean Rising".</li>
<li>In 1966 four English singers calling themselves <a href="/wiki/The_Master_Singers" title="The Master Singers">The Master Singers</a> released a record of "The Weather Forecast" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> which was a typical Shipping Forecast sung in Anglican chant.</li>
<li>The Creative Commons licensing artist Cakeflap's song "The Bakery Is Open" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> contains a mock version of the shipping forecast, with several areas and weathers altered.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mull_Historical_Society" title="Mull Historical Society">Mull Historical Society</a>'s single "The Final Arrears" ends with a twenty-second recording of the Shipping Forecast from "Tuesday 17th October" (presumably 2000).</li>
</ul>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Muir" title="Frank Muir">Frank Muir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denis_Norden" title="Denis Norden">Denis Norden</a> parodied the Shipping Forecast in a song written for an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Take_It_From_Here" title="Take It From Here">Take It From Here</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>In Ross and Finistère<br/>
The outlook is sinisterre<br/>
Rockall and Lundy<br/>
Will clear up by Monday</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Ringers_(comedy)" title="Dead Ringers (comedy)">Dead Ringers</a></i> parodied the Shipping Forecast using <a href="/wiki/Brian_Perkins" title="Brian Perkins">Brian Perkins</a> rapping the forecast (<i>Dogger, Fisher, German Bight – becoming quite cyclonic. Occasional showers making you feel cat-atatatatatata-tonic...</i>). Many other versions have been used including a "Dale Warning" to warn where <a href="/wiki/Dale_Winton" title="Dale Winton">Dale Winton</a> could be found over the coming period, and a spoof in which sailors are warned of ghostly galleons and other nightmarish apparitions.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a>, in his 1988 radio programme <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Fry" title="Saturday Night Fry">Saturday Night Fry</a></i>, issued the following "Shipping Forecast" in the first episode of the programme:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>And now, before the news and weather, here is the Shipping Forecast issued by the Meteorological Office at 1400 hours <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Mean_Time" title="Greenwich Mean Time">Greenwich Mean Time</a>.<br/>
Finisterre, Dogger, Rockall, Bailey: no.<br/>
Wednesday, variable, imminent, super.<br/>
South Utsire, North Utsire, Sheerness, Foulness, <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Ness" title="Eliot Ness">Eliot Ness</a>:<br/>
If you will, often, eminent, 447, 22 yards, touchdown, stupidly.<br/>
Malin, Hebrides, Shetland, Jersey, Fair Isle, Turtle-Neck, Tank Top, Courtelle:<br/>
Blowy, quite misty, sea sickness. Not many fish around, come home, veering suggestively.<br/>
That was the Shipping Forecast for 1700 hours, Wednesday 18 August.</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>The BBC Radio 4 monologue sketch show <i>One</i> features a number of Shipping Forecast parodies, written by <a href="/wiki/David_Quantick" title="David Quantick">David Quantick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Maier" title="Daniel Maier">Daniel Maier</a>, such as the following, originally broadcast on BBC Radio 4 Thursday 21 February 2008:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>And now with the time approaching 5 pm,<br/>
It's time for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-life_crisis" title="Mid-life crisis">mid-life crisis</a> forecast...<br/>
<br/>
Forties; restless: three or four.<br/>
Marriage: stale; becoming suffocating.<br/>
Sportscar, jeans and t-shirt; westerly, five.<br/>
Waitress; blonde; 19 or 20.<br/>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Converse_All-Stars" title="Converse All-Stars">Converse All-Stars</a>; haircut; earring; children;<br/>
becoming embarrassed.<br/>
Tail between legs; atmosphere frosty;<br/>
Spare room: five or six.</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>In an episode of BBC Radio 4 series <i><a href="/wiki/Live_on_Arrival" title="Live on Arrival">Live on Arrival</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Punt" title="Steve Punt">Steve Punt</a> reads the Shopping Forecast, in which the regions are replaced with supermarket names, e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fine_Fare" title="Fine Fare">Fine Fare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sainsbury%27s" title="Sainsbury's">Sainsbury</a>". The sketch ends with the information, "joke mileage decreasing, end of show imminent".</p>
<p>On the broadcast at 0048 on Saturday 19 March 2011, the area forecasts were delivered by <a href="/wiki/John_Prescott" title="John Prescott">John Prescott</a> to raise awareness of <a href="/wiki/Red_Nose_Day_2011" title="Red Nose Day 2011">Red Nose Day 2011</a>, a charity event organised by <a href="/wiki/Comic_Relief" title="Comic Relief">Comic Relief</a>. The format then reverted to the BBC continuity announcer <a href="/wiki/Alice_Arnold" title="Alice Arnold">Alice Arnold</a> for the reports on coastal areas. On delivering the area forecast for Humber, Prescott (who had represented the parliamentary constituency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hull_East" title="Hull East">Hull East</a> for almost 40 years before retiring) slipped deliberately into his distinctive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Yorkshire" title="East Yorkshire">East Yorkshire</a> accent – "'Umber – without the 'H', as we say it up there".</p>
<p>The comedian <a href="/wiki/Marti_Caine" title="Marti Caine">Marti Caine</a> listed the Shipping Forecast as one of her eight records when she made her second appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/Desert_Island_Discs" title="Desert Island Discs">Desert Island Discs</a></i> on 24 March 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Terence_Davies" title="Terence Davies">Terence Davies</a>' film <i><a href="/wiki/Distant_Voices,_Still_Lives" title="Distant Voices, Still Lives">Distant Voices, Still Lives</a></i>, a largely autobiographical account of growing up in <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> during the 1940s and 1950s, opens with a shipping forecast from this period.</p>
<p>In an episode of the BBC sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Keeping_Up_Appearances" title="Keeping Up Appearances">Keeping Up Appearances</a></i>, a soon-to-be-sailing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyacinth_Bucket" title="Hyacinth Bucket">Hyacinth Bucket</a> calls over the telephone for an advance shipping forecast, even though the yacht she and her husband Richard are to visit is moored on the Thames near Oxford. Names mentioned (in scene sequence) are: <a href="/wiki/Fisher_Bank" title="Fisher Bank">Fisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_Bight" title="German Bight">German Bight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cromarty_Firth" title="Cromarty Firth">Cromarty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dogger_Bank" title="Dogger Bank">Dogger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heligoland_Bight" title="Heligoland Bight">Heligoland</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/German_Bight" title="German Bight">German Bight</a>).</p>
<p>In an episode of the BBC sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Ever_Decreasing_Circles" title="Ever Decreasing Circles">Ever Decreasing Circles</a></i>, Howard and Hilda leave their neighbour Paul's house party early, explaining that they must get back to listen to the Shipping Forecast. Paul asks why, seeing as they have never owned a boat. Howard explains, "Well, it takes us nicely into the news."</p>
<p>Mentioned briefly in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Kes_(film)" title="Kes (film)">Kes</a></i> (see <i>Art and Literature</i> section above).</p>
<p>A recording of part of the forecast is played over the opening and closing credits of <a href="/wiki/Rick_Stein" title="Rick Stein">Rick Stein</a>'s 2000 TV series <i>Rick Stein's Seafood Lover's Guide</i>.</p>
<p>In an episode of the Channel 4 television series <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Books" title="Black Books">Black Books</a></i>, the character Fran Katzenjammer listens to the shipping forecast because a friend from her college is reading it. She finds his voice arousing.</p>
<p>In the BBC sitcom <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/As_Time_Goes_By_(UK_TV_series)" title="As Time Goes By (UK TV series)">As Time Goes By</a></i>, the character Mrs Bale is obsessed by and constantly mentions The Shipping Forecast much to the befuddlement of the other characters.</p>
<p>Many characters in the 1983 children's cartoon, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Portland_Bill" title="The Adventures of Portland Bill">The Adventures of Portland Bill</a></i> are named after features mentioned in the Shipping Forecast.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Funcom" title="Funcom">Funcom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_role-playing_game" title="Massively multiplayer online role-playing game">massively multiplayer online role-playing game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_World" title="The Secret World">The Secret World</a></i>, the shipping forecast plays over the radio in a <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> station, adding to the British flavour distinguishing the setting from other worldwide locations featured in the game.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_Stories">Sleep Stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sleep Stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2017, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jefferson_(radio)" title="Peter Jefferson (radio)">Peter Jefferson</a> recorded a reinvented version of the <b>Shipping Forecast</b> as "a bedtime story for grown-ups."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The 26-minute recording is available as a Sleep Story on the meditation app <a href="/wiki/Calm_(company)" title="Calm (company)"><i>Calm.</i></a> Sleep Stories, launched by <i>Calm</i> in December 2016, are bedtime stories for adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Online">Online</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipping_Forecast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Online">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Shipping Forecast is published online by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.metoffice.gov.uk/weather/marine/shipping_forecast.html" rel="nofollow">Met Office</a> and the <a class="external text" href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/weather/coast_and_sea/shipping_forecast/" rel="nofollow">BBC</a>.</p>
<p>The daily 0048 forecast is available online via <a class="external text" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b006qfvv" rel="nofollow">BBC iPlayer</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009 an <a class="external text" href="http://twitter.com/shipforecast" rel="nofollow">unofficial Twitter feed</a> was created, but has not been updated since 2014.</p>
