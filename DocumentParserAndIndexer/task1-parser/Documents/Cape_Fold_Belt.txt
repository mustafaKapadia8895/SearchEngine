<p>The <b>Cape Fold Belt</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fold_and_thrust_belt" title="Fold and thrust belt">fold and thrust belt</a> of late <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> age, which affected the sequence of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock layers</a> of the Cape Supergroup in the southwestern corner of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shone_Booth_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shone_Booth-1">[1]</a></sup> It was originally continuous with the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_la_Ventana_(mountains)" title="Sierra de la Ventana (mountains)">Ventana Mountains</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%ADa_Blanca" title="BahÃ­a Blanca">BahÃ­a Blanca</a> in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pensacola_Mountains" title="Pensacola Mountains">Pensacola Mountains</a> (East Antarctica), the <a href="/wiki/Ellsworth_Mountains" title="Ellsworth Mountains">Ellsworth Mountains</a> (West Antarctica) and the <a href="/wiki/Hunter-Bowen_orogeny" title="Hunter-Bowen orogeny">Hunter-Bowen orogeny</a> in eastern Australia. The rocks involved are generally <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shales" title="Shales">shales</a>, with the shales (Bokkeveld Group) persisting in the valley floors while the erosion resistant sandstones (belonging to the Peninsula Formation) form the parallel ranges, the Cape Fold Mountains, which reach a maximum height of 2325Â m at <a href="/wiki/Seweweekspoortpiek" title="Seweweekspoortpiek">Seweweekspoortpiek</a> (âSeven Weeks <a href="/wiki/Defile_(geography)" title="Defile (geography)">Defile</a> Peakâ in Afrikaans).</p>
<p>The Cape Fold Mountains form a series of parallel ranges that run along the south-western and southern coastlines of South Africa for 850Â km from the <a href="/wiki/Cederberg" title="Cederberg">Cederberg</a> 200Â km to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>, and then along the south coast as far as <a href="/wiki/Port_Elizabeth" title="Port Elizabeth">Port Elizabeth</a>, 650Â km to the east (see the two maps one above the other on the right).</p>

<p>The rocks were laid down as sediments in a <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> that developed in southern <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>, just south of Southern Africa, during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> Periods (starting about 510Â million years ago, and ending about 330-350Â million years ago).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> (See the yellow block labeled <b>C</b> on the Earth's geological timeline diagram on the right.) An 8-km-thick layer of sediment, known as the <b>Cape Supergroup</b> (see below), accumulated on the floor of this rift valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> Closure of the rift valley, starting 330Â million years ago, resulted from the development of a <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> zone along the southern margin of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>, and the consequent drift of the Falkland Plateau back towards Africa, during the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> periods. After closure of the rift valley, and rucking of the Cape Supergroup into a series of parallel folds, running mainly east-west (with a short section running north-south in the west, due to collision with eastward moving Patagonia), the continued subduction of the paleo-Pacific Plate beneath the Falkland Plateau and the resulting collision of the latter with Southern Africa, raised a mountain range of immense proportions to the south of the former rift valley. The folded Cape Supergroup formed the northern foothills of this towering mountain range.</p>
<p>The weight of the Falkland-Cape Supergroup mountains caused the continental crust of Southern Africa to sag, forming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroarc" title="Retroarc">retroarc</a> foreland system, into which the <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup" title="Karoo Supergroup">Karoo Supergroup</a> was deposited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shone_Booth_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shone_Booth-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catuneanu_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Catuneanu-6">[6]</a></sup> Eventually much of the Cape Supergroup became buried under these Karoo deposits, only to re-emerge as mountains when upliftment of the subcontinent, about 180Â million years ago, and again 20Â million years ago, started an episode of continuous erosion that was to remove many kilometers of surface deposits from Southern Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the tops of the original Cape Fold Mountains were eroded away, they eroded much slower than the considerably softer Karoo deposits to the north. Thus the Cape Fold Belt "erupted" from the eroding African landscape to form the parallel ranges of mountains that run for 800Â km along the southern and south-western Cape coastline today. In fact, they form the coastline, either sloping steeply directly into the sea, or are separated from it by a relatively narrow coastal plain.</p>
<p>The Falkland Mountain range had probably eroded into relative insignificance by the mid-Jurassic Period, and started drifting to the south-west soon after Gondwana began to break up 150Â million years ago, leaving the Cape Fold Belt to edge the southern portion of the newly formed African continent. Even though the mountains are very old by Andean and Alpine standards, they remain steep and rugged due to their <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> (see below) making them very resistant to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>. The famous <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a> forms part of the Cape Fold Belt, being made up of the local lowest (oldest) strata of the Cape Supergroup, composed predominantly of quartzitic sandstone which forms the impressive, almost vertical cliffs which characterize the mountain and the rest of the range which constitutes the backbone of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The degree to which the original Cape Fold mountains (formed during the Carboniferous and early Permian Periods) have been eroded is attested to by the fact that the 1Â km high Table Mountain on the Cape Peninsula is a <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">syncline mountain</a>, meaning that it formed part of the bottom of a valley when the Cape Supergroup was initially folded. The <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticline</a>, or highest elevation of the fold between Table Mountain and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hottentots-Holland" title="Hottentots-Holland">Hottentots-Holland</a> Mountains (1.2 to 1.6Â km elevation), on the opposite side of the isthmus connecting the Peninsula to the Mainland, has been eroded away. The <a href="/wiki/Marine_geology_of_the_Cape_Peninsula_and_False_Bay#The_geological_structure_of_the_region" title="Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay">Malmesbury shale and granite basement</a> on which this anticline mountain rested also formed an anticline; but being composed of much softer rocks, readily eroded into a 50Â km wide flat plain, (now covered with dune sands) called the "Cape Flats".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cape <i>Fold</i> Belt (i.e. the mountain ranges) extends from about <a href="/wiki/Clanwilliam,_Western_Cape" title="Clanwilliam, Western Cape">Clanwilliam</a> (approximately 200Â km north of Cape Town), to about <a href="/wiki/Port_Elizabeth" title="Port Elizabeth">Port Elizabeth</a> (approximately 650Â km east of Cape Town). The Cape Supergroup sediments beyond these points are not folded into mountain ranges, but do, in places, form steep cliffs or gorges, where the surrounding sediments have been eroded away (see, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Oribi_Gorge" title="Oribi Gorge">Oribi Gorge</a> in <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mountains, although only of moderate height, are majestic and dramatic. This is due in part to numerous geological factors; The ranges usually have few to no foothills and rise directly from valley floors. The bases of the mountains are usually at or near sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cape_Supergroup">Cape Supergroup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Fold_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cape Supergroup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mountains of the Cape Fold Belt are composed of rocks belonging to the <b>Cape Supergroup</b>, which is more extensive than the Fold Belt. The Supergroup is divided into several distinct Groups.</p>
<p>The western and southern extents of the Supergroup have been folded into a series of longitudinal mountain ranges, by the collision of the Falkland Plateau into what would later become South Africa (see diagrams on the left). However, the entire suite in this region slopes downwards towards the north and east, so that the oldest rocks are exposed in the south and west, while the youngest members of the Supergroup are exposed in the north, where the entire Cape Supergroup dives beneath the <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup" title="Karoo Supergroup">Karoo rocks</a>. Drilling in the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a> has established that Cape Supergroup rocks are found below the surface up to approximately 150Â km north of their northernmost exposure on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cape Supergroup extends eastwards beyond the Fold Belt into the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Cape" title="Eastern Cape">Eastern Cape</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>, where no folding took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Klipheuwel_and_Natal_Groups">Klipheuwel and Natal Groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Fold_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Klipheuwel and Natal Groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The initial sedimentation into the rift valley which developed in southern Gondwana (see diagram at top left) was confined to the western and eastern ends of the rift. Rivers diverted into these early rifts deposited sand and gravel to form the <b>Klipheuwel Group</b> in the west, and the <b>Natal Group</b> in the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> These formations contain no fossils. Today the Klipheuwel Group is exposed in several small patches near Lamberts Bay, Piketberg, and to the south-west of <a href="/wiki/Paarl" title="Paarl">Paarl Rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Natal Group, which is similar to the Table Mountain Group (see below), is found in several elongated patches near the coast of the northern <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Cape" title="Eastern Cape">Eastern Cape</a> and <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>. It forms the impressive cliffs of <a href="/wiki/Oribi_Gorge" title="Oribi Gorge">Oribi Gorge</a>, and can also be seen in a road cutting between <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pietermaritzburg" title="Pietermaritzburg">Pietermaritzburg</a>, particularly at the Marian Hill Toll Plaza.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> Most of the group has been deeply eroded by numerous rivers creating the ragged plateaus and scarps of the Durban-Pietermaritzburg region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tankard_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tankard-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Table_Mountain_Group">Table Mountain Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Fold_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Table Mountain Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the widening and deepening of the rift valley, the entire southern portion of what was to become South Africa, as far as a line extending from <a href="/wiki/Calvinia" title="Calvinia">Calvinia</a> in the west to <a href="/wiki/East_London,_Eastern_Cape" title="East London, Eastern Cape">East London</a> in the east, plus the KwaZulu-Natal coastline, became flooded,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> leading to a seaway across the southern and eastern parts of the country, called the Agulhas Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> The sea floor consisted of the Klipheuwel Group in the west, the Natal Group in the east, and eroded pre-Cambrian rocks in between.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first sediments into the initially still shallow, possibly inland, sea were alternating layers of maroon colored mudstones and buff colored sandstones, each mostly between 10 and 30Â cm thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup> The mudstone units commonly display ripple marks from the ebb and flow of tidal currents, as well as polygonal sand-filled mud cracks that indicate occasional exposure to desiccation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup> This layer, known as the <b>Graafwater Formation</b>, reaches a maximum thickness of 400Â m,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tankard_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-tankard-10">[10]</a></sup> but on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a> it is only 60â70Â m thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup> No fossils have been found in the Graafwater rocks, but shallow-water animal tracks have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tankard_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-tankard-10">[10]</a></sup> A particularly good example of these tracks can be viewed in the foyer of Geology Department of the University of Stellenbosch, where a slab of Graafwater rock from the <a href="/wiki/Cederberg" title="Cederberg">Cederberg mountains</a> has been built into the wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cutting for Chapmanâs Peak Drive, on the Cape Peninsula, is carved into the Graafwater Formation which overlays the Cape Granite basement rock below the road. The Graafwater Formation can also be clearly seen in the cutting on the second hairpin bend as the <a href="/wiki/Ou_Kaapse_Weg" title="Ou Kaapse Weg">Ou Kaapse Weg</a> (road) goes up the slope from Westlake on to the Silvermine plateau. In the cutting one can also see the abrupt and obvious transition into the Peninsula Formation above it. Looking up the slope from below to the first hairpin bend, the granite basement on which the Graafwater formation rests is visible. And in the cutting at the first hairpin bend, the ocher-colored, gritty clay into which the granite weathers is clearly displayed.</p>
<p>With further subsidence of the rift valley floor, and possibly breaking through to the ocean, the sediments abruptly become more sandy, indicative of a sudden increase in the depth of the Agulhas Sea (see photograph on the right). A deposit, known as the <b>Peninsula Formation</b> (also often referred to as <b><a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain_Sandstone_(Geological_Formation)" title="Table Mountain Sandstone (Geological Formation)">Table Mountain Sandstone</a></b>), consisting of thickly layered <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartzitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, with a maximum thickness of 2000Â m, was laid down. These sandstones are very hard, and erosion resistant. They therefore form the bulk of mountains and steep cliffs and rugged crags of the Cape Fold Belt, including the upper 600Â m of the 1Â km high <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a>, below which <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> is situated. It contains no fossils.</p>
<p>The Peninsula formation can be traced from 300Â km north of Cape Town (i.e. about 50Â km north of <a href="/wiki/Vanrhynsdorp" title="Vanrhynsdorp">Vanrhynsdorp</a> on the West Coast), southwards to Cape Town and then eastwards to northern <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>, a total distance of approximately 1800Â km, roughly along the South African coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup> Only the section between <a href="/wiki/Clanwilliam,_Western_Cape" title="Clanwilliam, Western Cape">Clanwilliam</a> (approximately 200Â km north of Cape Town) and <a href="/wiki/Port_Elizabeth" title="Port Elizabeth">Port Elizabeth</a> (approximately 650Â km east of Cape Town) is folded into the Cape Fold Mountains.</p>
<p>During the laying down of the Peninsula Formation sediments, the western portion of the region was covered for a brief period of time by glaciers. The <a href="/wiki/Diamictite" title="Diamictite">diamictite</a> sediments that were laid down by these glaciers, or in glacial lakes, are known as the <b>Pakhuis and Cederberg Formations</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> A small patch of Pakhuis <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tillite" title="Tillite">tillite</a> occurs on the top of Table Mountain at <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Maclear" title="Thomas Maclear">Maclear</a>âs beacon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup> but most of the Pakhuis and Cederberg Formations are found as a thin layer (on average only about 60Â m thick<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup>) in the Peninsula Formation rocks of the more inland mountains to the west of a line between <a href="/wiki/Swellendam" title="Swellendam">Swellendam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calvinia" title="Calvinia">Calvinia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> These <a href="/wiki/Diamictite" title="Diamictite">diamictite</a> rocks are composed of finely ground mud, containing a jumble of faceted pebbles. They can easily be recognized at a distance as this formation readily erodes into fertile, gently sloping, green swaths in a landscape where this contrasts starkly with the bare rocky surfaces of the quartzites above and below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> In several locations the quartzites below the glacial horizon have been rucked into a series of folds. This is believed to have been caused by the movement of ice ploughing into the underlying unconsolidated sands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> A good example of this can be seen on a ridge of rocks near <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Maclear" title="Thomas Maclear">Maclear</a>âs Beacon on Table Mountain, close to the edge of the plateau overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town City Bowl</a> and Table Bay.</p>
<p>The Pakhuis Formation is also well exposed on the road along Michellâs Pass just below the Tolhuis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup> and especially on the Pakhuis Pass near <a href="/wiki/Clanwilliam,_Western_Cape" title="Clanwilliam, Western Cape">Clanwilliam</a>, from which the formation derives its name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The glaciers which formed the Pakhuis and Cederberg formations came from the north west, in the direction of the South Pole which was located in the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tankard_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-tankard-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Upper Peninsula Formation, above the Pakhuis and Cederberg Formations, consists of much softer sandstone than the Lower Peninsula Formation, and is often referred to as the <b>Nardouw Formation</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitfield_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitfield-12">[12]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Cederberg" title="Cederberg">Cederberg</a> this formation has been eroded by the wind into a wide variety of "sculptures", caves, and other fascinating structures for which these mountains have become well-known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitfield_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-whitfield-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bokkeveld_Group">Bokkeveld Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Fold_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bokkeveld Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>About 400Â million years ago (in the early Devonian Period) there was further subsidence of the rift valley floor. This brought about the deposition of deeper-water, fine grained sediments of the <b>Bokkeveld Group</b>. This is in marked contrast to the predominantly sandy sediments of the Table Mountain Group. The Bokkeveld Group consists predominantly mudstones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Cape Supergroup had been folded into the Cape Fold Mountains, these soft mudstones readily washed away from the mountain tops, and only remain in the valleys. Here they form the fertile soils on which the vineyards and fruit orchards of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape" title="Western Cape">Western Cape</a> flourish with the help of irrigation from the rivers that have their sources in the surrounding mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bokkeveld Group does not extend on to the Cape Peninsula or its isthmus (the Cape Flats). Here the <a href="/wiki/Stellenbosch" title="Stellenbosch">Stellenbosch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franschhoek" title="Franschhoek">Franschhoek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paarl" title="Paarl">Paarl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durbanville" title="Durbanville">Durbanville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tulbagh" title="Tulbagh">Tulbagh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constantia,_Cape_Town" title="Constantia, Cape Town">Constantia</a> vineyards have been planted on the weathered <a href="/wiki/Marine_geology_of_the_Cape_Peninsula_and_False_Bay#The_geological_structure_of_the_region" title="Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay">Cape Granite and Malmesbury shale</a> soils, which form the basement rocks on which the Cape Supergroup rocks in this region rest.</p>
<p>The Bokkeveld Group extends eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Port_Alfred" title="Port Alfred">Port Alfred</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Grahamstown" title="Grahamstown">Grahamstown</a>), approximately 120Â km beyond the eastern extent of the Cape Fold Belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bulk of the fossils found in the Cape Supergroup occur in the Bokkeveld mudstones. They include a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brachiopods" title="Brachiopods">brachiopods</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trilobites" title="Trilobites">trilobites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molluscs" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echinoderms" title="Echinoderms">echinoderms</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Starfish" title="Starfish">starfish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crinoids" title="Crinoids">crinoids</a>, and the extinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blastoids" title="Blastoids">blastoids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cystoids" title="Cystoids">cystoids</a>), <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a> and fish with jaws (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placoderms" title="Placoderms">placoderms</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tankard_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-tankard-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Witteberg_Group">Witteberg Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Fold_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Witteberg Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The upper layers of the Bokkeveld Group become increasingly more sandy, grading into the sandstone of the <b>Witteberg Group</b>, named for the range of mountains to the south of <a href="/wiki/Matjiesfontein" title="Matjiesfontein">Matjiesfontein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laingsburg,_Western_Cape" title="Laingsburg, Western Cape">Laingsburg</a> in the southern <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a>. These rocks were laid down 370 - 330Â million years ago in the silted up, and therefore shallow marine conditions of what remained of the Agulhas Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> The group contains fewer fossils than the Bokkeveld Group, but the assemblage that is preserved includes primitive fish, an extinct species of shark, brachiopods, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalves" title="Bivalves">bivalves</a>, and a meter long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurypterida" title="Eurypterida">sea scorpion</a>. There are also plant fossils and numerous animal tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> The Witteberg Group is truncated by the overlying <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#Dwyka_group" title="Karoo Supergroup">Dwyka sediments</a> of glacial origin. The latter forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup" title="Karoo Supergroup">Karoo Supergroup</a>. Therefore, the Witteberg Group forms the uppermost layer of the Cape Supergroup. It tends to form the most inland outcrops of the Cape Supergroup, and can be traced eastwards as far as the Bokkeveld Group can be traced (i.e. to Port Alfred), about 120Â km beyond the Cape Fold Belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bokkeveld and Witteberg groups do not occur in the north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Cape" title="Eastern Cape">Eastern Cape</a> and <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>, where the Cape Supergroup is represent only by the Natal Group and a trace of the Peninsula Formation (without the intervening Graafwater Formation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_the_Fold_Mountains">Formation of the Fold Mountains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Fold_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Formation of the Fold Mountains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="703" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg/250px-Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg/375px-Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg/500px-Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agulhas_Bank_NOAA_ETOPO1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Bank" title="Agulhas Bank">Agulhas Bank</a> is the broad, shallow part of the southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> which extends up to 250Â km (160Â mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agulhas" title="Cape Agulhas">Cape Agulhas</a> before falling steeply to the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a>. It represents the remains of the Falkland Plateau still attached to Southern Africa. The Agulhas Ridge (or Agulhas-Falkland Fracture Zone) which runs from the south-eastern edge of the Bank towards the south west for 1,200 kilometres (750Â mi) across the South Atlantic, is the fault line along which the remainder of the Falkland Plateau moved to the southern tip of South America, a short while after <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> broke up, 110Â million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Witteberg sediments were laid down in what remained of the Agulhas Sea - a shallow, much reduced expanse of water compared to its size during the Bokkeveld period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> Immediately following the Witteberg period (about 330Â million years ago) much of Gondwana (particularly what was to become Africa and Antarctica, but also parts of South America and India) became covered in a kilometers thick layer of ice as the super-continent drifted over the South Pole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norman_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-norman-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Diamictite" title="Diamictite">diamictite</a> deposits left by these glaciers form the first layer of the <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup" title="Karoo Supergroup">Karoo Supergroup</a>, called the <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#The_Dwyka_group" title="Karoo Supergroup">Dwyka Group</a>. During the glaciation period the Falkland Plateau started to move northwards into what was to become southern Africa, closing the depression that had been the Agulhas Sea, and rucking the Cape Supergroup sediments into folds running roughly parallel to what would ultimately become the south-western and southern coastlines of South Africa. This mountain building continued into the next phase of the Karoo sedimentation, which began about 260 million years ago, after the ice sheets had melted, leaving a large lake (the Karoo Sea) extending over much of South Africa. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Marine_geology" title="Marine geology">marine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lacustrine_deposits" title="Lacustrine deposits">lacustrine deposits</a> form the Karoo's <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#The_Ecca_group" title="Karoo Supergroup">Ecca Group</a>. During these Ecca times continued collision of the Falkland Plateau into Southern Africa, and subduction of oceanic crust under the plateau caused the formation of a range of mountains of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> proportions to the south of South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> The Cape Fold Mountains became fully formed during this period to become the northern foothills of this enormous mountain range. The folding that occurred during this period therefore involved not only the Cape Supergroup sediments, but also the southern portions of the Dwyka and Ecca Groups of the Karoo Supergroup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time Gondwana broke up about 150Â million years ago, the Falkland Mountains had been all but eroded away, before drifting south-westwards to their present position off the coast of southern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, close to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a>, leaving behind only the submarine <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Bank" title="Agulhas Bank">Agulhas Bank</a> along the southern coastline of Africa . The Cape Fold Mountains possibly survived erosional obliteration, firstly because of the extremely hard rocks (the Peninsula Formation Sandstone) that form the backbone of the mountain chains, but also possibly because they had become buried under the Karoo deposits which originated in the Falkland Mountains. Thus traces of Karoo deposits can, for instance, be found in the Worcester-Robertson valley in the middle of the Fold Belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norman2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-norman2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the Dwyka and Ecca sediments adjoining the Cape Fold Mountains were subjected to the same compression forces that gave rise to the Cape Mountains, they do not form the same mountain ranges as do the Cape Fold Mountains. This is because they are composed of much softer rocks than the Peninsula Formation Sandstone, and were thus soon eroded into the flat plains of the <a href="/wiki/Karoo#Divisions" title="Karoo">"Lower Karoo"</a>, except where they were protected by hard, erosion resistant <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#Drakensberg_and_Lebombo_groups" title="Karoo Supergroup">dolerite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#The_Ecca_group" title="Karoo Supergroup">turbidite</a> caps, to form isolated mountains that stand out from the plain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norman2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-norman2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>The mountains are not particularly ancient, despite their old-looking appearance. They are considered middle-aged in geologic terms. They were created when the Falkland Plateau collided with Southern Africa, when <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, the supercontinent formed during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> periods (from 510 to about 330-350Â million years ago),.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-compton_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-compton-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-20"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup> Their stature, with heights varying from 1000m to 2300m, is mostly due to the weather-resistant rocks of <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> of the Peninsula Group (see above).</p>
<p>They occur in long parallel ranges each not much more than 10Â km wide, separated by equally long valleys with a maximum width of about 50Â km (mostly only 15â30Â km wide). Almost all of these ranges consist of hard erosion resistant Peninsula Group rocks. The valleys tend to be floored by Bokkeveld Group mudstones. A remarkable feature of these ranges is that the 1500Â m high mountains (from base to crest) are cut through by very narrow, almost vertically walled <a href="/wiki/Defile_(geography)" title="Defile (geography)">defiles</a>, not much more than 50â70Â m wide at the bottom, through which rivers flow from the inland <a href="/wiki/Great_Escarpment,_Southern_Africa" title="Great Escarpment, Southern Africa">Great Escarpment</a> to the sea. It is from within these narrow defiles, many of which can be traveled by road, that one is afforded a cross sectional view of the mountains, and can appreciate their intense folding and distortion (see the photographs on the top right above). Their origin is as follows:</p>
<p>After the fold mountains had been formed they became buried under sediments derived from the massive Himalaya-sized Falkland Mountains to the south of the Cape Fold Belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-22"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norman_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norman-14">[14]</a></sup> Sediments eroded from these mountains provided the bulk of the 6Â km thick <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#Beaufort_group" title="Karoo Supergroup">Beaufort sediments</a> in the Karoo Basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-23"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> but they also covered the Cape Fold Belt, thereby protecting them from erosion. At the end of the Karoo Period about 180Â million years ago, the subcontinent was covered by a thick layer of <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Supergroup#Drakensberg_and_Lebombo_groups" title="Karoo Supergroup">Drakensberg lavas</a>, an event that was accompanied by upliftment or bulging of Southern Africa, ushering in an almost uninterrupted period, continuing to the present, of erosion removing many kilometers of surface rocks from the entire subcontinent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-24"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> Rivers running off this bulging interior into the seas that were forming around South Africa as Gondwana was breaking up 150Â million years ago, eventually encountered rocky ridges as the protective layer over the Cape Fold Belt eroded away, exposing their mountain tops. The rivers breached these ridges, after possibly being dammed back for a short period, creating a narrow passage through the low rocky barrier. Continued erosion exposed more and more of these quartzitic mountain ranges, but the rivers, now confined to narrow, fast flowing gorges, continued breaking through each barrier as the surrounding landscape eroded to lower and lower levels, particularly during the past 20Â million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-25"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>These 150âmillion-year-old rivers therefore cut the defile, starting by flowing over, and then through the gradually erupting Cape Fold Mountains, to form the spectacular âpoorteâ and âkloweâ (plural of âpoortâ and âkloofâ, the <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a> for defile or chasm) that characterize these mountains today. The best known defiles are: <a href="/wiki/Meiringspoort" title="Meiringspoort">Meiringspoort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seweweekspoortpiek" title="Seweweekspoortpiek">Seweweekspoort</a>, the Tradouw pass, Kogmanskloof, Garciaâs pass, the Gourits River gorge (which does not have a road running through it), and <a href="/wiki/Michell%27s_Pass" title="Michell's Pass">Michell's Pass</a>, which is, however, more V-shaped than the others, but impressive nevertheless. In addition several roads and highways crest the mountains over cols, or go through them via tunnels.</p>
<p>A number of parallel faults still run roughly parallel with the coast, having formed during the <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rifting</a> when South America and the Falkland Plateau separated from Africa. <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> was to the west of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> were to the south during the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic Period</a> prior to separation. Most of these faults are inactive today, but in 1969 the towns of <a href="/wiki/Ceres,_Western_Cape" title="Ceres, Western Cape">Ceres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tulbagh" title="Tulbagh">Tulbagh</a>, about 160Â km north-east of Cape Town, were severely damaged by an earthquake, originating from movement of the northern end of the Worcester Fault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-26"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truswell_5-21"><a href="#cite_note-Truswell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another major (inactive) fault line runs for 300Â km along the southern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Swartberg" title="Swartberg">Swartberg</a> Mountains. The Swartberg Mountains were uplifted along this fault, to such an extent that in the Oudtshoorn region the rocks that form the base of the Cape Supergroup are exposed. These are locally known as the "Cango Group", but are probably continuous with the "<a href="/wiki/Marine_geology_of_the_Cape_Peninsula_and_False_Bay#The_geological_structure_of_the_region" title="Marine geology of the Cape Peninsula and False Bay">Malmesbury Group</a>" that forms the base of <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>, and similar outcrops in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape" title="Western Cape">Western Cape</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-27"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Little Karoo</a> the outcrop is composed of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, into which an underground stream has carved the impressively extensive <a href="/wiki/Cango_Caves" title="Cango Caves">Cango Caves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geologicalmap_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-geologicalmap-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norman_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norman-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Readers_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Readers-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bulpin_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bulpin-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
