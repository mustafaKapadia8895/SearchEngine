<p><b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Laacher See</span></b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈlaːxɐ ˈzeː]</a></span>) or <b>Lake Laach</b> (in English) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Crater_lake" title="Crater lake">caldera lake</a> with a diameter of 2 km (1.2 mi) in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, about 24 km (15 mi) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a> and 37 km (23 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, and is closest to the town of <a href="/wiki/Andernach" title="Andernach">Andernach</a> situated 8 km (5.0 mi) to the east on the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. It is in the <a href="/wiki/Eifel" title="Eifel">Eifel</a> mountain range, and is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Eifel_volcanic_field" title="East Eifel volcanic field">East Eifel volcanic field</a> within the larger <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulkan_Eifel" title="Vulkan Eifel">Vulkaneifel</a></i>. The lake was formed by a <a href="/wiki/Plinian_eruption" title="Plinian eruption">Plinian eruption</a> approximately 12,900 years ago with a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">Volcanic Explosivity Index</a> (VEI) of 6, on the same scale as the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo#The_1991_eruption" title="Mount Pinatubo">Pinatubo eruption of 1991</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klerk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klerk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaard_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bogaard-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laacher_See&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lake is oval in shape, and surrounded by high banks. The lava was quarried for millstones from the Roman period until the introduction of iron rollers for grinding corn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the western side lies the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine_order" title="Benedictine order">Benedictine</a> monastery of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Laach_Abbey" title="Maria Laach Abbey">Maria Laach Abbey</a> (<i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Abbatia Lacensis</span></i>), founded in 1093 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_II_of_Laach" title="Heinrich II of Laach">Henry II of Laach</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Luxembourg" title="House of Luxembourg">House of Luxembourg</a>, first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_Palatine_of_the_Rhine" title="Count Palatine of the Rhine">Count Palatine of the Rhine</a>, who had his castle opposite to the monastery above the eastern lakeside.</p>
<p>The lake has no natural outlet, but is drained by a tunnel dug before 1170 and rebuilt several times since. It is named after Abbot Fulbert, abbot from 1152–1177, who is believed to have built it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_eruption">The eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laacher_See&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Volcanism in Germany can be traced back for millions of years, due to the collision between the African and Eurasian plates, but it has been concentrated in bursts associated with the loading and unloading of ice during glacial advances and retreats. The initial blasts of Laacher See, which took place in late spring or early summer, flattened trees up to four kilometres away. The magma opened a route to the surface which erupted for about ten hours, with the plume probably reaching a height of 35 kilometres. Activity continued for several weeks or months, with <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">pyroclastic currents</a> which covered valleys up to ten kilometres away with sticky <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">tephra</a>. Near the crater deposits are over fifty metres, and even five kilometres away they are still ten metres thick. All plants and animals for a distance of about sixty kilometres to the northeast and forty kilometres to the southeast must have been exterminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> An estimated 6 km<sup>3</sup> (1.4 cu mi) of magma erupted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QI1999_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-QI1999-7">[7]</a></sup> producing around 16 km<sup>3</sup> (3.8 cu mi) of <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">tephra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This 'huge' <a href="/wiki/Plinian_eruption" title="Plinian eruption">Plinian eruption</a> thus had a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">Volcanic Explosivity Index</a> (VEI) of 6.</p>
<p>Tephra deposits from the eruption dammed the Rhine, creating a 140 km<sup>2</sup> (50 sq mi) lake. When the dam broke, an outburst flood swept downstream, leaving deposits as far away as Bonn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QI1999_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-QI1999-7">[7]</a></sup> The fallout has been identified in an area of more than 300,000 square kilometres, stretching from central France to northern Italy, and from southern Sweden to Poland, making it an invaluable tool for chronological correlation of archaeological and palaeoenvironmental layers across the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_of_the_eruption">Aftermath of the eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laacher_See&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath of the eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wider effects of the eruption were limited, amounting to several years of cold summers and up to two decades of environmental disruption in Germany. However, the lives of the local population, known as the <a href="/wiki/Federmesser_culture" title="Federmesser culture">Federmesser culture</a>, were disrupted. Before the eruption, they were a sparsely distributed people who subsisted by foraging and hunting, using both spears and bows and arrows. According to archaeologist Felix Riede, after the eruption the area most affected by the fallout, the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Basin" title="Thuringian Basin">Thuringian Basin</a> occupied by the Federmesser, appears to have been largely depopulated, whereas populations in southwest Germany and France increased. Two new cultures, the <a href="/wiki/Bromme_culture" title="Bromme culture">Bromme</a> of southern Scandinavia and the Perstunian of northeast Europe emerged. These cultures had a lower level of toolmaking skills than the Federmesser, particularly the Bromme who appear to have lost the bow and arrow technology. In Riede's view the decline was a result from the disruption caused by the Laacher See volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
