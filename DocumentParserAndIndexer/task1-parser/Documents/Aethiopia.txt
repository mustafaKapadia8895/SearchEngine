The <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">inhabited</a> world according to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>: <i>Libya</i> (Africa) is imagined as extending no further south than the Horn of Africa, terminating in uninhabitable desert. All peoples inhabiting the southernmost fringes of the inhabitable world are known as <i>Ethiopians</i> (after their dark skin). At the extreme south-east of the continent are the <a href="/wiki/Macrobians" title="Macrobians">Macrobians</a>, so called for their longevity.</div>
</div>

<p>Ancient <b>Aethiopia</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Αἰθιοπία</span> <i>Aithiopia</i>) first appears as a geographical term in classical documents in reference to the upper <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> region, as well as all certain areas south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert and south of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. Its earliest mention is in the works of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>: twice in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homer1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homer1-1">[1]</a></sup> and three times in the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homer2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homer2-2">[2]</a></sup> The Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> specifically uses the appellation to refer to such parts of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> as were then known within the <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">inhabitable world</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herodotus1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herodotus1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In classical antiquity, <i>Africa</i> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Ancient Libya</a></i>) referred to what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a> and south of the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Desert" title="Libyan Desert">Libyan Desert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>, including all the desert land west of the southern Nile river. Geographical knowledge of the continent gradually grew, with the first century AD Greek travelogue the <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythraean Sea">Periplus of the Erythraean Sea</a></i> describing areas as far south as <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>. <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Αἰθίοψ</span> (<i>Aithiops</i>), meaning "burnt-face", was used as a vague term for dark-skinned populations since the time of <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Greek" title="Homeric Greek">Homer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fage2526_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fage2526-5">[5]</a></sup> It was applied to such dark-skinned populations as came within the range of observation of the ancient geographers i.e. primarily in what was then <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>, and with the expansion of geographical knowledge, successively extended to certain other areas below the Sahara.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_Herodotus">Before Herodotus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Before Herodotus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> (c. 8th century BC) is the first to mention "Aethiopians" (Αἰθίοπες, Αἰθιοπῆες); he mentions that they are to be found at the east and west extremities of the world, divided by the sea into "eastern" (at the sunrise) and "western" (at the sunset). <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> (c. 8th century BC) speaks of <a href="/wiki/Memnon_(mythology)" title="Memnon (mythology)">Memnon</a> as the "king of Aethiopia".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 515 BC, <a href="/wiki/Scylax_of_Caryanda" title="Scylax of Caryanda">Scylax of Caryanda</a>, on orders from <a href="/wiki/Darius_I" title="Darius I">Darius I</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a>, sailed along the <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, circumnavigating the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. He mentioned "Aethiopians", but his writings on them have not survived. <a href="/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus" title="Hecataeus of Miletus">Hecataeus of Miletus</a> (c. 500 BC) is also said to have written a book about Aethiopia, but his writing is now known only through quotations from later authors. He stated that Aethiopia was located to the east of the Nile, as far as the Red Sea and Indian Ocean; he is also quoted as relating a myth that the <a href="/wiki/Monopod_(creature)" title="Monopod (creature)">Skiapods</a> ("Shade feet") lived there, whose feet were supposedly large enough to serve as shade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Herodotus">In Herodotus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Herodotus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his <i><a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)" title="Histories (Herodotus)">Histories</a></i> (c. 440 BC) Herodotus presents some of the most ancient and detailed information about "Aethiopia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herodotus1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herodotus1-3">[3]</a></sup> He relates that he personally traveled up the Nile to the border of Egypt as far as <a href="/wiki/Elephantine" title="Elephantine">Elephantine</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a>); in his view, "Aethiopia" is all of the inhabited land found to the south of Egypt, beginning at Elephantine. He describes a capital at <a href="/wiki/Mero%C3%AB" title="Meroë">Meroë</a>, adding that the only deities worshipped there were <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>). He relates that in the reign of Pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Psamtik_I" title="Psamtik I">Psamtik I</a> (c. 650 BCE), many Egyptian soldiers deserted their country and settled amidst the Aethiopians.</p>
<p>Herodotus tells us that king <a href="/wiki/Cambyses_II" title="Cambyses II">Cambyses II</a> (c. 570 BC) of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> sent spies to the Aethiopians "who dwelt in that part of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a> (Africa) which borders upon the southern sea." They found a strong and healthy people. Although Cambyses then campaigned toward their country, by not preparing enough provisions for the long march, his army completely failed and returned quickly.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Book 3, Herodotus defines "Aethiopia" as the farthest region of "Libya" (i.e. Africa): "Where the south declines towards the setting sun lies the country called Aethiopia, the last inhabited land in that direction. There <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> is obtained in great plenty, huge <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> abound, with wild trees of all sorts, and <a href="/wiki/Ebony" title="Ebony">ebony</a>; and the men are taller, handsomer, and longer lived than anywhere else."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herodotus2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herodotus2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Greco-Roman_historians">Other Greco-Roman historians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other Greco-Roman historians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Egyptian priest <a href="/wiki/Manetho" title="Manetho">Manetho</a> (c. 300 BC) listed Kushite (25th) dynasty, calling it the "Aethiopian dynasty". Moreover, when the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> was translated into Greek (c. 200 BC), the Hebrew appellation "Kush, Kushite" became in Greek "Aethiopia, Aethiopians", appearing as "Ethiopia, Ethiopians" in the English <i><a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">King James Version</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agatharchides" title="Agatharchides">Agatharchides</a> provides a relatively detailed description of the gold mining system of Aethiopia. His text was copied almost verbatim by virtually all subsequent ancient writers on the area, including <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photios_I_of_Constantinople" title="Photios I of Constantinople">Photius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jotras_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jotras-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>With regard to the Ethiopians, <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> indicates that they looked similar to Indians, remarking "those who are in Asia (Indus), and those who are in Africa, do not differ from each other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner480482_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner480482-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> in turn asserts that the place-name "Aethiopia" was derived from one "Aethiop, a son of Vulcan"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner480482_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turner480482-8">[8]</a></sup> [the smith-god <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plinius1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plinius1-9">[9]</a></sup>]. He also writes that the "Queen of the Ethiopians" bore the title <a href="/wiki/Kandake" title="Kandake">Kandake</a>, and avers that the Ethiopians had conquered ancient <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>. Following Strabo, the Greco-Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> notes that the Ethiopians had emigrated into the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> area from the <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus Valley</a> and that there were no people in the region by that name prior to their arrival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner480482_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Turner480482-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first century AD Greek travelogue known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythraean Sea">Periplus of the Erythraean Sea</a></i> first describes the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> littoral, based on its author's intimate knowledge of the area. The <i>Periplus</i> does not mention any dark-skinned "Ethiopians" among the area's inhabitants. They only later appear in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geographia</a></i>, but in a region far south, around the "Bantu nucleus" of northern <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/John_Donnelly_Fage" title="John Donnelly Fage">John Donnelly Fage</a>, these early Greek documents altogether suggest that the original inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Azania" title="Azania">Azania</a>, the "Azanians", were of the same ancestral stock as the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afroasiatic</a>-speaking populations to the north of them in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Barbara_(region)" title="Barbara (region)">Barbara</a> region along the Red Sea. Subsequently, by the tenth century, these original "Azanians" had been replaced by early waves of <a href="/wiki/Bantu_peoples" title="Bantu peoples">Bantu</a> settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fage2526_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fage2526-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_and_medieval_literature">Greek and medieval literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Greek and medieval literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several notable personalities in Greek and medieval literature were identified as Aethiopian, including several rulers, male and female: <a href="/wiki/Memnon_(mythology)" title="Memnon (mythology)">Memnon</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Emathion" title="Emathion">Emathion</a>, King of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cepheus,_King_of_Aethiopia" title="Cepheus, King of Aethiopia">Cepheus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cassiopeia_(mythology)" title="Cassiopeia (mythology)">Cassiopeia</a>, parents of <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(mythology)" title="Andromeda (mythology)">Andromeda</a>, were named as king and queen of Aethiopia. Homer in his description of the Trojan War mentions several other Aethiopians. <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> the geographer and other ancient Greek commentators believed that the "Aethiopian <a href="/wiki/Mount_Olympus" title="Mount Olympus">Olympus</a>" was where the gods lived when they were not in Greece.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
