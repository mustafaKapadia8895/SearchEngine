<p>The <b>Strait of Gibraltar</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مضيق جبل طارق</span>‎‎, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Estrecho de Gibraltar</i></span>) is a narrow <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">strait</a> that connects the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and separates <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spain" title="Peninsular Spain">Peninsular Spain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> from <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Gibraltar" title="Rock of Gibraltar">Rock of Gibraltar</a>, which in turn originates from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> <i>Jebel Tariq</i> (meaning "Tariq's mountain"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) named after <a href="/wiki/Tariq_ibn_Ziyad" title="Tariq ibn Ziyad">Tariq ibn Ziyad</a>. It is also known as the <b>Straits of Gibraltar,</b> the <a href="/wiki/Gut_(coastal_geography)" title="Gut (coastal geography)">Gut</a> of Gibraltar (although this is mostly archaic),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the <b>STROG</b> (<b>Str</b>ait <b>O</b>f <b>G</b>ibraltar) in <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">naval</a> use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and Bab Al Maghrib (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">باب المغرب</span>‎‎), "Gate of the West". In the Middle Ages, Muslims called it <i>Al-Zuqaq</i>, "The Passage", the Romans called it Fretum Gatitanum (Strait of Cadiz),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and in the ancient world it was known as the "<a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: αἱ Ἡράκλειοι στῆλαι).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Europe and Africa are separated by 7.7 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> (14.3 km; 8.9 mi) of ocean at the strait's narrowest point. The Strait's depth ranges between 300 and 900 metres (160 and 490 <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathoms</a>; 980 and 2,950 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which possibly interacted with the lower mean sea level of the last major glaciation 20,000 years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> when the level of the sea is believed to have been lower by 110–120 m (60–66 fathoms; 360–390 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Ferries cross between the two continents every day in as little as 35 minutes. The Spanish side of the Strait is protected under <a href="/wiki/El_Estrecho_Natural_Park" title="El Estrecho Natural Park">El Estrecho Natural Park</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>On the northern side of the Strait are <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> (a British overseas territory in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>), while on the southern side are <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> (a Spanish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>). Its boundaries were known in antiquity as the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a>. There are several islets, such as the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_dispute" title="Territorial dispute">disputed</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_Perejil" title="Isla Perejil">Isla Perejil</a>, that are claimed by both Morocco and Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its location, the Strait is commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigration" title="Illegal immigration">illegal immigration</a> from Africa to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StraitOfGibraltar.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1280" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/StraitOfGibraltar.JPG/220px-StraitOfGibraltar.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/StraitOfGibraltar.JPG/330px-StraitOfGibraltar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/StraitOfGibraltar.JPG/440px-StraitOfGibraltar.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:StraitOfGibraltar.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A view across the Strait of Gibraltar taken from the hills above Tarifa, Spain</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The seabed of the Strait is composed of synorogenic Betic-Rif clayey <a href="/wiki/Flysch" title="Flysch">flysch</a> covered by Pliocene and/or Quaternary calcareous sediments, sourced from thriving cold water coral communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Exposed bedrock surfaces, coarse sediments and local sand dunes attest to the strong bottom current conditions at the present time.</p>
<p>Around 5.9 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mainarticle_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mainarticle-13">[13]</a></sup> the connection between the Mediterranean Sea and the Atlantic Ocean along the <a href="/wiki/Betic_corridor" title="Betic corridor">Betic</a> and Rifan Corridor was progressively restricted until its total closure, effectively causing the salinity of the Mediterranean to rise periodically within the <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> and salt deposition range, during what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Messinian_salinity_crisis" title="Messinian salinity crisis">Messinian salinity crisis</a>. In this <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">water chemistry</a> environment, dissolved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">mineral concentrations</a>, temperature and stilled water currents combined and occurred regularly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a> many mineral salts in layers on the seabed. The resultant accumulation of various huge salt and mineral deposits about the Mediterranean basin are directly linked to this era. It is believed that this process took a short time, by geological standards, lasting between 500,000 and 600,000 years.</p>
<p>It is estimated that, were the straits closed even at today's higher sea level, most water in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_basin" title="Mediterranean basin">Mediterranean basin</a> would evaporate within only a thousand years, as it is believed to have done then,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mainarticle_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-mainarticle-13">[13]</a></sup> and such an event would lay down mineral deposits like the salt deposits now found under the sea floor all over the Mediterranean.</p>
<p>After a lengthy period of restricted intermittent or no water exchange between the Atlantic Ocean and Mediterranean basin, approximately 5.33 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the Atlantic-Mediterranean connection was completely reestablished through the Strait of Gibraltar by the <a href="/wiki/Zanclean_flood" title="Zanclean flood">Zanclean flood</a>, and has remained open ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The erosion produced by the incoming waters seems to be the main cause for the present depth of the strait (900 m at the narrows, 280 m at the <a href="/wiki/Camarinal_Sill" title="Camarinal Sill">Camarinal Sill</a>). The strait is expected to close again as the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African Plate</a> moves northward relative to the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but on geological rather than human timescales.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_Bird_Area">Important Bird Area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Important Bird Area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Strait has been identified as an <a href="/wiki/Important_Bird_Area" title="Important Bird Area">Important Bird Area</a> by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> because hundreds of thousands of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> use it every year to pass between the Mediterranean and the Atlantic, including large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Cory%27s_shearwater" title="Cory's shearwater">Cory's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balearic_shearwater" title="Balearic shearwater">Balearic shearwaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audouin%27s_gull" title="Audouin's gull">Audouin's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow-legged_gull" title="Yellow-legged gull">yellow-legged</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">lesser black-backed gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Razorbill" title="Razorbill">razorbills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Atlantic puffins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="813" data-file-width="570" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg/220px-Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg/330px-Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg/440px-Strait_of_Gibraltar_by_Piri_Reis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<dl>
<dd><i>For full articles on the history of the north Gibraltar shore, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gibraltar" title="History of Gibraltar">History of Gibraltar</a> or <a href="/wiki/History_of_Spain" title="History of Spain">History of Spain</a>. For the full article on the history of the south Gibraltar shore, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Morocco" title="History of Morocco">History of Morocco</a>.</i></dd>

<p>Evidence of the first human habitation of the area by <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a> dates back to 125,000 years ago. It is believed that the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Gibraltar" title="Rock of Gibraltar">Rock of Gibraltar</a> may have been one of the last outposts of Neanderthal habitation in the world, with evidence of their presence there dating to as recently as 24,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Archaeological evidence of Homo sapiens habitation of the area dates back c. 40,000 years.</p>
<p>The relatively short distance between the two shores has served as a quick crossing point for various groups and civilizations throughout history, including Carthaginians campaigning against Rome, Romans travelling between the provinces of Hispania and Mauritania, Vandals raiding south from Germania through Western Rome and into North Africa in the 5th century, Moors and Berbers in the 8th–11th centuries, and Spain and Portugal in the 16th century.</p>
<p>Beginning in 1492, the straits began to play a certain cultural role in acting as a barrier against cross-strait conquest and the flow of culture and language that would naturally follow such a conquest. In that year, the last Muslim government north of the straits was overthrown by a Spanish force. Since that time, the straits have come to foster the development of two very distinct and varied cultures on either side of the straits after sharing much the same culture and greater degrees of tolerance for over 300+ years from the 8th century to the early 13th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On the northern side, Christian-European culture has remained dominant since the expulsion of the last Muslim kingdom in 1492, along with the <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romance</a> <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish language</a>, while on the southern side, Muslim-Arabic/Mediterranean has been dominant since the spread of Islam into North Africa in the 700s, along with the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> language. For the last 500 years, religious and cultural intolerance, more than the small travel barrier that the straits present, has come to act as a powerful enforcing agent of the cultural separation that exists between these two groups.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The small <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> enclave of the city of <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> presents a third cultural group found in the straits. This enclave was first established in 1704 and has since been used by Britain to act as a surety for control of the sea lanes into and out of the Mediterranean.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_coup_of_July_1936" title="Spanish coup of July 1936">Spanish coup of July 1936</a> the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Republican_Navy" title="Spanish Republican Navy">Spanish Republican Navy</a> tried to <a href="/wiki/Blockade" title="Blockade">blockade</a> the Strait of Gibraltar to hamper the transport of <a href="/wiki/Army_of_Africa_(Spain)" title="Army of Africa (Spain)">Army of Africa</a> troops from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">Spanish Morocco</a> to Peninsular Spain. On 5 August 1936 the so-called <a href="/wiki/Convoy_de_la_victoria" title="Convoy de la victoria">Convoy de la victoria</a> was able to bring at least 2,500 men across the strait, breaking the republican blockade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antony_Beevor_1982_revised_2006_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Antony_Beevor_1982_revised_2006-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communications">Communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="917" data-file-width="1528" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg/220px-H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg/330px-H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg/440px-H%C3%A9rcules3D.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Strait is an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping_route" title="Shipping route">shipping route</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>. There are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferries" title="Ferries">ferries</a> that operate between Spain and Morocco across the strait, as well as between Spain and <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tangier" title="Tangier">Tangier</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tunnel_across_the_strait">Tunnel across the strait</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tunnel across the strait">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar_crossing" title="Strait of Gibraltar crossing">Strait of Gibraltar crossing</a></div>
<p>In December 2003, Spain and Morocco agreed to explore the construction of an <a href="/wiki/Underwater" title="Underwater">undersea</a> <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a> <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnel</a> to connect their rail systems across the Strait. The gauge of the rail would be 1,435 mm (4 ft 8.5 in) to match the proposed construction and conversion of significant parts of the existing broad gauge system to <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> While the project remains in a planning phase, Spanish and Moroccan officials have met to discuss it as recently as 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and proposals predict it could be completed by 2025.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_flow_and_wave_patterns">Special flow and wave patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Special flow and wave patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Strait of Gibraltar links the Atlantic Ocean directly to the Mediterranean Sea. This direct linkage creates certain unique flow and wave patterns. These unique patterns are created due to the interaction of various regional and global evaporative forces, tidal forces, and wind forces.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inflow_and_outflow">Inflow and outflow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Inflow and outflow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="808" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg/220px-InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg/330px-InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg/440px-InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:InternalWaves_Gibraltar_ISS009-E-09952_54.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Internal_wave" title="Internal wave">Internal waves</a> (marked with arrows) caused by the Strait of Gibraltar. (Mediterranean Sea in upper right.)</div>

<p>Through the strait, water generally flows more or less continually in both an eastward and a westward direction. A smaller amount of deeper saltier and therefore denser waters continually work their way westwards (the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_outflow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mediterranean outflow (page does not exist)">Mediterranean outflow</a>), while a larger amount of surface waters with lower salinity and density continually work their way eastwards (the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_inflow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mediterranean inflow (page does not exist)">Mediterranean inflow</a>). These general flow tendencies may be occasionally interrupted for brief periods to accommodate temporary tidal flow requirements, depending on various lunar and solar alignments. Still, on the whole and over time, the balance of the water flow is eastwards, due to an evaporation rate within the Mediterranean basin higher than the combined inflow of all the rivers that empty into it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The shallow <a href="/wiki/Camarinal_Sill" title="Camarinal Sill">Camarinal Sill</a> of the Strait of Gibraltar, which forms the shallowest point within the strait, acts to limit mixing between the cold, less saline Atlantic water and the warm Mediterranean waters. The Camarinal Sill is located at the far western end of the strait.</p>
<p>The Mediterranean waters are so much saltier than the Atlantic waters that they sink below the constantly incoming water and form a highly saline (<i><a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline</a></i>, both warm and salty) layer of bottom water. This layer of bottom-water constantly works its way out into the Atlantic as the Mediterranean outflow. On the Atlantic side of the strait, a density boundary separates the Mediterranean outflow waters from the rest at about 100 m (330 ft) depth. These waters flow out and down the continental slope, losing salinity, until they begin to mix and equilibrate more rapidly, much further out at a depth of about 1,000 m (3,300 ft). The Mediterranean outflow water layer can be traced for thousands of kilometres west of the strait, before completely losing its identity.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_(Simplified).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="536" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b1/Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_%28Simplified%29.jpg/220px-Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_%28Simplified%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b1/Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_%28Simplified%29.jpg/330px-Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_%28Simplified%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b1/Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_%28Simplified%29.jpg/440px-Camarinal_Still_Water_Mixing_%28Simplified%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U-boats" title="U-boats">U-boats</a> used the currents to pass into the Mediterranean Sea without detection, by maintaining silence with engines off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> From September 1941 to May 1944 Germany managed to send 62 U-boats into the Mediterranean. All these boats had to navigate the British-controlled Strait of Gibraltar where nine U-boats were sunk while attempting passage and 10 more had to break off their run due to damage. No U-boats ever made it back into the Atlantic and all were either sunk in battle or scuttled by their own crews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_waves">Internal waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Internal waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Internal_wave" title="Internal wave">Internal waves</a> (waves at the density boundary layer) are often produced by the strait. Like traffic merging on a highway, the water flow is constricted in both directions because it must pass over the Camarinal Sill. When large tidal flows enter the Strait and the high tide relaxes, internal waves are generated at the Camarinal Sill and proceed eastwards. Even though the waves may occur down to great depths, occasionally the waves are almost imperceptible at the surface, at other times they can be seen clearly in satellite imagery. These <i>internal waves</i> continue to flow eastward and to refract around coastal features. They can sometimes be traced for as much as 100 km (62 mi), and sometimes create <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">interference patterns</a> with refracted waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_waters">Territorial waters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Territorial waters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Strait lies mostly within the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">territorial waters</a> of Spain and Morocco, except for at the far eastern end. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (through Gibraltar) claims 3 nautical miles around Gibraltar putting part of the Strait inside British territorial waters, and the smaller-than-maximal claim also means that part of the Strait therefore lies in <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">international waters</a> according to the British claim. However, the ownership of Gibraltar and its territorial waters is <a href="/wiki/Disputed_status_of_Gibraltar#Territorial_waters" title="Disputed status of Gibraltar">disputed</a> by Spain, meanwhile Morocco dispute the far extern end (of Ceuta).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_generation">Power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strait_of_Gibraltar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some studies have proposed the possibility of erecting <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power#Categories_of_tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> generating stations within the strait, to be powered from the predictable current at the strait.</p>
<p>In the 1920s and 1930s, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantropa" title="Atlantropa">Atlantropa</a> project proposed damming the strait to generate large amounts of electricity and lower the sea level of the Mediterranean by several hundreds of meters to create large new lands for settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This proposal would however have devastating effects on the local climate and ecology and would dramatically change the strength of the West African Monsoon.</p>
