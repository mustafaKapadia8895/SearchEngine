<p><b>Eyjafjallajökull</b> (<a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> pronunciation <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Icelandic" title="Help:IPA/Icelandic">[ˈeɪjaˌfjatlaˌjœːkʏtl̥]</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Is-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_(3).oga" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Is-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_%283%29.oga" title="Is-Eyjafjallajökull (3).oga">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), (English pronunciation: ei-ya-fyat-LA-yer-kitle), English <b>Island Mountain Glacier</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is one of the smaller <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, north of <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B3gar" title="Skógar">Skógar</a> and west of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="Mýrdalsjökull">Mýrdalsjökull</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice cap</a> covers the <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> with a summit elevation of 1,651 metres (5,417 ft). The volcano has erupted relatively frequently since the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, most recently <a href="/wiki/2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull">in 2010</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC8621581_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC8621581-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eyjafjallajökull consists of a volcano completely covered by an ice cap. The ice cap covers an area of about 100 square kilometres (39 sq mi), feeding many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outlet_glacier" title="Outlet glacier">outlet glaciers</a>. The main outlet glaciers are to the north: Gígjökull, flowing into Lónið, and Steinholtsjökull, flowing into Steinholtslón.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vefur1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-vefur1-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1967 there was a massive landslide on the Steinholtsjökull glacial tongue. On 16 January 1967 at 13:47:55 there was an explosion on the glacier. It can be timed because the seismometers at <a href="/wiki/Kirkjub%C3%A6jarklaustur" title="Kirkjubæjarklaustur">Kirkjubæjarklaustur</a> monitored the movement. When about 15,000,000 cubic metres (530,000,000 cubic feet) of material hit the glacier a massive amount of air, ice, and water began to move out from under the glacier into the lagoon at the foot of the glacier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vefur1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-vefur1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mountain itself, a <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a>, stands 1,651 metres (5,417 ft) at its highest point, and has a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">crater</a> 3–4 kilometres (1.9–2.5 mi) in diameter, open to the north. The crater rim has three main peaks (clockwise from the north-east): Guðnasteinn, 1,500 metres (4,900 ft); Hámundur, 1,651 metres (5,417 ft); and Goðasteinn, 1,497 metres (4,911 ft). The south face of the mountain was once part of Iceland's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline">coastline</a>, from which, over thousands of years, the sea has retreated some 5 kilometres (3 mi). The former coastline now consists of sheer cliffs with many <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfalls</a>, of which the best known is <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B3gafoss" title="Skógafoss">Skógafoss</a>. In strong winds, the water of the smaller falls can even be blown up the mountain. The area between the mountain and the present coast is a relatively flat strand, 2 to 5 km (1 to 3 miles) wide, called Eyjafjöll.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name means "<a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a>" (or more properly here "<a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice cap</a>") of the Eyjafjöll. The word <i>jökull</i>, meaning glacier or ice cap, is a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> word <i>ikel</i> surviving in the <i>-icle</i> of English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/icicle" title="wikt:icicle">icicle</a></i>.</p>
<p><i>Eyjafjöll</i> is the name given to the southern side of the volcanic massif together with the small mountains which form the foot of the volcano.</p>
<p>The name <i>Eyjafjöll</i> is made up of the words <i>eyja</i> (genitive plural of <i>ey</i>, meaning <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eyot" title="wikt:eyot">eyot</a></i> or <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/island" title="wikt:island">island</a></i>), and the plural word <i>fjöll</i>, meaning <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fell#Etymology_3" title="wikt:fell">fells</a></i> or <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mountain" title="wikt:mountain">mountains</a></i>, and together literally means: "the mountains of the islands". The name probably refers to the close-by archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Vestmannaeyjar" title="Vestmannaeyjar">Vestmannaeyjar</a>.</p>
<p>The word <i>fjalla</i> is the genitive plural of <i>fjöll</i>, and so <i>Eyjafjalla</i> is the genitive form of <i>Eyjafjöll</i> and means: "of the Eyjafjöll". A literal part-by-part translation of <i>Eyjafjallajökull</i> would thus be "Islands' Mountains' ice cap".</p>
<p>Eyjafjallajökull is sometimes referred to by the <a href="/wiki/Numeronym" title="Numeronym">numeronym</a> "E15".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="726" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg/220px-Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg/330px-Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg/440px-Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Active volcanic areas and systems in Iceland</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a>, whose vents follow an east-west trend, is composed of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> to <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesite</a> <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lavas</a>. Most of its historical eruptions have been <a href="/wiki/Explosive_eruption" title="Explosive eruption">explosive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gvp_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gvp-7">[7]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">fissure vents</a> occur on both (mainly the west) sides of the volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gvp_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gvp-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The volcano is fed by a <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">magma chamber</a> under the mountain, which in turn derives from the <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">divergence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>. It is part of a chain of volcanoes stretching across Iceland. Its nearest active neighbours are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">Katla</a>, to the northeast, and <a href="/wiki/Eldfell" title="Eldfell">Eldfell</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Heimaey" title="Heimaey">Heimaey</a>, to the southwest. The volcano is thought to be related to Katla geologically, in that eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull have generally been followed by eruptions of Katla.</p>

<p>Eyjafjallajökull erupted in the years 920 and 1612.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1821_to_1823_eruptions">1821 to 1823 eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1821 to 1823 eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some damage was caused by a minor eruption in 1821.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larsen_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-larsen-8">[8]</a></sup> Notably, the ash released from the eruption contained a large fraction of <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, which in high doses may harm the bone structure of cattle, horses, sheep and humans. The eruption also caused some small and medium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacier_run" title="Glacier run">glacier runs</a> and flooding in nearby rivers <a href="/wiki/Markarflj%C3%B3t" title="Markarfljót">Markarfljót</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Holts%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Holtsá (page does not exist)">Holtsá</a>. The eruptive phase started on 19 and 20 December 1821 by a series of explosive eruptions and continued over the next several days. The sources describe heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ash_fall" title="Ash fall">ash fall</a> in the area around the volcano, especially to the south and west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larsen_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-larsen-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After that event the sequence of eruptions continued on a more subdued level until June 1822.</p>
<p>From the end of June until the beginning of August 1822, another sequence of explosive eruptions followed. The eruption columns were shot to considerable heights, with ashfall in both the far north of the country, in <a href="/wiki/Eyjafj%C3%B6r%C3%B0ur" title="Eyjafjörður">Eyjafjörður</a>, and in the southwest, on the peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Seltjarnarnes" title="Seltjarnarnes">Seltjarnarnes</a> near <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>.</p>
<p>The period from August to December 1822 seemed quieter, but farmers attributed the death of cattle and sheep in the Eyjafjörður area to poisoning from this eruption, which modern analysis identifies as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluoride_poisoning" title="Fluoride poisoning">fluoride poisoning</a>. Some small glacier runs occurred in the river Holtsá. A bigger one flooded the plains near the river Markarfljót. (The sources do not indicate the exact date.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1823, some men went hiking up on Eyjafjallajökull to inspect the <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">craters</a>. They discovered a <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">fissure vent</a> near the summit <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> a bit to the west of <i>Guðnasteinn</i>.</p>
<p>In early 1823, the nearby volcano <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">Katla</a> under the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="Mýrdalsjökull">Mýrdalsjökull</a> ice cap erupted and at the same time steam columns were seen on the summit of Eyjafjallajökull.</p>
<p>The ash of Eyjafjallajökull’s 1821 eruptions is to be found all over the south of Iceland. It is dark grey in colour, small-grained and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermediate_rock" title="Intermediate rock">intermediate rock</a> containing about 28–40% <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_eruptions">2010 eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010 eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull">2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_the_April_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Effects of the April 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption">Effects of the April 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption">Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption</a></div>

<p>On 26 February 2010, unusual seismic activity along with rapid expansion of the Earth's crust was registered by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorological_Institute_of_Iceland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meteorological Institute of Iceland (page does not exist)">Meteorological Institute of Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This gave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysicist" title="Geophysicist">geophysicists</a> evidence that magma was pouring from underneath the crust into the <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">magma chamber</a> of the Eyjafjallajökull volcano and that pressure stemming from the process caused the huge crustal displacement at Þorvaldseyri farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In March 2010, almost three thousand small <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> were detected near the volcano, all having a depth of 7–10 kilometres (4.3–6.2 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The seismic activity continued to increase and from 3–5 March, close to 3,000 earthquakes were measured at the epicenter of the volcano.</p>
<p>The eruption is thought to have begun on 20 March 2010, about 8 kilometres (5 mi) east of the top crater of the volcano, on <a href="/wiki/Fimmv%C3%B6r%C3%B0uh%C3%A1ls" title="Fimmvörðuháls">Fimmvörðuháls</a>, the high neck between Eyjafjallajökull and the neighbouring icecap, Mýrdalsjökull. This first eruption, in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">fissure vent</a>, did not occur under the glacier and was smaller in scale than had been expected by some geologists. The fissure opened on the north side of Fimmvörðuháls, directly across the popular hiking trail between Skógar, south of the pass, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3rsm%C3%B6rk" title="Þórsmörk">Þórsmörk</a>, immediately to the north.</p>

<p>On 14 April 2010 Eyjafjallajökull resumed erupting after a brief pause, this time from the top crater in the centre of the glacier, causing <i><a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kulhlaup" title="Jökulhlaup">jökulhlaup</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Meltwater" title="Meltwater">meltwater</a> floods) to rush down the nearby rivers, and requiring 800 people to be evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC8621581_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC8621581-4">[4]</a></sup> This eruption was explosive, due to meltwater getting into the volcanic vent. It was estimated to be ten to twenty times larger than the previous one in <a href="/wiki/Fimmv%C3%B6r%C3%B0uh%C3%A1ls" title="Fimmvörðuháls">Fimmvörðuháls</a>. This second eruption threw volcanic ash several kilometres up in the atmosphere, which led to <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption">air travel disruption</a> in northwest Europe for six days from 15 April to 21 April 2010 and again, in May 2010,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> including the closure of <a href="/wiki/Airspace" title="Airspace">airspace</a> over many parts of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The eruptions also created <a href="/wiki/Dirty_thunderstorm" title="Dirty thunderstorm">electrical storms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On 23 May 2010, the London <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_Ash_Advisory_Centre" title="Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre">Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre</a> declared the eruption to have stopped, but stated that they were continuing to monitor the volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The volcano continued to have several earthquakes daily, with <a href="/wiki/Volcanologist" title="Volcanologist">volcanologists</a> watching the volcano closely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en.vedur.is_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-en.vedur.is-16">[16]</a></sup> As of August 2010, Eyjafjallajökull was considered dormant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_Katla">Relationship to Katla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship to Katla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="917" data-file-width="2133" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png/400px-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png/600px-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png/800px-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cross section through Eyjafjöll and Katla</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Eyjafjallajökull lies 25 km (16 mi) west of another subglacial volcano, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">Katla</a>, under the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="Mýrdalsjökull">Mýrdalsjökull</a> ice cap, which is much more active and known for its powerful <a href="/wiki/Subglacial_eruption" title="Subglacial eruption">subglacial eruptions</a> and its large magma chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Each of the eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull in 920, 1612, and 1821–1823 has preceded an eruption of Katla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icelandprepares_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-icelandprepares-19">[19]</a></sup> Katla did not display any unusual activity (such as expansion of the crust or seismic activity) during the 2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull, though geologists have been concerned about the general instability of Katla since 1999. Some geophysicists in Iceland believe that the Eyjafjallajökull eruption may trigger an eruption of Katla, which would cause major flooding due to melting of glacial ice and send up massive plumes of ash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icelandprepares_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-icelandprepares-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On 20 April 2010, Icelandic President <a href="/wiki/%C3%93lafur_Ragnar_Gr%C3%ADmsson" title="Ólafur Ragnar Grímsson">Ólafur Grímsson</a> said "the time for Katla to erupt is coming close...we [Iceland] have prepared...it is high time for European governments and airline authorities all over the world to start planning for the eventual Katla eruption".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Volcanologists continue to monitor Katla, aware that any eruption from Katla following an eruption from Eyjafjallajökull has historically occurred within months of an Eyjafjallajökull eruption. The Icelandic Meteorological Office updates its website with reports of quakes at both Eyjafjallajökull and Katla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en.vedur.is_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-en.vedur.is-16">[16]</a></sup> On 8 July 2011 there was a <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kulhlaup" title="Jökulhlaup">jökulhlaup</a> that destroyed a bridge on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring_Road_(Iceland)" title="Ring Road (Iceland)">Ring Road</a> and caused cracks to appear on Katla's glacier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_May" title="James May">James May</a> drove a <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Hilux" title="Toyota Hilux">Toyota Hilux</a> up the volcano in a 2010 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_Gear_series_15" title="Top Gear series 15">episode</a> of the television show <i>Top Gear</i>. He reached the summit mere hours before the <a href="/wiki/2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajökull">eruption</a> happened, although May and the camera crew had left the volcano by the time the eruption started.</p>
<p>Eyjafjallajökull was featured in the 2013 version of the movie, <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Life_of_Walter_Mitty_(2013_film)" title="The Secret Life of Walter Mitty (2013 film)"><i>The Secret Life of Walter Mitty</i></a>.</p>
<p>Eyjafjallajökull is featured as one of the natural wonders in the computer game <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_VI" title="Civilization VI">Civilization VI</a></i>.</p>
<p>Eyjafjallajökull was featured in a lyric, performed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MF_DOOM" title="MF DOOM">MF DOOM</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JJ_DOOM" title="JJ DOOM">JJ DOOM</a>'s song Guv'nor off of the album <i><a href="/wiki/Key_to_the_Kuffs" title="Key to the Kuffs">Key to the Kuffs</a></i> released in 2012.</p>
