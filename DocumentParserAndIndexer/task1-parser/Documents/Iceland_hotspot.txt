<p>The <b>Iceland hotspot</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> which is partly responsible for the high volcanic activity which has formed the island of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceland_hotspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iceland is one of the most active <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> regions in the world, with eruptions occurring on average roughly every three years (in the 20th century there were 39 volcanic eruptions on and around Iceland). About a third of the <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lavas</a> erupted in recorded history have been produced by Icelandic eruptions. Notable eruptions have included that of <a href="/wiki/Eldgj%C3%A1" title="Eldgjá">Eldgjá</a>, a fissure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">Katla</a>, in 934 (the world's largest basaltic eruption ever witnessed), <a href="/wiki/Laki" title="Laki">Laki</a> in 1783 (the world's second largest), and several eruptions beneath <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>, which have generated devastating <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kulhlaup" title="Jökulhlaup">glacial bursts</a>, most recently in 2010 after the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="Eyjafjallajökull">Eyjafjallajökull</a>.</p>
<p>Iceland's location astride the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plates</a> are moving apart, is partly responsible for this intense volcanic activity, but an additional cause is necessary to explain why Iceland is a substantial island while the rest of the ridge mostly consists of <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a>, with peaks below <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>.</p>
<p>As well as being a region of higher temperature than the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, it is believed to have a higher concentration of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. The presence of water in <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> reduces the melting temperature, which may also play a role in enhancing Icelandic volcanism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_of_causation">Theories of causation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceland_hotspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theories of causation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is an ongoing discussion about whether the hotspot is caused by a deep <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a> or originates at a much shallower depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoulger2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoulger2005-1">[1]</a></sup> Recently, <a href="/wiki/Seismic_tomography" title="Seismic tomography">seismic tomography</a> studies have found seismic wave speed anomalies under Iceland, consistent with a hot conduit 100 km across that extends to the lower mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rickers2013_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rickers2013-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2017)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have questioned whether the Iceland hotspot has the same origin as other hotspots, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_hotspot" title="Hawaii hotspot">Hawaii hotspot</a>. While the Hawaiian island chain and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Seamounts" title="Emperor Seamounts">Emperor Seamounts</a> show a clear time-progressive volcanic track caused by the movement of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> over the Hawaiian hotspot, no such track can be seen at Iceland.</p>
<p>It is proposed that the line from <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmsv%C3%B6tn" title="Grímsvötn">Grímsvötn</a> volcano to <a href="/wiki/Surtsey" title="Surtsey">Surtsey</a> shows the movement of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, and the line from Grímsvötn volcano to <a href="/wiki/Reykjanes" title="Reykjanes">Reykjanes</a> volcanic belt shows the movement of the North American Plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorganMorgan2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorganMorgan2009-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mantle_plume_theory">Mantle plume theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceland_hotspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mantle plume theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iceland_plume" title="Iceland plume">Iceland plume</a></div>
<p>It is believed that a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a> underlies Iceland, of which the hotspot is thought to be the surface expression, and that the presence of the plume enhances the volcanism already caused by plate separation. Additionally, flood basalts on the <a href="/wiki/Continental_margin" title="Continental margin">continental margins</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the oblique orientation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reykjanes_Ridge" title="Reykjanes Ridge">Reykjanes Ridge</a> segments to their spreading direction, and the enhanced igneous crustal thickness found along the southern <a href="/wiki/Aegir_Ridge" title="Aegir Ridge">Aegir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kolbeinsey_Ridge" title="Kolbeinsey Ridge">Kolbeinsey Ridges</a> may be results of interaction between the plume and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> The plume stem is believed to be quite narrow, perhaps 100 km across and extending down to at least 400–650 km beneath the Earth's surface, and possibly down to the <a href="/wiki/Core%E2%80%93mantle_boundary" title="Core–mantle boundary">core-mantle boundary</a>, while the plume head may be &gt; 1,000 km in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is suggested that the lack of a time-progressive track of seamounts is due to the location of the plume beneath the thick Greenland <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">craton</a> for ~ 15 Myr after continental breakup,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the later entrenchment of the plume material into the northern Mid-Atlantic Ridge following its formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
