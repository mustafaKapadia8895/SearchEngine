<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Westerlies</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1425" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png/400px-Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png/600px-Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png/800px-Map_prevailing_winds_on_earth.png 2x" width="400"/></a>

The westerlies (blue) and trade winds (yellow)</div>

<p>The <b>westerlies</b>, <b>anti-trades</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>prevailing westerlies</b>, are <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">prevailing winds</a> from the west toward the east in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">middle latitudes</a> between 30 and 60 degrees <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. They originate from the high-pressure areas in the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a> and tend towards the <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a> and steer <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> in this general manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a> which cross the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> axis into the westerlies recurve due to the increased westerly flow. The <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> are predominantly from the southwest in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and from the northwest in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>.</p>
<p>The westerlies are strongest in the winter hemisphere and times when the pressure is lower over the poles, while they are weakest in the summer hemisphere and when pressures are higher over the poles. The westerlies are particularly strong, especially in the Southern Hemisphere, in areas where land is absent, because land amplifies the flow pattern, making the current more north-south oriented, slowing the westerlies. The strongest westerly winds in the middle latitudes can come in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roaring_forties" title="Roaring forties">roaring forties</a>, between 40 and 50 degrees latitude. The westerlies play an important role in carrying the warm, equatorial waters and winds to the western coasts of continents, especially in the southern hemisphere because of its vast oceanic expanse.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westerlies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cyclone" title="Polar cyclone">Polar cyclone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a></div>
<p>If the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> were a non-rotating <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, solar heating would cause winds across the mid-latitudes to blow in a poleward direction, away from the subtropical ridge. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> caused by the rotation of Earth causes winds to steer to the right of what would otherwise be expected across the Northern Hemisphere, and left of what would be expected in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is why winds across the Northern Hemisphere tend to blow from the southwest, but they tend to be from the northwest in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When pressures are lower over the poles, the strength of the westerlies increases, which has the effect of warming the mid-latitudes. This occurs when the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a> is positive, and during winter low pressure near the poles is stronger than it would be during the summer. When it is negative and pressures are higher over the poles, the flow is more meridional, blowing from the direction of the pole towards the equator, which brings cold air into the mid-latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the year, the westerlies vary in strength with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cyclone" title="Polar cyclone">polar cyclone</a>. As the cyclone reaches its maximum intensity in <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, the westerlies increase in strength. As the cyclone reaches its weakest intensity in <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, the Westerlies weaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> An example of the impact of the westerlies is when dust plumes, originating in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gobi_desert" title="Gobi desert">Gobi desert</a> combine with pollutants and spread large distances downwind, or eastward, into <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gobi_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gobi-7">[7]</a></sup> The westerlies can be particularly strong, especially in the Southern Hemisphere, where there is less land in the middle latitudes to cause the progression of west to east winds to slow down. In the Southern hemisphere, because of the stormy and cloudy conditions, it is usual to refer to the westerlies as the roaring forties, furious fifties and shrieking sixties according to the varying degrees of latitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_ocean_currents">Impact on ocean currents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westerlies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact on ocean currents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Franklingulfstream.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4761" data-file-width="5868" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Franklingulfstream.jpg/250px-Franklingulfstream.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Franklingulfstream.jpg/375px-Franklingulfstream.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Franklingulfstream.jpg/500px-Franklingulfstream.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Franklingulfstream.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Benjamin Franklin's map of the Gulf Stream</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to persistent winds from west to east on the poleward sides of the subtropical ridges located in the Atlantic and Pacific oceans, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> are driven in a similar manner in both hemispheres. The currents in the Northern Hemisphere are weaker than those in the Southern Hemisphere due to the differences in strength between the westerlies of each hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_intensification" title="Western intensification">western intensification</a> causes currents on the western boundary of an ocean basin to be stronger than those on the eastern boundary of an ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These western ocean currents transport warm, tropical water polewards toward the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a>. Ships crossing both oceans have taken advantage of the ocean currents for centuries.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a> (ACC), or the West Wind Drift, is an <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean current</a> that flows from west to east around <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. The ACC is the dominant circulation feature of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and, at approximately 125 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sverdrups</a>, the largest ocean current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the northern hemisphere, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>, part of the North Atlantic Subtropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyre" title="Gyre">Gyre</a>, has led to the development of strong <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> of all types at the base of the Westerlies, both within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and within the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Kuroshio (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> for "Black Tide") is a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_boundary_current" title="Western boundary current">western boundary current</a> in the western north <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, similar to the Gulf Stream, which has also contributed to the depth of ocean storms in that region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extratropical_cyclones">Extratropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westerlies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extratropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UK-Cyclone.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="507" data-file-width="700" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/UK-Cyclone.gif/250px-UK-Cyclone.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/UK-Cyclone.gif/375px-UK-Cyclone.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/UK-Cyclone.gif/500px-UK-Cyclone.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclone</a></div>
<p>An extratropical cyclone is a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure</a> weather system that has neither tropical nor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cyclone" title="Polar cyclone">polar</a> characteristics, being connected with <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">fronts</a> and horizontal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gradients" title="Gradients">gradients</a> in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> otherwise known as "baroclinic zones".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraLessonMillUni_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ExtraLessonMillUni-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The descriptor "extratropical" refers to the fact that this type of cyclone generally occurs outside of the tropics, in the middle latitudes of the planet, where the Westerlies steer the system generally from west to east. These systems may also be described as "mid-latitude cyclones" due to their area of formation, or "post-tropical cyclones" where <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone#Extratropical_transition" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical transition</a> has occurred,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraLessonMillUni_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ExtraLessonMillUni-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtratropicalPhases_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ExtratropicalPhases-16">[16]</a></sup> and are often described as "depressions" or "lows" by weather forecasters and the general public. These are the everyday phenomena which along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-cyclone" title="Anti-cyclone">anti-cyclones</a>, drive the weather over much of the Earth.</p>
<p>Although extratropical cyclones are almost always classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroclinic" title="Baroclinic">baroclinic</a> since they form along zones of temperature and dewpoint gradient, they can sometimes become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">barotropic</a> late in their life cycle when the temperature distribution around the cyclone becomes fairly uniform along the radius from the center of low pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> An extratropical cyclone can transform into a subtropical storm, and from there into a tropical cyclone, if it dwells over warm waters and develops central convection, which warms its core and causes temperature and dewpoint gradients near their centers to fade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_with_tropical_cyclones">Interaction with tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westerlies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interaction with tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bill_2009_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="805" data-file-width="1024" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bill_2009_track.png/250px-Bill_2009_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bill_2009_track.png/375px-Bill_2009_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bill_2009_track.png/500px-Bill_2009_track.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bill_2009_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Storm track of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bill_(2009)" title="Hurricane Bill (2009)">Hurricane Bill (2009)</a>, showing a classic recurvature off the American coast in <a href="/wiki/2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2009 Atlantic hurricane season">2009</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When a tropical cyclone crosses the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> axis, normally through a break in the <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> caused by a system traversing the Westerlies, its general track around the high-pressure area is deflected significantly by winds moving towards the general low-pressure area to its north. When the cyclone track becomes strongly poleward with an easterly component, the cyclone has begun <i>recurvature,</i> entering the Westerlies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A typhoon moving through the Pacific Ocean towards Asia, for example, will recurve offshore of Japan to the north, and then to the northeast, if the typhoon encounters southwesterly winds (blowing northeastward) around a low-pressure system passing over <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> or <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. Many tropical cyclones are eventually forced toward the northeast by <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> in this manner, which move from west to east to the north of the subtropical ridge. An example of a tropical cyclone in recurvature was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">Typhoon Ioke</a> in 2006, which took a similar trajectory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_Ioke_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Ioke-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
