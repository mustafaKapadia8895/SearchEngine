<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tornado warning</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">Tornado watch</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">

A tornado in <a href="/wiki/Douglas,_Oklahoma" title="Douglas, Oklahoma">Douglas, Oklahoma</a>, May 24, 2008.</div>

<p>A <b>tornado warning</b> (<a href="/wiki/Specific_Area_Message_Encoding" title="Specific Area Message Encoding">SAME</a> code: TOR) is an alert issued by national weather forecasting agencies to warn the public that <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">severe thunderstorms</a> with <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> are imminent or occurring. It can be issued after a tornado, <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">funnel cloud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">rotation in the clouds</a> has been spotted by the public, <a href="/wiki/Storm_chasing" title="Storm chasing">storm chasers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergency_management" title="Emergency management">emergency management</a> or <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement" title="Law enforcement">law enforcement</a>.</p>
<p>When this happens, the <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense_siren" title="Civil defense siren">tornado sirens</a> may sound in that area if any sirens are present, informing people that a tornado has been sighted or may be forming nearby (because sirens are not intended to be heard indoors, residents should not completely depend on them). The issuance of a tornado warning indicates that residents should take immediate safety precautions.</p>
<p>It is a higher level of alert than a <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">tornado watch</a>, but (in the United States) it can be surpassed by an even higher alert known as a <a href="/wiki/Tornado_emergency" title="Tornado emergency">tornado emergency</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornado_warning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first official tornado forecast – and tornado warning – was made by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> Capt. (later Col.) <a href="/wiki/Robert_C._Miller" title="Robert C. Miller">Robert C. Miller</a> and Major Ernest Fawbush, on March 25, 1948. The first such forecast came after the events that transpired five days earlier on March 20, 1948; Miller – a <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> native who became stationed at <a href="/wiki/Tinker_Air_Force_Base" title="Tinker Air Force Base">Tinker Air Force Base</a> three weeks earlier – was assigned to work the late shift as a forecaster for the base's Air Weather Service office that evening, analyzing <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">U.S. Weather Bureau</a> surface maps and upper-air charts that failed to note atmospheric instability and moisture content present over <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> that would be suitable for producing thunderstorm activity, erroneously forecasting dry conditions for that night. Thunderstorms soon developed southwest of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a>, and at 9:30 p.m., forecasters from <a href="/wiki/Will_Rogers_World_Airport" title="Will Rogers World Airport">Will Rogers Airport</a> sent a warning to Tinker that the storm encroaching the city was producing wind gusts of 92 miles per hour (148 km/h) and a "Tornado South on Ground Moving NE!" Base personnel received an alert written by the Staff Sarg. on duty with Miller, minutes before the twister struck Tinker several minutes later around 10:00 p.m., damaging several military aircraft (with total damage estimated at $10 million) that could not be secured in time before it crossed the base grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following an inquiry the next day before a tribunal of five generals who traveled to Tinker from <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, who ruled that the March 20 tornado was an "act of God[..] not forecastable given the present state of the art", base commander Gen. Fred Borum tasked Miller and Fawbush to follow up on the board's suggestion to consider methods of forecasting tornadic thunderstorms. Over the next three days, Miller and Fawbush studied reports and charts from previous tornado events to determine the atmospheric conditions favorable for the development of tornadic activity, in an effort to predict such events with some degree of accuracy. At the time, there had not been studies on how tornadoes formed; however, military radars were being adapted for forecasting use, allowing forecasters to see the outlines of storms but not their internal attributes such as rotation. Miller and Fawbush's findings on atmospheric phenomenon present in past outbreaks would aid in their initial forecast, as the day's surface and upper-air analysis charts determined the same conditions present on March 20 were present on the 25th, concluded that central Oklahoma would have the highest risk for tornadoes during the late-afternoon and evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Borum, who had put together a severe weather safety plan for base personnel, then suggested that Miller and Fawbush issue a severe thunderstorm forecast, and then asked the men if they would issue a tornado forecast based on the similarities between the conditions that produced the tornado which hit the base five days earlier, which they were reluctant to do. Fawbush wrote the forecast message that Miller would type and issued it to base operations at 2:50 p.m. as thunderstorms were approaching from <a href="/wiki/North_Texas" title="North Texas">North Texas</a>. Defying the high odds of two tornadoes hitting the same area in five days, one hit the Tinker campus around 6:00 p.m., to the surprise of Miller (who left the base an hour earlier, believing their forecast would not pan out), who found out about the storm (produced by two thunderstorms that merged to the southwest of Tinker) via a radio report. Miller and Fawbush would not put out another tornado forecast until March 25, 1949, when they successfully predicted tornadic activity would occur in southeastern Oklahoma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Miller and Fawbush soon would distribute their tornado forecasts to the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Highway_Patrol" title="Oklahoma Highway Patrol">Oklahoma Highway Patrol</a>, after giving William Maughan, chief meteorologist at the U.S. Weather Bureau's Oklahoma City office (who provided them with additional archived weather data to help fine-tune their forecasts), permission to relay their forecasts to those agencies. The relative accuracy of the forecasts restarted a debate over their reliability and whether military or civilian agencies should have jurisdiction over the issuance of weather warnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning2-2">[2]</a></sup> The USAF had pioneered tornado forecasting and tornado warnings, although John P. Finley had developed the first experimental tornado forecasts in 1885, before he and other officials with the agency were prohibited by the <a href="/wiki/Signal_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Signal Corps (United States Army)">United States Signal Service</a>'s weather service from using the word "tornado" in forecasts two years later, directing Finley to instead reference "severe local storms", a move motivated by concerns by businessmen in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> that Finley's forecasts would hurt economic development if potential investors believed their areas were tornado-prone. This position on tornado forecasting would be shared with the U.S. Weather Bureau after it was formation in 1890, fearing that it would incite panic among the public if tornadoes were predicted to occur; the side effect of this was that the lack of warning resulted in a steady increase in the number of tornado-related fatalities through the 1950s, with some events prior to 1948 (such as the deadliest tornado in U.S. history, the <a href="/wiki/Tri-State_Tornado" title="Tri-State Tornado">Tri-State Tornado</a> in March 1925, and the <a href="/wiki/1947_Glazier%E2%80%93Higgins%E2%80%93Woodward_tornadoes" title="1947 Glazier–Higgins–Woodward tornadoes">Glazier–Higgins–Woodward tornadoes</a> in April 1947) having death tolls that exceeded well over 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning3-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1938, the Weather Bureau rescinded its ban on the usage of the word "tornado" in weather products disseminated to emergency management personnel. The Bureau would develop a network of volunteer storm spotters in the early 1940s during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, to provide warning of tornadoes to workers in munitions plants and strategic factories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning3-3">[3]</a></sup> The ban on issuing tornado warnings to the general public would not be revoked until Chief of Bureau Francis W. Reichelderfer officially lifted the ban in a Circular Letter issued on July 12, 1950 to all first order stations: "Weather Bureau employees should avoid statements that can be interpreted as a negation of the Bureau's willingness or ability to make tornado forecasts", and that a "good probability of verification" exist when issuing such forecasts due to the difficulty in accurately predicting tornadic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> agreed to have Miller and Fawbush present their methodology for forecasting tornadoes during the organization's 1950 meeting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis</a>; after garnering press coverage for their successful prediction of past tornadoes, AMS representatives decided to open the presentation to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning3_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning3-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Air Force began issuing severe weather forecasts relayed to Weather Bureau offices and emergency personnel in tornado-prone regions through the formation of the Severe Weather Warning Center in 1951, before the Bureau's contention that the USAF intruded on its responsibility to relay such forecasts led to the SWWC limiting the release of its tornado forecasts to military personnel; however, the move to prohibit the USAF from widespread releasing of tornado forecasts led to disapproval and heavy criticism from Oklahoma media outlets, given the agency's continued refusal to provide public tornado warnings. The Weather Bureau issued its first experimental public tornado forecast in March 1952, which proved inaccurate and was released too late to become widely available for public consumption; however, a forecast issued the following evening managed to predict an outbreak of tornadoes across most of the warned seven-state area (from <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning3_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning3-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even after the U.S. Weather Bureau lifted their ban on tornado warnings, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> continued to ban television and radio outlets from broadcasting tornado warnings on-air for the same reasoning cited in the Bureau's abolished ban. Broadcast media followed this ban until 1954, when meteorologist <a href="/wiki/Harry_Volkman" title="Harry Volkman">Harry Volkman</a> broadcast the first televised tornado warning over WKY-TV (now <a href="/wiki/KFOR-TV" title="KFOR-TV">KFOR-TV</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a>, due to his belief that the banning of tornado warnings over broadcast media cost lives. Through an alert issued by the USAF Severe Weather Warning Center, Volkman opted to interrupt regular programming to warn viewers of a reported tornado approaching the Oklahoma City area; although station management and U.S. Weather Bureau officials were displeased with his move, WKY-TV received numerous telephone calls and letters thanking Volkman for the warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwarning4_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-stormwarning4-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornado_alert">Tornado alert</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornado_warning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tornado alert">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For many years until the early 1980s, an intermediate type of tornado advisory known as a tornado alert was defined by the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> and issued by the agency's local forecast offices, indicating that tornado formation was imminent. In theory, tornado alerts covered situations such as visible rotation in clouds and certain other phenomena which are portents of funnel cloud formation. The National Weather Service's use of this advisory began to decline after 1974, although it was still listed on public information materials issued by various media outlets, local NWS offices and other entities for another decade or so.</p>
<p>The criteria which called for tornado alerts in the past now generally result in a tornado warning with clarifying verbiage specifying that the warning was issued because rotation was detected in one way or another, that a <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a> has formed or a tornado has been spotted or detected. The preferred response to both the tornado alerts and warnings is to take shelter immediately, so distinguishing them could be seen as splitting hairs, especially since storm prediction methods have improved.</p>
<p>The tornado alert was finally eliminated outright because it was made largely obsolete by the advent of <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">Doppler</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a>, which can detect rotational funnel cloud formations earlier than is typically possible by trained spotters and members of the public. With fewer false-positives, radar also helped reduce public confusion over storm types, strengths and precise locations. The last tornado alert to be officially issued was discussed in earnest following the <a href="/wiki/1974_Super_Outbreak" title="1974 Super Outbreak">1974 Super Outbreak</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornado_emergency">Tornado emergency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornado_warning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tornado emergency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tornado_emergency" title="Tornado emergency">Tornado emergency</a></div>
<p>The National Weather Service has the option of issuing a <a href="/wiki/Tornado_emergency" title="Tornado emergency">tornado emergency</a>, a severe weather statement with unofficial, enhanced wording that is disseminated when a large, extremely violent tornado is about to impact a densely populated area. This category of weather statement is the highest and most urgent level relating to tornadoes, albeit an unofficial alert product. The first tornado emergency was declared on May 3, 1999, when an <a href="/wiki/1999_Bridge_Creek%E2%80%93Moore_tornado" title="1999 Bridge Creek–Moore tornado">F5 tornado</a> struck southern portions of the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a> metropolitan area, causing major damage exceeding $1 billion. In some cases, such as an F3 tornado that struck the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> metropolitan area on September 20, 2002, a tornado emergency has been declared within the initial issuance of the tornado warning. Not all confirmed tornadoes will be considered a "tornado emergency", and such statements are commonly declared when it is believed that the tornado is at a severity in which it would cause a significant threat to life and property.</p>
<p>The levels of severity increase as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convective_Outlook" title="Convective Outlook">Convective Outlook</a> mentioning tornado potential</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Severe_Weather_Outlook" title="Public Severe Weather Outlook">Public Severe Weather Outlook</a> mentioning tornado potential</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">Tornado Watch</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Particularly_Dangerous_Situation" title="Particularly Dangerous Situation">Particularly Dangerous Situation</a> Tornado Watch</li>
<li>Tornado Warning</li>
<li>Particularly Dangerous Situation Tornado Warning (used by Weather Forecast Offices within the National Weather Service Central Region Headquarters as an intermediate warning in the event that a tornado has been spotted or confirmed, or a significant tornado is expected)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tornado_emergency" title="Tornado emergency">Tornado Emergency</a></li>
</ol>
<p>Tornado warnings can also be intensified by added wording mentioning that the storm is life-threatening, that it is an extremely dangerous situation, that a large, violent and/or destructive tornado is on the ground or is capable of causing significant property damage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criteria">Criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornado_warning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A tornado warning is issued when any of the following conditions has occurred:</p>
<ul>
<li>a tornado is reported on the ground, or</li>
<li>a funnel cloud has been reported, or</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">strong low-level rotation</a> is indicated by <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Definition-9">[9]</a></sup> or</li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a> is headed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Definition-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A <i>tornado warning</i> means there is immediate danger for the warned area and immediate surrounding locations – if not from the relatively narrow tornado itself, from the severe thunderstorm producing (or likely to produce) it. Those in the path of such a storm are urged to take cover immediately, as it is a life-threatening situation. A warning is different from a <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">tornado watch</a> (issued in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> by a national guidance center, the <a href="/wiki/Storm_Prediction_Center" title="Storm Prediction Center">Storm Prediction Center</a>) which only indicates that conditions are favorable for the formation of tornadoes.</p>
<p>Generally (but not always), a tornado warning also indicates that the potential is there for severe <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">straight-line winds</a> and/or large <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> (in the United States, winds exceeding 58 miles per hour (93 km/h) and hail larger than 1 inch (2.5 cm) are the respective defined criteria to classify such phenomena as severe; the criteria varies in other countries) from the thunderstorm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Example2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Example2-10">[10]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm_warning" title="Severe thunderstorm warning">severe thunderstorm warning</a> can be upgraded suddenly to a tornado warning should conditions warrant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Example2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Example2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, local offices of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> outline warnings for tornadoes and severe thunderstorms in <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygonal</a> shapes for map-based weather hazard products, which are used to delineate sections of a <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">county</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parish_(administrative_division)" title="Parish (administrative division)">parish</a> or other jurisdiction that the warning covers (which are also referenced in NWS text warning products by the specified sections of the affected jurisdictions), based on the projected path of a storm as determined by Doppler radar at the time of the warning's issuance; however, entire counties/parishes are sometimes included in the warning polygon, especially if they encompass a small geographical area. Prior to October 2007, warnings were issued by the National Weather Service on a per-county basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storm-based_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storm-based-11">[11]</a></sup> Storm Prediction Center and National Weather Service products as well as <a href="/wiki/First_Warning" title="First Warning">severe weather alert displays</a> used by some television stations highlight tornado warnings with a red polygon or filled county/parish outline.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, similar criteria are used and warnings are issued by regional offices of the <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_Service_of_Canada" title="Meteorological Service of Canada">Meteorological Service of Canada</a> branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halifax_Regional_Municipality" title="Halifax Regional Municipality">Halifax</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-12">[12]</a></sup> (in the province of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Emergency Management Ontario recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> began issuing red alerts for areas of the province that are already under an Environment Canada-issued tornado warning; these red alerts sometimes override the tornado warning if local government or media are participating in the program).</p>
<p>Tornado warnings are generated via the Advance Weather Interactive Processing System (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AWIPS" title="AWIPS">AWIPS</a>) and then disseminated through various communication routes accessed by the media and various agencies, on the internet, to <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> satellites, and on <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Weather_Radio" title="NOAA Weather Radio">NOAA Weather Radio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dissemination_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dissemination-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense_siren" title="Civil defense siren">Tornado sirens</a> are also usually activated for the affected areas if present (the actual areas where sirens are activated may vary depending on the relay structure of a given jurisdiction's siren network, with some municipalities activating all sirens within their network even in areas not referenced as being included in the warning). Local police or fire departments may dispatch crews not assigned to an existing emergency call to travel within a designated area to warn residents to take tornado safety precautions if sirens are disabled due to technical problems or are not present, while <a href="/wiki/Auto_dialer" title="Auto dialer">automated phone calls</a> may be made to residents for the same purpose in some areas should such disruptions occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Additionally, if it is deemed necessary, the National Weather Service has the option of requesting activation of the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Alert_System" title="Emergency Alert System">Emergency Alert System</a> to interrupt television and radio broadcasts to get the bulletin out quickly.</p>
<p>Advances in technology, both in identifying conditions and in distributing warnings effectively, have been credited with reducing the death toll from tornadoes. The average warning times have increased substantially from -10 to -15 minutes in 1974 to about 15 minutes as of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tornado_warning&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> (in some cases, the lead time can extend to more than an hour's warning of impending tornadoes). In the United States, the tornado death rate has declined from 1.8 deaths per million people per year in 1925 to only 0.11 per million in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Much of this change is credited to improvements in the tornado warning system, via the various advances in the detection of severe local storms, along with an increase in reports visually confirming severe weather activity via storm spotters, public officials and citizens.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_truthing">Ground truthing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornado_warning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ground truthing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SKYWARN" title="SKYWARN">SKYWARN</a> program, which trains citizens on how to spot tornadoes, funnel clouds, wall clouds, and other severe weather phenomena, is offered by the National Weather Service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Used in tandem with Doppler radar information, eyewitness reports can be very helpful for warning the public of an impending tornado, especially when used for <a href="/wiki/Ground_truth" title="Ground truth">ground truthing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groundtruth3_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Groundtruth3-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other spotter groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Radio_Emergency_Service" title="Amateur Radio Emergency Service">Amateur Radio Emergency Service</a>, news media, local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_agencies" title="Law enforcement agencies">law enforcement agencies</a>/<a href="/wiki/Emergency_management" title="Emergency management">emergency management</a> organizations, <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service#Surface_observations" title="National Weather Service">cooperative observers</a>, and the general public also relay information to the National Weather Service for ground truthing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groundtruth4_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Groundtruth4-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
