<p>The <b>1993–94 Australian region cyclone season</b> was a slightly above average Australian <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">cyclone</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Australian_region_cyclone_seasons" title="List of Australian region cyclone seasons">season</a>. It was also an event in the ongoing cycle of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> formation. It ran from 1 November 1993 to 30 April 1994. The regional tropical cyclone operational plan also defines a <i>tropical cyclone year</i> separately from a <i>tropical cyclone season</i>, and the "tropical cyclone year" ran from 1 July 1993 to 30 June 1994.</p>
<p>Tropical cyclones in this area were monitored by four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone_Warning_Centre" title="Tropical Cyclone Warning Centre">Tropical Cyclone Warning Centres</a> (TCWCs): the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology_(Australia)" title="Bureau of Meteorology (Australia)">Australian Bureau of Meteorology</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brisbane,_Queensland" title="Brisbane, Queensland">Brisbane</a>; and TCWC <a href="/wiki/Port_Moresby" title="Port Moresby">Port Moresby</a> in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.</p>

<p>Naomi was the first cyclone of the 1993/94 season. Forming early on 15 December 1993, the storm moved south and strengthened into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> before making landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There was moderate damage and a fishing boat was disabled during the storm. There were no deaths.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Rewa" title="Cyclone Rewa">Cyclone Rewa</a></div>
<p>Cyclone Rewa formed on 26 December 1993. It looped around the Coral Sea for almost a month, crossed New Caledonia and the Solomon Island, and dissipated on 21 January. Rewa was the longest-lived South Pacific tropical cyclone on record, lasting 25 days, from 26 December to 21 January.</p>

<p>On January 11, a tropical low formed northwest of <a href="/wiki/Broome,_Western_Australia" title="Broome, Western Australia">Broome, Western Australia</a>. It was named Pearl a few hours later by the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearl_bom_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pearl_bom-3">[3]</a></sup> The cyclone continued westward and reached a peak intensity of 155 km/h (100 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearl_bom_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pearl_bom-3">[3]</a></sup> As the system moved west of 90°E, MFR took over warning responsibility on January 18 and renamed the cyclone Farah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-4">[4]</a></sup> At that time, MFR estimated winds of about 120 km/h (75 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fbt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fbt-5">[5]</a></sup> After having moved westward due to a ridge to the south, Farah turned to the south upon entering the basin due to an approaching trough, which previously absorbed Edmea. High wind shear caused rapid weakening, and by January 19, there was little remaining convection. The next day, Farah weakened to tropical depression status and turned to the southeast. The ridge built behind the trough, causing the depression to stall and drift northward, and by February 22, Farah dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-report-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Early on 29 January, the BoM reported that a tropical low had developed within the monsoon trough about 50 km (30 mi) to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Gove_Peninsula" title="Gove Peninsula">Gove Peninsula</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QLD_Flood_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-QLD_Flood-7">[7]</a></sup> During that day the low rapidly developed further as it moved south-eastwards, before early on 29 January TCWC Brisbane reported that the low had developed into a category 1 tropical cyclone and named it Sadie as the system started to move southwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup> Later that day the JTWC initiated advisories on Cyclone Sadie and designated it as Tropical Cyclone 14P, while the system started to move towards the east and was near its peak 1-minute maximum sustained windspeeds of 85 km/h (50 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_ATCR_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_ATCR-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sadie_JTWC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sadie_JTWC-9">[9]</a></sup> Early the next day Cyclone Sadie made landfall to the north of near Normanton, after TCWC Brisbane had reported that the system had peaked with 10-minute maximum sustained winds of 85 km/h (50 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QLD_Flood_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-QLD_Flood-7">[7]</a></sup> After making landfall, the system moved towards the south-southeast before weakening and degenerating into a tropical low/rain depression later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical Cyclone Sharon was the most intense cyclone in the Western Australian region during the 1993/94 season. It formed about 1100 km north of Northwest Cape on 12 March 1994, then rapidly intensified during the 14th while moving on a south-southwest path towards the west <a href="/wiki/Pilbara" title="Pilbara">Pilbara</a> coast. During the 16th the cyclone rapidly weakened due to movement into a region of strong westerly wind shear. Cyclone warnings were issued for the west Pilbara and upper west coast on the 16th but were cancelled on the morning of the 17th Continued shearing and subsequent movement over cooler waters weakened the storm to below cyclone strength by the morning of the 18th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom.gov.au_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-bom.gov.au-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical Cyclone Tim was a very small weak system that moved on a generally westward path from south of Sumatra to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cocos_Islands" title="Cocos Islands">Cocos Islands</a> from 28 March to 3 April 1994. <td>150 km/h (90 mph) <small>(10-min)</small>  955 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>)</td>

<p>Tropical Cyclone Vivienne formed from a tropical low that had moved westward across the Timor Sea. It intensified rapidly during the afternoon and night of 5 April 1994, reaching maximum intensity on 8 April when it was located 550 km to the north-northwest of Broome. Its development from here on was hampered by vertical wind shear and Vivienne moved on a generally west-southwest path parallel to, but well offshore from, the Western Australian coastline. It dissipated on 13 April and no watches or warnings were issued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom.gov.au_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-bom.gov.au-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 20 April, the JTWC started to monitor an area of low pressure that located over the Solomon Islands about 155 km (95 mi) to the north of <a href="/wiki/Honiara" title="Honiara">Honiara</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-29P-10">[10]</a></sup> During that day the disturbance moved towards the southeast and passed over several of the Solomon Islands, before emerging into the Australian basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-29P-10">[10]</a></sup> Over the next couple of days the disturbance gradually developed further while moving towards the southwest before re-curving and moving southeastwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-29P-10">[10]</a></sup> On 24 April, as it moved back into the South Pacific basin, the JTWC initiated advisories on the disturbance, designating it as Tropical Cyclone 29P, with peak windspeeds equivalent to a tropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-29P-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140_JB_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-140_JB-11">[11]</a></sup> As the system was classified, it recurved again and started to move slowly towards the northwest, and started to feel the effects of a high amount of vertical windshear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-29P-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140_JB_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-140_JB-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result of the windshear, the center became exposed and displaced from the deep convection before the JTWC issued their final advisory on 25 April as 29P weakened into an area of low pressure, before dissipating later that day about 600 km (375 mi) to the southwest of Honiara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-29P-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140_JB_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-140_JB-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1993%E2%80%9394_Australian_region_cyclone_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During March 23, a weak tropical low developed within the South Pacific Convergence Zone near Papua New Guinea's <a href="/wiki/Louisiade_Archipelago" title="Louisiade Archipelago">Louisiade Archipelago</a>, within an area of favourable surface and upper air conditions for further development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Usha_FMS_Report_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Usha_FMS_Report-12">[12]</a></sup> Over the next day the system moved eastwards and gradually developed further, before TCWC Port Moresby reported that the system had developed into a category 1 tropical cyclone on the Australian scale and named it Usha during the next day as it moved out of the Australian region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Usha_FMS_Report_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Usha_FMS_Report-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wellington_BT_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wellington_BT-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
