<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Multicellular thunderstorm</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>multicellular thunderstorm</b> cluster is a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> that is composed of multiple <a href="/wiki/Storm_cell" title="Storm cell">cells</a>, each being at a different stage in the life cycle of a thunderstorm. It appears as several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anvil_cloud" title="Anvil cloud">anvils</a> clustered together. A cell is an updraft/downdraft <a href="/wiki/Couplet" title="Couplet">couplet</a>. These different cells will dissipate as new cells form and continue the life of the multicellular thunderstorm cluster with each cell taking a turn as the dominant cell in the group.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multicellular_thunderstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The formation of multicellular thunderstorms imply that the updraft in the mother thunderstorm is offset from its downdraft. New cells usually form n the upwind (usually western or southwestern) part of the storm where the downdraft of the mature cells meet the environmental wind, lifting the air parcel and triggering new convection. The mature cells are thus usually in the center of the storm, and dissipating cells are usually in the downwind (usually eastern or northeastern) part of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsbasics_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tsbasics-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JetSteam_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-JetSteam-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multicellular_thunderstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The multicellular cluster can last for hours while each individual cell should only last for about 20 to 60 minutes. These storms can sometimes be severe and sometimes have awkward paths due to the thunderstorm sometimes not following the path of the cells that compose it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsbasics_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tsbasics-1">[1]</a></sup> The typical <a href="/wiki/Hodograph" title="Hodograph">hodograph</a>, plot of the wind versus altitude, associated with it shows a linear <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> with altitude<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louisville_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Louisville-3">[3]</a></sup> The moderate vertical wind shear leads to the development of a non-symmetric surface convergence associated with the <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">thunderstorm outflow</a>, with the strongest convergence taking place on the downwind side from the storm's motion. So individual while cells moves along the windshear while the line moves at 30° of it, and 70% of the mean wind speed in the layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">Convective available potential energy</a> (CAPE) is moderate to large, usually between 800 and 1,500 J/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary-4">[4]</a></sup> The radar structure of this type of thunderstorm is characterized by overhangs of reflectivity in the southwestern part of the cluster.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multicellular_thunderstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any severe activity in one of these storms will most likely come from the dominant cell near or after its peak updraft strength. This is because there could be severe <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> from a strong updraft that lasts only a short period of time, with damaging winds.</p>
<p>Rain is an important impact of such systems. The speed and direction at which the entire cluster of thunderstorms move downstream make the difference in the amount of rain received in any one location. Individual cells might move downstream but additional cells forming upwind of the cluster can move directly over the path of the previous cell, forming training echoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JetSteam_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-JetSteam-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A multicellular storm can sometimes develop into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_System" title="Mesoscale Convective System">Mesoscale Convective System</a> (MCS) or be a <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>. Updrafts reform new cells continually at the leading edge of system with rain and hail following behind. Individual thunderstorm updrafts and downdrafts along the line can become strong, producing large hail and strong <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> of straight-line winds ahead of system. Tornadoes are only occasionally reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JetSteam_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-JetSteam-2">[2]</a></sup> In certain conditions, squall line can extend on a very long line, moving extremely rapidly, and become a <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a>.</p>
