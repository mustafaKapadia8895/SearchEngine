<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Outflow boundary</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>An <b>outflow boundary</b>, also known as a <b>gust front</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Storm-scale" title="Storm-scale">storm-scale</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> boundary separating <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a>-cooled air (<a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a>) from the surrounding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>; similar in effect to a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, with passage marked by a <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> shift and usually a drop in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and a related pressure jump. Outflow boundaries can persist for 24 hours or more after the thunderstorms that generated them dissipate, and can travel hundreds of kilometers from their area of origin. New thunderstorms often develop along outflow boundaries, especially near the point of intersection with another boundary (<a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dry_line" title="Dry line">dry line</a>, another outflow boundary, etc.). Outflow boundaries can be seen either as fine lines on <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> imagery or else as arcs of low clouds on <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellite</a> imagery. From the ground, outflow boundaries can be co-located with the appearance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roll_cloud" title="Roll cloud">roll clouds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf clouds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nws-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outflow boundaries create low-level <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> which can be hazardous during aircraft takeoffs and landings. If a thunderstorm runs into an outflow boundary, the low-level wind shear from the boundary can cause thunderstorms to exhibit rotation at the base of the storm, at times causing tornadic activity. Strong versions of these features known as downbursts can be generated in environments of vertical wind shear and mid-level dry air. <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">Microbursts</a> have a diameter of influence less than 4 kilometres (2.5 mi), while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroburst" title="Macroburst">macrobursts</a> occur over a diameter greater than 4 kilometres (2.5 mi). Wet microbursts occur in atmospheres where the low levels are saturated, while dry microbursts occur in drier atmospheres from high-based thunderstorms. When an outflow boundary moves into a more stable low level environment, such as into a region of cooler air or over regions of cooler water temperatures out at sea, it can lead to the development of an <a href="/wiki/Undular_bore" title="Undular bore">undular bore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atkins_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-atkins-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outflow_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1208" data-file-width="1832" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg/250px-Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg/375px-Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg/500px-Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thunderstorm_with_lead_gust_front_-_NOAA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Thunderstorm with lead gust front near Brookhaven, New Mexico, United States, North America. The gust front is marked by a <a href="/wiki/Arcus_cloud#Shelf_cloud" title="Arcus cloud">shelf cloud</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>An outflow boundary, also known as a gust front or arc cloud, is the leading edge of gusty, cooler surface <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> from thunderstorm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">downdrafts</a>; sometimes associated with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf cloud</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roll_cloud" title="Roll cloud">roll cloud</a>. A pressure jump is associated with its passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Outflow boundaries can persist for over 24 hours and travel hundreds of kilometers (miles) from their area of origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nws-1">[1]</a></sup> A wrapping gust front is a front that wraps around the <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>, cutting off the inflow of warm moist air and resulting in occlusion. This is sometimes the case during the event of a collapsing storm, in which the wind literally "rips it apart".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nws-gust_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nws-gust-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outflow_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Microburstnasa.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="496" data-file-width="641" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Microburstnasa.JPG/250px-Microburstnasa.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Microburstnasa.JPG/375px-Microburstnasa.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Microburstnasa.JPG/500px-Microburstnasa.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Microburstnasa.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Illustration of a microburst. The wind regime in a microburst is opposite to that of a tornado.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">Downburst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">Squall line</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">microburst</a> is a very localized column of sinking air known as a downburst, producing damaging divergent and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_winds" title="Straight-line winds">straight-line winds</a> at the surface that are similar to but distinguishable from <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> which generally have convergent damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atkins_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-atkins-2">[2]</a></sup> The term was defined as affecting an area 4 kilometres (2.5 mi) in diameter or less,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> distinguishing them as a type of downburst and apart from common wind shear which can encompass greater areas. They are normally associated with individual thunderstorms. Microburst soundings show the presence of mid-level dry air, which enhances evaporative cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-micro-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Organized areas of thunderstorm activity reinforce pre-existing frontal zones, and can outrun cold fronts. This outrunning occurs within the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> in a pattern where the upper level jet splits into two streams. The resultant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_System" title="Mesoscale Convective System">mesoscale convective system</a> (MCS) forms at the point of the upper level split in the wind pattern in the area of best low level inflow. The convection then moves east and toward the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> into the warm sector, parallel to low-level thickness lines. When the convection is strong and linear or curved, the MCS is called a <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>, with the feature placed at the leading edge of the significant wind shift and pressure rise which is normally just ahead of its radar signature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This feature is commonly depicted in the warm season across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> on surface analyses, as they lie within sharp surface troughs.</p>
<p>A macroburst, normally associated with squall lines, is a strong downburst larger than 4 kilometres (2.5 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A wet microburst consists of precipitation and an atmosphere saturated in the low-levels. A dry microburst emanates from high-based thunderstorms with <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> falling from their base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-micro-6">[6]</a></sup> All types are formed by precipitation-cooled air rushing to the surface. Downbursts can occur over large areas. In the extreme case, a <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a> can cover a huge area more than 200 miles (320 km) wide and over 1,000 miles (1,600 km) long, lasting up to 12 hours or more, and is associated with some of the most intense straight-line winds, but the generative process is somewhat different from that of most downbursts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>At ground level, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf clouds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roll_cloud" title="Roll cloud">roll clouds</a> can be seen at the leading edge of outflow boundaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Through <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellite</a> imagery, an arc cloud is visible as an arc of low clouds spreading out from a thunderstorm. If the skies are cloudy behind the arc, or if the arc is moving quickly, high wind gusts are likely behind the gust front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Sometimes a gust front can be seen on <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a>, showing as a thin arc or line of weak radar echos pushing out from a collapsing storm. The thin line of weak radar echoes is known as a fine line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Occasionally, winds caused by the gust front are so high in velocity that they also show up on radar. This cool outdraft can then energize other storms which it hits by assisting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updrafts</a>. Gust fronts colliding from two storms can even create new storms. Usually, however, no rain accompanies the shifting winds. An expansion of the rain shaft near ground level, in the general shape of a human foot, is a telltale sign of a downburst. <i>Gustnadoes</i>, short-lived vertical circulations near ground level, can be spawned by outflow boundaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-micro-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Gust fronts create low-level <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> which can be hazardous to planes when they takeoff or land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Flying <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> are swept along by the <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">prevailing winds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As such, fine line patterns within <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> imagery, associated with converging winds, are dominated by insect returns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> At the surface, clouds of dust can be raised by outflow boundaries. If squall lines form over arid regions, a duststorm known as a <a href="/wiki/Haboob" title="Haboob">haboob</a> can result from the high winds picking up dust in their wake from the desert floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> If outflow boundaries move into areas of the atmosphere which are stable in the low levels, such as over colder pockets of ocean or through the cold sector of <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, they can create a phenomenon known as an undular bore, which shows up on satellite and radar imagery as a series of <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse waves</a> in the cloud field oriented perpendicular to the low-level winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
