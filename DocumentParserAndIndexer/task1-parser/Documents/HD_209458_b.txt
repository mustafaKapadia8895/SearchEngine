<p><b>HD 209458 b</b>, also given the nickname <b><a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a></b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, is an <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanet</a> that orbits the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_twin" title="Solar twin">solar analog</a> <a href="/wiki/HD_209458" title="HD 209458">HD 209458</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(constellation)" title="Pegasus (constellation)">Pegasus</a>, some 150 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. The radius of the planet's orbit is 7 million kilometres, about 0.047 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a>, or one eighth the radius of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>'s orbit. This small radius results in a year that is 3.5 Earth days long and an estimated surface temperature of about 1,000 °C (about 1,800 °F). Its mass is 220 times that of Earth (0.69 <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> masses) and its volume is some 2.5 times greater than that of Jupiter. The high mass and volume of HD 209458 b indicate that it is a <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giant</a>.</p>
<p>HD 209458 b represents a number of milestones in extraplanetary research. It was the first of many categories:</p>
<ul>
<li>a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transiting</a> extrasolar planet</li>
<li>the first planet detected through more than one method</li>
<li>an extrasolar planet known to have an <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a></li>
<li>an extrasolar planet observed to have an evaporating <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atmosphere</li>
<li>an extrasolar planet found to have an atmosphere containing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a></li>
<li>one of the first two extrasolar planets to be directly observed <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopically</a></li>
<li>the first extrasolar gas giant to have its superstorm measured</li>
<li>the first planet to have its orbital speed measured, determining its mass directly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ignas_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ignas-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Based on the application of new, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical" title="Theoretical">theoretical</a> models, as of April 2007, it is alleged to be the first extrasolar planet found to have <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> in its atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_water_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space.com_water-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20131203_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20131203-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ApJ-20130910_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ApJ-20130910-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July, 2014, NASA announced finding very dry <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet#Atmosphere" title="Exoplanet">atmospheres</a> on HD 209458 b and two other exoplanets (<a href="/wiki/HD_189733_b" title="HD 189733 b">HD 189733 b</a> and <a href="/wiki/WASP-12b" title="WASP-12b">WASP-12b</a>) orbiting Sun-like stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140724_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140724-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_and_discovery">Detection and discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Detection and discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transits">Transits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">Spectroscopic</a> studies first revealed the presence of a planet around HD 209458 on November 5, 1999. Astronomers had made careful <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometric</a> measurements of several stars known to be orbited by planets, in the hope that they might observe a dip in brightness caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transit</a> of the planet across the star's face. This would require the planet's orbit to be inclined such that it would pass between the Earth and the star, and previously no transits had been detected.</p>
<p>Soon after the discovery, separate teams, one led by <a href="/wiki/David_Charbonneau" title="David Charbonneau">David Charbonneau</a> including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Timothy_Brown_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Timothy Brown (astronomer) (page does not exist)">Timothy Brown</a> and others, and the other by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_W._Henry" title="Gregory W. Henry">Gregory W. Henry</a>, were able to detect a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transit</a> of the planet across the surface of the star making it the first known transiting extrasolar planet. On September 9 and 16, 1999, Charbonneau's team measured a 1.7% drop in HD 209458's brightness, which was attributed to the passage of the planet across the star. On November 8, Henry's team observed a partial transit, seeing only the ingress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transit_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-transit-10">[10]</a></sup> Initially unsure of their results, the Henry group decided to rush their results to publication after overhearing rumors that Charbonneau had successfully seen an entire transit in September. Papers from both teams were published simultaneously in the same issue of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrophysical_Journal" title="Astrophysical Journal">Astrophysical Journal</a>. Each transit lasts about three hours, during which the planet covers about 1.5% of the star's face.</p>
<p>The star had been observed many times by the <a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">Hipparcos</a> satellite, which allowed astronomers to calculate the orbital period of HD 209458 b very accurately at 3.524736 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectroscopic">Spectroscopic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spectroscopic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spectroscopic analysis had shown that the planet had a mass about 0.69 times that of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mass_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mass-12">[12]</a></sup> The occurrence of transits allowed astronomers to calculate the planet's radius, which had not been possible for any previously known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">exoplanet</a>, and it turned out to have a radius some 35% larger than Jupiter's.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_detection">Direct detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Direct detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 22, 2005, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> released news that <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> light from the planet had been measured by the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a>, the first ever direct detection of light from an extrasolar planet. This was done by subtracting the parent star's constant light and noting the difference as the planet transited in front of the star and was eclipsed behind it, providing a measure of the light from the planet itself. New measurements from this observation determined the planet's temperature as at least 750 °C (1300 °F). The circular orbit of HD 209458 b was also confirmed.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectral_observation">Spectral observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spectral observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 21, 2007, NASA and <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> released news that HD 209458 b was one of the first two extrasolar planets to have their spectra directly observed, the other one being <a href="/wiki/HD_189733_b" title="HD 189733 b">HD 189733 b</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardson2007_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson2007-14">[14]</a></sup> This was long seen as the first mechanism by which extrasolar but non-sentient life forms could be searched for, by way of influence on a planet's atmosphere. A group of investigators led by Jeremy Richardson of NASA's <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> spectrally measured HD 209458 b's atmosphere in the range of 7.5 to 13.2 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a>. The results defied theoretical expectations in several ways. The spectrum had been predicted to have a peak at 10 micrometres which would have indicated water vapor in the atmosphere, but such a peak was absent, indicating no detectable water vapor. Another unpredicted peak was observed at 9.65 micrometres, which the investigators attributed to clouds of silicate dust, a phenomenon not previously observed. Another unpredicted peak occurred at 7.78 micrometres, for which the investigators did not have an explanation. A separate team led by Mark Swain of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> reanalyzed the Richardson <i>et al.</i> data, and had not yet published their results when the Richardson <i>et al.</i> article came out, but made similar findings.</p>
<p>On 23 June 2010, astronomers announced they have measured a superstorm (with windspeeds of up to 7000 km/h) for the first time in the atmosphere of HD 209458 b.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The very high-precision observations done by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESO" title="ESO">ESO</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a> and its powerful <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope#Instruments" title="Very Large Telescope">CRIRES spectrograph</a> of carbon monoxide gas show that it is streaming at enormous speed from the extremely hot day side to the cooler night side of the planet. The observations also allow another exciting "first"—measuring the orbital speed of the exoplanet itself, providing a direct determination of its mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ignas_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ignas-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation">Rotation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rotation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of August 2008, the most recent calculation of HD 209458 b's <a href="/wiki/Rossiter%E2%80%93McLaughlin_effect" title="Rossiter–McLaughlin effect">Rossiter–McLaughlin effect</a> and hence spin–orbit angle is −4.4 ± 1.4°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It had been previously hypothesized that <a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">hot Jupiters</a> particularly close to their parent star should exhibit this kind <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of inflation due to intense heating of their outer atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">Tidal heating</a> due to its orbit's eccentricity, which may have been more eccentric at formation, may also have played a role over the past billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratosphere_and_upper_clouds">Stratosphere and upper clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Stratosphere and upper clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The atmosphere is at a pressure of one bar at an altitude of 1.29 Jupiter radii above the planet's center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rayleigh_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rayleigh-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Where the pressure is 33±5 millibars, the atmosphere is clear (probably hydrogen) and its <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh effect</a> is detectable. At that pressure the temperature is 2200±260 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rayleigh_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-rayleigh-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Observations by the orbiting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microvariability_and_Oscillations_of_STars_telescope" title="Microvariability and Oscillations of STars telescope">Microvariability and Oscillations of STars telescope</a> initially limited the planet's <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> (or reflectivity) below 0.3, making it a surprisingly dark object. (The geometric albedo has since been measured to be 0.038 ± 0.045.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>) In comparison, Jupiter has a much higher albedo of 0.52. This would suggest that HD 209458 b's upper cloud deck is either made of less reflective material than is Jupiter's, or else has no clouds and Rayleigh-scatters incoming radiation like Earth's dark ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albedo_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-albedo-21">[21]</a></sup> Models since then have shown that between the top of its atmosphere and the hot, high pressure gas surrounding the mantle, there exists a <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> of cooler gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dobbs_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-dobbs-23">[23]</a></sup> This implies an outer shell of dark, opaque, hot cloud; usually thought to consist of vanadium and titanium oxides like red dwarfs ("pM planets"), but other compounds like <a href="/wiki/Tholin" title="Tholin">tholins</a> cannot be ruled out yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dobbs_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-dobbs-23">[23]</a></sup> The Rayleigh-scattering heated hydrogen rests at the top of the stratosphere; the absorptive portion of the cloud deck floats above it at 25 millibars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exosphere">Exosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surrounding that level, on November 27, 2001 the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> detected <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, the first planetary atmosphere outside the Solar System to be measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This detection was predicted by <a href="/wiki/Sara_Seager" title="Sara Seager">Sara Seager</a> in late 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seagerprediction_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-seagerprediction-26">[26]</a></sup> The core of the sodium line runs from pressures of 50 millibar to a microbar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This turns out to be about a third the amount of sodium at <a href="/wiki/HD_189733_b" title="HD 189733 b">HD 189733 b</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003–4, astronomers used the Hubble Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph to discover an enormous <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoidal</a> envelope of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> around the planet that reaches 10,000 K. The hydrogen exosphere extends to a distance <i>R</i><sub>H</sub>=3.1 <i>R</i><sub>J</sub>, much larger than the planetary radius of 1.32 R<sub>J</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> At this temperature and distance, the <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution">Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution</a> of particle velocities gives rise to a significant 'tail' of atoms moving at speeds greater than the <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a>. The planet is estimated to be losing about 100–500 million (1–5×10<sup>8</sup>) kg of hydrogen per second. Analysis of the starlight passing through the envelope shows that the heavier carbon and oxygen atoms are being blown from the planet by the extreme "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic" title="Hydrodynamic">hydrodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a>" created by its evaporating hydrogen atmosphere. The hydrogen tail streaming from the planet is approximately 200,000 kilometres long, which is roughly equivalent to its diameter.</p>
<p>It is thought that this type of atmosphere loss may be common to all planets orbiting Sun-like stars closer than around 0.1 AU. HD 209458 b will not evaporate entirely, although it may have lost up to about 7% of its mass over its estimated lifetime of 5 billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loss_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-loss-30">[30]</a></sup> It may be possible that the planet's magnetic field may prevent this loss, because the exosphere would become ionized by the star, and the magnetic field would contain the ions from loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presumed_atmospheric_water_vapor">Presumed atmospheric water vapor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Presumed atmospheric water vapor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 10, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Travis_Barman" title="Travis Barman">Travis Barman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">Lowell Observatory</a> announced evidence that the atmosphere of HD 209458 b contained water vapor. Using a combination of previously published Hubble Space Telescope measurements and new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical" title="Theoretical">theoretical</a> models, Barman found strong evidence for water absorption in the planet's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_water_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Space.com_water-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barman_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-barman-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> His method modeled light passing directly through the atmosphere from the planet's star as the planet passed in front of it. However, this hypothesis is still being investigated for confirmation.</p>
<p>Barman drew on data and measurements taken by Heather Knutson, a student at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, and applied new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical" title="Theoretical">theoretical</a> models to demonstrate the likelihood of water absorption in the atmosphere of the planet. The planet orbits its parent star every three and a half days, and each time it passes in front of its parent star, the atmospheric contents can be analyzed by examining how the atmosphere absorbs light passing from the star directly through the atmosphere in the direction of Earth.</p>
<p>According to a summary of the research, atmospheric water absorption in such an exoplanet renders it larger in appearance across one part of the infrared spectrum, compared to wavelengths in the visible spectrum. Barman took Knutson's Hubble data on HD 209458 b, applied to his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical" title="Theoretical">theoretical</a> model, and allegedly identified water absorption in the planet's atmosphere.</p>
<p>On April 24, the astronomer <a href="/wiki/David_Charbonneau" title="David Charbonneau">David Charbonneau</a>, who led the team that made the Hubble observations, cautioned that the telescope itself may have introduced variations that caused the theoretical model to suggest the presence of water. He hoped that further observations would clear the matter up in the following months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> As of April 2007, further investigation is being conducted.</p>
<p>On October 20, 2009, researchers at JPL announced the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_field">Magnetic field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_209458_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Magnetic field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014, a magnetic field around HD 209458 b was inferred from the way hydrogen was evaporating from the planet. It is the first (indirect) detection of a magnetic field on an exoplanet. The magnetic field is estimated to be about one tenth as strong as Jupiter's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
