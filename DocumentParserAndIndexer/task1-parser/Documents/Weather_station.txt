<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Weather station</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg/220px-Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg/330px-Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg/440px-Mildura_Airport_Weatherstation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Weather station at <a href="/wiki/Mildura_Airport" title="Mildura Airport">Mildura Airport</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria, Australia</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>weather station</b> is a facility, either on land or sea, with instruments and equipment for measuring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmospheric</a> conditions to provide information for <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasts</a> and to study the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>. The measurements taken include <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> amounts. <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">Wind</a> measurements are taken with as few other obstructions as possible, while temperature and humidity measurements are kept free from direct solar radiation, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>. Manual observations are taken at least once daily, while automated measurements are taken at least once an hour. Weather conditions out at sea are taken by ships and buoys, which measure slightly different meteorological quantities such as <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> (SST), wave height, and wave period. Drifting <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">weather buoys</a> outnumber their moored versions by a significant amount.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="450" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg/220px-NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg/330px-NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg/440px-NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_weather_station_at_Wake_Island_harbor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> weather station at <a href="/wiki/Wake_Island" title="Wake Island">Wake Island</a> harbor measures and transmits data on <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">air temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Typical weather stations have the following instruments:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">Thermometer</a> for measuring air and sea surface temperature</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">Barometer</a> for measuring <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hygrometer" title="Hygrometer">Hygrometer</a> for measuring humidity</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">Anemometer</a> for measuring wind speed</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pyranometer" title="Pyranometer">Pyranometer</a> for measuring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rain_gauge" title="Rain gauge">Rain gauge</a> for measuring liquid precipitation over a set period of time.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, at certain <a href="/wiki/Automated_airport_weather_station" title="Automated airport weather station">Automated airport weather stations</a>, additional instruments may be employed, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Present Weather/Precipitation Identification Sensor for identifying falling precipitation</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Disdrometer" title="Disdrometer">Disdrometer</a> for measuring drop size distribution</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transmissometer" title="Transmissometer">Transmissometer</a> for measuring visibility</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ceilometer" title="Ceilometer">Ceilometer</a> for measuring cloud ceiling</li>
</ul>
<p>More sophisticated stations may also measure the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_index" title="Ultraviolet index">ultraviolet index</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leaf_wetness" title="Leaf wetness">leaf wetness</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_moisture" title="Soil moisture">soil moisture</a>, soil temperature, water temperature in ponds, lakes, creeks, or rivers, and occasionally other data.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure">Exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Except for those instruments requiring direct exposure to the elements (anemometer, rain gauge), the instruments should be sheltered in a vented box, usually a <a href="/wiki/Stevenson_screen" title="Stevenson screen">Stevenson screen</a>, to keep direct sunlight off the thermometer and wind off the hygrometer. The instrumentation may be specialized to allow for periodic recording otherwise significant manual labour is required for record keeping. Automatic transmission of data, in a format such as <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a>, is also desirable as many weather station's data is required for weather forecasting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_weather_station">Personal weather station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal weather station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Davis_VantagePro.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="300" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Davis_VantagePro.jpg/220px-Davis_VantagePro.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/40/Davis_VantagePro.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Davis_VantagePro.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Roof-mounted weather station instruments</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A personal weather station is a set of weather measuring instruments operated by a private individual, club, association, or even business (where obtaining and distributing weather data is not a part of the entity's business operation). Personal weather stations have become more advanced and can include many different sensors to measure weather conditions. These sensors can vary between models but most measure wind speed, wind direction, outdoor and indoor temperatures, outdoor and indoor humidity, barometric pressure, rainfall, and finally UV or solar radiation. Other sensors that may be available can measure soil moisture, soil temperature, and leaf wetness. The quality, number of instruments, and placement of personal weather stations can vary widely, making the determination of which stations collect accurate, meaningful, and comparable data difficult.</p>
<p>Personal weather stations also typically involve a digital console that provides readouts of the data being collected. These consoles may interface to a personal computer where data can be displayed, stored, and uploaded to websites or data ingestion/distribution systems. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source_hardware" title="Open source hardware">Open source</a> weather stations are available that are designed to be fully customizable by users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Personal weather stations may be operated solely for the enjoyment and education of the owner, however some owners share their results with others. They do this by either by manually compiling data and distributing it, distributing data over the internet, or sharing data via <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Weather_Observer_Program" title="Citizen Weather Observer Program">Citizen Weather Observer Program</a> (CWOP) is a service which facilitates the sharing of information from personal weather stations. This data is submitted through use of software, a personal computer, and internet connection (or amateur radio) and are utilized by groups such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS) when generating <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">forecast models</a>. Each weather station submitting data to CWOP will also have an individual Web page that depicts the data submitted by that station. The <a href="/wiki/Weather_Underground_(weather_service)" title="Weather Underground (weather service)">Weather Underground</a> Internet site is another popular destination for the submittal and sharing of data with others around the world. As with CWOP, each station submitting data to Weather Underground has a unique Web page displaying their submitted data. The UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>'s Weather Observations Website (WOW) also allows such data to be shared and displayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_weather_station">Home weather station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Home weather station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Home weather stations include hygrometers, thermometers, barographs, and barometers. Commonly wall mounted and made by manufacturers such as Airguide, Taylor, Springfield, Sputnik and Stormoguide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedicated_ships">Dedicated ships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dedicated ships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weather_ship" title="Weather ship">Weather ship</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Weather_ship" title="Weather ship">weather ship</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a> stationed in the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> as a platform for surface and upper air meteorological measurements for use in weather forecasting. It was also meant to aid in search and rescue operations and to support transatlantic flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pop_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pop-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cg-4">[4]</a></sup> The establishment of weather ships proved to be so useful during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> that the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">International Civil Aviation Organization</a> (ICAO) established a global network of 13 weather ships in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pop_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pop-3">[3]</a></sup> Of the 12 left in operation in 1996, nine were located in the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic ocean</a> while three were located in the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_ocean" title="Pacific ocean">Pacific ocean</a>. The agreement of the weather ships ended in 1990. Weather ship observations proved to be helpful in wind and wave studies, as they did not avoid weather systems like merchant ships tended to and were considered a valuable resource.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The last weather ship was <a href="/wiki/MS_Polarfront" title="MS Polarfront">MS <i>Polarfront</i></a>, known as weather station M ("jilindras") at 66°N, 02°E, run by the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Meteorological_Institute" title="Norwegian Meteorological Institute">Norwegian Meteorological Institute</a>. MS <i>Polarfront</i> was removed from service January 1, 2010. Since the 1960s this role has been largely superseded by <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, long range <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">weather buoys</a>. Weather observations from ships continue from thousands of <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_observing_ship_program" title="Voluntary observing ship program">voluntary merchant vessels</a> in routine commercial operation; the <a href="/wiki/Old_Weather" title="Old Weather">Old Weather</a> <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourcing</a> project transcribes naval logs from before the era of dedicated ships.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedicated_buoys">Dedicated buoys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dedicated buoys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">Weather buoy</a></div>
<p>Weather buoys are instruments which collect weather and <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> data within the world's oceans and lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grlks_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-grlks-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mskgn_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mskgn-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craterlake_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-craterlake-8">[8]</a></sup> Moored buoys have been in use since 1951,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> while drifting buoys have been used since the late 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration-10">[10]</a></sup> Moored buoys are connected with the seabed using either chains, <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a>, or buoyant <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NDBC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NDBC-11">[11]</a></sup> With the decline of the <a href="/wiki/Weather_ship" title="Weather ship">weather ship</a>, they have taken a more primary role in measuring conditions over the open seas since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During the 1980s and 1990s, a network of buoys in the central and eastern tropical Pacific ocean helped study the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Moored weather buoys range from 1.5 metres (4.9 ft) to 12 metres (39 ft) in diameter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NDBC_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NDBC-11">[11]</a></sup> while drifting buoys are smaller, with diameters of 30 centimetres (12 in) to 40 centimetres (16 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Drifting buoys are the dominant form of weather buoy in sheer number, with 1250 located worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration-10">[10]</a></sup> Wind data from buoys has smaller error than that from ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> There are differences in the values of sea surface temperature measurements between the two platforms as well, relating to the depth of the measurement and whether or not the water is heated by the ship which measures the quantity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synoptic_weather_station">Synoptic weather station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Synoptic weather station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PULSONIC_-_PULSIA_-_Station_Synoptique.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="170" height="359" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/PULSONIC_-_PULSIA_-_Station_Synoptique.png" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PULSONIC_-_PULSIA_-_Station_Synoptique.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Synoptic Automatic Weather Station</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">Synoptic</a> weather stations are instruments which collect meteorological information at synoptic time 00h00, 06h00, 12h00, 18h00 (UTC) and at intermediate synoptic hours 03h00, 09h00, 15h00, 21h00 (UTC).</p>
<p>The common instruments of measure are anemometer, wind vane, pressure sensor, thermometer, hygrometer, and rain gauge.</p>
<p>The weather measures are formatted in special format and transmit to WMO to help the weather forecast model.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Networks">Networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A variety of land-based weather station networks have been set up globally. Some of these are basic to analyzing <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather fronts</a> and pressure systems, such as the synoptic observation network, while others are more regional in nature, known as <a href="/wiki/Mesonet" title="Mesonet">mesonets</a>.</p>
