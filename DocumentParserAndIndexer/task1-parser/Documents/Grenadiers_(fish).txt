<p><b>Grenadiers</b> or <b>rattails</b> are generally large, brown to black <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gadiform" title="Gadiform">gadiform</a> marine <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Macrouridae</b>. Found at great depths from the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_Ocean" title="Antarctic Ocean">Antarctic</a>, members of this family are amongst the most abundant of the deep-sea fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FB_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FB-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The macrourids form a large and diverse family with 28 extant genera recognized (well over half of the total <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are contained in just three genera, <i><a href="/wiki/Coelorinchus" title="Coelorinchus">Coelorinchus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Coryphaenoides" title="Coryphaenoides">Coryphaenoides</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Nezumia" title="Nezumia">Nezumia</a></i>). They range in length from about 10 cm (3.9 in) in <i>Hymenogadus gracilis</i> to 1.5 m (4.9 ft) in <i>Albatrossia pectoralis</i>. An important commercial fishery exists for the larger species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Giant_grenadier" title="Giant grenadier">giant grenadier</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Coryphaenoides_rupestris" title="Coryphaenoides rupestris">Coryphaenoides rupestris</a></i>. The family as a whole may represent up to 15% of the deep-sea fish population.</p>
<p>Typified by large heads with large mouths and eyes, grenadiers have slender bodies that taper greatly to very thin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_peduncle" title="Caudal peduncle">caudal peduncles</a> or tails (excluding one species with no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">tail fin</a>): this <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rat</a>-like tail explains the common name 'rattail' and the family name Macrouridae, from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>makros</i> meaning "great" and <i>oura</i> meaning "tail". The first <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> is small, high, and pointed (and may be spinous); the second dorsal fin runs along the rest of the back and merges with the tail and extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a>. The scales are small.</p>
<p>As with many deep-living fish, the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> system in grenadiers is well-developed; it is further aided by numerous <a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptor" title="Chemoreceptor">chemoreceptors</a> located on the head and lips and chemosensory <a href="/wiki/Barbel_(anatomy)" title="Barbel (anatomy)">barbels</a> underneath the chin. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">Benthic</a> species have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_bladder" title="Gas bladder">gas bladders</a> with unique muscles attached to them. The animals are thought to use these muscles to "strum" their gas bladders and produce sound, possibly playing a role in courtship and mate location. Light-producing organs, <a href="/wiki/Photophore" title="Photophore">photophores</a>, are present in some species; they are located in the middle of the abdomen, just before the anus and underneath the skin.</p>
<p>Living at depths from 200 to 6,000 m (660 to 19,690 ft), grenadiers are the most common benthic fish of the deep (however, two genera are known to prefer the midwater). They may be solitary or may form large schools, as with the roundnose grenadiers. The benthic species are attracted to structural oases, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cold_seep" title="Cold seep">cold seeps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a>. They are thought to be generalists, feeding on smaller fish, pelagic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustacea" title="Crustacea">crustaceans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphipod" title="Amphipod">amphipods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumacea" title="Cumacea">cumaceans</a>, and less often <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lanternfish" title="Lanternfish">lanternfish</a>. As well as being important apex predators in the benthic habitat, some species are also notable as scavengers.</p>
<p>As few rattail <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> have been recovered, little is known of their life histories. They are known to produce a large number (over 100,000) of tiny (1–2 millimetres or 0.039–0.079 inches in diameter) eggs made buoyant by <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a> droplets. The eggs are presumed to float up to the <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a> (the interface between warmer surface waters and cold, deeper waters) where they develop. The juveniles remain in shallower waters, gradually migrating to greater depths with age.</p>
<p>Spawning may or may not be tied to the seasons, depending on the species. At least one species, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coryphaenoides_armatus" title="Coryphaenoides armatus">Coryphaenoides armatus</a></i>, is thought to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semelparous" title="Semelparous">semelparous</a>; that is, the adults die after spawning. Nonsemelparous species may live to 56 years or more. The macrourids, in general, are thought to have low resilience; commercially exploited species may be overfished and this could soon lead to a collapse of their fisheries.</p>
