<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Livestock</h1>

<p><b>Livestock</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated" title="Domesticated">domesticated</a> <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> raised in an agricultural setting to produce <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodities</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>. The term is often used to refer solely to those raised for food, and sometimes only farmed <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goats" title="Goats">goats</a>. In recent years, some organizations have also raised livestock to promote the survival of <a href="/wiki/Rare_breed_(agriculture)" title="Rare breed (agriculture)">rare breeds</a>. The breeding, maintenance, and slaughter of these animals, known as <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>, is a component of modern <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> that has been practiced in many cultures since humanity's transition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> lifestyles.</p>
<p>Animal husbandry practices have varied widely across cultures and time periods. Originally, livestock were not confined by fences or enclosures, but these practices have largely shifted to <a href="/wiki/Intensive_animal_farming" title="Intensive animal farming">intensive animal farming</a>, sometimes referred to as "factory farming". These practices increase yield of the various commercial outputs, but have led to increased concerns about <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a>. Livestock production continues to play a major economic and cultural role in numerous rural communities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_legal_definition">Etymology and legal definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and legal definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Give_Way_To_Stock_(6759026099).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2912" data-file-width="4368" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Give_Way_To_Stock_%286759026099%29.jpg/250px-Give_Way_To_Stock_%286759026099%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Give_Way_To_Stock_%286759026099%29.jpg/375px-Give_Way_To_Stock_%286759026099%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Give_Way_To_Stock_%286759026099%29.jpg/500px-Give_Way_To_Stock_%286759026099%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p><b>Livestock</b> as a word was first used between 1650 and 1660, as a merger between the words "live" and "stock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> In some periods the words "cattle" and "livestock" have been used interchangeably. One instance is Old North French <i>catel</i>, which meant all kinds of movable personal property,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> including livestock, which was differentiated from immovable real estate ("<a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a>"). In later English, sometimes small livestock such as chickens and pigs were referred to as "small cattle".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Today, the modern meaning of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, without a modifier, usually refers to domesticated bovines, but sometimes "livestock" refers only to this subgroup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>United States' federal legislation defines the term in broader or narrower ways to make specified agricultural commodities either eligible or ineligible for a program or activity. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Livestock_Mandatory_Reporting_Act_of_1999" title="Livestock Mandatory Reporting Act of 1999">Livestock Mandatory Reporting Act of 1999</a> (P.L. 106-78, Title IX) defines livestock only as cattle, swine, and sheep. The 1988 disaster assistance legislation defined the term as "cattle, sheep, goats, swine, poultry (including egg-producing poultry), equine animals used for food or in the production of food, fish used for food, and other animals designated by the Secretary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Deadstock</b> is defined in contradistinction to livestock as " animals that have died before slaughter, sometimes from illness". It is illegal in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to sell or process meat from dead animals for human consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">History of agriculture</a></div>
<p>Animal-rearing originated during the cultural transition to settled farming communities from <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> lifestyles. Animals are domesticated when their breeding and living conditions are controlled by humans. Over time, the collective behaviour, <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">lifecycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> of livestock have changed radically. Many modern farm animals are unsuited to life in the wild.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">Dogs</a> were domesticated in East Asia about 15,000 years ago. <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">Goats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a> were domesticated around 8000 BC in Asia. <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">Swine</a> were domesticated by 7000 BC in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. The earliest evidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equine" title="Equine">equine</a> domestication dates to around 4000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoL_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoL-5">[5]</a></sup> Cows have been domesticated since approximately 10,500 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McTavish_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-McTavish-6">[6]</a></sup> Chickens and other poultry may have been domesticated around 7000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Rabbits are said to have been domesticated in the 5th Century by the monks of the Champagne Region in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The term "livestock" is nebulous and may be defined narrowly or broadly. Broadly, livestock refers to any breed or population of animal kept by humans for a useful, commercial purpose. This can mean <a href="/wiki/List_of_domesticated_animals" title="List of domesticated animals">domestic animals</a>, semidomestic animals, or <a href="/wiki/Captivity_(animal)" title="Captivity (animal)">captive</a> <a href="/wiki/Wild_animal" title="Wild animal">wild animals</a>. Semidomesticated refers to animals which are only lightly domesticated or of disputed status. These populations may also be in the process of <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domestication</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_rearing">Animal rearing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Animal rearing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">Animal husbandry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CH_cow_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4368" data-file-width="2912" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CH_cow_2.jpg/200px-CH_cow_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CH_cow_2.jpg/300px-CH_cow_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CH_cow_2.jpg/400px-CH_cow_2.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CH_cow_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>Livestock are used by humans for a variety of purposes, many of which have an economic value. Livestock products include:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Meat</dt>
<dd>A useful form of dietary <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> and energy, meat is the edible tissue of the animal carcass.</dd>
<dt>Dairy products</dt>
<dd>Mammalian livestock can be used as a source of <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, which can in turn easily be processed into other dairy products, such as <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kefir" title="Kefir">kefir</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kumis" title="Kumis">kumis</a>. Using livestock for this purpose can often yield several times the <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Animal_slaughter" title="Animal slaughter">slaughtering</a> the animal outright.</dd>
<dt>Clothing and adornment</dt>
<dd>Livestock produce a range of fiber textiles. For example, domestic sheep and goats produce wool and mohair, respectively; cattle, swine, deer, and sheep skins can be made into leather; livestock <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hoof" title="Hoof">hooves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horn_(anatomy)" title="Horn (anatomy)">horns</a> can be used to fabricate <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a>, pendants, or <a href="/wiki/Headgear" title="Headgear">headgear</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">Fertilizer</a></dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">Manure</a> can be spread on fields to increase crop yields. This is an important reason why historically, plant and animal domestication have been intimately linked. Manure is also used to make plaster for walls and floors, and can be used as a fuel for fires. The blood and bone of animals are also used as fertilizer.</dd>
<dt>Labor</dt>
<dd>The muscles of animals such as horses, donkeys, and yaks can be used to provide mechanical work. Prior to <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam power</a>, livestock were the only available source of nonhuman labor. They are still used in many places of the world to <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">plough</a> fields (drafting), transport goods and people, in military functions, and to power treadmills for grinding grain.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Land_management" title="Land management">Land management</a></dt>
<dd>The grazing of livestock is sometimes used as a way to control weeds and undergrowth. For example, in areas prone to wildfires, goats and sheep are set to graze on dry scrub which removes combustible material and reduces the risk of fires.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">Conservation</a></dt>
<dd>The raising of livestock to conserve a rare breed can be achieved through gene banking and breeding programmes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>During the history of <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>, many secondary products have arisen in an attempt to increase carcass utilization and reduce waste. For example, animal <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a> and inedible parts may be transformed into products such as pet food and fertilizer. In the past, such waste products were sometimes also fed to livestock, as well. However, intraspecies recycling poses a disease risk, threatening animal and even human health (see <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">bovine spongiform encephalopathy</a> (BSE), <a href="/wiki/Scrapie" title="Scrapie">scrapie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prion" title="Prion">prion</a>). Due primarily to BSE (mad cow disease), feeding animal scraps to animals has been banned in many countries, at least for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruminants" title="Ruminants">ruminants</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Farming_practices">Farming practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Farming practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Farming practices vary dramatically worldwide and among types of animals. Livestock are generally kept in an enclosure, fed by humans, and intentionally bred. However, some livestock are not enclosed, are fed by access to natural foods, and are allowed to breed freely.</p>
<p>Historically, raising livestock was part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic" title="Nomadic">nomadic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Material_culture" title="Material culture">material culture</a>. The herding of camels and reindeer in some parts of the world remains dissociated from <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Transhumance" title="Transhumance">transhumance</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Herding" title="Herding">herding</a> in the California <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> still continues, as cattle, sheep, or goats are moved from winter <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> in lower-elevation valleys to spring and summer pasture in the foothills and <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> regions, as the seasons progress. Cattle were raised on the <a href="/wiki/Rangeland" title="Rangeland">open range</a> in the western United States and Canada, on the <a href="/wiki/Pampas" title="Pampas">Pampas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, and on other <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a>.</p>
<p>The enclosure of livestock in pastures and <a href="/wiki/Barn" title="Barn">barns</a> is a relatively new development in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">history of agriculture</a>. When cattle are enclosed, the type of confinement may vary from a small crate, a large-area <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_fencing" title="Agricultural fencing">fenced-in</a> pasture, or a <a href="/wiki/Paddock" title="Paddock">paddock</a>. The type of feed may vary from naturally growing grass to <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">animal feed</a>. Animals are usually intentionally bred through artificial insemination or supervised mating. Indoor production systems are typically used for pigs, <a href="/wiki/Dairy_cattle" title="Dairy cattle">dairy cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, veal cattle, dairy goats, and other animals depending on the region and season. Animals kept indoors are generally farmed intensively, as large space requirements could make indoor farming unprofitable if not impossible. However, indoor farming systems are controversial due to problems associated with handling animal waste, odours, the potential for <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> contamination, and animal welfare concerns. (For a further discussion on intensively farmed livestock, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factory_farming" title="Factory farming">factory farming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Intensive_pig_farming" title="Intensive pig farming">intensive pig farming</a>). <a href="/wiki/Livestock_source_verification" title="Livestock source verification">Livestock source verification</a> is used to track livestock.</p>
<p>Other livestock are farmed outdoors, where the size of enclosures and the level of supervision may vary. In large, open ranges, animals may be only occasionally inspected or yarded in "round-ups" or a <a href="/wiki/Muster_(livestock)" title="Muster (livestock)">muster</a>. <a href="/wiki/Herding_dog" title="Herding dog">Herding dogs</a> may be used for mustering livestock, as are <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockman_(Australia)" title="Stockman (Australia)">stockmen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jackaroo_(trainee)" title="Jackaroo (trainee)">jackaroos</a> on horses, in vehicles, and in helicopters. Since the advent of <a href="/wiki/Barbed_wire" title="Barbed wire">barbed wire</a> (in the 1870s) and <a href="/wiki/Electric_fence" title="Electric fence">electric fence</a> technology, fencing pastures has become much more feasible and pasture management simplified. Rotation of pasturage is a modern technique for improving nutrition and health while avoiding environmental damage to the land. In some cases, very large numbers of animals may be kept in indoor or outdoor feeding operations (on <a href="/wiki/Feedlot" title="Feedlot">feedlots</a>), where the animals' feed is processed either offsite or onsite, and stored on site before being fed to the animals.</p>
<p>Livestock—-especially cattle—-may be <a href="/wiki/Livestock_branding" title="Livestock branding">branded</a> to indicate ownership and age, but in modern farming, identification is more likely to be indicated by means of <a href="/wiki/Ear_tag" title="Ear tag">ear tags</a> and electronic identification, instead. Sheep are also frequently marked by means of ear marks and/or ear tags. As fears of BSE and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidemic_illnesses" title="Epidemic illnesses">epidemic illnesses</a> mount, the use of <a href="/wiki/Microchip_implant_(animal)" title="Microchip implant (animal)">implants</a> to monitor and trace animals in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_production" title="Food production">food production</a> system is increasingly common, and sometimes required by government regulations.</p>
<p>Modern farming techniques seek to minimize human involvement, increase yield, and improve animal health. <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">Economics</a>, quality, and consumer safety all play roles in how animals are raised. The use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_drugs" title="Hard and soft drugs">hard and soft drugs</a> and feed supplements (or even feed type) may be regulated, or prohibited, to ensure that yield is not increased at the expense of consumer health, safety, or animal welfare. Practices vary around the world, for example <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone" title="Growth hormone">growth hormone</a> use is permitted in the United States, but not in stock to be sold in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. The improvement of animal health using modern farming techniques has come into question. Feeding corn to cattle, which have historically eaten grasses, is an example; where the cattle are less adapted to this change, the rumen pH becomes more acidic, leading to liver damage and other health problems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The US <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> allows nonruminant animal proteins to be fed to cattle enclosed in feedlots. For example, it is acceptable to feed chicken manure and poultry meal to cattle, and beef or pork meat and bone meal to chickens.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predation">Predation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Livestock farmers have suffered from <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wild animal</a> predation and theft by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rustler" title="Rustler">rustlers</a>. In North America, animals such as the <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">gray wolf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">cougar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyote</a> are sometimes considered a threat to livestock. In Eurasia and Africa, predators include the wolf, <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dhole" title="Dhole">dhole</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asiatic_black_bear" title="Asiatic black bear">Asiatic black bear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spotted_hyena" title="Spotted hyena">spotted hyena</a>, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivores" title="Carnivores">carnivores</a>. In South America, <a href="/wiki/Pariah_dog" title="Pariah dog">feral dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">jaguar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eunectes_murinus" title="Eunectes murinus">anacondas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spectacled_bear" title="Spectacled bear">spectacled bears</a> are threats to livestock. In Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Dingo" title="Dingo">dingo</a>, fox, and <a href="/wiki/Wedge-tailed_eagle" title="Wedge-tailed eagle">wedge-tailed eagle</a> are common predators, with an additional threat from domestic dogs that may kill in response to a hunting instinct, leaving the carcass uneaten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease">Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Livestock diseases compromise animal welfare, reduce productivity, and can infect humans. Animal diseases may be tolerated, reduced through animal husbandry, or reduced through antibiotics and vaccines. In developing countries, animal diseases are tolerated in animal husbandry, resulting in considerably reduced productivity, especially given the low health-status of many developing country herds. Disease management to improve productivity is often the first step taken in implementing an agriculture policy.</p>
<p>Disease management can be achieved by modifying animal husbandry practices. These measures aim to prevent infection with biosecurity measures such as controlling animal mixing and entry to farm lots, wearing protective clothing, and quarantining sick animals. Diseases also may be controlled by the use of vaccines and antibiotics. Antibiotics in subtherapeutic doses may also be used as a growth promoter, sometimes increasing growth by 10-15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Concerns about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic_resistance" title="Antibiotic resistance">antibiotic resistance</a> have led in some cases to discouraging the practice of preventive dosing such as the use of antibiotic-laced feed. Countries often require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary" title="Veterinary">veterinary</a> certificates as a condition for transporting, selling, or exhibiting animals. Disease-free areas often rigorously enforce rules for preventing the entry of potentially diseased animals, including quarantine.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_and_marketing">Transportation and marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transportation and marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cattle_sale_1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Cattle_sale_1.JPG/250px-Cattle_sale_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Cattle_sale_1.JPG/375px-Cattle_sale_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Cattle_sale_1.JPG/500px-Cattle_sale_1.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cattle_sale_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grass-fed cattle, saleyards, <a href="/wiki/Walcha,_New_South_Wales" title="Walcha, New South Wales">Walcha, New South Wales</a></div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Livestock_transportation" title="Livestock transportation">Livestock transportation</a></div>
<p>Since many livestock are herd animals, they were historically <a href="/wiki/Droving" title="Droving">driven to market</a> "on the hoof" to a town or other central location. During the period after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the abundance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_longhorn_(cattle)" title="Texas longhorn (cattle)">Longhorn cattle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and the demand for beef in Northern markets led to the implementation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Old_West" title="American Old West">Old West</a> <a href="/wiki/Cattle_drive" title="Cattle drive">cattle drive</a>. The method is still used in some parts of the world. <a href="/wiki/Animal_transporter" title="Animal transporter">Truck</a> transport is now common in developed countries. Local and regional livestock <a href="/wiki/Auction" title="Auction">auctions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commodity_market" title="Commodity market">commodity markets</a> facilitate trade in livestock. In other areas, livestock may be bought and sold in a <a href="/wiki/Bazaar" title="Bazaar">bazaar</a>, such as may be found in many parts of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, or in an informal <a href="/wiki/Flea_market" title="Flea market">flea market</a>-type setting.</p>
<p>In developing countries, providing access to markets has encouraged farmers to invest in livestock, with the result being improved livelihoods. The <a href="/wiki/International_Crops_Research_Institute_for_the_Semi-Arid_Tropics" title="International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics">International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICRISAT" title="ICRISAT">ICRISAT</a>) has worked in <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> to help farmers make their most of their livestock herds. ICRISAT works to improve local farming systems through 'innovation platforms' at which farmers, traders, rural development agencies, and extension officers can discuss the challenges they faced. One finding was that if farmers devoted half of three hectares to maize and half to <i><a href="/wiki/Mucuna" title="Mucuna">mucuna</a></i> (velvet bean) in a rotation system, they could obtain 80% of the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> needed to see their livestock through the dry season. If they only grew maize, they could only meet 20% of their biomass needs. In the town of <a href="/wiki/Gwanda" title="Gwanda">Gwanda</a>, the platform helped create a strong local market for goats, raising the value of a single animal from US$10 to $60. This gave the farmers a great incentive to invest in their own goats by growing their own feed stock, buying commercial feed only as a supplement, and improving their rangeland management techniques. Because the platform has helped regulate prices, farmers now plan ahead and sell animals at auction, rather than just selling one or two animals at their farm gate as opportunities arise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_show" title="Stock show">Stock shows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fairs</a> are events where people bring their best livestock to compete with one another. Organizations such as <a href="/wiki/4-H" title="4-H">4-H</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Block_%26_Bridle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Block &amp; Bridle (page does not exist)">Block &amp; Bridle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/National_FFA_Organization" title="National FFA Organization">FFA</a> encourage young people to raise livestock for show purposes. Special feeds are purchased and prior to the show, hours may be spent grooming the animal to look its best. In cattle, sheep, and swine shows, the winning animals are frequently auctioned off to the highest bidder, and the funds are placed into a scholarship fund for its owner. The movie <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Champion" title="Grand Champion">Grand Champion</a></i>, released in 2004, tells the story of a young Texas boy's experience raising a prize steer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_welfare">Animal welfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Animal welfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="757" data-file-width="1024" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg/250px-Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg/375px-Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg/500px-Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sheep_and_herder_India.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Shepherd" title="Shepherd">shepherd</a> boy in India: Livestock are extremely important to the livelihoods of rural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smallholder" title="Smallholder">smallholder</a> farmers, particularly in the developing world.</div>

<p>The issue of raising livestock for human benefit raises the issue of the suitable relationship between humans and animals, in terms of the status of animals and the obligations of people. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> reflects the viewpoint that animals under human care should be treated in such a way that they do not suffer unnecessarily. What is considered 'unnecessary' suffering may vary. Generally, though, the animal welfare perspective is based on an interpretation of scientific research on farming practices. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> defends the viewpoint that using animals for human benefit is, on principle, exploitation, regardless of the farming practices used. Animal rights activists are often vegan or vegetarian, whereas it is consistent with the animal welfare perspective to eat meat as long as the production processes are defensible.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">Animal welfare</a> groups generally seek to generate public discussion on livestock raising practices and to secure greater regulation and scrutiny of livestock industry practices. Animal rights groups usually seek to abolish livestock farming, although some groups may recognise the necessity of first achieving more stringent regulation . Animal welfare groups such as the RSPCA are often, in first-world countries, given a voice at governmental level in the development of policy. Animal rights groups find it harder to convey their concerns, and as a result, may advocate civil disobedience or violence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A number of animal husbandry practices have been the subject of campaigns in the 1990s and 2000s and have led to legislation in some countries. Confinement of livestock in small and unnatural spaces is often done for economic or health reasons. Animals may be kept in the minimum size of cage or pen with little or no space to exercise. Where livestock are used as a source of power, they may be pushed beyond their limits to the point of exhaustion. Increased public awareness and visibility of such abuse meant it was one of the first areas to receive legislation in the 19th century in European countries, but it continues in parts of Asia. Broiler hens may be debeaked, pigs may have deciduous teeth pulled, cattle may be dehorned and branded, dairy cows and sheep may have tails cropped, Merino sheep may undergo <a href="/wiki/Mulesing" title="Mulesing">mulesing</a>, and many types of male animals may be castrated. Animals may be transported long distances to market and slaughter, often under overcrowded conditions, heat stress, lack of feed and water, and without rest breaks. Such practices have been subject to legislation and protest (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_Export" title="Live Export">Live Export</a>). Appropriate methods to <a href="/wiki/Animal_slaughter" title="Animal slaughter">slaughter livestock</a> were an early target for legislation. Campaigns continue to target <a href="/wiki/Halal" title="Halal">halal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher">kosher</a> religious ritual slaughter.</p>

<p>Reports such as the United Nations report "<a href="/wiki/Livestock%27s_Long_Shadow" title="Livestock's Long Shadow">Livestock's Long Shadow</a>" cast a pall over the livestock sector (primarily cattle, chickens, and pigs) for 'emerging as one of the top two or three most significant contributors to our most serious environmental problems.' In April 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> released a major stock-taking of emissions in the United States entitled <i>Inventory of U.S. Greenhouse Gas Emissions and Sinks: 1990-2006</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It found that "In 2006, the agricultural sector was responsible for emissions of 454.1 teragrams of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent (Tg CO<sub>2</sub> Eq.), or 6 percent of total U.S. greenhouse gas emissions." By way of comparison, transportation in the US produces more than 25% of all emissions. In 2009, Worldwatch Institute released a report which revealed that 51% of greenhouse gas emissions came from the animal agriculture sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The issue of livestock as a major policy focus remains, especially when dealing with problems of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in neotropical areas, <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_shortage" title="Water shortage">water shortage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>, and loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. A research team at <a href="/wiki/Obihiro_University_of_Agriculture_and_Veterinary_Medicine" title="Obihiro University of Agriculture and Veterinary Medicine">Obihiro University of Agriculture and Veterinary Medicine</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaidō">Hokkaidō</a> found that supplementing animals' diets with cysteine, a type of amino acid, and nitrate can reduce the amount of methane gas produced without jeopardising the cattle's productivity or the quality of their meat and milk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> impacts the carbon cycle, as well as the global and regional climate, and causes the habitat loss of many species. Forests that are sinks for the carbon cycle are lost through deforestation. Forests are either logged or burned to make room for mining activities or for grasslands, and often the area needed for such purposes is extensive. Deforestation can also create fragmentation, allowing the survival of only patches of habitat in which species can live. If these patches are distant and small, gene flow is reduced, habitat is altered, edge effects occur, and more opportunities for invasive species to intrude occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao.org_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fao.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_degradation">Land degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Land degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research from the University of <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> in 2008 found that farmers' common practice of overstocking cattle to make up for drought losses made ecosystems more vulnerable and risked long-term damage to cattle herds by depleting scarce biomass. The study of the Kgatleng district of Botswana predicted that by 2050, the cycle of mild drought is likely to become shorter for the region (18 months instead of two years) due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_and_air_pollution">Climate change and air pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Climate change and air pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Methane is one of the gasses emitted from livestock manure; it persists in the atmosphere for long periods of time and is a potent greenhouse gas, the second-most abundant after carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalmethane.org_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-globalmethane.org-19">[19]</a></sup> Though less methane than carbon dioxide is produced, its ability to warm the atmosphere is 25 times greater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalmethane.org_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-globalmethane.org-19">[19]</a></sup> Nitrous oxide, another gaseous byproduct of animal agriculture, is about 300 times more potent at trapping heat in the atmosphere. Animal agriculture contributes 65% of anthropogenic nitrous oxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao.org_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-fao.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_shortage">Water shortage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Water shortage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Livestock require water not only for their own consumption, but also for watering the crops needed to produce their feed. Grains are often used to feed livestock; about 50% of US grains and 40% of world grains are used for this purpose<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.cornell.edu_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.cornell.edu-20">[20]</a></sup> Grain and crop production in general require various amounts of water depending on the end product. For example, 100,000 liters of water are needed to yield a kilogram of grain-fed beef, compared to 900 liters for a kilogram of wheat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.cornell.edu_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-news.cornell.edu-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_pollution">Water pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Water pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fertilizers that often contain manure are used to grow crops (such as cereals and fodder) that contain phosphorus and nitrogen, 95% of which is estimated to be lost to the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Water pollution from agricultural runoff causes dead zones for plants and aquatic animals due to the lack of oxygen in the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This lack of oxygen, known as <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>, is caused when organisms present in the water grow excessively and then later decompose, in the process using up the oxygen in the water. A prominent example is the Gulf of Mexico, where much of the nutrients in fertilizer used in the US Midwest is funneled down the Mississippi River into the Gulf, causing massive dead zones. Other pollutants not commonly considered are antibiotics and hormones. In southern Asia, vultures that consumed carcasses of livestock declined 95% due to their ingestion of the antibiotic known as <a href="/wiki/Diclofenac" title="Diclofenac">diclofenac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao.org_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-fao.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_waste">Agricultural waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Agricultural waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The amount of waste material produced by livestock globally is considerable, as is the effort required to dispose of it. Every minute, 7 million pounds of excrement are produced by animals raised for food in the US (not including animals raised outside of USDA jurisdiction, or aquaculture); another estimate is 335 million tons of "dry matter" / year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A farm with 2,500 dairy cows produces the same amount of waste as a city of 411,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> 130 times more animal waste than human waste is produced in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAO1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAO1-25">[25]</a></sup> – 1.4 billion tons from the meat industry annually.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Daily production has been calculated as: dairy cows, 120 lbs x 9 million cows; cattle, 63 lbs x 90 million cattle; pigs, 14 lbs x 67 million pigs; sheep/goats, 5lbs x 9 million sheep/goats; poultry, .25-1 lbs x 9 billion birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Some waste is disposed of via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composting" title="Composting">composting</a> or use as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>. In some countries, animal waste is even <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">used for energy generation</a>. However, improper waste management practices can pollute both surface and groundwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAO1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-GAO1-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives">Alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Researchers in Australia are looking into the possibility of reducing methane from cattle and sheep by introducing digestive bacteria from kangaroo intestines into livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Furthermore, as a means to conserve traditional livestock, <a href="/wiki/Cryoconservation_of_animal_genetic_resources" title="Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources">cryoconservation of animal genetic resources</a> have been put into action. (Cryoconservation is a practice that involves collecting genetic material and storing it in low temperatures with an intent of conserving a particular breed.)</p>
<p><br/>
In semiarid rangelands such as the Great Plains in the U.S., research has provided evidence that livestock can be beneficial to maintaining grassland habitats for big game species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_and_social_benefits">Economic and social benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Livestock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Economic and social benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The value of global livestock production in 2013 has been estimated at about <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(currency)" title="Orders of magnitude (currency)">883 billion dollars</a>, (constant 2005-2006 dollars).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, economic implications of livestock production extend further: to downstream industry (saleyards, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abattoir" title="Abattoir">abattoirs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butcher" title="Butcher">butchers</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Milk_processor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Milk processor (page does not exist)">milk processors</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigerated_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Refrigerated transport (page does not exist)">refrigerated transport</a>, wholesalers, retailers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_service" title="Food service">food services</a>, tanneries, etc.), upstream industry (feed producers, feed transport, farm and ranch supply companies, equipment manufacturers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_companies" title="Seed companies">seed companies</a>, vaccine manufacturers, etc.) and associated services (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian">veterinarians</a>, nutrition consultants, shearers, etc.).</p>
<p>Livestock provide a variety of food and nonfood products; the latter include leather, wool, pharmaceuticals, bone products, industrial protein, and fats. For many abattoirs, very little animal biomass may be wasted at slaughter. Even intestinal contents removed at slaughter may be recovered for use as fertilizer. Livestock manure helps maintain the fertility of grazing lands. Manure is commonly collected from barns and feeding areas to fertilize cropland. In some places, animal manure is used as fuel, either directly (as in some developing countries), or indirectly (as a source of methane for heating or for generating electricity). In regions where machine power is limited, some classes of livestock are used as draft stock, not only for <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a> and other on-farm use, but also for transport of people and goods. In 1997, livestock provided energy for between an estimated 25 and 64% of cultivation energy in the world's irrigated systems, and that 300 million draft animals were used globally in <a href="/wiki/Small-scale_agriculture" title="Small-scale agriculture">small-scale agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although livestock production serves as a source of income, it can provide additional economic values for rural families, often serving as a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_security" title="Economic security">economic security</a>. Livestock can serve as insurance against risk<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanepoel_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swanepoel-31">[31]</a></sup> and is an economic buffer (of income and/or food supply) in some regions and some economies (e.g., during some African droughts). However, its use as a buffer may sometimes be limited where alternatives are present,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> which may reflect strategic maintenance of insurance in addition to a desire to retain productive assets. Even for some livestock owners in developed nations, livestock can serve as a kind of insurance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Some crop growers may produce livestock as a strategy for diversification of their income sources, to reduce risks related to weather, markets and other factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many studies have found evidence of the social, as well as economic, importance of livestock in developing countries and in regions of rural poverty, and such evidence is not confined to pastoral and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic" title="Nomadic">nomadic</a> societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanepoel_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Swanepoel-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Social values in developed countries can also be considerable. For example, in a study of livestock ranching permitted on national forest land in New Mexico, USA, it was concluded that "ranching maintains traditional values and connects families to ancestral lands and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural heritage</a>", and that a "sense of place, attachment to land, and the value of preserving open space were common themes". "The importance of land and animals as means of maintaining culture and way of life figured repeatedly in permittee responses, as did the subjects of responsibility and respect for land, animals, family, and community."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the US, profit tends to rank low among motivations for involvement in livestock ranching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Instead, family, tradition and a desired way of life tend to be major motivators for ranch purchase, and ranchers "historically have been willing to accept low returns from livestock production."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
