<p>The terms <b>international waters</b> or <b>trans-boundary waters</b> apply where any of the following types of bodies of water (or their <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a>) transcend international boundaries: oceans, <a href="/wiki/Large_marine_ecosystem" title="Large marine ecosystem">large marine ecosystems</a>, enclosed or semi-enclosed regional seas and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">estuaries</a>, rivers, lakes, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> systems (<a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>International waters have no sovereignty, ergo is "Terra nullius" as no state controls it. All states have the freedom of: fishing, navigation, overflight, laying cables and pipelines, and research.</p>
<p>Oceans, seas, and waters outside national jurisdiction are also referred to as the <b>high seas</b> or, in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <i>mare liberum</i> (meaning <i>free sea</i>). The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_High_Seas" title="Convention on the High Seas">Convention on the High Seas</a>, signed in 1958, which has 63 signatories, defined "high seas" to mean "all parts of the sea that are not included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territorial_sea" title="Territorial sea">territorial sea</a> or in the <a href="/wiki/Internal_waters" title="Internal waters">internal waters</a> of a State" and where "no State may validly purport to subject any part of them to its sovereignty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Convention on the High Seas was replaced by <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a>, signed in 1982, which recognized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zones" title="Exclusive Economic Zones">Exclusive Economic Zones</a> extending 200 nautical miles from the baseline, where coastal States have sovereign rights to the water column and sea floor as well as the natural resources found there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ships sailing the high seas are generally under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_state" title="Flag state">flag state</a> (if there is one);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> however, when a ship is involved in certain criminal acts, such as <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> any nation can exercise jurisdiction under the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Universal_jurisdiction" title="Universal jurisdiction">universal jurisdiction</a>. International waters can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Internal_waters" title="Internal waters">internal waters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">territorial waters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zones" title="Exclusive economic zones">exclusive economic zones</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_waterways">International waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_waters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: International waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG/220px-Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG/330px-Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG/440px-Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kom%C3%A1rom114.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Kom%C3%A1rno" title="Komárno">Komárno</a> in Slovakia is an <a href="/wiki/Inland_port" title="Inland port">inland port</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">Danube River</a> which is an important international <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">waterway</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several international treaties have established <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_seas" title="Freedom of the seas">freedom of navigation</a> on semi-enclosed seas.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sound_Dues#Copenhagen_Convention" title="Sound Dues">Copenhagen Convention</a> of 1857 opened access to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> by abolishing the <a href="/wiki/Sound_Dues" title="Sound Dues">Sound Dues</a> and making the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_Straits" title="Danish Straits">Danish Straits</a> an international waterway free to all commercial and military shipping.</li>
<li>Several conventions have opened the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dardanelles" title="Dardanelles">Dardanelles</a> to shipping. The latest, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montreux_Convention_Regarding_the_Regime_of_the_Turkish_Straits" title="Montreux Convention Regarding the Regime of the Turkish Straits">Montreux Convention Regarding the Regime of the Turkish Straits</a>, maintains the straits' status as an international waterway.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other international treaties have opened up rivers, which are not traditionally international waterways.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">Danube River</a> is an international waterway so that landlocked <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">Moldova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> can have secure access to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes_over_international_waters">Disputes over international waters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_waters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disputes over international waters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2513" data-file-width="3755" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/250px-Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/375px-Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/500px-Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clouds_over_the_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Atlantic Ocean – the main zone of sea transport in 15th–20th centuries.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Territorial_claims_in_the_Arctic" title="Territorial claims in the Arctic">Territorial claims in the Arctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Antarctic_territorial_waters" title="Australian Antarctic territorial waters">Australian Antarctic territorial waters</a></div>
<p>Current unresolved disputes over whether particular waters are "International waters" include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Arctic Ocean: While Canada, Denmark, Russia and Norway all regard parts of the Arctic seas as <i>national waters</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Internal_waters" title="Internal waters">internal waters</a></i>, most <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> countries and the United States officially regard the whole region as international waters. The <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_Archipelago" title="Canadian Arctic Archipelago">Canadian Arctic Archipelago</a> is one of the more prominent examples, with Canada claiming it as <a href="/wiki/Internal_waters" title="Internal waters">internal waters</a>, while the United States and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> considers it an international strait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>: Australia claims an <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> (EEZ) around its Antarctic territorial claim. Since this claim is only recognised by four other countries, the EEZ claim is also disputed.</li>
<li>Area around <a href="/wiki/Okinotorishima" title="Okinotorishima">Okinotorishima</a>: Japan claims Okinotorishima is an <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islet</a> and thus they should have an EEZ around it, but some neighboring countries claim it is an <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atoll</a> and thus should not have an EEZ.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>: See <a href="/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea" title="Territorial disputes in the South China Sea">Territorial disputes in the South China Sea</a>. Some countries [like Japan, India, the United States, an arbitral tribunal constituted under Annex VII to the 1982 United Nations Convention on Law of the Sea, and the People's Republic of China, which opposed any suggestion that coastal States could be obliged to share the resources of the exclusive economic zone with other powers that had historically fished in those waters during the Third Conference of the United Nations on the Law of the Seas.] consider (at least part of) the South China Sea as international waters, but this viewpoint is not universal. Notably, China, which opposes any suggestion that coastal States could be obliged to share the resources of the exclusive economic zone with other powers that had historically fished there, claims historical rights to the resources of the exclusive economic zones of all other coastal States in the South China Sea. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In addition to formal disputes, the government of Somalia exercises little control <i>de facto</i> over Somali territorial waters. Consequently, much <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piracy_in_Somalia" title="Piracy in Somalia">piracy</a>, illegal dumping of waste and fishing without permit has occurred.</p>
<p>Although water is often seen as a source of conflict, recent research suggests that water management can be a source for cooperation between countries. Such cooperation will benefit participating countries by being the catalyst for larger socio-economic development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For instance, the countries of the Senegal River Basin that cooperate through the Organisation pour la Mise en Valeur du Fleuve Sénégal (OMVS) have achieved greater socio-economic development and overcome challenges relating to agriculture and other issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_waters_agreements">International waters agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_waters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International waters agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table cellpadding="5" class="wikitable" style="text-align:center;">

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_agreements">Regional agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_waters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regional agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barcelona_Convention.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="1160" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Barcelona_Convention.png/220px-Barcelona_Convention.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Barcelona_Convention.png/330px-Barcelona_Convention.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Barcelona_Convention.png/440px-Barcelona_Convention.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barcelona_Convention.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing the parties of the <a href="/wiki/Barcelona_Convention" title="Barcelona Convention">Barcelona Convention</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At least ten conventions are included within the Regional Seas Program of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> including:</p>
<ol>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> Coast of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West</a> and Central Africa;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>the North-East Pacific (Antigua Convention);</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> (<a href="/wiki/Barcelona_Convention" title="Barcelona Convention">Barcelona Convention</a>);</li>
<li>the wider <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> (Cartagena Convention);</li>
<li>the South-East Pacific;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">South Pacific</a> (Nouméa Convention);</li>
<li>the East African seaboard;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Kuwait region (Kuwait Convention);</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a> (Jeddah Convention).</li>
</ol>
<p>Addressing regional freshwater issues is the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a> Convention on the Protection and Use of Transboundary Watercourses and International Lakes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNECE" title="UNECE">UNECE</a>/Helsinki Water Convention)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
