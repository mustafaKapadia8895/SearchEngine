<p><b>Lyman Frank Baum</b> (May 15, 1856 – May 6, 1919), better known as <b>L. Frank Baum</b>, was an American author chiefly famous for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_books" title="Children's books">children's books</a>, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> and its sequels. He wrote a total of 14 novels in the <i>Oz</i> series, plus 41 other novels, 83 short stories, over 200 poems, and at least 42 scripts. He made numerous attempts to bring his works to the stage and the nascent medium of film; the <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">1939 adaptation of the first <i>Oz</i> book</a> would become a landmark of 20th century cinema. His works anticipated such century-later commonplaces as <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptop</a> computers (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Master_Key_(novel)" title="The Master Key (novel)">The Master Key</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_telephone" title="Wireless telephone">wireless telephones</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i>), women in high-risk and action-heavy occupations (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Louise_in_the_Country" title="Mary Louise in the Country">Mary Louise in the Country</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Police_corruption" title="Police corruption">police corruption</a> and <a href="/wiki/False_evidence" title="False evidence">false evidence</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Phoebe_Daring" title="Phoebe Daring">Phoebe Daring</a></i>), and the ubiquity of advertising on clothing (<i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jane%27s_Nieces_at_Work" title="Aunt Jane's Nieces at Work">Aunt Jane's Nieces at Work</a></i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood_and_early_life">Childhood and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baum was born in <a href="/wiki/Chittenango,_New_York" title="Chittenango, New York">Chittenango, New York</a> in 1856 into a devout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> family. He had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American" title="German American">German</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scots-Irish_American" title="Scots-Irish American">Scots-Irish</a>, and English ancestry, and was the seventh of nine children of Cynthia Ann (née Stanton) and Benjamin Ward Baum, only five of whom survived into adulthood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-google-2">[2]</a></sup> "Lyman" was the name of his father's brother, but he always disliked it and preferred his middle name "Frank".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Benjamin Baum succeeded in many businesses, including barrel-making, oil drilling in Pennsylvania, and real estate. L. Frank Baum grew up on his parents' expansive estate called Rose Lawn, which he fondly recalled as a sort of paradise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Rose Lawn was located in <a href="/wiki/Mattydale,_New_York" title="Mattydale, New York">Mattydale, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Frank was a sickly, dreamy child, tutored at home with his siblings. From the age of 12, he spent two miserable years at <a href="/wiki/Peekskill_Military_Academy" title="Peekskill Military Academy">Peekskill Military Academy</a> but, after being severely disciplined for daydreaming, he had a possibly <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Psychogenic_heart_attack&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Psychogenic heart attack (page does not exist)">psychogenic heart attack</a> and was allowed to return home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baum started writing early in life, possibly prompted by his father buying him a cheap <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>. He had always been close to his younger brother Henry (Harry) Clay Baum, who helped in the production of <i>The Rose Lawn Home Journal</i>. The brothers published several issues of the journal, which included advertisements; they may have sold issues<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. By the age of 17, Baum established a second amateur journal called <i>The Stamp Collector</i>, printed an 11-page pamphlet called <i>Baum's Complete Stamp Dealers' Directory</i>, and started a <a href="/wiki/Stamp_dealer" title="Stamp dealer">stamp dealership</a> with friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 20, Baum took on the national craze of breeding fancy poultry. He specialized in raising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamburg_(chicken)" title="Hamburg (chicken)">Hamburg</a>. In March 1880, he established a monthly trade journal, <i>The Poultry Record</i>, and in 1886, when Baum was 30 years old, his first book was published: <i>The Book of the Hamburgs: A Brief Treatise upon the Mating, Rearing, and Management of the Different Varieties of Hamburgs</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baum had a flair for being the spotlight of fun in the household, including during times of financial difficulties. His selling of fireworks made the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_of_July" title="Fourth of July">Fourth of July</a> memorable. His skyrockets, Roman candles, and fireworks filled the sky, while many people around the neighborhood would gather in front of the house to watch the displays. Christmas was even more festive. Baum dressed as <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">Santa Claus</a> for the family. His father would place the Christmas tree behind a curtain in the front parlor so that Baum could talk to everyone while he decorated the tree without people managing to see him. He maintained this tradition all his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theater">Theater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baum embarked on his lifetime infatuation—and wavering financial success—with the theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A local theatrical company duped him into replenishing their stock of costumes on the promise of leading roles coming his way. Disillusioned, Baum left the theater — temporarily — and went to work as a clerk in his brother-in-law's dry goods company in <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_New_York" title="Syracuse, New York">Syracuse</a>. This experience may have influenced his story "The Suicide of Kiaros", first published in the literary journal <i>The White Elephant</i>. A fellow clerk one day was found locked in a store room dead, probably from suicide.</p>
<p>Baum could never stay away long from the stage. He performed in plays under the stage names of Louis F. Baum and George Brooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1880, his father built him a theater in <a href="/wiki/Richburg,_New_York" title="Richburg, New York">Richburg, New York</a>, and Baum set about writing plays and gathering a company to act in them. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maid_of_Arran" title="The Maid of Arran">The Maid of Arran</a></i> proved a modest success, a <a href="/wiki/Melodrama" title="Melodrama">melodrama</a> with songs based on <a href="/wiki/William_Black_(novelist)" title="William Black (novelist)">William Black</a>'s novel <i>A Princess of Thule</i>. Baum wrote the play and composed songs for it (making it a prototypical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_play" title="Musical play">musical</a>, as its songs relate to the narrative), and acted in the leading role. His aunt Katharine Gray played his character's aunt. She was the founder of Syracuse Oratory School, and Baum advertised his services in her catalog to teach theater, including stage business, play writing, directing, translating (French, German, and Italian), revision, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operettas" title="Operettas">operettas</a>.</p>
<p>On November 9, 1882, Baum married <a href="/wiki/Maud_Gage_Baum" title="Maud Gage Baum">Maud Gage</a>, a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Matilda_Joslyn_Gage" title="Matilda Joslyn Gage">Matilda Joslyn Gage</a>, a famous <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">women's suffrage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_wave_feminism" title="First wave feminism">feminist</a> activist. While Baum was touring with <i>The Maid of Arran</i>, the theater in Richburg caught fire during a production of Baum's ironically titled parlor drama <i>Matches</i>, destroying the theater as well as the only known copies of many of Baum's scripts, including <i>Matches</i>, as well as costumes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_South_Dakota_years">The South Dakota years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The South Dakota years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 1888, Baum and his wife moved to <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_South_Dakota" title="Aberdeen, South Dakota">Aberdeen, Dakota Territory</a> where he opened a store called "Baum's Bazaar". His habit of giving out wares on credit led to the eventual bankrupting of the store,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> so Baum turned to editing the local newspaper <i>The Aberdeen Saturday Pioneer</i> where he wrote the column <i>Our Landlady</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Following the death of <a href="/wiki/Sitting_Bull" title="Sitting Bull">Sitting Bull</a> at the hands of Indian agency police, Baum urged the wholesale extermination of all America's native peoples in a column that he wrote on December 20, 1890.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On January 3, 1891 he returned to the subject in an editorial response to the <a href="/wiki/Wounded_Knee_Massacre" title="Wounded Knee Massacre">Wounded Knee Massacre</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>The Pioneer has before declared that our only safety depends upon the total extirmination  [</i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a><i>] of the Indians. Having wronged them for centuries, we had better, in order to protect our civilization, follow it up by one more wrong and wipe these untamed and untamable creatures from the face of the earth.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A recent analysis of these editorials has challenged their literal interpretation, suggesting that the actual intent of Baum was to generate sympathy for the Indians via obnoxious argument, ostensibly promoting the contrary position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baum's description of <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> is based on his experiences in drought-ridden <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>. During much of this time, <a href="/wiki/Matilda_Joslyn_Gage" title="Matilda Joslyn Gage">Matilda Joslyn Gage</a> was living in the Baum household. While Baum was in South Dakota, he sang in a quartet which included <a href="/wiki/James_H._Kyle" title="James H. Kyle">James Kyle</a>, who became one of the first <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">Populist</a> (People's Party) Senators in the U.S.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Baum's newspaper failed in 1891, and he, Maud, and their four sons moved to the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Park,_Chicago" title="Humboldt Park, Chicago">Humboldt Park</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, where Baum took a job reporting for the <i>Evening Post</i>. Beginning in 1897, he founded and edited a magazine called <i>The Show Window</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">[20]</a></sup> later known as the <i>Merchants Record and Show Window</i>, which focused on store window displays, retail strategies and visual merchandising. The major department stores of the time created elaborate Christmastime fantasies, using clockwork mechanisms that made people and animals appear to move. The former <i>Show Window</i> magazine is still currently in operation, now known as <i>VMSD</i> magazine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">[20]</a></sup> (visual merchandising + store design), based in Cincinnati.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1900, Baum published a book about window displays in which he stressed the importance of mannequins in drawing customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-22">[22]</a></sup> He also had to work as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vendor_(supply_chain)" title="Vendor (supply chain)">traveling salesman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1897, he wrote and published <i><a href="/wiki/Mother_Goose_in_Prose" title="Mother Goose in Prose">Mother Goose in Prose</a></i>, a collection of <a href="/wiki/Mother_Goose" title="Mother Goose">Mother Goose</a> rhymes written as prose stories and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Maxfield_Parrish" title="Maxfield Parrish">Maxfield Parrish</a>. <i>Mother Goose</i> was a moderate success and allowed Baum to quit his sales job (which had had a negative impact on his health). In 1899, Baum partnered with illustrator <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_Denslow" title="William Wallace Denslow">W.W. Denslow</a> to publish <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Father_Goose,_His_Book" title="Father Goose, His Book">Father Goose, His Book</a></i>, a collection of nonsense poetry. The book was a success, becoming the best-selling children's book of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz"><i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></div>
<p>In 1900, Baum and Denslow (with whom he shared the copyright) published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> to much critical acclaim and financial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The book was the best-selling children's book for two years after its initial publication. Baum went on to write thirteen more novels based on the places and people of the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Oz" title="Land of Oz">Land of Oz</a>.</p>
<h4><span id="The_Wizard_of_Oz:_Fred_R._Hamlin's_Musical_Extravaganza"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Wizard_of_Oz:_Fred_R._Hamlin.27s_Musical_Extravaganza"><i>The Wizard of Oz: Fred R. Hamlin's Musical Extravaganza</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Wizard of Oz: Fred R. Hamlin's Musical Extravaganza">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2662" data-file-width="1772" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg/170px-Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg/255px-Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg/340px-Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tin-Man-poster-Hamlin.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1903 poster of Dave Montgomery as the Tin Man in Hamlin's musical stage version.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Two years after <i>Wizard'</i>s publication, Baum and Denslow teamed up with composer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Tietjens" title="Paul Tietjens">Paul Tietjens</a> and director Julian Mitchell to produce a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_stage_play)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1902 stage play)">musical stage version</a> of the book under Fred R. Hamlin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Baum and Tietjens had worked on a musical of <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> in 1901 and based closely upon the book, but it was rejected. This stage version opened in Chicago in 1902 (the first to use the shortened title "The Wizard of Oz"), then ran on Broadway for 293 stage nights from January to October 1903. It returned to Broadway in 1904, where it played from March to May and again from November to December. It successfully toured the United States with much of the same cast, as was done in those days, until 1911, and then became available for amateur use. The stage version starred Anna Laughlin as <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a>, alongside David C. Montgomery and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Stone" title="Fred Stone">Fred Stone</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">Tin Woodman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scarecrow_(Oz)" title="Scarecrow (Oz)">Scarecrow</a> respectively, which shot the pair to instant fame.</p>
<p>The stage version differed quite a bit from the book, and was aimed primarily at adults. Toto was replaced with Imogene the Cow, and Tryxie Tryfle (a waitress) and <a href="/wiki/Pastoria" title="Pastoria">Pastoria</a> (a streetcar operator) were added as fellow cyclone victims. The Wicked Witch of the West was eliminated entirely in the script, and the plot became about how the four friends were allied with the usurping Wizard and were hunted as traitors to Pastoria II, the rightful King of Oz. It is unclear how much control or influence Baum had on the script; it appears that many of the changes were written by Baum against his wishes due to contractual requirements with Hamlin. Jokes in the script, mostly written by Glen MacDonough, called for explicit references to President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hanna" title="Mark Hanna">Mark Hanna</a>, Rev. Andrew Danquer, and oil magnate <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a>. Although use of the script was rather free-form, the line about Hanna was ordered dropped as soon as Hamlin got word of his death in 1904.</p>
<p>Beginning with the success of the stage version, most subsequent versions of the story, including newer editions of the novel, have been titled "The Wizard of Oz", rather than using the full, original title. In more recent years, restoring the full title has become increasingly common, particularly to distinguish the novel from the <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood film</a>.</p>
<p>Baum wrote a new Oz book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i>, with a view to making it into a stage production, which was titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Woggle-Bug_(musical)" title="The Woggle-Bug (musical)">The Woggle-Bug</a></i>, but Montgomery and Stone balked at appearing when the original was still running. The Scarecrow and Tin Woodman were then omitted from this adaptation, which was seen as a self-rip-off by critics and proved to be a major flop before it could reach Broadway. He also worked for years on a musical version of <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>, which eventually became <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Tik-Tok_Man_Of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Tik-Tok Man Of Oz (page does not exist)">The Tik-Tok Man Of Oz</a></i>. This did fairly well in Los Angeles, but not well enough to convince producer <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Morosco" title="Oliver Morosco">Oliver Morosco</a> to mount a production in New York. He also began a stage version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Patchwork_Girl_of_Oz" title="The Patchwork Girl of Oz">The Patchwork Girl of Oz</a></i>, but this was ultimately realized as a <a href="/wiki/The_Patchwork_Girl_of_Oz_(1914_film)" title="The Patchwork Girl of Oz (1914 film)">film</a><i>.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_work">Later life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Later life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the success of <i>Wizard</i> on page and stage, Baum and Denslow hoped for further success and published <i><a href="/wiki/Dot_and_Tot_of_Merryland" title="Dot and Tot of Merryland">Dot and Tot of Merryland</a></i> in 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The book was one of Baum's weakest, and its failure further strained his faltering relationship with Denslow. It was their last collaboration. Baum worked primarily with <a href="/wiki/John_R._Neill" title="John R. Neill">John R. Neill</a> on his fantasy work beginning in 1904, but Baum met Neill few times (all before he moved to California) and often found Neill's art not humorous enough for his liking. He was particularly offended when Neill published <i>The Oz Toy Book: Cut-outs for the Kiddies</i> without authorization.</p>
<p>Several times during the development of the Oz series, Baum declared that he had written his last Oz book and devoted himself to other works of fantasy fiction based in other magical lands, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Adventures_of_Santa_Claus" title="The Life and Adventures of Santa Claus">The Life and Adventures of Santa Claus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Zixi_of_Ix" title="Queen Zixi of Ix">Queen Zixi of Ix</a></i>. However, he returned to the series each time, persuaded by popular demand, letters from children, and the failure of his new books. Even so, his other works remained very popular after his death, with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Master_Key_(novel)" title="The Master Key (novel)">The Master Key</a></i> appearing on <i><a href="/wiki/St._Nicholas_Magazine" title="St. Nicholas Magazine">St. Nicholas Magazine</a>'</i>s survey of readers' favorite books well into the 1920s.</p>
<p>In 1905, Baum declared plans for an Oz amusement park. In an interview, he mentioned buying Pedloe Island off the coast of California to turn it into an Oz park. However, there is no evidence that he purchased such an island, and no one has ever been able to find any island whose name even resembles Pedloe in that area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thewizardofoz_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-thewizardofoz-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandiegohistory_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-sandiegohistory-29">[29]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Baum stated to the press that he had discovered a Pedloe Island off the coast of California and that he had purchased it to be "the Marvelous Land of Oz," intending it to be "a fairy paradise for children." Eleven year old Dorothy Talbot of San Francisco was reported to be ascendant to the throne on March 1, 1906, when the Palace of Oz was expected to be completed. Baum planned to live on the island, with administrative duties handled by the princess and her all-child advisers. Plans included statues of the Scarecrow, Tin Woodman, Jack Pumpkinhead, and <a href="/wiki/Woggle-Bug" title="Woggle-Bug">H.M. Woggle-Bug, T.E.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Baum abandoned his Oz park project after the failure of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Woggle-Bug_(musical)" title="The Woggle-Bug (musical)">The Woggle-Bug</a></i>, which was playing at the Garrick Theatre in 1905.</p>
<p>Because of his lifelong love of theatre, he financed elaborate musicals, often to his financial detriment. One of Baum's worst financial endeavors was his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fairylogue_and_Radio-Plays" title="The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays">The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays</a></i> (1908), which combined a slideshow, film, and live actors with a lecture by Baum as if he were giving a <a href="/wiki/Travel_literature" title="Travel literature">travelogue</a> to Oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, Baum ran into trouble and could not pay his debts to the company who produced the films. He did not get back to a stable financial situation for several years, after he sold the royalty rights to many of his earlier works, including <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>. This resulted in the M.A. Donahue Company publishing cheap editions of his early works with advertising which purported that Baum's newer output was inferior to the less expensive books that they were releasing. Baum had shrewdly transferred most of his property into Maud's name, except for his clothing, his typewriter, and his library (mostly of children's books, such as the fairy tales of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lang" title="Andrew Lang">Andrew Lang</a>, whose portrait he kept in his study)—all of which, he successfully argued, were essential to his occupation. Maud handled the finances anyway, and thus Baum lost much less than he could have.</p>
<p>Baum made use of several pseudonyms for some of his other non-Oz books. They include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Edith Van Dyne (the <i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jane%27s_Nieces" title="Aunt Jane's Nieces">Aunt Jane's Nieces</a></i> series)</li>
<li>Laura Bancroft (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Twinkle_Tales" title="The Twinkle Tales">The Twinkle Tales</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Policeman_Bluejay" title="Policeman Bluejay">Policeman Bluejay</a></i>)</li>
<li>Floyd Akers (<i>The Boy Fortune Hunters</i> series, continuing the <i>Sam Steele</i> series)</li>
<li>Suzanne Metcalf (<i>Annabel</i>)</li>
<li>Schuyler Staunton (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Fate_of_a_Crown" title="The Fate of a Crown">The Fate of a Crown</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Daughters_of_Destiny_(novel)" title="Daughters of Destiny (novel)">Daughters of Destiny</a></i>)</li>
<li>John Estes Cooke (<i>Tamawaca Folks</i>)</li>
<li>Capt. Hugh Fitzgerald (the <i>Sam Steele</i> series)</li>
</ul>
<p>Baum also anonymously wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Egyptian" title="The Last Egyptian">The Last Egyptian</a>: A Romance of the Nile</i>. He continued theatrical work with Harry Marston Haldeman's <a href="/wiki/Gentlemen%27s_club" title="Gentlemen's club">men's social group</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Uplifters" title="The Uplifters">The Uplifters</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> for which he wrote several plays for various celebrations. He also wrote the group's parodic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/By-laws" title="By-laws">by-laws</a>. The group also included <a href="/wiki/Will_Rogers" title="Will Rogers">Will Rogers</a>, but was proud to have had Baum as a member and posthumously revived many of his works despite their ephemeral intent. Many of these play's titles are known, but only <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Uplift_of_Lucifer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Uplift of Lucifer (page does not exist)">The Uplift of Lucifer</a></i> is known to survive (it was published in a limited edition in the 1960s). Prior to that, his last produced play was <i>The Tik-Tok Man of Oz</i> (based on <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i> and the basis for <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i>), a modest success in Hollywood that producer <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Morosco" title="Oliver Morosco">Oliver Morosco</a> decided did not do well enough to take to Broadway. Morosco, incidentally, quickly turned to film production, as did Baum.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale,_California.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale%2C_California.JPG/220px-L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale%2C_California.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale%2C_California.JPG/330px-L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale%2C_California.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale%2C_California.JPG/440px-L._Frank_Baum_grave_at_Forest_Lawn_Cemetery_in_Glendale%2C_California.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>In 1914, Baum started his own film production company <a href="/wiki/The_Oz_Film_Manufacturing_Company" title="The Oz Film Manufacturing Company">The Oz Film Manufacturing Company</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> which came as an outgrowth of the Uplifters. He served as its president and principal producer and screenwriter. The rest of the board consisted of <a href="/wiki/Louis_F._Gottschalk" title="Louis F. Gottschalk">Louis F. Gottschalk</a>, Harry Marston Haldeman, and Clarence R. Rundel. The films were directed by <a href="/wiki/J._Farrell_MacDonald" title="J. Farrell MacDonald">J. Farrell MacDonald</a>, with casts that included <a href="/wiki/Violet_MacMillan" title="Violet MacMillan">Violet MacMillan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Reed_(silent_film_actress)" title="Vivian Reed (silent film actress)">Vivian Reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mildred_Harris" title="Mildred Harris">Mildred Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juanita_Hansen" title="Juanita Hansen">Juanita Hansen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Couderc" title="Pierre Couderc">Pierre Couderc</a>, Mai Welles, Louise Emmons, <a href="/wiki/J._Charles_Haydon" title="J. Charles Haydon">J. Charles Haydon</a>, and early appearances by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Lloyd" title="Harold Lloyd">Harold Lloyd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hal_Roach" title="Hal Roach">Hal Roach</a>. Silent film actor Richard Rosson appeared in one of the films, whose younger brother <a href="/wiki/Harold_Rosson" title="Harold Rosson">Harold Rosson</a> <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">photographed</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i> (1939). After little success probing the unrealized children's film market, Baum acknowledged his authorship of <i>The Last Egyptian</i> and made a film of it (portions of which are included in <i><a href="/wiki/Decasia" title="Decasia">Decasia</a></i>), but the Oz name had become box office poison for the time being, and even a name change to <a href="/wiki/Dramatic_Feature_Films" title="Dramatic Feature Films">Dramatic Feature Films</a> and transfer of ownership to <a href="/wiki/Frank_Joslyn_Baum" title="Frank Joslyn Baum">Frank Joslyn Baum</a> did not help. Baum invested none of his own money in the venture, unlike <i>The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays</i>, but the stress probably took its toll on his health.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 5, 1919, Baum suffered a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>. The following day he slipped into a coma but briefly awoke and spoke his last words to his wife, "Now we can cross the <a href="/wiki/Deadly_Desert" title="Deadly Desert">Shifting Sands</a>." Frank died on May 6, 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He was buried in Glendale's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_Lawn_Memorial_Park,_Glendale" title="Forest Lawn Memorial Park, Glendale">Forest Lawn Memorial Park Cemetery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>His final Oz book, <i><a href="/wiki/Glinda_of_Oz" title="Glinda of Oz">Glinda of Oz</a></i>, was published on July 10, 1920, a year after his death. The Oz series was continued long after his death by other authors, notably <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Plumly_Thompson" title="Ruth Plumly Thompson">Ruth Plumly Thompson</a>, who wrote an additional nineteen Oz books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RuthPlumlyThompson_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-RuthPlumlyThompson-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Baum's_beliefs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Baum.27s_beliefs">Baum's beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Baum's beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary">Literary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Literary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baum's avowed intentions with the Oz books and other fairy tales was to retell tales such as are found in the works of the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a>, make them in an American vein, update them, avoid stereotypical characters such as dwarfs or genies, and remove the association of violence and moral teachings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The first books contained a fair amount of violence, but it decreased with the series; in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Ozma</a> objected to doing violence even to the Nomes who threaten Oz with invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> His introduction is often cited as the beginnings of the sanitization of children's stories, although he did not do a great deal more than eliminate harsh moral lessons.</p>
<p>Another traditional element that Baum intentionally omitted was the emphasis on romance. He considered romantic love to be uninteresting for young children, as well as largely incomprehensible. In <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, the only element of romance lay in the background of the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">Tin Woodman</a> and his love for <a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_characters#Nimmie_Amee" title="List of Oz characters">Nimmie Amee</a>, which explains his condition and does not otherwise affect the tale, and that of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_characters#Gayelette" title="List of Oz characters">Gayelette</a> and the enchantment of the <a href="/wiki/Winged_monkeys" title="Winged monkeys">Winged monkeys</a>. The only other stories with such elements were <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scarecrow_of_Oz" title="The Scarecrow of Oz">The Scarecrow of Oz</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i>, both based on dramatizations, which Baum regarded warily until his readers accepted them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political">Political</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Women's_suffrage_advocate"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_suffrage_advocate">Women's suffrage advocate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Women's suffrage advocate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sally Roesch Wagner of The <a href="/wiki/Matilda_Joslyn_Gage" title="Matilda Joslyn Gage">Matilda Joslyn Gage</a> Foundation has published a pamphlet titled <i>The Wonderful Mother of Oz</i> describing how Matilda Gage's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> politics were sympathetically channeled by Baum into his Oz books. Much of the politics in the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <i>Aberdeen Saturday Pioneer</i> dealt with trying to convince the populace to vote for <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">women's suffrage</a>. Baum was the secretary of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_South_Dakota" title="Aberdeen, South Dakota">Aberdeen</a>'s Woman's Suffrage Club. <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a> visited Aberdeen and stayed with the Baums. Nancy Tystad Koupal notes an apparent loss of interest in editorializing after Aberdeen failed to pass the bill for women's enfranchisement.</p>
<p>Some of Baum's contacts with suffragists of his day seem to have inspired much of his second Oz story <i>The Marvelous Land of Oz</i>. In this story, General <a href="/wiki/Jinjur" title="Jinjur">Jinjur</a> leads the girls and women of Oz in a revolt, armed with knitting needles; they succeed and make the men do the household chores. Jinjur proves to be an incompetent ruler, but <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">a female</a> advocating <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> is ultimately placed on the throne. His Edith Van Dyne stories depict girls and young women engaging in traditionally masculine activities, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jane%27s_Nieces" title="Aunt Jane's Nieces">Aunt Jane's Nieces</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Flying_Girl" title="The Flying Girl">The Flying Girl</a></i> and its sequel, and his girl sleuth Josie O'Gorman from <a href="/wiki/The_Bluebird_Books" title="The Bluebird Books">The Bluebird Books</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Editorials_about_Native_Americans">Editorials about Native Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Editorials about Native Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the period surrounding the 1890 <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Dance" title="Ghost Dance">Ghost Dance</a> movement and <a href="/wiki/Wounded_Knee_Massacre" title="Wounded Knee Massacre">Wounded Knee Massacre</a>, Baum wrote two editorials about Native Americans for the <i>Aberdeen Saturday Pioneer</i> which have provoked controversy in recent times because of his assertion that the safety of White settlers depended on the wholesale <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">American Indians</a>. Sociologist Robert Venables has argued that Baum was not using sarcasm in the editorials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first piece was published on December 20, 1890, five days after the killing of the <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a> <a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">Sioux</a> <a href="/wiki/Medicine_man" title="Medicine man">holy man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sitting_Bull" title="Sitting Bull">Sitting Bull</a> (who was being held in custody at the time). Following is the complete text of the editorial:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Sitting Bull, most renowned Sioux of modern history, is dead.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>He was not a Chief, but without Kingly lineage he arose from a lowly position to the greatest Medicine Man of his time, by virtue of his shrewdness and daring.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>He was an Indian with a white man's spirit of hatred and revenge for those who had wronged him and his. In his day he saw his son and his tribe gradually driven from their possessions: forced to give up their old hunting grounds and espouse the hard working and uncongenial avocations of the whites. And these, his conquerors, were marked in their dealings with his people by selfishness, falsehood and treachery. What wonder that his wild nature, untamed by years of subjection, should still revolt? What wonder that a fiery rage still burned within his breast and that he should seek every opportunity of obtaining vengeance upon his natural enemies.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>The proud spirit of the original owners of these vast prairies inherited through centuries of fierce and bloody wars for their possession, lingered last in the bosom of Sitting Bull. With his fall the nobility of the <a href="/wiki/Redskin_(slang)" title="Redskin (slang)">Redskin</a> is extinguished, and what few are left are a pack of whining curs who lick the hand that smites them. The Whites, by law of conquest, by justice of civilization, are masters of the American continent, and the best safety of the frontier settlements will be secured by the total annihilation of the few remaining Indians. Why not annihilation? Their glory has fled, their spirit broken, their manhood effaced; better that they die than live the miserable wretches that they are. History would forget these latter despicable beings, and speak, in latter ages of the glory of these grand Kings of forest and plain that <a href="/wiki/James_Fenimore_Cooper" title="James Fenimore Cooper">Cooper</a> loved to heroize.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>We cannot honestly regret their extermination, but we at least do justice to the manly characteristics possessed, according to their lights and education, by the early Redskins of America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-editorials_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-editorials-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Baum wrote a second editorial following the December 29, 1890 massacre and published on January 3, 1891:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>The peculiar policy of the government in employing so weak and vacillating a person as <a href="/wiki/Nelson_A._Miles" title="Nelson A. Miles">General Miles</a> to look after the uneasy Indians, has resulted in a terrible loss of blood to our soldiers, and a battle which, at best, is a disgrace to the war department. There has been plenty of time for prompt and decisive measures, the employment of which would have prevented this disaster.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>The <i>Pioneer</i> has before declared that our only safety depends upon the total extirmination  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] of the Indians. Having wronged them for centuries we had better, in order to protect our civilization, follow it up by one more wrong and wipe these untamed and untamable creatures from the face of the earth. In this lies safety for our settlers and the soldiers who are under incompetent commands. Otherwise, we may expect future years to be as full of trouble with the redskins as those have been in the past.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>An eastern contemporary, with a grain of wisdom in its wit, says that "when the whites win a fight, it is a victory, and when the Indians win it, it is a massacre."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-editorials_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-editorials-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>These two short editorials continue to haunt his legacy. In 2006, two descendants of Baum apologized to the Sioux nation for any hurt that their ancestor had caused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apology_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-apology-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The short story "The Enchanted Buffalo" claims to be a legend of a tribe of bison, and states that a key element made it into legends of Native American tribes. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Father_Goose,_His_Book" title="Father Goose, His Book">Father Goose, His Book</a></i> contains poems such as "There Was a Little <a href="/wiki/Nigger" title="Nigger">Nigger</a> Boy" and "Lee-Hi-Lung-Whan." In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Egyptian" title="The Last Egyptian">The Last Egyptian</a></i>, Lord Roane uses "nigger" to insult the title character, while in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daring_Twins" title="The Daring Twins">The Daring Twins</a></i>, set in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_South" title="American South">American South</a>, the only character to use the term is a boy from Boston complaining that his mother uses their money to help "naked niggers in Africa." Baum mentions his characters' distaste for a <a href="/wiki/Hopi" title="Hopi">Hopi</a> <a href="/wiki/Snake_dance" title="Snake dance">snake dance</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jane%27s_Nieces_and_Uncle_John" title="Aunt Jane's Nieces and Uncle John">Aunt Jane's Nieces and Uncle John</a></i>, but also deplores the horrible situation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Reservation" title="Indian Reservation">Indian Reservations</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Jane%27s_Nieces_on_the_Ranch" title="Aunt Jane's Nieces on the Ranch">Aunt Jane's Nieces on the Ranch</a></i> has a hard-working <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_people" title="Mexican people">Mexican</a> present himself as an exception to reiterate Anglo stereotypes of Mexican laziness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Baum's mother-in-law and Woman's Suffrage leader Matilda Joslyn Gage had great influence over Baum's views. Gage was initiated into the Wolf Clan and admitted into the Iroquois Council of Matrons for her outspoken respect and sympathy for Native American people; it would seem unlikely that Baum could have harbored animosity for them in his mature years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_imagery_in_The_Wizard_of_Oz">Political imagery in <i>The Wizard of Oz</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Political imagery in The Wizard of Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_interpretations_of_The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="Political interpretations of The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">Political interpretations of The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></div>
<p>Numerous political references to the "Wizard" appeared early in the 20th century. Henry Littlefield, an upstate New York high school history teacher, wrote a scholarly article which was the first full-fledged interpretation of the novel as an extended political allegory of the politics and characters of the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Special attention was paid to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Populist_Party_(United_States)" title="Populist Party (United States)">Populist</a> metaphors and debates over silver and gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Baum was a Republican and avid supporter of Women's Suffrage, and it is thought that he personally did not support the political ideals of either the Populist movement of 1890–92 or the <a href="/wiki/William_Jennings_Bryan" title="William Jennings Bryan">Bryanite</a>-silver crusade of 1896–1900. He published a poem in support of <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>.<sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></sup></p>
<p>Since 1964, many scholars, economists, and historians have expanded on Littlefield's interpretation, pointing to multiple similarities between the characters (especially as depicted in Denslow's illustrations) and stock figures from editorial cartoons of the period. Littlefield himself wrote to <i>The New York Times</i> letters to the editor section spelling out that his theory had no basis in fact, but that his original point was "not to label Baum, or to lessen any of his magic, but rather, as a history teacher at Mount Vernon High School, to invest turn-of-the-century America with the imagery and wonder I have always found in his stories."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baum's newspaper had addressed politics in the 1890s, and Denslow was an editorial cartoonist as well as an illustrator of children's books. A series of political references is included in the 1902 stage version, such as references by name to the President, to a powerful senator, and to John D. Rockefeller for providing the oil needed by the Tin Woodman. Scholars have found few political references in Baum's Oz books after 1902.</p>
<p>Baum himself was asked whether his stories had hidden meanings, but he always replied that they were written to "please children".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baum was originally a Methodist, but he joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_in_the_United_States_of_America" title="Episcopal Church in the United States of America">Episcopal Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_South_Dakota" title="Aberdeen, South Dakota">Aberdeen</a> to participate in community theatricals. Later, he and his wife were encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Matilda_Joslyn_Gage" title="Matilda Joslyn Gage">Matilda Joslyn Gage</a> to become members of the <a href="/wiki/Theosophical_Society" title="Theosophical Society">Theosophical Society</a> in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Baum's beliefs are often reflected in his writing. The only mention of a church in his Oz books is the <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> one which the Cowardly Lion breaks in the Dainty China Country in <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>. The Baums sent their older sons to "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethical_Culture" title="Ethical Culture">Ethical Culture</a> Sunday School" in Chicago, which taught morality, not religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture_and_legacy">Popular culture and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Frank_Baum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Popular culture and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conlan_Carter" title="Conlan Carter">Conlan Carter</a> portrayed Baum in "The Wizard of Aberdeen" (1970) episode of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_syndication" title="Television syndication">syndicated</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_Days" title="Death Valley Days">Death Valley Days</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Ritter" title="John Ritter">John Ritter</a> portrayed Baum in the television movie <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dreamer_of_Oz" title="Dreamer of Oz">Dreamer of Oz</a></i> (1990).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">theme park</a> <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_South_Dakota#Storybook_Land" title="Aberdeen, South Dakota">Storybook Land</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_South_Dakota" title="Aberdeen, South Dakota">Aberdeen, South Dakota</a>, features the Land of Oz, with characters and attractions from the books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2013, Baum was inducted into the Chicago Literary Hall of Fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
