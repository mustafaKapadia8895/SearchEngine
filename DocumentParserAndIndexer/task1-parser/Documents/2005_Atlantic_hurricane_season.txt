<p>The <b>2005 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was the most active <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> in <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>, shattering numerous records. The impact of the season was widespread and ruinous with an estimated 3,913 deaths and damage of about $158.9 billion, the second-costliest on record. Of the storms that made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>, five of the season's seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Categories" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">major hurricanes</a>—<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Dennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Emily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Rita</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>—were responsible for most of the destruction. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Stan</a> was the most destructive storm that was not a major hurricane. The <a href="/wiki/States_of_Mexico" title="States of Mexico">Mexican states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_(state)" title="Yucatán (state)">Yucatán</a> and the US states of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> were each struck twice by major hurricanes; <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> were each struck once and in each case brushed by at least one more. The most catastrophic effects of the season were felt on the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast</a>, where a 30 ft (10 m) <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> from Hurricane Katrina caused devastating flooding that destroyed most structures on the Mississippi coastline; subsequent <a href="/wiki/2005_levee_failures_in_Greater_New_Orleans" title="2005 levee failures in Greater New Orleans">levee failures</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans, Louisiana</a> caused by the storm crippled the city. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Hurricane Stan</a> combined with an extratropical system to cause deadly mudslides across Central America, with <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> being hardest-hit.</p>
<p>The 2005 season was the first to observe more tropical storms and hurricanes in the Atlantic than the West Pacific; on average, the latter experiences 26 tropical storms while the Atlantic only averages 12. This event was repeated in the <a href="/wiki/2010_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2010 Atlantic hurricane season">2010 season</a>; however, the <a href="/wiki/2010_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2010 Pacific typhoon season">2010 typhoon season</a> broke the record for the fewest storms observed in a single year, while the 2005 typhoon season featured near average activity.</p>
<p>The season officially began on June 1, 2005, and lasted until November 30, although it effectively persisted into January 2006 due to continued storm activity. A record twenty-eight <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical</a> storms formed, of which a record fifteen became hurricanes. Of these, a record-tying seven strengthened into major hurricanes, a record-tying five became Category 4 hurricanes and a record four reached Category 5 strength, the highest categorization for hurricanes on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. Among these Category 5 storms were hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>, respectively the second costliest and the most intense (by lowest pressure) Atlantic hurricanes on record. This season was also notable because the list of storm names was used up and six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_letter" title="Greek letter">Greek letter</a> names had to be used.</p>

<p>Forecasts of hurricane activity are issued before each hurricane season by noted hurricane expert <a href="/wiki/William_M._Gray" title="William M. Gray">William M. Gray</a> and his associates at <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University</a> (CSU), and separately by forecasters with the U.S. Government's <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA). Prior to and during the 2005 season, Gray issued four forecasts, each time increasing the predicted level of activity. The NOAA issued two forecasts, one shortly before the season and one two months into the season, drastically increasing the predicted level of activity in the second release. Nonetheless, all forecasts fell far short of the actual activity of the season.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preseason_forecasts">Preseason forecasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preseason forecasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 3, 2004, Gray's team issued its first extended-range forecast for the 2005 season, predicting a slightly above-average season. Additionally, the team predicted a greatly increased chance of a major hurricane striking the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> and the Florida peninsula. Though the forecast predicted above-average activity, the level predicted was significantly less than the <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csu-dec2004_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-csu-dec2004-1">[1]</a></sup> On April 1, 2005, after confirming that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a> conditions would not develop, Gray and his team revised the December forecast upward, expecting 13 tropical storms instead of 11 and seven hurricanes instead of six. In addition, the chance of a storm impacting the United States was raised slightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> On May 16, 2005, 16 days before the season began, NOAA issued its outlook for the 2005 season, forecasting a 70% chance of above-normal activity. The <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) value for the season was predicted to be 120–190% of the median ACE of 87.5 × 10<sup>4</sup> <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kt</a><sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, on May 31, the day before the season officially began, Gray's team revised its April forecast upwards to 15 named storms, 8 hurricanes, and 4 major hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midseason_outlook">Midseason outlook</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Midseason outlook">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 2, after an extraordinarily active early season, the NOAA released an updated outlook on the remainder of the season, significantly raising the expected level of activity to numbers about double those of a normal season. The ACE value was now forecast to be 180 to 270% of the median. The NOAA also noted a higher than normal confidence in the forecast of above-normal activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On August 5, 2005, Gray and his associates followed suit and issued their updated forecast; it was consistent with NOAA's update.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Although neither the NOAA nor Gray had ever forecast such high levels of activity, even the midseason outlooks fell far short of the actual level of activity. The actual ACE would prove to be 248 × 10<sup>4</sup> kt<sup>2</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> — 277% of the median.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="June_and_July">June and July</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: June and July">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1199" data-file-width="1919" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg/220px-Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg/330px-Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg/440px-Dennis-7-10-05-1915z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On June 8, nearly two months earlier than when the <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004 season</a> started, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Arlene_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Arlene (2005)">Tropical Storm Arlene</a> formed in the western <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, crossing <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> before making landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> on June 11. Arlene caused only moderate damage, although one swimmer was caught in a <a href="/wiki/Rip_current" title="Rip current">riptide</a> and drowned in <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach,_Florida" title="Miami Beach, Florida">Miami Beach, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArleneTCR_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArleneTCR-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Bret_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Bret (2005)">Tropical Storm Bret</a> formed in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on June 28 and made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> the next morning. The storm damaged hundreds of homes and caused flooding which killed two people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cindy_(2005)" title="Hurricane Cindy (2005)">Hurricane Cindy</a> formed in the Gulf of Mexico on July 4. Originally thought to be a tropical storm, Cindy made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> on July 5 as a minimal hurricane, dropping up to five in (130 mm) of rain, spawning several tornadoes, flooding some coastal areas including Coden, Alabama, and killing three people. Cindy was upgraded to a hurricane in the post-storm analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CindyTCR_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CindyTCR-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Emily.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="4000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Hurricane_Emily.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Emily.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Hurricane_Emily.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Emily.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Hurricane_Emily.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Emily.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Emily.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Satellite photo of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a> near peak intensity</div>

<p>On July 5, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a> formed in the eastern Caribbean; it crossed <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> before intensifying into a Category 4 hurricane, the strongest ever recorded in July with a pressure of 930 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>). Dennis struck <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> at full force, then made a final landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>. The hurricane killed 89 people (mostly in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>) and caused $4–$6 billion (2005 USD, $4.91 billion – $7.36 billion 2017 USD) in damage in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DennisTCR_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DennisTCR-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon thereafter, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a> formed in the Atlantic on July 11. It entered the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and quickly intensified to a Category 4 storm, breaking Dennis's record for July intensity when its pressure reached 929 mbar (hPa). Emily then briefly reached Category 5 intensity—the earliest such storm ever recorded in the Atlantic. Emily crossed the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> at Category 4 strength before hitting <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> at Category 3 strength. Emily killed at least 14 people over the course of its path. An estimated $400 million (2005 USD; $491 million 2017 USD) in damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EmilyTCR_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-EmilyTCR-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Franklin_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Franklin (2005)">Tropical Storm Franklin</a> formed off <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> on July 18. The storm moved northeast and became extratropical off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> without ever having threatened land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Gert_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Gert (2005)">Tropical Storm Gert</a> followed soon after on July 24. Gert struck Veracruz near where Emily had hit a few days before; roughly 1,000 people were evacuated for fear of flooding, but no damage or deaths were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="August">August</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: August">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like July, August also got off to a fast start: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Harvey_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Harvey (2005)">Tropical Storm Harvey</a> formed southwest of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on August 3. Harvey dropped some rain on Bermuda as it moved to the northeast; it became extratropical on August 8 in the open Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tropical depression that would become <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(2005)" title="Hurricane Irene (2005)">Hurricane Irene</a> formed west of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a> on August 4. The system moved west and north and did not reach hurricane strength until August 14, at which point it became the second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> of the season. Irene turned northeast and briefly reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 2</a> status before weakening and becoming extratropical on August 18. It never posed a threat to land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Depression_Ten_(2005)" title="Tropical Depression Ten (2005)">Tropical Depression Ten</a> formed east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> on August 13. The system dissipated the next day. Its remnants soon merged with another system and eventually contributed to the formation of Hurricane Katrina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Jose_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Jose (2005)">Tropical Storm Jose</a> followed, forming in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on August 22. It strengthened rapidly but quickly reached the coast and made landfall in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Mexico" title="States of Mexico">Mexican state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> on August 23, preventing further strengthening. Jose forced 25,000 people to evacuate their homes in Veracruz and killed six people in the state of <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a>; two more were reported missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoseTCR_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-JoseTCR-22">[22]</a></sup> In all, damage in Mexico amounted to $45 million (2005 USD; $55.2 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Josenews_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Josenews-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> formed in mid-August over <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>. It became a tropical storm on August 24 and reached hurricane intensity before making landfall in south Florida as a minimal hurricane. A few hours later, the storm entered the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and intensified rapidly into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 5 hurricane</a> while crossing the <a href="/wiki/Loop_Current" title="Loop Current">Loop Current</a> on August 28. Katrina made landfall shortly after 06:00 CST on August 29 near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> as an extremely large Category 3 hurricane. Category 5-level storm surges (as the storm had weakened only in the previous several hours) caused catastrophic damage along the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>. Within hours, levees separating <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pontchartrain" title="Lake Pontchartrain">Lake Pontchartrain</a> from New Orleans, Louisiana were breached by the surge, ultimately flooding about 80% of the city. Wind damage was reported well inland, impeding relief efforts. Katrina is estimated to be responsible for at least $108 billion (2005 USD; $132 billion in 2017 USD) in damage, breaking <span id="Hurricane_Andrew"></span><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a>'s record in 1992 and making it the costliest natural disaster in U.S. history until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Harvey" title="Hurricane Harvey">Harvey</a> surpassed it in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_Hurricane_Season" title="2017 Atlantic Hurricane Season">2017</a>. It was the deadliest U.S. hurricane since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1928_Okeechobee_Hurricane" title="1928 Okeechobee Hurricane">1928 Okeechobee Hurricane</a>, killing at least 1,836 people. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1900_Galveston_Hurricane" title="1900 Galveston Hurricane">1900 Galveston Hurricane</a> remains the deadliest natural disaster in United States history, with a death toll of approximately 6,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KatrinaTCR_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-KatrinaTCR-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 24, a vigorous <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> developed off the coast of Africa which eventually spawned an area of low pressure. The low gradually became better organized and was designated Tropical Depression Thirteen on August 28, about midway between the Lesser Antilles and Africa. However, northerly wind shear prevented the depression from strengthening and it degenerated into a broad low-pressure area the next day. After traveling rapidly towards the north the low regenerated into a tropical depression and shortly after was declared <a href="/wiki/List_of_storms_in_the_2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Lee" title="List of storms in the 2005 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Lee</a> on August 31. Lee was only briefly a tropical storm before it weakened back to a tropical depression. The depression degenerated into a broad low-pressure area again before being absorbed by a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">cold front</a> on September 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria_(2005)" title="Hurricane Maria (2005)">Hurricane Maria</a> led off the month of September, and formed into a tropical storm well east of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> on September 2. Maria reached its peak as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3 hurricane</a> on September 5, turning northeast and weakening before becoming extratropical on September 10. Unusually, this extratropical storm strengthened as it moved toward <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>; its remnants struck Norway where one person was killed in a landslide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Nate_(2005)" title="Hurricane Nate (2005)">Hurricane Nate</a> formed southwest of Bermuda on September 5 and moved northeast as it strengthened into a strong Category 1 hurricane. Nate became extratropical on September 10; the storm never approached land, although it did interfere with Canadian naval vessels en route to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast</a> to help in Katrina relief efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ophelia_(2005)" title="Hurricane Ophelia (2005)">Hurricane Ophelia</a> formed as a tropical depression in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> on September 6 and almost immediately made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bahama" title="Grand Bahama">Grand Bahama</a>. It became a tropical storm off the coast of Florida before strengthening into a large Category 1 storm and raking a long stretch of the southern <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> coast with heavy winds and storm surge on September 12 and September 13. The hurricane's eye never made landfall and moved back out to sea before becoming extratropical on September 17 and striking <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>. Total damage estimates were around $70 million (2005 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpheliaTCR_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-OpheliaTCR-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Philippe_(2005)" title="Hurricane Philippe (2005)">Hurricane Philippe</a> formed east of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> on September 17. It moved northwards, reaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 1</a> intensity before weakening and dissipating on September 23. No landmasses were affected, although Bermuda's lowest pressure of September was recorded after Philippe passed near the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a> formed as a tropical storm over the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a> on September 18. The storm reached Category 2 intensity as it moved south of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> on September 20. Rapid intensification ensued as Rita moved into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, and Rita became a Category 5 hurricane on September 21, becoming the third (now fourth) most intense hurricane ever recorded in the Atlantic Basin. Rita made landfall near the Texas-<a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> border on September 24. Major flooding was reported in <a href="/wiki/Port_Arthur,_Texas" title="Port Arthur, Texas">Port Arthur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beaumont,_Texas" title="Beaumont, Texas">Beaumont</a>, Texas, while <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Cameron Parish, Louisiana">Cameron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcasieu_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Calcasieu Parish, Louisiana">Calcasieu</a> parishes in Louisiana were devastated. Offshore oil platforms throughout Rita's path also suffered significant damage. Six people are confirmed dead from Rita's direct effects, and total damage from the storm is estimated at about $10 billion (2005 USD; $12.3 billion 2017 USD). One hundred and thirteen indirect deaths were reported, mostly from the exodus from <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas and surrounding counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RitaTCR_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-RitaTCR-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Tropical_Depression_Nineteen"></span><a href="/wiki/List_of_storms_in_the_2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Depression_Nineteen" title="List of storms in the 2005 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Depression Nineteen</a> formed west of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a> on September 30 but dissipated on October 2 without having threatened land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="October">October</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: October">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Hurricane Stan</a> was the first October storm, reaching tropical storm status on October 2 just before crossing the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>, Stan briefly reached hurricane strength before making landfall south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz, Veracruz</a>, on October 4. Stan was a part of a large system of rainstorms, which dropped torrential rainfall that caused catastrophic flooding and mudslides over southern Mexico and Central America. Well over 1,000 total deaths were caused by the flooding, of which 80–100 are directly attributed to Stan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StanTCR_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-StanTCR-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>An initially unnoticed <a href="/wiki/2005_Azores_subtropical_storm" title="2005 Azores subtropical storm">Unnamed Subtropical Storm</a> was discovered by the NHC during the postseason analysis. This short-lived <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical storm</a> formed on October 4 south of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and was absorbed by an extratropical low the next day, after passing over those islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Tammy_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Tammy (2005)">Tropical Storm Tammy</a> led a brief existence before making landfall in northeastern Florida on October 5. Tammy dropped heavy rains over portions of the southeast United States before merging with a frontal system that would eventually cause the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_U.S._flooding_of_October_2005" title="Northeast U.S. flooding of October 2005">Northeast U.S. flooding of October 2005</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Subtropical_Depression_Twenty-two"></span><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_Depression_Twenty-two_(2005)" title="Subtropical Depression Twenty-two (2005)">Subtropical Depression Twenty-two</a> formed southeast of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on October 8. It dissipated the next day, although its remnants approached New England and contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_U.S._flooding_of_October_2005" title="Northeast U.S. flooding of October 2005">Northeast U.S. flooding of October 2005</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwentyTwoTCR_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-TwentyTwoTCR-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Vince_(2005)" title="Hurricane Vince (2005)">Hurricane Vince</a> formed over unfavorably cold water in the East Atlantic near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madeira_Islands" title="Madeira Islands">Madeira Islands</a> on October 8 as a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical storm</a>. This Hurricane was first recorded by the NHC on October 9 when it became tropical, and shortly thereafter, it briefly strengthened into a hurricane. The storm made an even more unusual landfall in Spain on October 11, making it the first tropical cyclone to impact Spain since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1842_Spain_hurricane" title="1842 Spain hurricane">1842 Spain hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VinceTCR_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-VinceTCR-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> formed on October 17 in the western Caribbean southwest of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and rapidly strengthened. On October 19 it became the strongest tropical cyclone on record in the Atlantic basin, with 185 mph (295 km/h) winds and a central pressure of 882 millibars (26.0 inHg). It then moved slowly and struck <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> on October 22 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4 hurricane</a>, causing very heavy damage to <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a>. Likewise, after emerging into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, Wilma sharply changed directions and passed north of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> before striking southern Florida on the 24th as a Category 3 storm, then moving into the Atlantic Ocean and becoming extratropical. Wilma is directly credited with 23 deaths; total damage is estimated at around $29 billion (2005 USD; $35.6 billion 2017 USD), mostly in the United States, Mexico, and Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilmaTCR_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-WilmaTCR-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alpha_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Alpha (2005)">Tropical Storm Alpha</a> formed in the eastern Caribbean on October 22 and crossed <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, causing major flooding before merging with Wilma. A total of 42 people are reported dead from the storm in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Beta_(2005)" title="Hurricane Beta (2005)">Hurricane Beta</a> formed in the southern Caribbean on October 26 and strengthened into a Category 3 hurricane before making landfall in the <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombian</a> islands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Andr%C3%A9s_and_Providencia_Department" title="San Andrés and Providencia Department">San Andrés &amp; Providencia</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on October 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BetaTCR_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-BetaTCR-42">[42]</a></sup> As of 2012, Hurricane Beta was responsible for nine fatalities and more than $15.5 million in damage across four countries.</p>

<p>Tropical activity declined very slowly as the season wound down. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Gamma_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Gamma (2005)">Tropical Storm Gamma</a> initially formed on November 15 in the central Caribbean, and degenerated into a tropical wave before reforming. Although the storm dissipated on November 20 after having made landfall in northern Honduras, rainfall from Gamma caused 41 deaths in <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Delta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Delta (2005)">Tropical Storm Delta</a> formed in the eastern Atlantic on November 23; it approached but never attained hurricane strength. Delta became extratropical on November 28 shortly before striking the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> at full force, causing seven deaths and toppling <a href="/wiki/El_Dedo_de_Dios" title="El Dedo de Dios">El Dedo de Dios</a>, a famous land formation on <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Epsilon" title="Hurricane Epsilon">Hurricane Epsilon</a> formed as a tropical storm on November 29 in a hostile environment in the middle of the Atlantic. It reached hurricane strength on December 2 and defied forecasting by persisting for over a week before dissipating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EpsilonTCR_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-EpsilonTCR-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> became the final storm of the season when it formed on December 30, six hours short of tying the record of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> of 1954 as the latest-forming named storm in a season. Zeta dissipated on January 6, 2006, having become the longest-lived January tropical cyclone in Atlantic basin history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deaths_and_damage">Deaths and damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deaths and damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season_statistics" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season statistics">2005 Atlantic hurricane season statistics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

Damage in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navarre_Beach" title="Navarre Beach">Navarre Beach</a>, Florida from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a></div>

<p>The storms of the season were extraordinarily damaging and were responsible for significant loss of life. Total damage is estimated to be about $159.2 billion (2005 US$), and an estimated 2,067 people were confirmed dead. However, due to uncertainties in the death toll caused by Stan, the death toll from the season could be as high as 4,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hardest-hit area was the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">United States Gulf Coast</a> from eastern Texas to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>. First to strike the area was Hurricane Dennis, which caused $2.23 billion in damage along the Florida Panhandle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DennisTCR_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-DennisTCR-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> caused catastrophic damage to the Gulf Coast, devastating a long stretch of coast along <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> with a 30-foot (9 m) storm surge. Wind damage was reported well inland, slowing down recovery efforts. Storm surge also breached levees in the city of New Orleans, Louisiana, flooding about 80% of the city. Total damage has been estimated at $108 billion, and at least 1,836 people were killed by the storm; Katrina is the second costliest hurricane in U.S. history, surpassing 1992's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a>, and the deadliest hurricane in the U.S. since 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KatrinaTCR_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-KatrinaTCR-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-katreport_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-katreport-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a> struck near the same area, re-flooded New Orleans (though to a far less degree than Katrina), and caused extensive damage along the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and Texas; total damage is estimated at about $12 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dead_2011_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dead_2011-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Arlene_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Arlene (2005)">Tropical Storm Arlene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cindy_(2005)" title="Hurricane Cindy (2005)">Hurricane Cindy</a> also struck the Gulf Coast but caused much lighter damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArleneTCR_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArleneTCR-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CindyTCR_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-CindyTCR-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

Flooding in New Orleans from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Mexican state of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> was also heavily hit, suffering billions of dollars in damage when Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Emily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a> both made landfall between <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a>. Wilma was particularly devastating, lashing the area with major hurricane-force winds for over a full day, and was possibly the most damaging hurricane in Mexican history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilmaTCR_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-WilmaTCR-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wilma caused widespread heavy damage in south Florida, causing $20.6 billion in damage total in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HurricaneHistoryWilma_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-HurricaneHistoryWilma-51">[51]</a></sup> Hurricanes Katrina and Rita had struck the same area earlier, causing lesser (but still significant) damage, and Tropical Storm Arlene killed one person caught in a <a href="/wiki/Rip_current" title="Rip current">rip current</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArleneTCR_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ArleneTCR-9">[9]</a></sup> Hurricane Dennis also brushed the area on its track northward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DennisTCR_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-DennisTCR-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October the remnants of Tropical Storm Tammy and Subtropical Depression Twenty-two met over the Northeastern United States, causing <a href="/wiki/Northeast_U.S._flooding_of_October_2005" title="Northeast U.S. flooding of October 2005">intense flooding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwentyTwoTCR_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-TwentyTwoTCR-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Southeastern <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> suffered some damage from the slow-moving <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ophelia_(2005)" title="Hurricane Ophelia (2005)">Hurricane Ophelia</a>; damage from that storm were originally estimated at $1.6 billion, but finalized at only $70 million. The remainder of the Atlantic coast escaped the major storms, although some regions were affected by the remnants of several storms (including Katrina, Ophelia, Tammy, Subtropical Depression Twenty-two, and Wilma).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpheliaTCR_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-OpheliaTCR-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Northeastern Mexico, including <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a>, was struck repeatedly. Hurricane Emily struck Tamaulipas directly, causing severe damage. Tropical Storms Bret, Gert, and Jose also made landfall in the area but caused minimal damage, although they did cause 12 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoseTCR_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-JoseTCR-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Southern Mexico, along with portions of Central America, suffered heavy flooding and mudslides from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Hurricane Stan</a> and nearby nontropical rains. Over 1,600 people were confirmed dead in total, with some towns completely wiped out, though most of these deaths were not directly related to the hurricane. Central America also suffered flooding from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Gamma_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Gamma (2005)">Tropical Storm Gamma</a> and Hurricane Wilma, and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> was struck directly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Beta_(2005)" title="Hurricane Beta (2005)">Hurricane Beta</a>. Gamma caused $13–18 million (2005 USD) in damage to crops; no damage figures are available for any of the other storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> escaped the worst storms; however, at least 89 people were killed in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> from the effects of Hurricanes Dennis and Wilma and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alpha_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Alpha (2005)">Tropical Storm Alpha</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> were generally spared all the storms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-47">[47]</a></sup> although Dennis and Emily caused significant damage in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> was struck by Hurricane Dennis at peak strength, causing $1.4 billion in damage; it was the worst hurricane to hit Cuba in over 40 years. Some areas of Cuba also suffered heavy damage from Hurricanes Rita and Wilma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DennisTCR_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-DennisTCR-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unusual impacts were felt in Europe and nearby islands from four storms. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria_(2005)" title="Hurricane Maria (2005)">Hurricane Maria</a> intensified and affected northern Europe as a vicious extratropical storm, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Vince_(2005)" title="Hurricane Vince (2005)">Hurricane Vince</a> maintained tropical characteristics onto the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> as a weak tropical depression. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Delta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Delta (2005)">Tropical Storm Delta</a> struck the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> just after becoming extratropical, causing extensive damage before reaching <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> as a weak extratropical system. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> were affected by the <a href="/wiki/2005_Azores_subtropical_storm" title="2005 Azores subtropical storm">unnamed subtropical storm</a> at its peak strength. Eight people were killed by those storms, and significant damage was reported as a result of Maria and Delta, although no figures are available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact">Economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="679" data-file-width="856" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif/220px-Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif/330px-Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif/440px-Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Rita%27s_Path_at_Landfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a> at landfall, along with the location of several refineries</div>
</div>

<p>The damage had far-reaching economic consequences due to vulnerability of both oil extracting and <a href="/wiki/Refining" title="Refining">refining</a> capacity in the Gulf of Mexico, storms led to speculative spikes in the price of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">crude oil</a> whereas, the damage to refinery capacity in the United States caused gasoline to soar to record prices (even adjusted for inflation). Governments in Europe and the United States tapped strategic reserves of gasoline and petroleum, and shortages were reported in the days after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a> in areas heavily dependent on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> for refined gasoline. Even weeks after the storm, prices remained elevated as the shortage in production remained over 1 million barrels (160,000 m<sup>3</sup>) per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KatrinaTCR_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-KatrinaTCR-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Rita</a> damaged wells in the western Gulf of Mexico which were primarily exploratory, leading to concerns that future production would be damped for some time to come. Additionally, as the storm churned in the Gulf, forecasters predicted that it would strike <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas, the location of many major <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a> that survived Katrina, leading to additional spikes in oil prices before the predictions changed. In Georgia, Governor <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Perdue" title="Sonny Perdue">Sonny Perdue</a> declared "<a href="/wiki/Weather-related_cancellation" title="Weather-related cancellation">snow days</a>" on September 26 and September 27, 2005, at all Georgia public schools to conserve fuel for <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">school buses</a> in anticipation of Rita's impact. However, as the storm veered away from Houston shortly before landfall, damage to refining capacity was not as great as feared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RitaTCR_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-RitaTCR-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agriculture in multiple countries was hard hit by extremely heavy rains from severe storms during the season. Early in the season, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a> caused significant damage to various <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> and vegetable crops in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, though the damage was not crippling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DennisTCR_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-DennisTCR-14">[14]</a></sup> In Central America, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Hurricane Stan</a> and associated nontropical storms dropped upwards of 20 inches (500 mm) of rain, causing, in addition to severe flash floods and mud slides, heavy damage to crops, especially to the banana and coffee crops, which were nearly ready to be harvested. This caused significant economic disruption in <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> and surrounding nations, as the rural economies are highly dependent on the coffee and banana crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StanTCR_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-StanTCR-34">[34]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Beta" title="Hurricane Beta">Hurricane Beta</a> struck <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> later in the season, it also caused heavy damage to the banana crop, but the harvests had already ended, mitigating economic disruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BetaTCR_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-BetaTCR-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Katrina also had significant political consequences, as President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> governor <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Blanco" title="Kathleen Blanco">Kathleen Blanco</a>, and New Orleans mayor <a href="/wiki/Ray_Nagin" title="Ray Nagin">Ray Nagin</a> all came under heavy criticism for what were considered sluggish or inappropriate responses to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>. On December 14, 2005, congressional hearings began to investigate whether these claims had any merit. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Brown" title="Michael D. Brown">Michael Brown</a>, head of the United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency_of_the_United_States" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency of the United States">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> (FEMA), was forced to resign from his post after the organization came under fire for what was perceived as an insufficient response to Katrina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katrina_GPO_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katrina_GPO-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting_uncertainty">Forecasting uncertainty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Forecasting uncertainty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A number of storms that formed in 2005 exhibited unusual behavior and challenged forecasters' ability to make correct predictions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Vince_(2005)" title="Hurricane Vince (2005)">Hurricane Vince</a> formed farther northeast in the Atlantic than any other tropical cyclone on record at the time—only <a href="/wiki/2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2009 Atlantic hurricane season">2009</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Grace_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Grace (2009)">Grace</a> has formed farther north—and then unexpectedly reached hurricane strength over waters considered too cold to support a hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VinceTCR_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-VinceTCR-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> became the fastest-intensifying hurricane on record, and later strengthened unexpectedly in the face of strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Delta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Delta (2005)">Tropical Storm Delta</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Epsilon_(2005)" title="Hurricane Epsilon (2005)">Hurricane Epsilon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Zeta (2005)">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> all formed over the cold waters of the late-season eastern Atlantic, much like Hurricane Vince (though at lower latitudes). All three persisted in the face of heavy wind shear, and Epsilon managed to reach hurricane strength over waters well below the temperatures previously thought necessary for hurricane formation. Epsilon became the longest-lasting December hurricane<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EpsilonTCR_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-EpsilonTCR-45">[45]</a></sup> while Zeta became the longest-lasting storm in January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZetaTCR_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZetaTCR-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records_and_notable_events">Records and notable events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Records and notable events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2005 season broke numerous records for tropical cyclone activity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> though it might not actually be the most active season in history, since records before 1944 are incomplete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">During the season 28 storms formed (27 named and one unnamed), surpassing almost all records for storm formation in the Atlantic. More tropical storms, hurricanes, major hurricanes and Category 5 hurricanes formed during the season than in any previously recorded Atlantic season.</span></p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">The season was the first season to use "V" and "W" names, and when the season ran out of official alphabetical names after the use of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>, forecasters resorted to using letters from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> for the first time (although <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alpha_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Alpha (2005)">Alpha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Delta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Delta (2005)">Delta</a> had been used for subtropical storms in the 1970s).</span></p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Almost every storm in 2005 set a record for early formation. Of the twenty-eight storms which formed, twenty-two of them qualified as the earliest-forming storm of that number. Starting with <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a>, almost every storm was such. (The record set by Dennis was later broken by Debby in 2012, which was again broken in 2016 with Danielle.)</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The season's activity was reflected with a very high cumulative <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 250. This total ranks it as the second-most active season on record, with only the 1933 season having a higher ACE value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. ACE is only calculated for full advisories on tropical systems at or exceeding 34 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (39 mph, 63 km/h) or tropical storm strength. Although officially, <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a> are excluded from the total,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> the figure above includes periods when storms were in a subtropical phase.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intense_storms">Intense storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Intense storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Three out of the six most intense Atlantic hurricanes (by central pressure) on record occurred in 2005, topped off by Hurricane Wilma's 882 mbar (26.0 inHg) minimum pressure, shattering the 17-year-old record set by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilmaTCR_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-WilmaTCR-40">[40]</a></sup> Hurricanes Emily, Katrina and Rita also attained Category 5 intensity, and Hurricanes Rita and Katrina became the fourth- and sixth-most intense recorded Atlantic storms, respectively. Hurricane Emily was not originally recorded as a Category 5 storm, but it was upgraded into the post-storm status by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EmilyTCR_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-EmilyTCR-16">[16]</a></sup> The 2005 season is the only season on record with four Category 5 storms on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>; the previous record was only two. In addition, Hurricane Dennis reached Category 4 status, tying the record set by the <a href="/wiki/1999_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1999 Atlantic hurricane season">1999 season</a> with five Category 4 storms.</p>

<p>In July, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a> became the strongest storm to form prior to August and the earliest Category 4 storm to form in the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DennisTCR_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-DennisTCR-14">[14]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">When <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a> reached Category 5 intensity later in the month, the 2005 season became the only season to have two hurricanes reach Category 4 intensity before the end of July; Emily also broke Dennis's nine-day-old record for the strongest storm on record before August. Emily was also the first Category 5 hurricane ever recorded in July and the earliest by nearly three weeks (beating <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Hurricane Allen</a>). The high level of activity and strength was reflected in the <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> value at the end of July; at 63 it was the highest ever.</span></p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Additionally, seven storms formed before the end of July, breaking the record of five set in the <a href="/wiki/1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1887 Atlantic hurricane season">1887</a>, <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a>, <a href="/wiki/1936_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1936 Atlantic hurricane season">1936</a>, <a href="/wiki/1959_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1959 Atlantic hurricane season">1959</a>, <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1966 Atlantic hurricane season">1966</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1995_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1995 Atlantic hurricane season">1995</a> seasons. Five of those storms formed during July, also a new record.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_activity">Late activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Late activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">After forming on November 29, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Epsilon_(2005)" title="Hurricane Epsilon (2005)">Hurricane Epsilon</a> became the longest-lasting December hurricane on record when it maintained hurricane strength from December 2 to December 7. Epsilon is the second-strongest hurricane ever recorded in the month of December; only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Nicole_(1998)" title="Hurricane Nicole (1998)">Hurricane Nicole</a> of 1998 was stronger.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Zeta (2005)">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> formed on December 30, it came second only to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> (also December 30, 1954, but later in the day) as the latest ever that the last storm of the season formed. Zeta also became only the second storm, after Alice, to persist through the end of the year and still be active at the start of the next.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>This was the same list used for the <a href="/wiki/1999_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1999 Atlantic hurricane season">1999 season</a>, with the exceptions of Franklin and Lee, which replaced <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Floyd" title="Hurricane Floyd">Floyd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lenny" title="Hurricane Lenny">Lenny</a>. The names not retired from this list were used again in the <a href="/wiki/2011_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2011 Atlantic hurricane season">2011 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Storms were named Franklin, Lee, Maria, Nate, Ophelia, Philippe, Rita, Stan, Tammy, Vince, Wilma, Beta, Gamma, Epsilon, and Zeta for the first (and only, in the cases of Rita, Stan, and Wilma) time in 2005 (the names Alpha and Delta had been previously used in <a href="/wiki/1972_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1972 Atlantic hurricane season">1972</a> for two <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical storms</a>, but this is the first time they have been used in this way). This season used fifteen previously unused names, the most ever in an Atlantic season. Additionally, a subtropical storm that formed in early October was not recognized as such at the time and so did not receive a name. Vince and Wilma were the first named "<a href="/wiki/V" title="V">V</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/W" title="W">W</a>" storms ever in the Atlantic basin. In the spring of 2006, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> five hurricane names: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Dennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Rita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Stan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>. Their replacements in the 2011 season were Don, Katia, Rina, Sean, and Whitney, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> It was also decided it "was not practical to 'retire into hurricane history' a letter in the Greek Alphabet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> This surpassed the previous record for the number of hurricane names retired after a single season, four (held by the <a href="/wiki/1955_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1955 Atlantic hurricane season">1955</a>, <a href="/wiki/1995_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1995 Atlantic hurricane season">1995</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a> seasons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup></p>
