<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Noah</h1>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a>, <b>Noah</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> was the tenth and last of the <a href="/wiki/Antediluvian" title="Antediluvian">pre-flood</a> <a href="/wiki/Patriarchs_(Bible)" title="Patriarchs (Bible)">Patriarchs</a>. The story of <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">Noah's Ark</a> is told in the Bible's <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_narrative" title="Genesis flood narrative">Genesis flood narrative</a>. The biblical account is followed by the story of the <a href="/wiki/Curse_of_Ham" title="Curse of Ham">Curse of Canaan</a>.</p>
<p>In addition to the Book of Genesis, Noah is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Chronicles" title="Books of Chronicles">First Book of Chronicles</a>, and the books of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Tobit" title="Book of Tobit">Tobit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Wisdom" title="Book of Wisdom">Wisdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sirach" title="Sirach">Sirach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">Isaiah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezekiel" title="Book of Ezekiel">Ezekiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/2_Esdras" title="2 Esdras">2 Esdras</a>, <a href="/wiki/4_Maccabees" title="4 Maccabees">4 Maccabees</a>; in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, he is mentioned in the gospels of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Hebrews" title="Epistle to the Hebrews">Epistle to the Hebrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_of_Peter" title="First Epistle of Peter">1st Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_of_Peter" title="Second Epistle of Peter">2nd Peter</a>. Noah was the subject of much elaboration in the literature of later Abrahamic religions, including the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> (<a href="/wiki/Surah" title="Surah">Surahs</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura_71" title="Sura 71">71</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura_7" title="Sura 7">7</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura_11" title="Sura 11">1</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura_21" title="Sura 21">21</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_account">Biblical account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biblical account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noah_mosaic.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1756" data-file-width="1312" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Noah_mosaic.JPG/220px-Noah_mosaic.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Noah_mosaic.JPG/330px-Noah_mosaic.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Noah_mosaic.JPG/440px-Noah_mosaic.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The primary account of Noah in the Bible is in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>.</p>
<p>Noah was the tenth of the pre-flood (<a href="/wiki/Antediluvian" title="Antediluvian">antediluvian</a>) Patriarchs. His father was <a href="/wiki/Lamech_(father_of_Noah)" title="Lamech (father of Noah)">Lamech</a> and his mother is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> When Noah was five hundred years old, he became the father of <a href="/wiki/Shem" title="Shem">Shem</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ham,_son_of_Noah" title="Ham, son of Noah">Ham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japheth" title="Japheth">Japheth</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Genesis#5:32" title="s:Bible (King James)/Genesis">Genesis 5:32</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genesis_flood_narrative">Genesis flood narrative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genesis flood narrative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_narrative" title="Genesis flood narrative">Genesis flood narrative</a></div>
<p>The Genesis flood narrative makes up chapters 6–9 in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> The narrative, one of many <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">flood myths</a> found in human cultures, indicates that <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">God</a> intended to return the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to its pre-Creation state of watery <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">chaos</a> by flooding the Earth because of humanity's misdeeds and then remake it using the microcosm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noah%27s_ark" title="Noah's ark">Noah's ark</a>. Thus, the flood was no ordinary overflow but a reversal of <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">creation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBandstra200961_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBandstra200961-5">[4]</a></sup> The narrative discusses the evil of mankind that moved God to destroy the world by the way of the flood, the preparation of the ark for certain animals, Noah, and his family, and God's guarantee (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noahic_Covenant" title="Noahic Covenant">Noahic Covenant</a>) for the continued existence of life under the promise that he would never send another flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECotter200349.2C_50_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECotter200349.2C_50-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_flood">After the flood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After the flood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Covenant_(biblical)#Noahic_covenant" title="Covenant (biblical)">Covenant (biblical) § Noahic covenant</a></div>
<p>After the flood, Noah offered burnt offerings to God, who said: "I will not again curse the ground any more for man's sake; for the imagination of man's heart <i>[is]</i> evil from his youth; neither will I again smite any more every thing living, as I have done." (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Genesis#8:20" title="s:Bible (King James)/Genesis">8:20–21</a>)</p>
<p>"And God blessed Noah and his sons, and said unto them, Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth." (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Genesis#9:1" title="s:Bible (King James)/Genesis">9:1</a>) They were also told that all fowls, land animals, and fishes would be afraid of them. Furthermore, as well as green plants, every moving thing would be their food with the exception that the blood was not to be eaten. Man's life blood would be required from the beasts and from man. "Whoso sheddeth man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed: for in the image of God made he man." (9:6) A rainbow, called "my bow", was given as the sign of a covenant "between me and you and every living creature that <i>[is]</i> with you, for perpetual generations", (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Genesis#9:2" title="s:Bible (King James)/Genesis">9:2–17</a>) called the Noahic covenant or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbow_covenant" title="Rainbow covenant">rainbow covenant</a>.</p>
<p>Noah died 350 years after the flood, at the age of 950,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> the last of the extremely long-lived <a href="/wiki/Antediluvian" title="Antediluvian">antediluvian</a> Patriarchs. The maximum human lifespan, as depicted by the Bible, diminishes rapidly thereafter, from almost 1,000 years to the 120 years of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Noah's_drunkenness"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Noah.27s_drunkenness">Noah's drunkenness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Noah's drunkenness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="646" data-file-width="510" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png/220px-Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png/330px-Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png/440px-Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Egerton_Genesis_Noahs_Drunkeness.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Noah's drunkenness, Ham mocks Noah, Noah is covered, Canaan is cursed. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Egerton_Genesis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Egerton Genesis (page does not exist)">Egerton Genesis</a></div>

<p>After the flood, the bible says that Noah became a <a href="/wiki/Husbandman" title="Husbandman">husbandman</a> and he planted a vineyard. He drank wine made from this vinyard, and got drunk; and lay "uncovered" within his tent. Noah's son Ham, the father of Canaan, saw his father naked and told his brothers, which led to Ham's son <a href="/wiki/Curse_of_Ham" title="Curse of Ham">Canaan being cursed</a> by Noah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> As early as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_era" title="Classical era">Classical era</a>, commentators on <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0109.htm#20" rel="nofollow">Genesis 9:20–21</a> have excused Noah's excessive drinking because he was considered to be the first wine drinker; the first person to discover the soothing, consoling, and enlivening<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Tone" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The tone or style of material in the vicinity of this tag may not be appropriate for Wikipedia. (October 2014)">tone</span></a></i>]</sup> effects of wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellen.26Rollins_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellen.26Rollins-10">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Constantinople" title="Archbishop of Constantinople">Archbishop of Constantinople</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_Father" title="Church Father">Church Father</a>, wrote in the 4th Century that Noah's behaviour is defensible: as the first human to taste wine, he would not know its effects: "Through ignorance and inexperience of the proper amount to drink, fell into a drunken stupor".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">Philo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy" title="Hellenistic philosophy">Hellenistic</a> Jewish philosopher, also excused Noah by noting that one can drink in two different manners: (1) to drink wine in excess, a peculiar sin to the vicious evil man or (2) to partake of wine as the wise man, Noah being the latter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_tradition" title="Jewish tradition">Jewish tradition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noah_in_rabbinic_literature#His_lapse" title="Noah in rabbinic literature">rabbinic literature on Noah</a>, rabbis blame <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a> for the intoxicating properties of the wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noah.27s_wine_JewEnyc_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Noah.27s_wine_JewEnyc-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curse_of_Ham">Curse of Ham</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Curse of Ham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Curse_of_Ham" title="Curse of Ham">Curse of Ham</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noah-Curses-Ham.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="406" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Noah-Curses-Ham.jpg/220px-Noah-Curses-Ham.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Noah-Curses-Ham.jpg/330px-Noah-Curses-Ham.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Noah-Curses-Ham.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Psychological_biblical_criticism" title="Psychological biblical criticism">psychological biblical criticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Harold_Ellens" title="J. Harold Ellens">J. H. Ellens</a> and W. G. Rollins address the narrative of <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0109.htm#18" rel="nofollow">Genesis 9:18–27</a> that narrates the unconventional behavior that occurs between Noah and Ham. Because of its brevity and textual inconsistencies, it has been suggested that this narrative is a "splinter from a more substantial tale".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> A fuller account would explain what exactly Ham had done to his father, or why Noah directed a curse at <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a> for Ham's misdeed, or how Noah came to know what occurred. The narrator relates two facts: (1) Noah became drunken and "he was uncovered within his tent", and (2) Ham "saw the nakedness of his father, and told his two brethren without". Thus, these passages revolve around sexuality and the exposure of genitalia as compared with other Hebrew Bible texts, such as <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt2002.htm#15" rel="nofollow">Habakkuk 2:15</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt3204.htm#21" rel="nofollow">Lamentations 4:21</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Table_of_nations">Table of nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Table of nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sons_of_Noah" title="Sons of Noah">Sons of Noah</a></div>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0110.htm#1" rel="nofollow">Genesis 10</a> sets forth the descendants of Shem, Ham, and Japheth, from whom the nations branched out over the earth after the flood. Among Japheth’s descendants were the maritime nations. (10:2–5) Ham’s son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical_Cush" title="Biblical Cush">Cush</a> had a son named <a href="/wiki/Nimrod" title="Nimrod">Nimrod</a>, who became the first man of might on earth, a mighty hunter, king in <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a> and the land of <a href="/wiki/Shinar" title="Shinar">Shinar</a>. (10:6–10) From there <a href="/wiki/Ashur" title="Ashur">Asshur</a> went and built <a href="/wiki/Nineveh" title="Nineveh">Nineveh</a>. (10:11–12) Canaan’s descendants –  Sidon, <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Hittites" title="Biblical Hittites">Heth</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jebusite" title="Jebusite">Jebusites</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorite" title="Amorite">Amorites</a>, the Girgashites, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hivites" title="Hivites">Hivites</a>, the Arkites, the Sinites, the Arvadites, the Zemarites, and the Hamathites –  spread out from <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a> as far as <a href="/wiki/Gerar" title="Gerar">Gerar</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Gaza_City" title="Gaza City">Gaza</a>, and as far as <a href="/wiki/Sodom_and_Gomorrah" title="Sodom and Gomorrah">Sodom and Gomorrah</a>. (10:15–19) Among Shem’s descendants was <a href="/wiki/Eber" title="Eber">Eber</a>. (10:21)</p>
<p>These genealogies differ structurally from those set out in Genesis 5 and 11. It has a segmented or treelike structure, going from one father to many offspring. It is strange that the table, which assumes that the population is distributed about the Earth, precedes the account of the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_Babel" title="Tower of Babel">Tower of Babel</a>, which says that all the population is in one place before it is dispersed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrative_analysis">Narrative analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Narrative analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Documentary_hypothesis" title="Documentary hypothesis">documentary hypothesis</a>, the first five books of the Bible (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentateuch" title="Pentateuch">Pentateuch</a>/<a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>), including Genesis, were collated during the 5th century BC from four main sources, which themselves date from no earlier than the 10th century BC. Two of these, the <a href="/wiki/Jahwist" title="Jahwist">Jahwist</a>, composed in the 10th century BC, and the <a href="/wiki/Priestly_source" title="Priestly source">Priestly source</a>, from the late 7th century BC, make up the chapters of Genesis which concern Noah. The attempt by the 5th-century editor to accommodate two independent and sometimes conflicting sources accounts for the confusion over such matters as how many of each animal Noah took, and how long the flood lasted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Oxford Encyclopedia of the Books of the Bible</i> notes that this story echoes parts of the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a> story: Noah is the first vintner, while Adam is the first farmer; both have problems with their produce; both stories involve nakedness; and both involve a division between brothers leading to a curse. However, after the flood, the stories differ. Noah plants the vineyard and utters the curse, not God, so "God is less involved".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_accounts">Other accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Noah appears in several non-canonical books.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudepigrapha">Pseudepigrapha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pseudepigrapha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jubilees" title="Book of Jubilees">Book of Jubilees</a> refers to Noah and says that he was taught the arts of healing by an angel so that his children could overcome "the offspring of the <a href="/wiki/Watcher_(angel)" title="Watcher (angel)">Watchers</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 10:1–3 of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Enoch" title="Book of Enoch">Book of Enoch</a> (which is part of the <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Tewahedo_biblical_canon" title="Orthodox Tewahedo biblical canon">Orthodox Tewahedo biblical canon</a>), <a href="/wiki/Uriel" title="Uriel">Uriel</a> was dispatched by "the Most High" to inform Noah of the approaching "deluge".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dead_Sea_scrolls">Dead Sea scrolls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Dead Sea scrolls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are 20 or so fragments of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_scrolls" title="Dead Sea scrolls">Dead Sea scrolls</a> that appear to refer to Noah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Schiffman" title="Lawrence Schiffman">Lawrence Schiffman</a> writes, "Among the Dead Sea Scrolls at least three different versions of this legend are preserved."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> In particular, "The <a href="/wiki/Genesis_Apocryphon" title="Genesis Apocryphon">Genesis Apocryphon</a> devotes considerable space to Noah." However, "The material seems to have little in common with Genesis 5 which reports the birth of Noah." Also, Noah's father is reported as worrying that his son was actually fathered by one of the <a href="/wiki/Watcher_(angel)" title="Watcher (angel)">Watchers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_mythology">Comparative mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Comparative mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">Flood myth</a></div>
<p>Indian and Greek flood-myths also exist, although there is little evidence that they were derived from the Mesopotamian flood-myth that underlies the biblical account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesopotamian">Mesopotamian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mesopotamian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Noah story of the Pentateuch is almost identical to a flood story contained in the Mesopotamian <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a>, composed about 2000 BC. The few variations including the number of days of the deluge, the order of the birds, and the name of the mountain on which the ark rests. The flood story in Genesis 6–8 matches the <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh_flood_myth" title="Gilgamesh flood myth">Gilgamesh flood myth</a> so closely that "few doubt that [it] derives from a Mesopotamian account."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George2003_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-George2003-28">[27]</a></sup> What is particularly noticeable is the way the Genesis flood story follows the Gilgamesh flood tale "point by point and in the same order", even when the story permits other alternatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest written <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">flood myth</a> is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian" title="Mesopotamian">Mesopotamian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epic_of_Atrahasis" title="Epic of Atrahasis">Epic of Atrahasis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a> texts. The <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> says "These mythologies are the source of such features of the biblical Flood story as the building and provisioning of the ark, its flotation, and the subsidence of the waters, as well as the part played by the human protagonist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> The <i>Encyclopedia Judaica</i> adds that there is a strong suggestion that "an intermediate agent was active. The people most likely to have fulfilled this role are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurrian" title="Hurrian">Hurrians</a>, whose territory included the city of <a href="/wiki/Haran" title="Haran">Haran</a>, where the Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> had his roots. The Hurrians inherited the Flood story from Babylonia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skolnik287_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skolnik287-31">[30]</a></sup> The encyclopedia mentions another similarity between the stories: Noah is the tenth patriarch and <a href="/wiki/Berossus" title="Berossus">Berossus</a> notes that "the hero of the great flood was Babylonia’s tenth antediluvian king." However, there is a discrepancy in the ages of the heroes. For the Mesopotamian antecedents, "the reigns of the antediluvian kings range from 18,600 to nearly 65,000 years." In the Bible, the lifespans "fall far short of the briefest reign mentioned in the related Mesopotamian texts." Also the name of the hero differs between the traditions: "The earliest Mesopotamian flood account, written in the Sumerian language, calls the deluge hero <a href="/wiki/Ziusudra" title="Ziusudra">Ziusudra</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skolnik287_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Skolnik287-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh" title="Gilgamesh">Gilgamesh</a>’s historical reign is believed to have been approximately 2700 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> shortly before the earliest known written stories. The discovery of artifacts associated with <a href="/wiki/Aga_of_Kish" title="Aga of Kish">Aga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enmebaragesi" title="Enmebaragesi">Enmebaragesi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kish_(Sumer)" title="Kish (Sumer)">Kish</a>, two other kings named in the stories, has lent credibility to the historical existence of Gilgamesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> Gilgamesh poems date from as early as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_dynasty_of_Ur" title="Third dynasty of Ur">Third dynasty of Ur</a> (2100–2000 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> One of these poems mentions Gilgamesh’s journey to meet the flood hero, as well as a short version of the flood story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> The earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> versions of the unified epic are dated to ca. 2000–1500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Due to the fragmentary nature of these Old Babylonian versions, it is unclear whether they included an expanded account of the flood myth; although one fragment definitely includes the story of Gilgamesh’s journey to meet <a href="/wiki/Utnapishtim" title="Utnapishtim">Utnapishtim</a>. The "standard" Akkadian version included a long version of the flood story and was edited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sin-liqe-unninni" title="Sin-liqe-unninni">Sin-liqe-unninni</a> sometime between 1300 and 1000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sumerian">Sumerian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sumerian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Utnapishtim, a character in The Epic of Gilgamesh, tells the story of a flood very similar to that of Noah. In this story, the gods are enraged by the noise that man has raised from the earth. To quiet them they decide to send a great flood to silence mankind. Various correlations between the stories of Noah and Utnapishtim (the flood, the construction of the ark, the salvation of animals, and the release of birds following the flood) have led to this story being seen as the inspiration for the story of Noah. However, his role in Gilgamesh is to provide the secret of everlasting life to the hero, who promptly falls asleep before Utnapishtim gives him the secret of life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greek">Ancient Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ancient Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Noah has often been compared to <a href="/wiki/Deucalion" title="Deucalion">Deucalion</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pronoia" title="Pronoia">Pronoia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>. Like Noah, Deucalion is warned of the flood (by <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a>); he builds an ark and staffs it with creatures – and when he completes his voyage, gives thanks and takes advice from the gods on how to repopulate the Earth. Deucalion also sends a pigeon to find out about the situation of the world and the bird returns with an olive branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Deucalion, in some versions of the myth, also becomes the inventor of wine, like Noah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">Philo</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a> equate Deucalion with Noah, and <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a> used the story of Deucalion as evidence that the flood actually occurred and that, therefore, Noah existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religious views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Noah_in_rabbinic_literature" title="Noah in rabbinic literature">Noah in rabbinic literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noach_(parsha)" title="Noach (parsha)">Noach (parsha)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:207px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ararat_Ms._11639_521a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="301" data-file-width="300" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ararat_Ms._11639_521a.jpg/205px-Ararat_Ms._11639_521a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/23/Ararat_Ms._11639_521a.jpg 1.5x" width="205"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ararat_Ms._11639_521a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Jewish depiction of Noah</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The righteousness of Noah is the subject of much discussion among rabbis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> The description of Noah as "righteous in his generation" implied to some that his perfection was only relative: In his generation of wicked people, he could be considered righteous, but in the generation of a <i><a href="/wiki/Tzadik" title="Tzadik">tzadik</a></i> like <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, he would not be considered so righteous. They point out that Noah did not pray to God on behalf of those about to be destroyed, as Abraham prayed for the wicked of <a href="/wiki/Sodom_and_Gomorrah" title="Sodom and Gomorrah">Sodom and Gomorrah</a>. In fact, Noah is never seen to speak; he simply listens to God and acts on his orders. This led such commentators to offer the figure of Noah as "the man in a fur coat," who ensured his own comfort while ignoring his neighbour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> Others, such as the medieval commentator <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a>, held on the contrary that the building of the Ark was stretched over 120 years, deliberately in order to give sinners time to repent. Rashi interprets his father's statement of the naming of Noah (in Hebrew נֹחַ) "This one will comfort us (in Hebrew– yeNaHamainu יְנַחֲמֵנו) in our work and in the toil of our hands, which come from the ground that the Lord had cursed",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> by saying Noah heralded a new era of prosperity, when there was easing (in Hebrew – nahah – נחה) from the curse from the time of Adam when the Earth produced thorns and thistles even where men sowed wheat and that Noah then introduced the plow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Jewish_Encyclopedia" title="Jewish Encyclopedia">Jewish Encyclopedia</a></i>, "The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> contains two accounts of Noah." In the first, Noah is the hero of the flood, and in the second, he is the father of mankind and a husbandman who planted the first vineyard. "The disparity of character between these two narratives has caused some critics to insist that the subject of the latter account was not the same as the subject of the former." Perhaps the original name of the hero of the flood was actually <i>Enoch</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_Judaica" title="Encyclopedia Judaica">Encyclopedia Judaica</a> notes that Noah's drunkenness is not presented as reprehensible behavior. Rather, "It is clear that ... Noah’s venture into viticulture provides the setting for the castigation of Israel’s Canaanite neighbors." It was Ham who committed an offense when he viewed his father’s nakedness. Yet, "Noah’s curse, ...is strangely aimed at Canaan rather than the disrespectful Ham." (p. 288)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skolnik287_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Skolnik287-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noah_catacombe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="323" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Noah_catacombe.jpg/150px-Noah_catacombe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Noah_catacombe.jpg/225px-Noah_catacombe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Noah_catacombe.jpg/300px-Noah_catacombe.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noah_catacombe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An early Christian depiction showing Noah giving the gesture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orant" title="Orant">orant</a> as the dove returns</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.bibler.org/versions/nasb/nasb_2peter02_sml.htm#2peter_2:5" rel="nofollow">2 Peter 2:5</a> refers to Noah as a "preacher of righteousness". In the Gospel of Matthew and the Gospel of Luke, Jesus compares Noah's flood with the coming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_Judgement" title="Last Judgement">Day of Judgement</a>: "Just as it was in the days of Noah, so too it will be in the days of the coming of the <a href="/wiki/Son_of_man_(Christianity)" title="Son of man (Christianity)">Son of Man</a>. For in the days before the flood, people were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, up to the day Noah entered the ark; and they knew nothing about what would happen until the flood came and took them all away. That is how it will be at the coming of the Son of Man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_of_Peter" title="First Epistle of Peter">First Epistle of Peter</a> compares the saving power of <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a> with the Ark saving those who were in it. In later Christian thought, the Ark came to be compared to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Church</a>: <a href="/wiki/Salvation_in_Christianity" title="Salvation in Christianity">salvation</a> was to be found only within Christ and his Lordship, as in Noah's time it had been found only within the Ark. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Augustine_of_Hippo" title="St Augustine of Hippo">St Augustine of Hippo</a> (354–430), demonstrated in <i><a href="/wiki/The_City_of_God_(book)" title="The City of God (book)">The City of God</a></i> that the dimensions of the Ark corresponded to the dimensions of the human body, which corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Body_of_Christ" title="Body of Christ">body of Christ</a>; the equation of Ark and Church is still found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> rite of baptism, which asks God, "who of thy great mercy didst save Noah," to receive into the Church the infant about to be baptised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_medieval_Christianity" title="History of medieval Christianity">medieval Christianity</a>, Noah's three sons were generally considered as the founders of the populations of the three known <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japheth" title="Japheth">Japheth</a>/Europe, <a href="/wiki/Shem" title="Shem">Shem</a>/Asia, and <a href="/wiki/Ham_(son_of_Noah)" title="Ham (son of Noah)">Ham</a>/Africa, although a rarer variation held that they represented the three classes of medieval society – the priests (Shem), the warriors (Japheth), and the peasants (Ham). In medieval Christian thought, Ham was considered to be the ancestor of the people of black Africa. So, in racialist arguments, the curse of Ham became a justification for the slavery of the black races.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Isaac_Newton">Isaac Newton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Isaac Newton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>, in his religious works on the development of religion, wrote about Noah and his offspring. In Newton's view, while Noah was a monotheist, the gods of pagan antiquity are identified with Noah and his descendants. "Newton argues that Noah is ultimately deified as the god Saturn." "Newton thus traces all ancient political and religious history back to Noah and Noah's offspring and simultaneously gives a historical account of the rise of polytheism and idolatry in these gentile nations as the result of the posthumous deification of their leaders and heroes, a polytheistic process which thoroughly corrupts the core monotheistic truth ... in the original religion of Noah".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mormon_theology">Mormon theology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mormon theology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mormonism" title="Mormonism">Mormon theology</a>, Noah plays an important role prior to his birth as the angel <a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">Gabriel</a> and then lived in his mortal life as the patriarch-prophet Noah. Gabriel and Noah are regarded as the same individual under different names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormons</a> also believe that Noah returned to earth as Gabriel after his earthly life<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> and appeared to <a href="/wiki/Daniel_(biblical_figure)" title="Daniel (biblical figure)">Daniel</a> to teach him about the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">Second Coming</a>; to <a href="/wiki/Zechariah_(priest)" title="Zechariah (priest)">Zacharias</a>, the father of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>; and to <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_views_on_Mary" title="Latter Day Saint views on Mary">Mary</a>, the mother of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Noah is considered the head of a <a href="/wiki/Dispensation_(period)" title="Dispensation (period)">dispensation</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve_(LDS_Church)" title="Adam and Eve (LDS Church)">Adam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enoch_(ancestor_of_Noah)" title="Enoch (ancestor of Noah)">Enoch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a>. A dispensation is a period of time in which the Lord has at least one authorized servant on earth who bears the keys of the holy priesthood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> Noah became the means by which the gospel of Jesus Christ— the <a href="/wiki/Plan_of_salvation_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Plan of salvation (Latter Day Saints)">plan of salvation</a> —is revealed anew, the means by which divine transforming powers, including saving covenants and ordinances, are extended to people during an age of time called a dispensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Noah_in_Islam" title="Noah in Islam">Noah in Islam</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Noah is a highly important figure in <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and is seen as one of the most significant of all prophets. The <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> contains 43 references to Noah, or <i>Nuḥ</i>, in 28 chapters, and the seventy-first chapter, <a href="/wiki/Nuh_(surah)" title="Nuh (surah)"><i>Sūrat Nūḥ</i></a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">سورة نوح</span>‎‎), is named after him. His life is also spoken of in the commentaries and in Islamic legends.</p>
<p>Noah's narratives largely cover his preaching as well the story of the <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">Deluge</a>. Noah's narrative sets the prototype for many of the subsequent prophetic stories, which begin with the prophet warning his people and then the community rejecting the message and facing a punishment.</p>
<p>Noah has several titles in Islam, based primarily on praise for him in the Qur'an, including "True Messenger of God" (XXVI: 107) and "Grateful Servant of God" (XVII: 3).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skolnik287_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Skolnik287-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enc_Islam_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enc_Islam-60">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Qur'an focuses on several instances from Noah's life more than others, and one of the most significant events is the Flood. God makes a <a href="/wiki/Covenant_(biblical)" title="Covenant (biblical)">covenant</a> with Noah just as he did with Abraham, <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> later on (33:7). Noah is later reviled by his people and reproached by them for being a mere human messenger and not an angel (10:72–74). Moreover, the people mock Noah's words and call him a liar (7:62), and they even suggest that Noah is possessed by a devil when the prophet ceases to preach (54:9). Only the lowest in the community join Noah in believing in God's message (11:29), and Noah's narrative further describes him preaching both in private and public. Noah prays to God, "Lord, leave not one single family of Infidels from the land: / For if thou leave them they will beguile thy servants and will beget only sinners, infidels."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since March 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> The Qur'an narrates that Noah received a revelation to build an <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">Ark</a>, after his people refused to believe in his message and hear the warning. The narrative goes on to describe that waters poured forth from the Heavens, destroying all the sinners. Even one of his sons disbelieved him, stayed behind, and was drowned. In the Qur'an, Noah originally had four sons, but they are not named. After the Flood ended, the Ark rested atop <a href="/wiki/Mount_Judi" title="Mount Judi">Mount Judi</a> (<a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D11%3Averse%3D44" rel="nofollow">11:44</a>).</p>
<p>Also, Islamic beliefs deny the idea of Noah being the first person to drink wine and experience the aftereffects of doing so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skolnik287_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-Skolnik287-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enc_Islam_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Enc_Islam-60">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D29%3Averse%3D14" rel="nofollow">29:14</a> states that Noah had been living among the people who he was sent to for 950 years when the flood started.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>And, indeed, [in times long past] We sent forth Noah unto his people, and he dwelt among them a thousand years bar fifty; and then the floods overwhelmed them while they were still lost in evildoing.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Ahmadiyya" title="Ahmadiyya">Ahmadiyya</a> understanding of the Quran, the period described in the Quran is the age of his <a href="/wiki/Dispensation_(period)" title="Dispensation (period)">dispensation</a>, which extended until the time of <a href="/wiki/Abraham_in_Islam" title="Abraham in Islam">Ibrahim</a> (Abraham, 950 years). The first 50 years were the years of spiritual progress, which were followed by 900 years of spiritual deterioration of the people of Noah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gnostic">Gnostic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Gnostic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An important Gnostic text, the <i><a href="/wiki/Apocryphon_of_John" title="Apocryphon of John">Apocryphon of John</a></i>, reports that the chief <a href="/wiki/Archon_(Gnosticism)" title="Archon (Gnosticism)">archon</a> caused the flood because he desired to destroy the world he had made, but the First Thought informed Noah of the chief archon's plans, and Noah informed the remainder of humanity. Unlike the account of Genesis, not only are Noah's family saved, but many others also heed Noah's call. There is no ark in this account. According to <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Pagels" title="Elaine Pagels">Elaine Pagels</a>, "Rather, they hid in a particular place, not only Noah, but also many other people from the unshakable race. They entered that place and hid in a bright cloud."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Bahá'í"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD">Bahá'í</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Bahá'í">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a> regards the Ark and the Flood as symbolic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup> In Bahá'í belief, only Noah's followers were spiritually alive, preserved in the ark of his teachings, as others were spiritually dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> The Bahá'í scripture <i><a href="/wiki/Kit%C3%A1b-i-%C3%8Dq%C3%A1n" title="Kitáb-i-Íqán">Kitáb-i-Íqán</a></i> endorses the Islamic belief that Noah had a large number of companions, either 40 or 72, besides his family on the Ark, and that he taught for 950 (symbolic) years before the flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
