<p>The <b>Tibetan Plateau</b> (<a href="/wiki/Tibetan_alphabet" title="Tibetan alphabet">Tibetan</a>: <span lang="bo" style="font-family: 'Qomolangma-Dunhuang', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Sarchen', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Sarchung', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Suring', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Sutung', 'Qomolangma-Title', 'Qomolangma-Subtitle', 'Qomolangma-Woodblock', 'DDC Uchen', 'DDC Rinzin', Kailash, Jomolhari, 'TCRC Youtso Unicode', 'Tibetan Machine Uni', Wangdi29, 'Noto Sans Tibetan', 'Microsoft Himalaya'; font-size:1.25em; vertical-align:top; word-wrap: break-word;" xml:lang="bo">བོད་ས་མཐོ།</span>, <a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a>: <i>bod sa mtho</i>), also known in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> as the <b>Qinghai–Tibet Plateau</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or the <b>Qingzang Plateau</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">青藏高原</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Qīngzàng Gāoyuán</span></i>) or <b>Himalayan Plateau</b>, is a vast elevated <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> covering most of the <a href="/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="Tibet Autonomous Region">Tibet Autonomous Region</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_China" title="Western China">western China</a>, as well as part of <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jammu_and_Kashmir" title="Jammu and Kashmir">Jammu and Kashmir</a>, India. It stretches approximately 1,000 kilometres (620 mi) north to south and 2,500 kilometres (1,600 mi) east to west. With an average elevation exceeding 4,500 metres (14,800 ft), the Tibetan Plateau is sometimes called "the <a href="/wiki/Roof_of_the_World" title="Roof of the World">Roof of the World</a>" because it stands over 3 miles above sea level and is surrounded by imposing mountain ranges that harbor the world's two highest summits, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> and <a href="/wiki/K2" title="K2">K2</a>, and is the world's highest and largest plateau, with an area of 2,500,000 square kilometres (970,000 sq mi) (about five times the size of <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">Metropolitan France</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Sometimes termed the <b>Third Pole</b>, the Tibetan Plateau contains the <a href="/wiki/River_source" title="River source">headwaters</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a> of most of the <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a> in surrounding regions. Its tens of thousands of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and other geographical and ecological features serve as a "water tower" <a href="/wiki/Water_storage" title="Water storage">storing water</a> and maintaining <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">flow</a>. The impact of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> on the Tibetan Plateau is of intense scientific interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT83013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT83013-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Tibetan Plateau is surrounded by massive mountain ranges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The plateau is bordered to the south by the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">inner Himalayan range</a>, to the north by the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun Mountains</a>, which separate it from the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>, and to the northeast by the <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian Mountains</a>, which separate the plateau from the <a href="/wiki/Hexi_Corridor" title="Hexi Corridor">Hexi Corridor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a>. To the east and southeast the plateau gives way to the forested gorge and ridge geography of the mountainous headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/Salween_River" title="Salween River">Salween</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> rivers in northwest <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Hengduan_Mountains" title="Hengduan Mountains">Hengduan Mountains</a>). In the west the curve of the rugged <a href="/wiki/Karakoram" title="Karakoram">Karakoram</a> range of northern <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> embraces the plateau. The <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus River</a> originates in the western Tibetan Plateau in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Manasarovar" title="Lake Manasarovar">Lake Manasarovar</a>.</p>

<p>The Tibetan Plateau is bounded in the north by a broad escarpment where the altitude drops from around 5,000 metres (16,000 ft) to 1,500 metres (4,900 ft) over a horizontal distance of less than 150 kilometres (93 mi). Along the escarpment is a range of mountains. In the west the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun Mountains</a> separate the plateau from the Tarim Basin. About halfway across the Tarim the bounding range becomes the <a href="/wiki/Altyn-Tagh" title="Altyn-Tagh">Altyn-Tagh</a> and the Kunluns, by convention, continue somewhat to the south. In the 'V' formed by this split is the western part of the <a href="/wiki/Qaidam_Basin" title="Qaidam Basin">Qaidam Basin</a>. The Altyn-Tagh ends near the Dangjin pass on the <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a>-<a href="/wiki/Golmud" title="Golmud">Golmud</a> road. To the west are short ranges called the Danghe, Yema, Shule, and Tulai Nanshans. The easternmost range is the Qilian Mountains. The line of mountains continues east of the plateau as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qinling" title="Qinling">Qinling</a>, which separates the <a href="/wiki/Ordos_Plateau" title="Ordos Plateau">Ordos Plateau</a> from Sichuan. North of the mountains runs the Gansu or <a href="/wiki/Hexi_Corridor" title="Hexi Corridor">Hexi Corridor</a> which was the main silk-road route from <a href="/wiki/China_proper" title="China proper">China proper</a> to the West.</p>
<p>The plateau is a high-altitude arid <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> interspersed with mountain ranges and large <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish</a> lakes. Annual precipitation ranges from 100 to 300 millimetres (3.9 to 11.8 in) and falls mainly as hail. The southern and eastern edges of the steppe have grasslands which can sustainably support populations of nomadic herdsmen, although frost occurs for six months of the year. <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a> occurs over extensive parts of the plateau. Proceeding to the north and northwest, the plateau becomes progressively higher, colder and drier, until reaching the remote <a href="/wiki/Changtang" title="Changtang">Changtang</a> region in the northwestern part of the plateau. Here the average altitude exceeds 5,000 metres (16,000 ft) and winter temperatures can drop to −40 °C (−40 °F). As a result of this extremely inhospitable environment, the Changthang region (together with the adjoining Kekexili region) is the least populous region in Asia, and the third least populous area in the world after <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.</p>

<p>The geological history of the Tibetan Plateau is closely related to that of the Himalayas. The Himalayas are among the youngest mountain ranges on the planet and consist mostly of uplifted <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rock</a>. Their formation is a result of a <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">continental collision</a> or <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogeny</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">convergent boundary</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>.</p>
<p>The collision began in the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Upper Cretaceous</a> period about 70 million years ago, when the north-moving <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian Plate</a>, moving at about 15 cm (6 in) per year, collided with the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>. About 50 million years ago, this fast moving Indo-Australian plate had completely closed the <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Ocean</a>, the existence of which has been determined by <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> settled on the ocean floor, and the <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> that fringed its edges. Since these sediments were light, they crumpled into mountain ranges rather than sinking to the floor. The Indo-Australian plate continues to be driven horizontally below the Tibetan Plateau, which forces the plateau to move upwards; the plateau is still <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">rising</a> at a rate of approximately 5 mm (0.2 in) per year.</p>
<p>Much of the Tibetan Plateau is of relatively low relief. The cause of this is debated among geologists. Some argue that the Tibetan Plateau is an uplifted <a href="/wiki/Peneplain" title="Peneplain">peneplain</a> formed at low altitude, while others argue that the low relief stems from erosion and infill of topographic depressions that occurred at already high elevations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Tibetan Plateau supports a variety of ecosystems, most of them classified as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Montane_grasslands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Montane grasslands (page does not exist)">montane grasslands</a>. While parts of the plateau feature an <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">alpine tundra</a>-like environment, other areas feature monsoon-influenced shrublands and forests. <a href="/wiki/Species_diversity" title="Species diversity">Species diversity</a> is generally reduced on the plateau due to the elevation of low precipitation. The Tibetan Plateau hosts species of <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">gray wolf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow_leopard" title="Snow leopard">snow leopard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_yak" title="Wild yak">wild yak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donkey#Feral_donkeys_and_wild_asses" title="Donkey">wild donkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">cranes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">geese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_buffalo" title="Water buffalo">water buffalo</a>. One notable animal is the <a href="/wiki/Euophrys_omnisuperstes" title="Euophrys omnisuperstes">high-altitude jumping spider</a>, that can live at elevations of over 6,500 metres (21,300 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">Ecoregions</a> found on the Tibetan Plateau, as defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund for Nature</a>, are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pamir_alpine_desert_and_tundra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pamir alpine desert and tundra (page does not exist)">Pamir alpine desert and tundra</a> covers the western end of the Tibetan Plateau where it transitions to the <a href="/wiki/Pamir_Mountains" title="Pamir Mountains">Pamir Mountains</a></li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=North_Tibetan_Plateau-Kunlun_Mountains_alpine_desert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="North Tibetan Plateau-Kunlun Mountains alpine desert (page does not exist)">North Tibetan Plateau-Kunlun Mountains alpine desert</a> covers the northwestern limits of the Tibetan Plateau along the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun Mountains</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Karakoram-West_Tibetan_Plateau_alpine_steppe" title="Karakoram-West Tibetan Plateau alpine steppe">Karakoram-West Tibetan Plateau alpine steppe</a> covers the westernmost parts of the Tibetan Plateau and <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Himalayan_alpine_shrub_and_meadows" title="Northwestern Himalayan alpine shrub and meadows">Northwestern Himalayan alpine shrub and meadows</a> on the edges mountains bordering the extreme west of the Tibetan Plateau</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Tibetan_Plateau_alpine_steppe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Central Tibetan Plateau alpine steppe (page does not exist)">Central Tibetan Plateau alpine steppe</a> covers most of the central portions of the Tibetan Plateau and the eastern <a href="/wiki/Changtang" title="Changtang">Changtang</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Western_Himalayan_alpine_shrub_and_meadows" title="Western Himalayan alpine shrub and meadows">Western Himalayan alpine shrub and meadows</a> covers the southwestern plateau in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Garuda_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Garuda Valley (page does not exist)">Garuda Valley</a> region</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Qaidam_Basin_semi-desert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Qaidam Basin semi-desert (page does not exist)">Qaidam Basin semi-desert</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Qaidam_Basin" title="Qaidam Basin">Qaidam Basin</a> on the northern Tibetan Plateau</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Qilian_Mountains_subalpine_meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Qilian Mountains subalpine meadows (page does not exist)">Qilian Mountains subalpine meadows</a> covering the <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian Mountains</a> in the northernmost portions of the plateau</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Qilian_Mountains_conifer_forests&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Qilian Mountains conifer forests (page does not exist)">Qilian Mountains conifer forests</a> covering parts of the mountain ranges in the northeastern Tibetan Plateau</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau_alpine_shrub_and_meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tibetan Plateau alpine shrub and meadows (page does not exist)">Tibetan Plateau alpine shrub and meadows</a> covering a swath of the central and northeastern Tibetan Plateau</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yarlung_Tsangpo_arid_steppe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yarlung Tsangpo arid steppe (page does not exist)">Yarlung Tsangpo arid steppe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Yarlung_Tsangpo_River" title="Yarlung Tsangpo River">Yarlung Tsangpo River Valley</a>, where most of the permanent human population on the Tibetan Plateau lives</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Himalayan_alpine_shrub_and_meadows" title="Eastern Himalayan alpine shrub and meadows">Eastern Himalayan alpine shrub and meadows</a> cover the southern Tibetan Plateau on the north side of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Tibet_shrub_and_meadows" title="Southeast Tibet shrub and meadows">Southeast Tibet shrub and meadows</a> cover the southeastern and eastern parts of the plateau and are generally rainier than the other high-altitude Tibetan Plateau regions</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Himalayan_subalpine_conifer_forests" title="Northeastern Himalayan subalpine conifer forests">Northeastern Himalayan subalpine conifer forests</a> reach up mountain valleys in the southern plateau and contain some of the highest altitude forests in the world</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Nujiang_Langcang_Gorge_alpine_conifer_and_mixed_forests" title="Nujiang Langcang Gorge alpine conifer and mixed forests">Nujiang Langcang Gorge alpine conifer and mixed forests</a> cover the mountain valleys that reach 500 km (310 mi) into the southeastern Tibetan Plateau</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hengduan_Mountains_subalpine_conifer_forests" title="Hengduan Mountains subalpine conifer forests">Hengduan Mountains subalpine conifer forests</a> cover the southeasternmost mountain valleys on the plateau</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Qionglai-Minshan_conifer_forests" title="Qionglai-Minshan conifer forests">Qionglai-Minshan conifer forests</a> cover the eastern edges of the plateau and are the densest forests to be found anywhere on the Tibetan Plateau</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_history">Human history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Human history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">Nomads</a> on the Tibetan Plateau and in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> are the remainders of nomadic practices historically once widespread in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Pastoral nomads constitute about 40% of the ethnic <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">Tibetan</a> population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The presence of nomadic peoples on the plateau is predicated on their adaptation to survival on the world's <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> by raising livestock rather than crops, which are unsuitable to the terrain. Archaeological evidence suggests that the colonization leading to the full-time occupation of the plateau occurred much later than the previously thought 30,000 years ago.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2014)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> Since colonization of the Tibetan Plateau, Tibetan culture has adapted and flourished in the western, southern, and eastern regions of the plateau. The northern portion, the <a href="/wiki/Changtang" title="Changtang">Changtang</a>, is generally too high and cold to support permanent population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historical_atlas_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-historical_atlas-21">[21]</a></sup> One of the most notable civilizations to have developed on the Tibetan Plateau is the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Empire" title="Tibetan Empire">Tibetan Empire</a> from the 7th century to the 9th century AD.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_other_regions">Impact on other regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impact on other regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_monsoons">Role in monsoons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Role in monsoons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Monsoons are caused by the different amplitudes of surface temperature seasonal cycles between land and oceans. This differential warming happens because heating rates differ between land and water. Ocean heating is distributed vertically through a "mixed layer" that may be fifty meters deep through the action of wind and buoyancy-generated <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>, whereas the land surface conducts heat slowly, with the seasonal signal penetrating only a meter or so. Additionally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> of liquid water is significantly greater than that of most materials that make up land. Together, these factors mean that the heat capacity of the layer participating in the seasonal cycle is much larger over the oceans than over land, with the consequence that the land warms and cools faster than the ocean. In turn, air over the land warms faster and reaches a higher temperature than does air over the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oracle_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oracle-22">[22]</a></sup> The warmer air over land tends to rise, creating an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure" title="Low pressure">low pressure</a>. The pressure anomaly then causes a steady wind to blow toward the land, which brings the moist air over the ocean surface with it. Rainfall is then increased by the presence of the moist ocean air. The rainfall is stimulated by a variety of mechanisms, such as low-level air being lifted upwards by mountains, surface heating, convergence at the surface, divergence aloft, or from storm-produced outflows near the surface. When such lifting occurs, the air cools due expansion in lower pressure, which in turn produces <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> and precipitation.</p>
<p>In winter, the land cools off quickly, but the ocean maintains the heat longer. The hot air over the ocean rises, creating a low-pressure area and a breeze from land to ocean while a large area of drying high pressure is formed over the land, increased by wintertime cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oracle_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oracle-22">[22]</a></sup> Monsoons are similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_breezes" title="Sea breezes">sea and land breezes</a>, a term usually referring to the localized, <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal cycle</a> of circulation near coastlines everywhere, but they are much larger in scale, stronger and seasonal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The seasonal monsoon wind shift and weather associated with the heating and cooling of the Tibetan plateau is the strongest such monsoon on Earth.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciology:_the_Ice_Age_and_at_present">Glaciology: the Ice Age and at present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Glaciology: the Ice Age and at present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Himalayas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1998" data-file-width="3032" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Himalayas.jpg/260px-Himalayas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Himalayas.jpg/390px-Himalayas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Himalayas.jpg/520px-Himalayas.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Himalayas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Himalayas as seen from space looking south from over the Tibetan Plateau.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Today, Tibet is an important heating surface of the atmosphere. However, during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, an approximately 2,400,000 square kilometres (930,000 sq mi) ice sheet covered the plateau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K1999_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-K1999-26">[26]</a></sup> Due to its great extent, this glaciation in the subtropics was an important element of <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>. With a much lower latitude, the ice in Tibet reflected at least four times more radiation energy per unit area into space than ice at higher <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>. Thus, while the modern plateau heats the overlying atmosphere, during the Last Ice Age it helped to cool it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>This cooling had multiple effects on regional climate. Without the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">thermal low</a> pressure caused by the heating, there was no <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> over the Indian subcontinent. This lack of monsoon caused <a href="/wiki/Pluvial" title="Pluvial">extensive rainfall</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">Thar Desert</a>, more dust deposited into the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, and a lowering of the <a href="/wiki/Life_zone" title="Life zone">biotic life zones</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. Animals responded to this shift in climate, with the <a href="/wiki/Javan_rusa" title="Javan rusa">Javan rusa</a> migrating into India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K2001_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-K2001-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, the glaciers in Tibet created <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">meltwater lakes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Qaidam_Basin" title="Qaidam Basin">Qaidam Basin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a>, despite the strong <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> caused by the low latitude. <a href="/wiki/Rock_flour" title="Rock flour">Silt</a> and clay from the glaciers accumulated in these lakes; when the lakes dried at the end of the ice age, the silt and clay were <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">blown</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Katabatic_wind" title="Katabatic wind">downslope wind</a> off the Plateau. These airborne fine grains produced the enormous amount of <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> in the Chinese lowlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K2001_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-K2001-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_climate_change">Effect of climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibetan_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effect of climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Tibetan Plateau contains the world's third-largest store of ice. Qin Dahe, the former head of the China Meteorological Administration, issued the following assessment in 2009:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Temperatures are rising four times faster than elsewhere in China, and the Tibetan glaciers are retreating at a higher speed than in any other part of the world. [...] In the short term, this will cause lakes to expand and bring floods and mudflows. [...] In the long run, the glaciers are vital lifelines for Asian rivers, including the Indus and the Ganges. Once they vanish, water supplies in those regions will be in peril.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
