<p><b>Pedro Álvares Cabral</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[A]</a></sup> (<small>European Portuguese: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ˈpeðɾu ˈaɫvɐr(ɨ)ʃ kɐˈβɾaɫ]</a></span> <small>or</small> <small>Brazilian Portuguese: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ˈpedɾu ˈawvaɾis kaˈbɾaw]</a></span>; c. 1467 or 1468 – c. 1520) was a <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> nobleman, military commander, <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">navigator</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_explorers" title="List of explorers">explorer</a> regarded as the discoverer of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. Cabral conducted the first substantial exploration of the northeast coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and claimed it for Portugal. While details of Cabral's early life are unclear, it is known that he came from a minor noble family and received a good education. He was appointed to head an expedition to India in 1500, following <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_sea_route_to_India" title="Discovery of the sea route to India">newly opened route</a> around Africa. The object of the undertaking was to return with valuable spices and to establish trade relations in India—bypassing the monopoly on the spice trade then in the hands of Arab, Turkish and Italian merchants. Although the previous expedition of Vasco da Gama to India, on its sea route, recorded signs of land west of the southern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> (in 1497), Cabral is regarded as the first captain who ever touched four continents, leading the first expedition that united <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>His fleet of 13 ships sailed far into the western <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, perhaps intentionally, where he made landfall on what he initially assumed to be a large island. As the new land was within the Portuguese sphere according to the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">Treaty of Tordesillas</a>, Cabral claimed it for the Portuguese Crown. He explored the coast, realizing that the large land mass was probably a continent, and dispatched a ship to notify King <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal" title="Manuel I of Portugal">Manuel I</a> of the new territory. The continent was South America, and the land he had claimed for Portugal later came to be known as Brazil. The fleet reprovisioned and then turned eastward to resume the journey to India.</p>
<p>A storm in the southern Atlantic caused the loss of several ships, and the six remaining ships eventually rendezvoused in the <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Channel" title="Mozambique Channel">Mozambique Channel</a> before proceeding to <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Calicut</a> in India. Cabral was originally successful in negotiating trading rights, but Arab merchants saw Portugal's venture as a threat to their monopoly and stirred up an attack by both Muslims and Hindus on the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Entrep%C3%B4t" title="Entrepôt">entrepôt</a>. The Portuguese sustained many casualties and their facilities were destroyed. Cabral took vengeance by looting and burning the Arab fleet and then bombarded the city in retaliation for its ruler having failed to explain the unexpected attack. From Calicut the expedition sailed to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Cochin" title="Kingdom of Cochin">Kingdom of Cochin</a>, another Indian <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a>, where Cabral befriended its ruler and loaded his ships with coveted spices before returning to Europe. Despite the loss of human lives and ships, Cabral's voyage was deemed a success upon his return to Portugal. The extraordinary profits resulting from the sale of the spices bolstered the Portuguese Crown's finances and helped lay the foundation of a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a> that would stretch from the Americas to the Far East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[B]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cabral was later passed over, possibly as a result of a quarrel with Manuel I, when a new fleet was assembled to establish a more robust presence in India. Having lost favor with the King, he retired to a private life of which few records survive. His accomplishments slipped mostly into obscurity for more than 300 years. Decades after Brazil's independence from Portugal in the 19th century, Cabral's reputation began to be rehabilitated by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Pedro_II_of_Brazil" title="Pedro II of Brazil">Pedro II of Brazil</a>. Historians have long argued whether Cabral was Brazil's discoverer, and whether the discovery was accidental or intentional. The first question has been settled by the observation that the few, cursory encounters by explorers before him were barely noticed at the time and contributed nothing to the future development and history of the land which would become Brazil, the sole Portuguese-speaking nation in the Americas. On the second question, no definite consensus has been formed, and the intentional discovery hypothesis lacks solid proof. Nevertheless, although he was overshadowed by contemporary explorers, Cabral today is regarded as a major figure of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>.</p>

</div>
<p>Little is certain regarding Pedro Álvares Cabral's life before, or following, his voyage which led to the discovery of Brazil. He was born in 1467 or 1468—the former year being the most likely<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxxix_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxxix-5">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191413_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191413-6">[4]</a></sup>—at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belmonte_Municipality" title="Belmonte Municipality">Belmonte</a>, about 30 kilometres (19 mi) from present-day <a href="/wiki/Covilh%C3%A3" title="Covilhã">Covilhã</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> He was a son of Fernão Álvares Cabral and Isabel Gouveia—one of five boys and six girls in the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup> Cabral was christened Pedro Álvares de Gouveia and only later, supposedly upon his elder brother's death in 1503,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> did he begin using his father's surname.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[C]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001232_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001232-11">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFernandes196953_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFernandes196953-12">[9]</a></sup> The coat of arms of his family was drawn with two purple goats on a field of silver. Purple represented fidelity, and the goats were derived from the family name (<i>cabral</i> pertains to <i>goats</i> in English).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxxix_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxxix-5">[3]</a></sup> However, only his elder brother was entitled to make use of the family arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont19142_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont19142-13">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Family lore said that the <i>Cabrais</i> were descendants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caranus_(king)" title="Caranus (king)">Caranus</a>, the legendary first king of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedonia</a>. Caranus was, in turn, a supposed 7th-generation scion of the demigod <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[D]</a></sup> Myths aside, the historian James McClymont believes that another family tale might hold clues to the true origin of Cabral's family. According to that tradition, the <i>Cabrais</i> derive from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castilian_people" title="Castilian people">Castilian</a> clan named the <i>Cabreiras</i> (<i>cabra</i> is Spanish for goat) who bore a similar coat of arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[E]</a></sup> The Cabral family rose to prominence during the 14th century. Álvaro Gil Cabral (Cabral's great-great-grandfather and a frontier military commander) was one of the few Portuguese nobles to remain loyal to <a href="/wiki/Dom_(title)" title="Dom (title)">Dom</a> <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal" title="John I of Portugal">João I</a>, King of Portugal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1383%E2%80%931385_Crisis" title="1383–1385 Crisis">during the war against</a> the King of Castile. As a reward, João I presented Álvaro Gil with the hereditary <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefdom</a> of Belmonte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[11]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Raised as a member of the lower nobility,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESubrahmanyam1997177_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESubrahmanyam1997177-17">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewitt200564_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewitt200564-18">[13]</a></sup> Cabral was sent to the court of King Dom <a href="/wiki/Afonso_V_of_Portugal" title="Afonso V of Portugal">Afonso V</a> in 1479 at around age 12. He received an education in the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> and learned to bear arms and fight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAbramo196934_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbramo196934-19">[14]</a></sup> He would have been roughly age 17 on 30 June 1484 when he was named <i>moço fidalgo</i> (young nobleman; a minor title then commonly granted to young nobles) by King Dom <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">João II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAbramo196934_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbramo196934-19">[14]</a></sup> Records of his deeds prior to 1500 are extremely fragmentary, but Cabral may have campaigned in North Africa, as had his ancestors and as was commonly done by other young nobles of his day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[15]</a></sup> King Dom <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal" title="Manuel I of Portugal">Manuel I</a>, who had acceded to the throne two years previously, awarded him an annual allowance worth 30,000 <i><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_real" title="Portuguese real">reais</a></i> on 12 April 1497.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191433_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191433-21">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xl_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xl-22">[17]</a></sup> He was concurrently given the title <i>fidalgo</i> (nobleman) in the King's Council and was named a Knight of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Christ_(Portugal)" title="Order of Christ (Portugal)">Order of Christ</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xl_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xl-22">[17]</a></sup> There is no contemporary image or detailed physical description of Cabral. It is known that he had a strong build<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeres1949114_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeres1949114-23">[18]</a></sup> and matched his father's height of 1.90 meters (6 ft 2.8 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[19]</a></sup> Cabral's character has been described as well-learned, courteous,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001231_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001231-25">[20]</a></sup> prudent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon198142_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon198142-26">[21]</a></sup> generous, tolerant with enemies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFernandes196953_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFernandes196953-12">[9]</a></sup> humble,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeres1949114_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeres1949114-23">[18]</a></sup> but also vain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001231_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001231-25">[20]</a></sup> and too concerned with the respect he felt his honor and position demanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFernandes196952_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFernandes196952-27">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_Brazil">Discovery of Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery of Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2nd_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Cabral,_1500)" title="2nd Portuguese India Armada (Cabral, 1500)">2nd Portuguese India Armada (Cabral, 1500)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fleet_commander-in-chief">Fleet commander-in-chief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fleet commander-in-chief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cabral_voyage_1500.svg"><img alt="A map showing the southern Atlantic and western Indian Ocean with two routes traced which go around the southern tip of Africa" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1058" data-file-width="1410" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Cabral_voyage_1500.svg/400px-Cabral_voyage_1500.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Cabral_voyage_1500.svg/600px-Cabral_voyage_1500.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Cabral_voyage_1500.svg/800px-Cabral_voyage_1500.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cabral_voyage_1500.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Route taken by Cabral from Portugal to India in 1500 (in red), and the return route (in blue)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 15 February 1500, Cabral was appointed <i>Capitão-mor</i> (literally Major-Captain, or commander-in-chief) of a <a href="/wiki/2nd_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Cabral,_1500)" title="2nd Portuguese India Armada (Cabral, 1500)">fleet sailing for India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[23]</a></sup> It was then the custom for the Portuguese Crown to appoint nobles to naval and military commands, regardless of experience or professional competence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer2002128_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer2002128-29">[24]</a></sup> This was the case for the captains of the ships under Cabral's command—most were nobles like himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199818_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199818-30">[25]</a></sup> The practice had obvious pitfalls, since authority could as easily be given to highly incompetent and unfit people as it could fall to talented leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> or Dom <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Castro" title="João de Castro">João de Castro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer2002312_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer2002312-31">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scant details have survived regarding the criteria used by the Portuguese government in its selection of Cabral as head of the India expedition. In the royal decree naming him commander-in-chief, the only reasons given are "merits and services". Nothing more is known about these qualifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199834_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199834-32">[27]</a></sup> Historian William Greenlee argued that King Manuel I "had undoubtedly known him well at court". That, along with the "standing of the Cabral family, their unquestioned loyalty to the Crown, the personal appearance of Cabral, and the ability which he had shown at court and in the council were important factors".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xli_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xli-33">[28]</a></sup> Also in his favor may have been the influence of two of his brothers who sat on the King's Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xli_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xli-33">[28]</a></sup> Given the political intrigue present at court, Cabral may have been part of a faction that furthered his appointment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xli_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xli-33">[28]</a></sup> The historian Malyn Newitt subscribes to some sort of ulterior maneuvering and has said that the choice of Cabral "was a deliberate attempt to balance the interests of rival factions of noble families, for he appears to have no other quality to recommend him and no known experience in commanding major expeditions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewitt200565_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewitt200565-34">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cabral became the military chief, while far more experienced navigators were seconded to the expedition to aid him in naval matters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199837_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199837-35">[30]</a></sup> The most important of these were <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Bartolomeu Dias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Dias" title="Diogo Dias">Diogo Dias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicolau_Coelho" title="Nicolau Coelho">Nicolau Coelho</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[31]</a></sup> They would, along with the other captains, command 13 ships<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[32]</a></sup> and 1,500 men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[33]</a></sup> Of this contingent, 700 were soldiers, although most were simple commoners who had no training or previous experience in combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199838_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199838-39">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fleet had two divisions. The first division was composed of nine <i>naus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">carracks</a>) and two <a href="/wiki/Square-rigged_caravel" title="Square-rigged caravel">round caravels</a>, and was headed to Calicut (today more often known as <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Kozhikode</a>) in India with the goal of establishing trade relations and a <a href="/wiki/Factory_(trading_post)" title="Factory (trading post)">factory (trading post)</a>. The second division, consisting of one <i>nau</i> and one round caravel, set sail for the port of <a href="/wiki/Sofala" title="Sofala">Sofala</a> in what is today <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199822_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199822-40">[35]</a></sup> In exchange for leading the fleet, Cabral was entitled to 10,000 <i>cruzados</i> (an old Portuguese currency equivalent to approximately 35 kg of gold) and the right to purchase 30 tonnes (33 short tons; 30 long tons) of <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">pepper</a> at his own expense for transport back to Europe. The pepper could then be resold, tax-free, to the Portuguese Crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199826_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199826-41">[36]</a></sup> He was also allowed to import 10 boxes of any other kind of spice, duty-free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199826_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199826-41">[36]</a></sup> Although the voyage was extremely hazardous, Cabral had the prospect of becoming a very rich man if he returned safely to Portugal with the cargo. Spices were then rare in Europe and keenly sought-after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199826_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199826-41">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>An earlier fleet had been the first to reach India by circumnavigating Africa. That expedition had been led by Vasco da Gama and returned to Portugal in 1499.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199888_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199888-42">[37]</a></sup> For decades Portugal had been searching for an alternate route to the East, in order to bypass the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> which was under the control of the Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_Republics" title="Maritime Republics">Maritime Republics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. Portugal's expansionism would lead first to a route to India, and later to worldwide colonization. A desire to spread Catholic Christianity to pagan lands was another factor motivating exploration. There also was a long tradition of pushing back Muslims, which stemmed from Portugal's fight for nationhood against the Moors. The fight expanded first to North Africa and eventually to the Indian subcontinent. An additional ambition which galvanized the explorers was the search for the mythical <a href="/wiki/Prester_John" title="Prester John">Prester John</a>—a powerful Christian king with whom an alliance against Islam could be forged. Finally, the Portuguese Crown sought a share in the lucrative West African trade in slaves and gold, and India's <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer200234.E2.80.9341_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer200234.E2.80.9341-43">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Departure_and_arrival_in_a_new_land">Departure and arrival in a new land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Departure and arrival in a new land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg"><img alt="A painting depicting the deck of a wooden sailing ship on which stands a group of men pointing toward the horizon and with the sails of several other ships visible in the background" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1748" data-file-width="2432" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg/250px-Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg/375px-Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg/500px-Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pedro_alvares_cabral_discovery_of_brazil_1500.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The fleet under the command of the 32–33-year-old Cabral departed from Lisbon on 9 March 1500 at noon. The previous day it had been given a public send-off which included a Mass and celebrations attended by the King, his court and a huge crowd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[39]</a></sup> On the morning of 14 March, the flotilla passed <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVianna199443_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVianna199443-45">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199842_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199842-46">[41]</a></sup> It sailed onward to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, a Portuguese colony situated on the West African coast, which was reached on 22 March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVianna199443_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVianna199443-45">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199843_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199843-47">[42]</a></sup> The next day, a <i>nau</i> commanded by <a href="/wiki/Vasco_de_Ata%C3%ADde" title="Vasco de Ataíde">Vasco de Ataíde</a> with 150 men disappeared without a trace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[43]</a></sup> The fleet crossed the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a> on 9 April, and sailed westward as far as possible from the African continent in what was known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Volta_do_mar" title="Volta do mar">volta do mar</a></i> (literally "turn of the sea") navigational technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977189_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977189-49">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199845_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199845-50">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">Seaweed</a> was sighted on 21 April, which led the sailors to believe that they were nearing the coast. They were proven correct the next afternoon, Wednesday 22 April 1500, when the fleet anchored near what Cabral christened the <a href="/wiki/Monte_Pascoal" title="Monte Pascoal">Monte Pascoal</a> ("Easter Mount", it being the week of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>). The spot is on the northeast coast of present-day Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Portuguese detected inhabitants on the shore, and all ships' captains gathered aboard Cabral's lead ship on 23 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199889_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199889-52">[47]</a></sup> Cabral ordered Nicolau Coelho, a captain who had experience from Vasco da Gama's voyage to India, to go ashore and make contact. He set foot on land and exchanged gifts with the indigenous people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199890_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199890-53">[48]</a></sup> After Coelho returned, Cabral took the fleet north, where after traveling 65 kilometres (40 mi) along the coast, it anchored on 24 April in what the commander-in-chief named <i><a href="/wiki/Porto_Seguro" title="Porto Seguro">Porto Seguro</a></i> (Safe Port).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVianna199444_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVianna199444-54">[49]</a></sup> The place was a natural harbor, and Afonso Lopes (pilot of the lead ship) brought two natives aboard to confer with Cabral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199895_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199895-55">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>As in the first contact, the meeting was friendly and Cabral presented the locals with gifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno199897_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno199897-56">[51]</a></sup> The inhabitants were <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">stone age</a> hunter-gatherers, to whom the Europeans assigned the generic label "<a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indians</a>". The men collected food by stalking game, fishing and foraging, while the women engaged in small-scale farming. They were divided into countless rival tribes. The tribe which Cabral met was the <a href="/wiki/Tupiniquim" title="Tupiniquim">Tupiniquim</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer200298.E2.80.93100_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer200298.E2.80.93100-57">[52]</a></sup> Some of these groups were nomadic and others sedentary—having a knowledge of fire but not metalworking. A few tribes engaged in cannibalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer200298_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer200298-58">[53]</a></sup> On 26 April, as more and more curious and friendly natives appeared, Cabral ordered his men to build an altar inland where a Christian Mass was held—the first celebrated on the soil of what would later become Brazil. He, along with the ships' crews, participated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998100_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998100-59">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following days were spent stockpiling water, food, wood and other provisions. The Portuguese also built a massive—perhaps 7 metres (23 ft) long—wooden cross. Cabral ascertained that the new land lay east of the demarcation line between Portugal and Spain that had been specified in the Treaty of Tordesillas. The territory was thus within the sphere allotted to Portugal. To solemnize Portugal's claim to the land, the wooden cross was erected and a second religious service held on 1 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVianna199444_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVianna199444-54">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998106.E2.80.93108_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998106.E2.80.93108-60">[55]</a></sup> In honor of the cross, Cabral named the newly discovered land <i><a href="/wiki/Ilha_de_Vera_Cruz" title="Ilha de Vera Cruz">Ilha de Vera Cruz</a></i> (Island of the True Cross).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998109_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998109-61">[56]</a></sup> The next day a supply ship under the command of either <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_de_Lemos" title="Gaspar de Lemos">Gaspar de Lemos</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998110_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998110-62">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xx_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xx-63">[58]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gon%C3%A7alves_(explorer)" title="André Gonçalves (explorer)">André Gonçalves</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191421_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191421-64">[59]</a></sup> (the sources conflict on who was sent)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977193_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977193-65">[60]</a></sup> returned to Portugal to apprise the King of the discovery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voyage_to_India">Voyage to India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Voyage to India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tragedy_off_southern_Africa">Tragedy off southern Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tragedy off southern Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg"><img alt="Pen and ink sketch depicting various sailing ships, some of which are in the process of foundering" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2040" data-file-width="1296" height="346" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg/220px-Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg/330px-Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg/440px-Pedro_Alvares_Cabral_fleet.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The fleet resumed its voyage on either 2<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977194_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977194-66">[61]</a></sup> or 3<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191421_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191421-64">[59]</a></sup> May 1500 and sailed along the east coast of South America. Cabral became convinced that he had found an entire continent, rather than an island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116-67">[62]</a></sup> Around 5 May, the fleet veered eastwards towards Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116-67">[62]</a></sup> On 23<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116-67">[62]</a></sup> or 24<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xx_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xx-63">[58]</a></sup> May they encountered a storm in the South Atlantic's high-pressure zone, resulting in the loss of four ships. The exact location of the disaster is unknown—speculations range from near the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> at the southern tip of the African continent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116_67-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998116-67">[62]</a></sup> to "within sight of the South American coast".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191423_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191423-68">[63]</a></sup> Three <i>naus</i> and a caravel commanded by Bartolomeu Dias—the first European to reach the Cape of Good Hope in 1488—foundered, and 380 men were lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998117_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998117-69">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remaining vessels, hindered by rough weather and damaged rigging, were separated. One ship that had been separated, commanded by Diogo Dias, wandered onward alone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi-70">[65]</a></sup> and the other six ships were able to regroup. They gathered into two formations consisting of three ships each, and Cabral's group sailed east, past the Cape of Good Hope. Fixing their position and sighting land, they turned north and landed somewhere in the <a href="/wiki/Primeiras_and_Segundas_Archipelago" title="Primeiras and Segundas Archipelago">Primeiras and Segundas Archipelago</a>, off East Africa and north of <a href="/wiki/Sofala" title="Sofala">Sofala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi-70">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191425_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191425-71">[66]</a></sup> The main fleet remained near Sofala ten days undergoing repairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi-70">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191426.E2.80.9327_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191426.E2.80.9327-72">[67]</a></sup> The expedition then went north, and on 26 May reached <a href="/wiki/Kilwa_Kisiwani" title="Kilwa Kisiwani">Kilwa Kisiwani</a>, where Cabral made an unsuccessful attempt to negotiate a treaty with its king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi.E2.80.93xxii_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxi.E2.80.93xxii-73">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>From Kilwa Kisiwani, the fleet departed to <a href="/wiki/Malindi" title="Malindi">Malindi</a>, which was reached on 2 August. Cabral met with its king, with whom he established friendly relations and exchanged gifts. Pilots were recruited at Malindi for the last leg to India and the fleet set sail. Land was reached at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anjadip_Island" title="Anjadip Island">Anjadip</a>, an island frequented by ships to obtain supplies on their way to Calicut. Here the ships were beached, recaulked and painted. Final arrangements were put into place for the encounter with the ruler of Calicut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Massacre_in_Calicut">Massacre in Calicut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Massacre in Calicut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fleet departed Anjadip and arrived in Calicut on 13 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[70]</a></sup> Cabral successfully negotiated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saamoothiri" title="Saamoothiri">Zamorin</a> (the title of the ruler of Calicut) and obtained permission to establish a factory and a warehouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191427_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191427-76">[71]</a></sup> In hopes of further improving relations, Cabral dispatched his men on several military missions at the Zamorin's request.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[F]</a></sup> However, on 16<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998119_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998119-78">[72]</a></sup> or 17<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191428_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191428-79">[73]</a></sup> December, the factory suffered a surprise attack by some 300<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998119_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998119-78">[72]</a></sup> (according to other accounts, perhaps as many as several thousand)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191427_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191427-76">[71]</a></sup> Muslim Arabs and Hindu Indians. Despite a desperate defense by crossbowmen, more than 50 Portuguese were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[G]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998119_78-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998119-78">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurup199710_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurup199710-81">[74]</a></sup> The remaining defenders retreated to the ships, some by swimming. Thinking that the attack was the result of unauthorized incitement by jealous Arab merchants, Cabral waited 24 hours for an explanation from the ruler of Calicut, but no apology was forthcoming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Portuguese were outraged by the attack on the factory and the death of their comrades and seized 10 Arab merchant ships at anchor in the harbor. Around 600 of their crews<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurup199710_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurup199710-81">[74]</a></sup> were killed and the cargoes confiscated before the merchantmen were set afire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191428_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191428-79">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiii_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiii-83">[76]</a></sup> Cabral also ordered his ships to bombard Calicut for an entire day in reprisal for the violation of the agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191428_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191428-79">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiii_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiii-83">[76]</a></sup> The massacre was blamed in part on Portuguese animosity towards Muslims, which had developed over centuries of conflict with the Moors on the Iberian peninsula and in North Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiv_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiv-84">[77]</a></sup> Moreover, the Portuguese were determined to dominate the spice trade and had no intention of allowing competition to flourish. The Arabs also had no desire to allow the Portuguese to break their monopoly on access to spices. The Portuguese had started out by insisting on being given preferential treatment in every aspect of the trade. The letter from King Manuel I brought by Cabral to the ruler of Calicut, which was translated by the ruler's Arab interpreters, sought the exclusion of Arab traders. The Muslim merchants believed that they were about to lose both their trading opportunities and livelihoods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxv_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxv-85">[78]</a></sup> and attempted to sway the Hindu ruler against the Portuguese. The Portuguese and Arabs were extremely suspicious of each other's every action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxvi.2C_xxxiii_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxvi.2C_xxxiii-86">[79]</a></sup></p>

The <i>nau</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">carrack</a>) was a type of vessel that was larger than a <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">caravel</a> but smaller than the later <a href="/wiki/Galleon" title="Galleon">galleon</a>. They were used in the voyages of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, Vasco da Gama and Cabral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer2002222.E2.80.93223gallery_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer2002222.E2.80.93223gallery-87">[80]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Historian William Greenlee has argued that the Portuguese realized that "they were few in numbers and that those who would come to India in the future fleets would always be at numerical disavantage; so that this treachery must be punished in a manner so decisive that the Portuguese would be feared and respected in the future. It was their superior artillery which would enable them to accomplish this end." Thus, they created a precedent for <a href="/wiki/Gunboat_diplomacy" title="Gunboat diplomacy">European behavior in Asia</a> during the following centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiv.2C_xxxiii_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxiv.2C_xxxiii-88">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Europe">Return to Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Return to Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Warnings in reports from Vasco da Gama's voyage to India had prompted King Manuel I to brief Cabral regarding another port to the south of Calicut where he could also trade. This city was <a href="/wiki/Kochi" title="Kochi">Kochi</a> and the fleet set sail, reaching it on 24 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxvii_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxvii-89">[82]</a></sup> Kochi was nominally a vassal of Calicut, as well as being dominated by other Indian cities. Kochi was eager to achieve independence, and the Portuguese were willing to exploit Indian disunity—as the British would three hundred years later. This tactic eventually ensured Portuguese hegemony over the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxvii_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxvii-89">[82]</a></sup> Cabral forged an alliance with Kochi's ruler, as well with rulers of other Indian cities, and was able to establish a factory. At last, loaded with precious spices, the fleet went to <a href="/wiki/Kannur" title="Kannur">Kannur</a> for further trade before setting out on its return voyage to Portugal on 16 January 1501.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The expedition headed for the east coast of Africa. One of the ships became stranded on a sandbar and the vessel began to founder. As there was no space in the other ships, its cargo was lost and Cabral ordered the carrack to be set on fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[84]</a></sup> The fleet then proceeded to the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Mozambique" title="Island of Mozambique">Island of Mozambique</a> (northeast of Sofala), in order to take on provisions and make the ships ready for the rough passage around the Cape of Good Hope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxix_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxix-92">[85]</a></sup> One caravel was sent to Sofala—another of the expedition's goals. A second caravel, considered the fastest ship in the fleet and captained by Nicolau Coelho, was sent ahead to give the King advance notice of the voyage's success. A third vessel, commanded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Ata%C3%ADde" title="Pedro de Ataíde">Pedro de Ataíde</a>, became separated from the fleet after leaving Mozambique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxix_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxix-92">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 May, the fleet—now reduced to only two ships—rounded the Cape of Good Hope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191435_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191435-93">[86]</a></sup> They arrived in Beseguiche (now <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, located near Cape Verde) on 2 June. There they found not only Nicolau Coelho's caravel but also the <i>nau</i> captained by Diogo Dias—which had been lost for over a year following the disaster in the South Atlantic. The <i>nau</i> had experienced several adventures of its own<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[H]</a></sup> and was now in poor condition with only seven sick and malnourished men aboard—one of whom was so weak that he died of happiness upon again seeing his comrades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998120_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998120-95">[87]</a></sup> Another Portuguese fleet was also found riding at anchor in Beseguiche. After Manuel I had been told of the discovery of what is now Brazil, he sent another and smaller fleet to explore it. One of its navigators was <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> (for whom the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> would be named), who told Cabral of his exploration, confirming that he had indeed made landfall on an entire continent and not merely an island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998121_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998121-96">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nicolau Coelho's caravel departed first from Beseguiche and arrived in Portugal on 23 June 1501.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998122_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998122-97">[89]</a></sup> Cabral stayed behind, waiting for Pedro de Ataíde's missing ship and for the caravel that had been sent to Sofala. Both eventually appeared and Cabral arrived in Portugal on 21 July 1501, with the other vessels coming home during the following days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxx_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxx-98">[90]</a></sup> In all, two ships returned empty, five were fully loaded and six were lost. Nonetheless, the cargoes carried by the fleet returned up to 800% profit to the Portuguese Crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001234_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEsp.C3.ADnola2001234-99">[91]</a></sup> Once sold, the proceeds covered the outlay in equipping the fleet, covered the cost of the vessels which had been lost, and cleared a profit which itself exceeded the total sum of those costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191431.E2.80.9332_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191431.E2.80.9332-100">[92]</a></sup> "Undeterred by the unprecedented losses which he had sustained", asserts historian James McClymont, when Cabral "reached the East African coast, pressed forward to the accomplishment of the task which had been assigned to him and was able to inspire the surviving officers and men with like courage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191435_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191435-93">[86]</a></sup> "Few voyages to Brazil and India were so well executed as Cabral's", affirmed historian <a href="/wiki/Bailey_W._Diffie" title="Bailey W. Diffie">Bailey Diffie</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977190_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977190-101">[93]</a></sup> which laid down a path leading to the immediate commencement "of a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese seagoing empire</a> from Africa to the far East", and eventually to "a <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">land empire in Brazil</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977194_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiffieWinius1977194-66">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years_and_death">Later years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg"><img alt="Photograph of a small chapel in which a roped-off inscribed stone slab is set into the floor and with a stone altar in the background on which are a wreath and a large metal cross" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg/220px-Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg/330px-Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg/440px-Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Igr_n_s_graca_9.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cabral's tomb in <a href="/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Portugal" title="Santarém, Portugal">Santarém, Portugal</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Upon Cabral's return, King Manuel I began planning another fleet to make the journey to India and to avenge the Portuguese losses in Calicut. Cabral was selected to command this "<a href="/wiki/4th_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Gama,_1502)" title="4th Portuguese India Armada (Gama, 1502)">Revenge Fleet</a>", as it was called. For eight months Cabral made all preparations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliii_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliii-102">[94]</a></sup> but for reasons which remain uncertain, he was relieved of command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998125_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998125-103">[95]</a></sup> It had apparently been proposed to give another navigator, <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Sodr%C3%A9" title="Vicente Sodré">Vicente Sodré</a>, independent command over a section of the fleet, and Cabral strongly opposed this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[96]</a></sup> Whether he was dismissed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewitt200567_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewitt200567-105">[97]</a></sup> or requested himself that he be relieved of command,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191432_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191432-106">[98]</a></sup> the result was that when the fleet departed in March 1502, its commander was Vasco da Gama—a maternal nephew of Vicente Sodré—and not Cabral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[99]</a></sup> It is known that hostility had developed between a faction supporting da Gama and another supporting Cabral. At some point, Cabral left the court permanently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliii_102-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliii-102">[94]</a></sup> The King was greatly irritated by the feud, to such an extent that mentioning the matter in his presence could result in banishment, as it did for one of da Gama's supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv-108">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the loss of favor with Manuel I,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998125_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998125-103">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAbramo196942_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbramo196942-109">[101]</a></sup> Cabral was able to contract an advantageous marriage in 1503<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv-108">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPresser2006249_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPresser2006249-110">[102]</a></sup> to <i>Dona</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lady" title="Lady">Lady</a>) Isabel de Castro, a wealthy noblewoman and descendant of King Dom <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Portugal" title="Ferdinand I of Portugal">Fernando I of Portugal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv_108-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv-108">[100]</a></sup> Her mother was a sister of <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a>, one of the greatest Portuguese military leaders during the Age of Discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[103]</a></sup> The couple had at least four children: two boys (Fernão Álvares Cabral and António Cabral) and two girls (Catarina de Castro and Guiomar de Castro).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont19143_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont19143-112">[104]</a></sup> There were two additional daughters named Isabel and Leonor according to other sources, which also say that Guiomar, Isabel and Leonor joined religious orders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xlv_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xlv-113">[105]</a></sup> Afonso de Albuquerque attempted to intercede on Cabral's behalf and on 2 December 1514 asked Manuel I to forgive him and allow his return to court, but to no avail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suffering from recurrent fever and a tremor (possibly <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>) since his voyage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998126_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998126-115">[107]</a></sup> Cabral withdrew to Santarém in 1509. He spent his remaining years there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESubrahmanyam1997177_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESubrahmanyam1997177-17">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAbramo196942_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbramo196942-109">[101]</a></sup> Only sketchy information is available as to his activities during that time. According to a royal letter dated 17 December 1509, Cabral was party to a dispute over a transaction involving property which belonged to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv_108-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv-108">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191432.E2.80.9333_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191432.E2.80.9333-116">[108]</a></sup> Another letter of that same year reported that he was to receive certain privileges for an undisclosed military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191433_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191433-21">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv_108-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xliv-108">[100]</a></sup> In 1518, or perhaps previously, he was raised from <i>fidalgo</i> to knight in the King's Council and was entitled to a monthly allowance of 2,437 <i>reais</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[109]</a></sup> This was in addition to the annual allowance granted to him in 1497, and still being paid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191433_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191433-21">[16]</a></sup> Cabral died of unspecified causes, most probably in 1520. He was buried in the São João Evangelista chapel of the <a href="/wiki/Igreja_da_Gra%C3%A7a_(Santar%C3%A9m)" title="Igreja da Graça (Santarém)">Convento da Graça</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Portugal" title="Santarém, Portugal">Santarém</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[110]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_rehabilitation">Posthumous rehabilitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Posthumous rehabilitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cantino_planisphere_(1502).jpg"><img alt="Photograph showing an old map which covers the area from the Atlantic coast of the South American continent eastwards around Africa to India and on to the Malay peninsula at the eastern edge of the map, with Europe in the far north." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4998" data-file-width="10643" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Cantino_planisphere_%281502%29.jpg/400px-Cantino_planisphere_%281502%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Cantino_planisphere_%281502%29.jpg/600px-Cantino_planisphere_%281502%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Cantino_planisphere_%281502%29.jpg/800px-Cantino_planisphere_%281502%29.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>

<p>The first permanent Portuguese settlement in the land which would become Brazil was <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Vicente,_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Vicente, São Paulo">São Vicente</a>, which was established in 1532 by <a href="/wiki/Martim_Afonso_de_Sousa" title="Martim Afonso de Sousa">Martim Afonso de Sousa</a>. As the years passed, the Portuguese would slowly expand their frontiers westward, conquering more lands from both indigenous Americans and the Spanish. Brazil had secured most of its present-day borders by 1750 and was regarded by Portugal as the most important part of its far-flung <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">maritime Empire</a>. On 7 September 1822, the heir of Portuguese King Dom <a href="/wiki/John_VI_of_Portugal" title="John VI of Portugal">João VI</a> secured the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Brazilian Declaration of Independence">independence of Brazil</a> from Portugal and, as Dom <a href="/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil" title="Pedro I of Brazil">Pedro I</a>, became its first Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERevista_Trimestral_de_Hist.C3.B3ria_e_Geografia1840137_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERevista_Trimestral_de_Hist.C3.B3ria_e_Geografia1840137-119">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVieira200028.E2.80.9329_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVieira200028.E2.80.9329-120">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cabral's discovery, and even his resting place in the land of his birth, had been almost completely forgotten during the span of nearly 300 years since his expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERevista_Trimestral_de_Hist.C3.B3ria_e_Geografia1840137_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERevista_Trimestral_de_Hist.C3.B3ria_e_Geografia1840137-119">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVieira200028.E2.80.9329_120-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVieira200028.E2.80.9329-120">[112]</a></sup> This began to change beginning in the 1840s when Emperor Dom Pedro II, successor and son of Pedro I, sponsored research and publications dealing with Cabral's life and expedition through the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Historic_and_Geographic_Institute" title="Brazilian Historic and Geographic Institute">Brazilian Historic and Geographic Institute</a>. This was part of the Emperor's ambitious larger plan to foster and strengthen a sense of nationalism among Brazil's diverse citizenry—giving them a common identity and history as residents of a <a href="/wiki/Exceptionalism" title="Exceptionalism">unique</a> <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>-speaking empire, surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Hispanic-American Republics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchwarcz1998126_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchwarcz1998126-121">[113]</a></sup> The initial resurgence of interest in Cabral had resulted from the rediscovery, in 1839, of his resting place by the Brazilian historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Adolfo_de_Varnhagen" title="Francisco Adolfo de Varnhagen">Francisco Adolfo de Varnhagen</a> (later Viscount of Porto Seguro).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998126_115-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998126-115">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975985_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975985-122">[114]</a></sup> The completely neglected state in which Cabral's tomb was found nearly led to a diplomatic crisis between Brazil and Portugal—the latter then ruled by Pedro II's eldest sister, <a href="/wiki/Maria_II_of_Portugal" title="Maria II of Portugal">Maria II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998130_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998130-123">[115]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1871 the Brazilian Emperor—then on a trip to Europe—visited Cabral's gravesite and proposed an exhumation for scientific study, which was carried out in 1882.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975985_122-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975985-122">[114]</a></sup> In a second exhumation during 1896, an urn containing earth and bone fragments was allowed to be removed. Although his remains still lay in Portugal, the urn was eventually brought to the old <a href="/wiki/Old_Cathedral_of_Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Old Cathedral of Rio de Janeiro">Cathedral of Rio de Janeiro</a> in Brazil on 30 December 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975985_122-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975985-122">[114]</a></sup> Cabral has since become a national hero in Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith19905_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith19905-124">[116]</a></sup> In Portugal, however, he has been much overshadowed by his rival Vasco da Gama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlves_Filho1997195_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlves_Filho1997195-125">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerrini2000168_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerrini2000168-126">[118]</a></sup> Historian William Greenlee argued that Cabral's exploration is important "not only because of its position in the history of geography but because of its influence on the history and economics of the period." Though he acknowledges that few voyages have "been of greater importance to posterity", he also says that "few have been less appreciated in their time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxxiv_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenlee1995xxxiv-127">[119]</a></sup> Nevertheless, historian James McClymont affirmed that "Cabral's position in the history of Portuguese conquest and discovery is inexpungable despite the supremacy of greater or more fortunate men."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191436_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191436-128">[120]</a></sup> He concluded that Cabral "will always be remembered in history as the chief, if not the first discoverer of Brazil."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191436_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcClymont191436-128">[120]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intentional_discovery_hypothesis">Intentional discovery hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Intentional discovery hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A controversy that has occupied scholars for more than a century concerns whether Cabral's discovery was by chance or intentional. If the latter, that would mean that the Portuguese had at least some hint that a land existed to the west. The matter was first raised by Emperor Pedro II in 1854 during a session of the Brazilian Historic and Geographic Institute, when he asked if the discovery might have been intentional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPereira197954_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPereira197954-129">[121]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until the 1854 conference, the widespread presumption was that the discovery had been an accident. Early works on the subject supported this view, including <i>História do Descobrimento e Conquista da Índia</i> (History of the Discovery and Conquest of India, published in 1541) by <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_Lopes_de_Castanheda" title="Fernão Lopes de Castanheda">Fernão Lopes de Castanheda</a>, <i>Décadas da Ásia</i> (Decades of Asia, 1552) by <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Barros" title="João de Barros">João de Barros</a>, <i>Crônicas do Felicíssimo Rei D. Manuel</i> (Chronicles of the most fortunate D. Manuel, 1558) by <a href="/wiki/Dami%C3%A3o_de_G%C3%B3is" title="Damião de Góis">Damião de Góis</a>, <i>Lendas da Índia</i> (Legends of India, 1561) by <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Correia" title="Gaspar Correia">Gaspar Correia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998127_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998127-130">[122]</a></sup> <i>História do Brasil</i> (History of Brazil, 1627) by friar Vicente do Salvador and <i>História da América Portuguesa</i> (History of Portuguese America, 1730) by Sebastião da Rocha Pita.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVainfas2001183_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVainfas2001183-131">[123]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first work to advocate the idea of intentionality was published in 1854 by Joaquim Noberto de Sousa e Silva, after Pedro II had opened the debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998129_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998129-132">[124]</a></sup> Since then, several scholars have subscribed to that view, including Francisco Adolfo de Varnhagen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998130_123-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998130-123">[115]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Capistrano_de_Abreu" title="Capistrano de Abreu">Capistrano de Abreu</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998130_123-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998130-123">[115]</a></sup> Pedro Calmon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon198151_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon198151-133">[125]</a></sup> Fábio Ramos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERamos2008168_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERamos2008168-134">[126]</a></sup> and Mário Barata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarata199146_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarata199146-135">[127]</a></sup> Historian Hélio Vianna affirmed that "although there are signs of the intentionality" in Cabral's discovery, "based mainly in the knowledge or previous suspicion of the existence of lands at the edge of the South Atlantic", there are no irrefutable proofs to support it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVianna199419_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVianna199419-136">[128]</a></sup> This opinion is also shared by historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Skidmore" title="Thomas Skidmore">Thomas Skidmore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkidmore200321_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkidmore200321-137">[129]</a></sup> The debate on whether it was a deliberate voyage of discovery or not is considered "irrelevant" by historian <a href="/wiki/C._R._Boxer" title="C. R. Boxer">Charles R. Boxer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer200298_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer200298-58">[53]</a></sup> Historian Anthony Smith concludes that the conflicting contentions will "probably never be resolved".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith19909_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith19909-138">[130]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forerunners">Forerunners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Forerunners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG"><img alt="An old, hand-drawn map with a large compass rose on the right side and a coastline drawn on the left side with writing occupying the center" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="316" data-file-width="760" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG/220px-TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG/330px-TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG/440px-TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TextoVYP_mapaLaCosa.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Map_of_Juan_de_la_Cosa" title="Map of Juan de la Cosa">map of Juan de la Cosa</a>, dated 1500, mentions the travel to northern Brazil by <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Y%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Pinz%C3%B3n" title="Vicente Yáñez Pinzón">Vicente Yáñez Pinzón</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cabral was not the first European to stumble upon areas of present-day Brazil, not to mention other parts of South America. Roman coins have been found in today's <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, northwest of Brazil, presumably from ships that were carried away by storm in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient times</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer200231_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer200231-139">[131]</a></sup> Norsemen reached North America and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">established settlements</a>, though these ended in failure sometime before the end of the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoxer200231_139-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoxer200231-139">[131]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, on his third voyage to the New World in 1498, traveled along part of what would later become Venezuela.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarata199146_135-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarata199146-135">[127]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the case of Brazil, it was once considered probable that the Portuguese navigator <a href="/wiki/Duarte_Pacheco_Pereira" title="Duarte Pacheco Pereira">Duarte Pacheco Pereira</a> had made a voyage to the Brazilian coast in 1498. This belief has since been dismissed, however, and it is now thought that he voyaged to North America instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[132]</a></sup> There is more certain evidence that two Spaniards, <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Y%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Pinz%C3%B3n" title="Vicente Yáñez Pinzón">Vicente Yáñez Pinzón</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_de_Lepe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diego de Lepe (page does not exist)">Diego de Lepe</a>, traveled along the northern coast of Brazil between January and March 1500. Pinzón went from what is today <a href="/wiki/Fortaleza" title="Fortaleza">Fortaleza</a> (capital of the Brazilian state of <a href="/wiki/Cear%C3%A1" title="Ceará">Ceará</a>) to the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a>. There he encountered another Spanish expedition led by Lepe, which would reach as far as the <a href="/wiki/Oyapock" title="Oyapock">Oyapock</a> River in March. The reason Cabral is credited with having discovered Brazil, rather than the Spanish explorers, is because the visits by Pinzón and Lepe were cursory and had no lasting impact. Historians <a href="/wiki/Capistrano_de_Abreu" title="Capistrano de Abreu">Capistrano de Abreu</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBueno1998132_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBueno1998132-141">[133]</a></sup> Francisco Adolfo de Varnhagen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVarnhagen81_142-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarnhagen81-142">[134]</a></sup> Mário Barata<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarata199147.E2.80.9348_143-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarata199147.E2.80.9348-143">[135]</a></sup> and Hélio Vianna<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVianna199446_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVianna199446-144">[136]</a></sup> concur that the Spanish expeditions did not influence the development of what would become the only Portuguese-speaking nation in the Americas—with a unique history, culture and society which sets it apart from the Hispanic-American societies which dominate the rest of the continent.</p>
