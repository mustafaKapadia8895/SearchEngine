<p><b>Newfoundland</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/nj/: 'n' in 'new'">nj</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Newfoundland.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Newfoundland.ogg" title="Newfoundland.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><small title="English pronunciation respelling"><i>new-fən-<b>LAND</b></i></small></a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Terre-Neuve</i></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> island off the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> mainland, and the most populous part of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">Canadian province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>. It has 29 percent of the province's land area. The island is separated from the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Peninsula" title="Labrador Peninsula">Labrador Peninsula</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Belle_Isle" title="Strait of Belle Isle">Strait of Belle Isle</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Breton_Island" title="Cape Breton Island">Cape Breton Island</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Cabot_Strait" title="Cabot Strait">Cabot Strait</a>. It blocks the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a>, creating the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Saint_Lawrence" title="Gulf of Saint Lawrence">Gulf of Saint Lawrence</a>, the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a>. Newfoundland's nearest neighbour is the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collectivit%C3%A9_d%27outre-mer" title="Collectivité d'outre-mer">overseas community</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint-Pierre and Miquelon">Saint-Pierre and Miquelon</a>.</p>
<p>With an area of 108,860 square kilometres (42,031 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atlas_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-atlas-7">[7]</a></sup> Newfoundland is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">world's 16th-largest island</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Canadian_islands_by_area" title="List of Canadian islands by area">Canada's fourth-largest island</a>, and the largest Canadian island outside <a href="/wiki/Northern_Canada" title="Northern Canada">the North</a>. The provincial capital, <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador">St. John's</a>, is located on the southeastern coast of the island; <a href="/wiki/Cape_Spear" title="Cape Spear">Cape Spear</a>, just south of the capital, is the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_North_America" title="Extreme points of North America">easternmost point of North America</a>, excluding Greenland. It is common to consider all directly neighbouring islands such as New World, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twillingate,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Twillingate, Newfoundland and Labrador">Twillingate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fogo_Island,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Fogo Island, Newfoundland and Labrador">Fogo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bell_Island_(Newfoundland_and_Labrador)" title="Bell Island (Newfoundland and Labrador)">Bell Island</a> to be 'part of Newfoundland' (as distinct from Labrador). By that classification, Newfoundland and its associated small islands have a total area of 111,390 square kilometres (43,008 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NF_gov_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NF_gov-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to 2006 official Census Canada statistics, 57% of responding Newfoundland and Labradorians claim British or Irish ancestry, with 43.2% claiming at least one English parent, 21.5% at least one Irish parent, and 7% at least one parent of Scottish origin. Additionally 6.1% claimed at least one parent of French ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006_Statistics_Canada_National_Census:_Newfoundland_and_Labrador_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-2006_Statistics_Canada_National_Census:_Newfoundland_and_Labrador-9">[9]</a></sup> The island's total population as of the 2006 census was 479,105.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newfoundland_(island)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Long settled by indigenous peoples of the <a href="/wiki/Dorset_culture" title="Dorset culture">Dorset culture</a>, the island was visited by the Icelandic Viking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leif_Eriksson" title="Leif Eriksson">Leif Eriksson</a> in the 11th century, who called the new land "<a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The next European visitors to Newfoundland were Portuguese, Basque, Spanish, French and English migratory fishermen. The island was visited by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> navigator <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> (Giovanni Caboto), working under contract to <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">King Henry VII</a> of England on his expedition from <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> in 1497. In 1501, Portuguese explorers <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Corte-Real" title="Gaspar Corte-Real">Gaspar Corte-Real</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Corte-Real" title="Miguel Corte-Real">Miguel Corte-Real</a> charted part of the coast of Newfoundland in a failed attempt to find the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>. (After European settlement, colonists first called the island <i>Terra Nova</i>, from "New Land" in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>.)</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gilbert_plaque.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="663" data-file-width="651" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gilbert_plaque.jpg/150px-Gilbert_plaque.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gilbert_plaque.jpg/225px-Gilbert_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gilbert_plaque.jpg/300px-Gilbert_plaque.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gilbert_plaque.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plaque commemorating Gilbert's founding of the British Empire</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On August 5, 1583, Sir <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Gilbert" title="Humphrey Gilbert">Humphrey Gilbert</a> claimed <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_Colony" title="Newfoundland Colony">Newfoundland as England's first overseas colony</a> under Royal Charter of Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>, thus officially establishing a fore-runner to the much later British Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sir_Humphrey_Gilbert_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sir_Humphrey_Gilbert-10">[10]</a></sup> Newfoundland is considered Britain's oldest colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_British_Empire:_The_Map_Room_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_British_Empire:_The_Map_Room-11">[11]</a></sup> At the time of English settlement, the <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a> inhabited the island.</p>
<p>While there is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> evidence of ancient indigenous peoples on the island, it was abandoned when the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> arrived from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">Norse settlement</a> near the northernmost tip of Newfoundland (Cape Norman), which has been dated to be approximately 1,000 years old. The site is considered the only undisputed evidence of Pre-Columbian contact between the Old and New Worlds, if the Norse-<a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> contact on Greenland is not counted. There is a second suspected Norse site in <a href="/wiki/Point_Rosee" title="Point Rosee">Point Rosee</a>. The island is a likely location of <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, mentioned in the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Viking_Chronicles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Viking Chronicles (page does not exist)">Viking Chronicles</a></i>, although this has been disputed.</p>
<p>The indigenous people on the island at the time of European settlement were the <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a>, who spoke an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">Amerindian language</a> <a href="/wiki/Beothuk_language" title="Beothuk language">of the same name</a>. Later immigrants developed a variety of dialects associated with settlement on the island: <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_English" title="Newfoundland English">Newfoundland English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_French" title="Newfoundland French">Newfoundland French</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the 19th century, it also had a dialect of Irish known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland_Irish" title="Newfoundland Irish">Newfoundland Irish</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> was spoken on the island during the 19th and early 20th centuries, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Codroy_Valley" title="Codroy Valley">Codroy Valley</a> area, chiefly by settlers from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Breton_Island" title="Cape Breton Island">Cape Breton Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Gaelic names reflected the association with fishing: in Scottish Gaelic, it was called <i>Eilean a' Trosg,</i> or literally, "Island of the Cod".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Similarly, the Irish Gaelic name <i>Talamh an Éisc</i> means "Land of the Fish".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_inhabitants">First inhabitants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newfoundland_(island)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First inhabitants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first inhabitants of Newfoundland were the <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Eskimo" title="Paleo-Eskimo">Paleo-Eskimo</a>, who have no known link to other groups in Newfoundland history. Little is known about them beyond <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeological" title="Archeological">archeological</a> evidence of early settlements. Evidence of successive cultures have been found. The Late Paleo-Eskimo, or <a href="/wiki/Dorset_culture" title="Dorset culture">Dorset culture</a>, settled there about 4,000 years ago. They were descendants of migrations of ancient prehistoric peoples across the High Arctic thousands of years ago, after crossing from <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bering_land_bridge" title="Bering land bridge">Bering land bridge</a>. The Dorset died off or abandoned the island prior to the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After this period, the <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a> settled Newfoundland, migrating from Labrador on the mainland. There is no evidence that the Beothuk inhabited the island prior to Norse settlement. Scholars believe that the Beothuk are related closely to the <a href="/wiki/Innu" title="Innu">Innu</a> of Labrador.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The tribe later became extinct although people of partial Beothuk descent have been documented<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The name <i><a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a></i> means "people" in their own language which is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian language</a> family which itself is common to many Atlantic coastal tribes.</p>
<p>The tribe is now extinct but evidence of its culture is preserved in museum, historical and archaeological records. <a href="/wiki/Shanawdithit" title="Shanawdithit">Shanawdithit</a>, a woman who is often regarded as the last full-blood Beothuk, died in St. John's in 1829 of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. However, Santu Toney, who was born around 1835 and died in 1910, was a woman of mixed Mi'kmaq and Beothuk descent which means that some Beothuk must have lived on beyond 1829. Her father was a Beothuk and mother a Mi'kmaq, both from Newfoundland. The Beothuk may have intermingled and assimilated with <a href="/wiki/Innu" title="Innu">Innu</a> in Labrador and Mi'kmaq in Newfoundland. Oral histories also suggest potential historical competition and hostility between the Beothuk and Mi'kmaq.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Mi'kmaq, Innu and <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> all hunted and fished around Newfoundland before the arrival of Europeans but no evidence indicates that they lived on the island for long periods of time and would only travel to Newfoundland temporarily. Inuit have been documented on the Great Northern Peninsula as late as the 18th-Century. Newfoundland was historically the southernmost part of the Inuit's territorial range.</p>
<p>When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europeans" title="Europeans">Europeans</a> arrived from 1497 and later, starting with <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a>, they established contact with the Beothuk. Estimates of the number of Beothuk on the island at this time vary, ranging from 700 to 5,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Later both the <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> settled the island. They were followed by the <a href="/wiki/Mi%27kmaq" title="Mi'kmaq">Mi'kmaq</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a>-speaking indigenous people from eastern Canada and present-day Nova Scotia. As European and Mi'kmaq settlement became year-round and expanded to new areas of the coast, the area available to the Beothuk to harvest the marine resources they relied upon was diminished. By the beginning of the 19th century, few Beothuk remained. Most died due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a> carried by Europeans, to which they had no <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">immunity</a>, and starvation. Government attempts to engage with the Beothuk and aid them came too late. The Beothuk were exceptionally hostile to foreigners, unlike the Mi'kmaq. The latter readily traded with Europeans and became established in settlements in Newfoundland.</p>
<h3><span id="European_contact,_colonisation_and_settlement"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="European_contact.2C_colonisation_and_settlement">European contact, colonisation and settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newfoundland_(island)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European contact, colonisation and settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="History of Newfoundland and Labrador">History of Newfoundland and Labrador</a></div>

<p>Newfoundland is the site of the only authenticated <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> settlement in North America. This archeological site was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Helge_Ingstad" title="Helge Ingstad">Helge Ingstad</a> and his wife, archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Anne_Stine_Ingstad" title="Anne Stine Ingstad">Anne Stine Ingstad</a>, at <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a> in 1960. The site has been the subject of multi-year <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> digs in the 1960s and 1970s.</p>
<p>These have revealed that the settlement dated to more than 500 years before <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a>; it contains the earliest-known European structures in North America. Designated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" title="World Heritage site">World Heritage site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>, it is believed to be the <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a> settlement of explorer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leif_Ericson" title="Leif Ericson">Leif Eiriksson</a>. (The Icelandic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1lholt_Map" title="Skálholt Map">Skálholt Vinland Map</a> of 1570 refers to the area as "Promontorium Winlandiæ" and correctly shows it on a 51°N parallel with <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, England). The Norse stayed for a relatively short period of time, believed to be between 999 and 1001 AD.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cabot_tower.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cabot_tower.jpg/250px-Cabot_tower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cabot_tower.jpg/375px-Cabot_tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cabot_tower.jpg/500px-Cabot_tower.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cabot_tower.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabot_Tower_(Newfoundland)" title="Cabot Tower (Newfoundland)">Cabot Tower</a> located in <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador">St John's</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before and after the departure of the Norse, the island was inhabited by aboriginal populations. About 500 years later, in 1497, the Italian navigator <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> (Zuan/Giovanni Cabotto) became the first European since the Norse settlers to set foot on Newfoundland, working under commission of King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII of England</a>. His landing site is unknown but popularly believed to be <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bonavista" title="Cape Bonavista">Cape Bonavista</a>, along the island's East coast.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kevin_major_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kevin_major-14">[14]</a></sup> Another site claimed is <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bauld" title="Cape Bauld">Cape Bauld</a>, at the tip of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_Peninsula" title="Great Northern Peninsula">Great Northern Peninsula</a>. A document found in the Spanish National Archives, written by a Bristol merchant, reports that Cabot's crew landed 1,800 miles (2,900 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Dursey_Island" title="Dursey Island">Dursey Head</a>, Ireland (latitude 51° 35'N), which would put Cabot within sight of Cape Bauld. This document mentions an island that Cabot sailed past to go ashore on the mainland. This description fits with the Cape Bauld theory, as <a href="/wiki/Belle_Isle_(Newfoundland_and_Labrador)" title="Belle Isle (Newfoundland and Labrador)">Belle Isle</a> is not far offshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kevin_major_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-kevin_major-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Cabot, the first European visitors to Newfoundland were Portuguese, Basque, Spanish, French and English migratory fishermen. In 1501, Portuguese explorers <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Corte-Real" title="Gaspar Corte-Real">Gaspar Corte-Real</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Corte-Real" title="Miguel Corte-Real">Miguel Corte-Real</a> charted part of the coast of Newfoundland in a failed attempt to find the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>. Late in the 17th century came Irish fishermen, who named the island <b>Talamh an Éisc</b>, meaning "land of the fish", or "the fishing grounds" in <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish Gaelic</a>. This reflected the abundance of fisheries.</p>
<p>In 1583, when Sir <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Gilbert" title="Humphrey Gilbert">Humphrey Gilbert</a> formally claimed Newfoundland as a colony of England, he found numerous English, French and Portuguese vessels at St. John's. There was no permanent population. Gilbert was lost at sea during his return voyage, and plans of settlement were postponed.</p>
<p>On July 5, 1610, <a href="/wiki/John_Guy_(governor)" title="John Guy (governor)">John Guy</a> set sail from <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, England with 39 other colonists for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuper%27s_Cove,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Cuper's Cove, Newfoundland and Labrador">Cuper's Cove</a>. This, and other early attempts at permanent settlement failed to make a profit for the English investors, but some settlers remained, forming the very earliest modern European population on the island. By 1620, the fishermen of England's <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> dominated the east coast of Newfoundland. French fishermen dominated the island's south coast and Northern Peninsula.</p>

<p>After 1713, with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht" title="Treaty of Utrecht">Treaty of Utrecht</a>, the French ceded control of south and north shores of the island to the British. They kept only the nearby islands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="St. Pierre and Miquelon">St. Pierre and Miquelon</a>, located in the fish-rich <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks</a> off the south coast. Despite some early settlements by the English, the Crown discouraged permanent, year-round settlement of Newfoundland by migratory fishery workers. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nash_(Newfoundland)" title="Thomas Nash (Newfoundland)">Thomas Nash</a> was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">Irish Catholic</a> fisherman who permanently settled in Newfoundland despite English rule. He established the fishing town of <a href="/wiki/Branch,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Branch, Newfoundland and Labrador">Branch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He and his cousin Father Patrick Power of <a href="/wiki/Callan,_County_Kilkenny" title="Callan, County Kilkenny">Callan</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Kilkenny" title="County Kilkenny">County Kilkenny</a>, spread Catholicism in Newfoundland. This settlement attracted a major migration of Irish Catholic immigrants to Newfoundland in the early eighteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the late 18th century, permanent settlement increased, peaking in the early years of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French name for the island is <b>Terre Neuve.</b> The name "Newfoundland"' is one of the oldest European place names in Canada in continuous geographical and <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartographical</a> use, dating from a 1502 letter. It was stated in the following 1628 poem:</p>
<p><i>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skeltonic_verse" title="Skeltonic verse">Skeltonicall</a> continued ryme, in praise of my New-found-Land</i></p>
<dl>
<dd>Although in cloaths, company, buildings faire</dd>
<dd>With England, New-found-land cannot compare:</dd>
<dd>Did some know what contentment I found there,</dd>
<dd>Alwayes enough, most times somewhat to spare,</dd>
<dd>With little paines, lesse toyle, and lesser care,</dd>
<dd>Exempt from taxings, ill newes, Lawing, feare,</dd>
<dd>If cleane, and warme, no matter what you weare,</dd>
<dd>Healthy, and wealthy, if men careful are,</dd>
<dd>With much-much more, then I will now declare,</dd>
<dd>(I say) if some wise men knew what this were</dd>
<dd>(I doe beleeue) they'd live no other where.</dd>
</dl>

<dl>

<dd>Composed and done at <i>Harbor-Grace</i> in</dd>
<dd><i>Britaniola</i>, anciently called <i>Newfound-Land</i></dd>

</dl>
</dd>
</dl>

<p>The European immigrants, mostly English, Scots, Irish and French, built a society in the New World unlike the ones they had left. It was also different from those other immigrants would build on the North American mainland. As a fish-exporting society, Newfoundland was in contact with many ports and societies around the Atlantic rim. But its geographic location and political distinctiveness isolated it from its closest neighbours, Canada and the United States. Internally, most of its population was spread widely around a rugged coastline in small outport settlements. Many were distant from larger centres of population and isolated for long periods by winter ice or bad weather. These conditions had an effect on the cultures of the immigrants. They generated new ways of thinking and acting. Newfoundland and Labrador developed a wide variety of distinctive customs, beliefs, stories, songs and dialects.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> had a powerful and lasting effect on the society. From a population of about a quarter of a million, 5,482 men went overseas. Nearly 1,500 were killed and 2,300 wounded. On July 1, 1916, at Beaumont-Hamel, France, 753 men of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Newfoundland_Regiment" title="Royal Newfoundland Regiment">Royal Newfoundland Regiment</a> went over the top of a trench. The next morning, only 68 men answered the roll-call. Even now, when the rest of Canada celebrates the founding of the country on July 1, many Newfoundlanders take part in solemn ceremonies of remembrance.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> also had a lasting effect on Newfoundland. In particular, the United States assigned forces to the military bases at Argentia, Gander, Stephenville, Goose Bay and St. John's.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Newfoundland and Labrador is the youngest province in Canada. Newfoundland was organised as a colony in 1825, was self-governing from 1855–1934, and held dominion status from 1907–1949 (see <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Dominion of Newfoundland</a>). In late 1948, the population of the colony voted 52.3% to 47.7%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in favour of joining Canada as a province. Opposition was concentrated among residents of the capital St. John's, and on the <a href="/wiki/Avalon_Peninsula" title="Avalon Peninsula">Avalon Peninsula</a>.</p>
<p>Newfoundland joined Canada on March 31, 1949. Union with Canada has done little to reduce Newfoundlanders' self-image as a unique group. In 2003, 72% of residents responding identified first as Newfoundlanders, secondarily as Canadians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Separatist sentiment is low, though, less than 12% in the same 2003 study.</p>
<p>The referendum campaign of 1948 was bitterly fought, and interests in both Canada and Britain favoured and supported confederation with Canada. <a href="/wiki/Jack_Pickersgill" title="Jack Pickersgill">Jack Pickersgill</a>, a western Canadian native and politician, worked with the confederation camp during the campaign. The Catholic Church, whose members were a minority on the island, lobbied for continued independence. Canada offered financial incentives, including a "baby bonus" for each child in a family. The Confederates were led by the charismatic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Smallwood" title="Joseph Smallwood">Joseph Smallwood</a>, a former radio broadcaster, who had developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> political inclinations while working for a socialist newspaper in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. His policies as premier were closer to liberalism than socialism.</p>
<p>Following confederation, Smallwood led Newfoundland for decades as the elected premier. He was said to have a "cult of personality" among his many supporters. Some residents featured photographs of "Joey" in their living rooms in a place of prominence.</p>

<p>The first flag to specifically represent Newfoundland is thought to have been an image of a green fir tree on a pink background that was in use in the early 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THE_PROVINCES_Chap_XIX_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-THE_PROVINCES_Chap_XIX-20">[20]</a></sup> The first official flag identifying Newfoundland, flown by vessels in service of the colonial government, was the Newfoundland Blue Ensign, adopted in 1870 and used until 1904, when it was modified slightly. In 1904, the crown of the Blue Ensign was replaced with the Great Seal of Newfoundland (having been given royal approval in 1827) and the British Parliament designated Newfoundland Red and Blue ensigns as official flags specifically for Newfoundland. The Red and Blue ensigns with the Great Seal of Newfoundland in the fly were used officially from 1904 until 1965, with the Red Ensign being flown as <a href="/wiki/Civil_ensign" title="Civil ensign">civil ensign</a> by merchant shipping, and the Blue being flown by governmental ships (after the British tradition of having different flags for merchant/naval and government vessel identification).</p>
<p>On September 26, 1907, King Edward VII of the United Kingdom declared the Colony of Newfoundland, as an independent Dominion within the British Empire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-God_Guard_Thee.2C_Newfoundland_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-God_Guard_Thee.2C_Newfoundland-21">[21]</a></sup> and from that point until 1965, the Newfoundland Red Ensign was used as the civil ensign of the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Dominion of Newfoundland</a> with the Blue Ensign, again, reserved for government shipping identification. In 1931 the Newfoundland National Assembly adopted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Flag" title="Union Flag">Union Jack</a> as the official national flag, with the Red and Blue Ensigns retained as ensigns for shipping identification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historic_Flags_of_Newfoundland_.28Canada.29_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Historic_Flags_of_Newfoundland_.28Canada.29-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>On March 31, 1949, Newfoundland became a province of Canada but retained the Union Jack in legislature, still designating it as the "national" flag. This was later reaffirmed by the Revised Statutes Act of 1952, and the Union Jack remained the official flag of Newfoundland until 1980, when it was replaced by the current provincial flag. (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Province of Newfoundland and Labrador">Province of Newfoundland and Labrador</a> for continued discussion of provincial flags.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Points_of_interest_and_major_settlements">Points of interest and major settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newfoundland_(island)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Points of interest and major settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg/250px-Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg/375px-Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg/500px-Carlb-nfld-codflakes.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

Cod, the traditional mainstay of Newfoundland fisheries</div>

<p>Newfoundland has the most Dorset culture archeological sites. The Beothuk and Mi'kmaq did not leave as much evidence of their cultures.</p>
<p>As one of the first places in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> where Europeans settled, Newfoundland also has a history of European colonization. <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador">St. John's</a> is considered to be the oldest city in Canada and the oldest continuously settled location in English-speaking North America.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s_CMA" title="St. John's CMA">St. John's census metropolitan area</a> includes 12 suburban communities, the largest of which are the city of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pearl" title="Mount Pearl">Mount Pearl</a> and the towns of <a href="/wiki/Conception_Bay_South" title="Conception Bay South">Conception Bay South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paradise,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Paradise, Newfoundland and Labrador">Paradise</a>. The province's third-largest city is <a href="/wiki/Corner_Brook" title="Corner Brook">Corner Brook</a>, which is situated on the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Islands,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Bay of Islands, Newfoundland and Labrador">Bay of Islands</a> on the west coast of the island. This was recorded as a discovery by <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">Captain James Cook</a>.</p>
<p>The island of Newfoundland has numerous provincial parks such as <a href="/wiki/Barachois_Pond_Provincial_Park" title="Barachois Pond Provincial Park">Barachois Pond Provincial Park</a>, considered to be a model forest, as well as two national parks.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gros_Morne_National_Park" title="Gros Morne National Park">Gros Morne National Park</a> is located on the west coast; it was designated as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" title="World Heritage site">World Heritage site</a> in 1987 due to its complex geology and remarkable scenery. It is the largest national park in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> at 1,805 km<sup>2</sup> (697 sq mi) and is a popular tourist destination.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_National_Park" title="Terra Nova National Park">Terra Nova National Park</a>, on the island's east side, preserves the rugged geography of the <a href="/wiki/Bonavista_Bay" title="Bonavista Bay">Bonavista Bay</a> region. It allows visitors to explore the historic interplay of land, sea and man.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a> is an archaeological site located near the northernmost tip of the island (Cape Norman). It is the only known site of a Norse village in North America outside of Greenland, and is designated as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" title="World Heritage site">World Heritage site</a>. It is the only widely accepted site of pre-Columbian trans-oceanic contact. It has associations with the attempted colony of Vinland established by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leif_Ericson" title="Leif Ericson">Leif Ericson</a> around 1003.</li>
</ul>
<p>The island has many tourism opportunities, ranging from sea kayaking, camping, fishing and hunting, to hiking. The <a href="/wiki/International_Appalachian_Trail" title="International Appalachian Trail">International Appalachian Trail</a> (IAT) is being extended along the island's mountainous west coast. On the east coast, the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Trail" title="East Coast Trail">East Coast Trail</a> extends through the <a href="/wiki/Avalon_Peninsula" title="Avalon Peninsula">Avalon Peninsula</a> for 220 km (140 mi), beginning near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Amherst_(Newfoundland_and_Labrador)" title="Fort Amherst (Newfoundland and Labrador)">Fort Amherst</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland" title="St. John's, Newfoundland">St. John's</a> and ending in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cappahayden,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Cappahayden, Newfoundland and Labrador">Cappahayden</a>, with an additional 320 km (200 mi) of trail under construction.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marble_Mountain_(Newfoundland)" title="Marble Mountain (Newfoundland)">Marble Mountain Ski Resort</a> near Corner Brook is a major attraction in the winter for skiers in eastern Canada.</p>
<p>Other major communities include the following towns:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gander,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Gander, Newfoundland and Labrador">Gander</a>, home to the <a href="/wiki/Gander_International_Airport" title="Gander International Airport">Gander International Airport</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grand_Falls-Windsor" title="Grand Falls-Windsor">Grand Falls-Windsor</a>, a service centre for the central part of the island.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Channel-Port_aux_Basques" title="Channel-Port aux Basques">Channel-Port aux Basques</a>, the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_aux_Basques,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Port aux Basques, Newfoundland and Labrador">Gateway to Newfoundland</a>", as it is the closest point on the island to the province of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, as well as the location of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Atlantic" title="Marine Atlantic">Marine Atlantic</a> ferry terminal connecting the island to the rest of Canada.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stephenville,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Stephenville, Newfoundland and Labrador">Stephenville</a>, former location of the <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Harmon_Air_Force_Base" title="Ernest Harmon Air Force Base">Ernest Harmon Air Force Base</a> and currently the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephenville_Airport" title="Stephenville Airport">Stephenville Airport</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Educational institutions include the provincial university, <a href="/wiki/Memorial_University_of_Newfoundland" title="Memorial University of Newfoundland">Memorial University of Newfoundland</a> whose main campus is situated in St. John's, along with the <a href="/wiki/Grenfell_Campus" title="Grenfell Campus">Grenfell Campus</a> in Corner Brook, in addition to the <a href="/wiki/College_of_the_North_Atlantic" title="College of the North Atlantic">College of the North Atlantic</a> based in Stephenville and other communities.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bonavista,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Bonavista, Newfoundland and Labrador">Bonavista</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placentia,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Placentia, Newfoundland and Labrador">Placentia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferryland,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Ferryland, Newfoundland and Labrador">Ferryland</a> are all historic locations for various early European settlement or discovery activities. <a href="/wiki/Tilting,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Tilting, Newfoundland and Labrador">Tilting Harbour</a> on <a href="/wiki/Fogo_Island,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Fogo Island, Newfoundland and Labrador">Fogo Island</a> is a Provincial Heritage District, as well as a National Cultural Landscape District of Canada. This is one of only two national historic sites in Canada so recognized for their Irish heritage.</p>
<p>Entertainment opportunities abound in the island's three cities and numerous towns, particularly during summer festivals. For nightlife, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Street,_St._John%27s" title="George Street, St. John's">George Street</a>, located in downtown St. John's, is closed to traffic 20 hours per day, and is widely understood to have the most pubs per square foot of any street in North America. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mile_One_Stadium" title="Mile One Stadium">Mile One Stadium</a> in St. John's is the venue for large sporting and concert events in the province.</p>
<p>In March, the annual seal hunt (of the <a href="/wiki/Harp_seal" title="Harp seal">harp seal</a>) takes place.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newfoundland_(island)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Newfoundland_koppen.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1260" data-file-width="810" height="342" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Newfoundland_koppen.svg/220px-Newfoundland_koppen.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Newfoundland_koppen.svg/330px-Newfoundland_koppen.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Newfoundland_koppen.svg/440px-Newfoundland_koppen.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Newfoundland_koppen.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate types</a> of Newfoundland</div>

<p>Newfoundland is primarily characterized by having a subarctic (Köppen Dfc) or a humid continental climate (Köppen Dfb). Locations on the extreme southeast of the island receive sufficient maritime influence to qualify as having a subpolar oceanic climate (Köppen Cfc).</p>
