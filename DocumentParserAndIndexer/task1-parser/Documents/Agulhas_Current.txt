<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Agulhas Current</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5368" data-file-width="4920" height="327" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg/300px-Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg/450px-Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg/600px-Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Benguela_and_Agulhas_Currents.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The courses of the warm Agulhas current (red) along the east coast of South Africa, and the cold Benguela current (blue) along the west coast. The Agulhas Current is formed by the confluence of the warm <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Current" title="Mozambique Current">Mozambique</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Madagascar_Current" title="East Madagascar Current">East Madagascar Currents</a>, which meet south-west of Madagascar (not shown in the diagram). The cold <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a> does not originate from Antarctic waters in the South Atlantic Ocean, but from upwelling of water from the cold depths of the Atlantic Ocean against the west coast of the continent. The two currents do not "meet" anywhere along the south coast of Africa.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Agulhas Current</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_boundary_current" title="Western boundary current">western boundary current</a> of the southwest <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. It flows down the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> from 27°S to 40°S. It is narrow, swift and strong. It is even suggested that the Agulhas is the largest western boundary current in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_ocean" title="World ocean">world ocean</a>, with an estimated net transport of 70 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sverdrups</a> (Sv, millions m<sup>3</sup>/s), as western boundary currents at comparable latitudes transport less — <a href="/wiki/Brazil_Current" title="Brazil Current">Brazil Current</a> (16.2 Sv), <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> (34 Sv), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuroshio" title="Kuroshio">Kuroshio</a> (42 Sv).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sources of the Agulhas Current are the <a href="/wiki/East_Madagascar_Current" title="East Madagascar Current">East Madagascar Current</a> (25 Sv), the <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Current" title="Mozambique Current">Mozambique Current</a> (5 Sv) and a recirculated part of the south-west Indian subgyre south of Madagascar (35 Sv).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The net transport of the Agulhas Current is estimated as 100 Sv. The flow of the Agulhas Current is directed by the <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>. The current follows the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> from <a href="/wiki/Maputo" title="Maputo">Maputo</a> to the tip of the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Bank" title="Agulhas Bank">Agulhas Bank</a> (250 km south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agulhas" title="Cape Agulhas">Cape Agulhas</a>). Here the momentum of the current overcomes the <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> balance holding the current to the topography and the current leaves the shelf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siedler_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siedler-3">[3]</a></sup> The current reaches its maximum transport near the Agulhas Bank where it ranges between 95-136 Sv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baum_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The core of the current is defined as where the surface velocities reaches 100 cm/s (39 in/s), which gives the core an average width of 34 km (21 mi). The mean peak speed is 136 cm/s (54 in/s), but the current can reach 245 cm/s (96 in/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baum_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agulhas_meanders_and_Natal_pulses">Agulhas meanders and Natal pulses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agulhas meanders and Natal pulses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the Agulhas Current flows south along the African east coast, it tends to bulge inshore frequently, a deviation from the current's normal path known as Agulhas Current meanders (ACM). These bulges are occasionally (1-7 times per year) followed by a much larger offshore bulge, known as Natal pulses (NP). Natal pulses move along the coast at 20 km (12 mi) per day. An ACM can bulge up to 20 km (12 mi) and a NP up to 120 km (75 mi) from the current's mean position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The AC passes 34 km (21 mi) offshore and an ACM can reach 123 km (76 mi) offshore. When the AC meanders, its width broadens from 88 km (55 mi) to 125 km (78 mi) and its velocity weakens from 208 cm/s (82 in/s) to 136 cm/s (54 in/s). An ACM induces a strong inshore counter-current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large-scale cyclonic meanders known as Natal pulses are formed as the Agulhas Current reaches the continental shelf on the South African east-coast (i.e. the eastern Agulhas Bank off <a href="/wiki/Natal_Province" title="Natal Province">Natal</a>). As these pulses moves along the coast on the Agulhas Bank, they tend to pinch off Agulhas rings from the Agulhas Current. Such a ring shedding can be triggered by a Natal pulse alone, but sometimes meanders on the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Return_Current" title="Agulhas Return Current">Agulhas Return Current</a> merge to contribute to the shedding of an Agulhas ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retroflection">Retroflection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Retroflection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the southeast <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> the current <a href="/wiki/Retroflect" title="Retroflect">retroflects</a> (turns back on itself) in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Retroflection&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agulhas Retroflection (page does not exist)">Agulhas Retroflection</a> due to shear interactions with the strong <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a>, also known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Wind_Drift" title="West Wind Drift">West Wind Drift</a>" despite referring to the ocean current rather than to the surface winds. This water becomes the Agulhas Return Current, rejoining the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean_Gyre" title="Indian Ocean Gyre">Indian Ocean Gyre</a>. It is estimated that up to 85 Sv (Sv) of the net transport is returned to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> through the retroflection. The remaining water is transported into the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Gyre" title="South Atlantic Gyre">South Atlantic Gyre</a> in the Agulhas Leakage. Along with direct branch currents, this leakage takes place in surface water filaments, and Agulhas Eddies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agulhas_leakage_and_rings">Agulhas leakage and rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Agulhas leakage and rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="792" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png/220px-Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png/330px-Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png/440px-Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agulhas_Current_NLOM_.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Agulhas rings are peeled off the Agulhas Current in the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Basin" title="Agulhas Basin">Agulhas Basin</a> where it retroflects back into the Indian Ocean.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is estimated that as much as 15 Sv of <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> water is leaked directly into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">South Atlantic</a>. 10 Sv of this is relatively warm, salty <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a> water, with the remaining 5 Sv being cold, low salinity <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Intermediate_Water" title="Antarctic Intermediate Water">Antarctic Intermediate Water</a>. Since Indian Ocean water is significantly warmer (24-26 °C) and saltier than South Atlantic water, the Agulhas Leakage is a significant source of salt and heat for the South Atlantic Gyre. This heat flux is believed to contribute to the high rate of evaporation in the South Atlantic, a key mechanism in the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Meridional Overturning Circulation</a>. It should be noted that a small amount of the Agulhas Leakage joins the <a href="/wiki/North_Brazil_Current" title="North Brazil Current">North Brazil Current</a>, carrying Indian Ocean water into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Subtropical_Gyre" title="North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre">North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siedler_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siedler-3">[3]</a></sup> Before reaching the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, this leakage gets heated up by the sun around the equator, and, when finally joining the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>, this warm and salty water contributes to the formation of deep water in the North Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surface water filaments are estimated to account for up to 13% of the total salt transport from the Agulhas Current into the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a> and South Atlantic Gyre. Due to surface dissipation, these filaments are not believed to significantly contribute to inter-basin heat flux.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siedler_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Siedler-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Where the Agulhas turns back on itself the loop of the retroflection pinches off periodically, releasing an <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddy</a> into the South Atlantic Gyre. This "Agulhas Ring" enters the flow of the Benguela Current or is advected northwestward across the South Atlantic where it joins the <a href="/wiki/South_Equatorial_Current" title="South Equatorial Current">South Equatorial Current</a>, where they dissipate into the larger background currents. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticyclonic" title="Anticyclonic">anticyclonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Warm_core_ring" title="Warm core ring">warm core rings</a> are estimated to have a transport of 3-9 Sv each, in total injecting salt at a rate of 2.5<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoclimate">Paleoclimate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paleoclimate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, the buoyancy of the South Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a> and the strength of the Atlantic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridional_overturning_circulation" title="Meridional overturning circulation">meridional overturning circulation</a> has been regulated by the shedding of warm, saline Agulhas Rings. The Agulhas leakage affects the Atlantic thermocline on a decadal timescale and over centuries it can change the buoyancy of the Atlantic thermocline and therefore the formation rates of North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The provenance of ocean sediments can be determined by analysing <a href="/wiki/Terrigenous_sediment" title="Terrigenous sediment">terrigenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> isotope ratios in deep ocean cores. Sediments underlying the Agulhas Current and Return Current have significantly higher ratios than surrounding sediments. <a href="#CITEREFFranzeseGoldsteinSkrivanek2012">Franzese, Goldstein &amp; Skrivanek 2012</a> analysed cores in the South Atlantic deposited during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> (LGM, 20 000 years ago), and concluded that the Agulhas leakage was significantly reduced. The trajectory of the current was the same during the LGM and that the reduced leakage must be explained by a weaker current. Furthermore, it can be predicted that a stronger Agulhas Current will result in a more eastward retroflection and an increased Agulhas leakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="#CITEREFSimonArthurHallPeeters2013">Simon et al. 2013</a>, however, noted that changes in temperature and salinity in the Agulhas leakage is at least partly the result of variability in the composition in the current itself and can be a poor indicator of the strength of the leakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rogue_waves">Rogue waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rogue waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The south-east coast of South Africa is on the main shipping route between the Middle-East and Europe/the U.S. and several large ships sustain major damage because of <a href="/wiki/Rogue_wave" title="Rogue wave">rogue waves</a> in the area where these waves occasionally can reach a height of more than 30 m (98 ft). Some 30 larger ships were sunk by rogue waves along the South African east-coast between 1981 and 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agulhas_Undercurrent">Agulhas Undercurrent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Agulhas Undercurrent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Directly under the core of the Agulhas Current, at a depth of 800 m (2,600 ft), there is an Agulhas Undercurrent which flows equatorward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The undercurrent is 2,000 m (6,600 ft) deep and 40 km (25 mi) wide and can reach 90 cm/s (35 in/s) at 1,400 metres (4,600 ft), one of greatest speeds observed in any current at this depth, but it also displays a great variance with a transport of 4.2±5.2 Sv. The undercurrent can represent as much as 40% of the Indian Ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridional_overturning_circulation" title="Meridional overturning circulation">overturning transport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beal-2009-Intro_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beal-2009-Intro-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Below 1,800 m (5,900 ft) a separate layer of the undercurrent can be distinguished: the more coherent <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a> (NADW) which transports an average of 2.3±3.0 Sv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beal-2009-Intro_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beal-2009-Intro-14">[14]</a></sup> NADW rounds the southern tip of Africa after which the major part (9 Sv) flow eastward and a smaller part (2 Sv) northward through the Agulhas Undercurrent and into the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Natal_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Natal Valley (page does not exist)">Natal Valley</a> (the basin between South Africa and the Mocambique Plateau); remnants of NADW has been observed in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mozambique Basin (page does not exist)">Mozambique Basin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Channel" title="Mozambique Channel">Channel</a>. The undercurrent is more leaky than the Agulhas above, resulting in a relatively well-mixed composition of water masses — at intermediate depth there is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Intermediate_Water" title="Antarctic Intermediate Water">Antarctic Intermediate Water</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Read_Sea_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Read Sea Water (page does not exist)">Read Sea Water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beal-2009-Disc_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beal-2009-Disc-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The periodicity of the meanders and Natal pulses of the Agulhas is matched by the Agulhas Undercurrent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beal-2009-Intro_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beal-2009-Intro-14">[14]</a></sup> More research is needed but observations seem to indicate that during a meander event the Agulhas moves first onshore, then offshore, and finally onshore again, first weakening then strengthening 10-15 Sv. At the same time the undercurrent is first squeezed offshore and weakened when the Agulhas moves onshore, then strengthened and forced upward when the Agulhas moves offshore, and finally returns to normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beal-2009-Disc_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beal-2009-Disc-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_production">Primary production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Primary production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Agulhas acts as an oceanic <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">convergence zone</a>. Due to <a href="/wiki/Continuity_equation" title="Continuity equation">mass continuity</a> this drives surface waters down, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> of cold, nutrient rich water south of the current. Additionally, the convergence tends to increase the concentration of plankton in and around the Agulhas. Both of these factors result in the area being one of enhanced <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary productivity</a> as compared to the surrounding waters. This is especially notable in the Agulhas Retroflection waters, where chlorophyll-a concentrations tend to be significantly higher than the surrounding South Indian Ocean and South Atlantic Ocean waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mann-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_rings">Impact of rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Impact of rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Warm_core_ring" title="Warm core ring">Warm core rings</a> are known to have lower primary productivity than surrounding cold waters. Agulhas Rings are no exception, and have been observed to carry waters with low <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll_a" title="Chlorophyll a">chlorophyll-a</a> concentration water into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">South Atlantic</a>. It can also be noted that the size of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> in Agulhas Rings tends to be smaller than in the surrounding water (around 20 µm in diameter).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mann-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agulhas Rings have also been observed as removing larval and juvenile fish from the continental shelf. This removal of young fish can result in a reduced <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a> catch in the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela</a> system if a ring passes through the fishery.</p>
