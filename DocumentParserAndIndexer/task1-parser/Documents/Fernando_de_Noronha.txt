<p><b>Fernando de Noronha</b> (<small>Portuguese pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[feʁˈnɐ̃du d(ʒ)i noˈɾoɲɐ]</a></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> of 21 <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islets</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, 354 km (220 mi) offshore from the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> coast. The archipelago's name is a corruption of the name of the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> merchant <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_de_Loronha" title="Fernão de Loronha">Fernão de Loronha</a>, to whom it was given by the Portuguese crown for services rendered regarding wood imported from Brazil. Only the homonymous main island is inhabited; it has an area of 18.4 km<sup>2</sup> (7.1 sq mi) and a population estimated at 2,718 in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBGE2012_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBGE2012-2">[2]</a></sup> The archipelago's total area is 26 km<sup>2</sup> (10 sq mi).</p>
<p>Administratively, the islands are a unique case in Brazil of a special "state district" (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>distrito estadual</i></span>) that is not part of any <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipality</a> and is administered directly by the government of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Brazil" title="States of Brazil">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pernambuco" title="Pernambuco">Pernambuco</a> (despite being closer to the state of <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Norte" title="Rio Grande do Norte">Rio Grande do Norte</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pernambuco_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pernambuco-3">[3]</a></sup> The state district's jurisdiction also includes the very remote <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter_and_Saint_Paul_Archipelago" title="Saint Peter and Saint Paul Archipelago">Saint Peter and Saint Paul Archipelago</a>, located 625 kilometres (388 mi) northeast of Fernando de Noronha. 70% of the islands' area were established in 1988 as a national maritime park.</p>
<p>In 2001 <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> designated it as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> because of the importance of its environment. Its <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">time zone</a> is <a href="/wiki/UTC%E2%88%9202:00" title="UTC−02:00">UTC−02:00</a> all year round. The local population and travelers can get to Noronha by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha_Airport" title="Fernando de Noronha Airport">plane</a> from <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natal,_Rio_Grande_do_Norte" title="Natal, Rio Grande do Norte">Natal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> An "environmental preservation" daily fee is charged from tourists upon arrival by <a href="/wiki/Pernambuco" title="Pernambuco">Pernambuco State administration</a>, while another fee is paid once to have access to the <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha_Marine_National_Park" title="Fernando de Noronha Marine National Park">National Park</a> attractions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The islands of this archipelago are the visible parts of a range of submerged mountains. It consists of 21 islands, islets and rocks of volcanic origin. The main island has an area of 18 km<sup>2</sup> (6.9 sq mi), being 10 km (6.2 mi) long and 3.5 km (2.2 mi) wide at its maximum. The base of this enormous volcanic formation is 756 metres (2,480 ft) below the surface. The volcanic rocks are of variable though mainly silica-undersaturated character with <a href="/wiki/Basanite" title="Basanite">basanite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nephelinite" title="Nephelinite">nephelinite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phonolite" title="Phonolite">phonolite</a> among the lava types found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The main island, from which the group gets its name, makes up 91% of the total area; the islands of Rata, Sela Gineta, Cabeluda and São José, together with the islets of Leão and Viúva make up the rest. The central upland of the main island is called the Quixaba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United Nations Environment Programme lists 15 possible <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> plant species, including species of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Capparis" title="Capparis">Capparis</a> noronhae</i> (2 species), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratosanthes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ceratosanthes (page does not exist)">Ceratosanthes</a> noronhae</i> (3 species), <i><a href="/wiki/Cayaponia" title="Cayaponia">Cayaponia</a> noronhae</i> (2 species), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moriordica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moriordica (page does not exist)">Moriordica</a> noronhae,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Cereus_(plant)" title="Cereus (plant)">Cereus</a> noronhae</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Palicourea" title="Palicourea">Palicourea</a> noronhae</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Guettarda" title="Guettarda">Guettarda</a> noronhae</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bumelia" title="Bumelia">Bumelia</a> noronhae</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Physalis" title="Physalis">Physalis</a> noronhae</i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ficus_noronhae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ficus noronhae (page does not exist)">Ficus noronhae</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The islands have two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds">endemic birds</a> — the <a href="/wiki/Noronha_elaenia" title="Noronha elaenia">Noronha elaenia</a> <i>(Elaenia ridleyana)</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Noronha_vireo" title="Noronha vireo">Noronha vireo</a> <i>(Vireo gracilirostris)</i>. Both are present on the main island; Noronha vireo is also present on Ilha Rata. In addition there is an endemic subspecie of <a href="/wiki/Eared_dove" title="Eared dove">eared dove</a> <i>Zenaida auriculata noronha</i>. Subfossil remains of an extinct <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rallidae" title="Rallidae">rail</a> have also been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The archipelago is also an important site for breeding seabirds. An endemic <a href="/wiki/Sigmodontinae" title="Sigmodontinae">sigmodontine</a> rodent, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noronhomys_vespuccii" title="Noronhomys vespuccii">Noronhomys vespuccii</a></i>, mentioned by Amerigo Vespucci, is now extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The islands have two endemic reptiles, the Noronha wormlizard <i><a href="/wiki/Amphisbaena_ridleyi" title="Amphisbaena ridleyi">Amphisbaena ridleyi</a></i> and the Noronha skink <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trachylepis_atlantica" title="Trachylepis atlantica">Trachylepis atlantica</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_life">Marine life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Marine life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The life above and below sea is the main attraction of the island. <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">Sea turtles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetacean" title="Cetacean">cetaceans</a> (most common among these are <a href="/wiki/Spinner_dolphin" title="Spinner dolphin">spinner dolphins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">humpback whales</a>, followed by many others such as <a href="/wiki/Pantropical_spotted_dolphin" title="Pantropical spotted dolphin">Pantropical spotted dolphins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short-finned_pilot_whale" title="Short-finned pilot whale">Short-finned pilot whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melon-headed_whale" title="Melon-headed whale">Melon-headed whales</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a> and many other species are frequently observed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The climate is tropical, with two well-defined seasons for rainfall, if not temperature. The rainy season lasts from February to July; the rest of the year sees little rain. The temperature ranges, both diurnal and monthly, are unusually slight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Many controversies mark the discovery of the archipelago by Europeans. At least three names – <i>São Lourenço</i>, <i>São João</i>, and <i>Quaresma</i> – have been associated with the island around the time of its discovery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Based on the written record, Fernando de Noronha island was discovered on August 10, 1503, by a <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Portuguese</a> expedition, organized and financed by a private commercial consortium headed by the Lisbon merchant <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_de_Loronha" title="Fernão de Loronha">Fernão de Loronha</a>. The expedition was under the overall command of captain <a href="/wiki/Gon%C3%A7alo_Coelho" title="Gonçalo Coelho">Gonçalo Coelho</a> and carried the Italian adventurer <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> aboard, who wrote an account of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The flagship of the expedition hit a reef and foundered near the island, and the crew and contents had to be salvaged. On Coelho's orders, Vespucci anchored at the island, and spent a week there, while the rest of the Coelho fleet went on south. In his letter to Soderini, Vespucci describes the uninhabited island and reports its name as the "island of St. Lawrence" (August 10 is the feast day of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence" title="St. Lawrence">St. Lawrence</a>; it was a custom of Portuguese explorations to name locations by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_calendar" title="Liturgical calendar">liturgical calendar</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Its existence was reported back to Lisbon sometime between then and January 16, 1504, when King <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal" title="Manuel I of Portugal">Manuel I of Portugal</a> issued a charter granting the "island of St. John" (<i>São João</i>) as a hereditary captaincy to <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_de_Loronha" title="Fernão de Loronha">Fernão de Loronha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The date and new name in the charter has presented historians with a puzzle. As Vespucci did not return to Lisbon until September, 1504, the discovery must have been earlier. Historians have hypothesized that a stray ship of the Coelho fleet, under an unknown captain, may have returned to the island (prob. on August 29, 1503, feast day of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beheading_of_St._John_the_Baptist" title="Beheading of St. John the Baptist">beheading of St. John the Baptist</a>) to collect Vespucci, did not find him or anyone else there, and went back to Lisbon by himself with the news.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> (Vespucci in his letter, claims he left the island August 18, 1503, and upon his arrival in Lisbon a year later, on September 7, 1504, the people of Lisbon were surprised, as they "had been told" (presumably by the earlier captain?) that his ship had been lost.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The captain who returned to Lisbon with the news (and the St. John name) is unknown. (Some have speculated this captain was Loronha himself, the chief financier of this expedition, but that is highly unlikely.)</p>
<p>This account, reconstructed from the written record, is severely marred by the cartographic record. An island, named <i>Quaresma</i>, looking very much like Fernando de Noronha island, appears in the <a href="/wiki/Cantino_planisphere" title="Cantino planisphere">Cantino planisphere</a>. The Cantino map was composed by an anonymous Portuguese cartographer, and completed before November 1502, well before the Coelho expedition even set out. This has led to speculation that the island was discovered by a previous expedition. However, there is no consensus on which expedition that might have been. The name, "Quaresma" means <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a>, suggesting it must have been discovered in March or early April, which does not correspond well with the known expeditions. There is also a mysterious red island to the left of Quaresma in the Cantino map that does not fit with Fernando de Noronha island. Some have explained these anomalies away by reading <i>quaresma</i> as <i>anaresma</i> (meaning unknown, but sidesteps the Lent timing),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and proposing that the red island is just an accidental inkblot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cantino_west.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail from the 1502 Cantino planisphere, showing the island of "Quaresma" (Fernando de Noronha?) off the Brazilian coast.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Assuming Quaresma is indeed Fernando de Noronha, then who discovered it? One proposal is that it was discovered by a royal Portuguese mapping expedition that was sent out in May, 1501, commanded by an unknown captain (possibly <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gon%C3%A7alves_(explorer)" title="André Gonçalves (explorer)">André Gonçalves</a>) and also accompanied by Amerigo Vespucci.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> According to Vespucci, this expedition returned to Lisbon in September 1502, just on time to influence the final composition of the Cantino map. Unfortunately, Vespucci does <i>not</i> report discovering this island then – indeed he is quite clear that the first time he (and his fellow sailors) saw the island was on the 1503 Coelho expedition. However, there is a letter written by an Italian saying that a ship arrived "from the land of Parrots" in Lisbon on July 22, 1502 (three months before Vespucci).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This could be a stray ship from the mapping expedition that returned prematurely, or another expedition altogether, about which we have no information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The timing of its reputed arrival (July 1502), makes it possible that it stumbled on the island sometime in March 1502, on the homeward voyage, well within Lent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A third possible (but unlikely) theory is that the island was discovered already in 1500, shortly after the discovery of Brazil by the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Cabral,_1500)" title="2nd Portuguese India Armada (Cabral, 1500)">Second India Armada</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro_Alvares_Cabral" title="Pedro Alvares Cabral">Pedro Alvares Cabral</a>. After his brief landfall at <a href="/wiki/Porto_Seguro" title="Porto Seguro">Porto Seguro</a>, Cabral dispatched a supply ship under either <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_de_Lemos" title="Gaspar de Lemos">Gaspar de Lemos</a> or <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gon%C3%A7alves_(explorer)" title="André Gonçalves (explorer)">André Gonçalves</a> (sources conflict) back to Lisbon, to report the discovery. This returning supply ship would have returned north along the Brazilian coast and might have come across Fernando de Noronha island, and reported its existence in Lisbon by July 1500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> However, this contradicts the Quaresma name, since the returning supply ship was sailing well after Lent.</p>
<p>A fourth (but also unlikely) possibility is that it was discovered by the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Nova,_1501)" title="3rd Portuguese India Armada (Nova, 1501)">Third India Armada</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_da_Nova" title="João da Nova">João da Nova</a>, which set out from Lisbon in March or April 1501, and arrived back in September 1502, also in time to influence the Cantino map. Chronicler <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Correia" title="Gaspar Correia">Gaspar Correia</a> asserts that on the outward voyage, the Third Armada made a stop on the Brazilian coast around <a href="/wiki/Cabo_de_Santo_Agostinho" title="Cabo de Santo Agostinho">Cape Santo Agostinho</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Two other chroniclers (<a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Barros" title="João de Barros">João de Barros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dami%C3%A3o_de_G%C3%B3is" title="Damião de Góis">Damião de Góis</a>) do not mention a landfall, but do report they discovered an island (which they believe to be identified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascension_island" title="Ascension island">Ascension island</a>, but this is not certain).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> So it is possible that the Third Armada may indeed have discovered Fernando de Noronha island on their outward leg. However, the timing is very tight: Easter landed on April 11, 1501, while the estimated departure date of the Third Armada from Lisbon ranges from March 5 to April 15, not leaving enough time to reach those environs within Lent.</p>
<p>As a result of these anomalies, some modern historians have proposed that Fernando de Noronha is <i>not</i> depicted on the 1502 Cantino map at all. Instead, they have proposed that Quaresma island and the accompanying red "inkblot" are in fact the <a href="/wiki/Rocas_Atoll" title="Rocas Atoll">Rocas Atoll</a>, slightly misplaced on the map. This reserves the discovery of Fernando de Noronha island itself as indeed on August 10, 1503, by the Gonçalo Coelho expedition, as originally reported by Vespucci.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The transition of the name from "São João" to "Fernando de Noronha" was probably just natural usage. A royal letter dated May 20, 1559, to descendants of the Loronha family, still refers to the island by its official name of <i>ilha de São João</i>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> but already in other places, e.g. the logbook of <a href="/wiki/Martim_Afonso_de_Sousa" title="Martim Afonso de Sousa">Martim Afonso de Sousa</a> in the 1530s, it was referred to as the "island of Fernão de Noronha" ("Noronha" being a common misspelling of "Loronha"). The informal name eventually displaced the official name.</p>
<h3><span id="1500–1700"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1500.E2.80.931700">1500–1700</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1500–1700">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Lisbon merchant <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_de_Loronha" title="Fernão de Loronha">Fernão de Loronha</a> held not only Fernando de Noronha island as a hereditary captaincy but also (from 1503 to around 1512) a commercial monopoly on trade in Brazil. Between 1503 and 1512, Noronha's agents set up a string of warehouses (<i>feitorias</i>) along the Brazilian coast, and engaged in trade with the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">indigenous peoples in Brazil</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilwood" title="Brazilwood">brazilwood</a>, a native red dye wood highly valued by European clothmakers. Fernando de Noronha island was the central collection point of this network. Brazilwood, continuously harvested by the coastal Indians and delivered to the various coastal warehouses, was shipped to the central warehouse on Fernando de Noronha island, which was intermittently visited by a larger transport ship that would carry the collected loads back to Europe. After the expiration of Loronha's commercial charter in 1512, the organization of the brazilwood enterprise was taken over by the Portuguese crown, but Loronha and his descendents retained private ownership of Fernando de Noronha island itself as a hereditary <a href="/wiki/Captaincy" title="Captaincy">captaincy</a>, at least down to the 1560s.</p>
<h3><span id="1700–1900"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1700.E2.80.931900">1700–1900</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1700–1900">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<a href="/wiki/Forte_de_Nossa_Senhora_dos_Rem%C3%A9dios" title="Forte de Nossa Senhora dos Remédios">Fort of Nossa Senhora dos Remédios</a></div>

<p>Captain <a href="/wiki/Henry_Foster_(scientist)" title="Henry Foster (scientist)">Henry Foster</a> stopped at Fernando de Noronha during his scientific survey expedition as commander of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Chanticleer_(1808)" title="HMS Chanticleer (1808)">HMS Chanticleer</a>, which had set out in 1828. As well as surveying coasts and ocean currents, Foster used a <a href="/wiki/Kater%27s_pendulum" title="Kater's pendulum">Kater invariable pendulum</a> to make observations on gravity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He took the island as the point of junction of his double line of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitudes</a> setting out his survey. He was given considerable assistance by the Governor of Fernando Noronha who let Foster use part of his own house for the pendulum experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-admiralty_1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-admiralty_1-31">[31]</a></sup> The longitude of <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> taken by Foster was among those on one side of a significant discrepancy, which meant that the charts of South America were in doubt.</p>
<p>To resolve this, the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> instructed Captain <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a> to command <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS <i>Beagle</i></a> on a survey expedition. One of its essential tasks was a stop at Fernando Noronha to confirm its exact longitude, using the <a href="/wiki/List_of_chronometers_on_HMS_Beagle" title="List of chronometers on HMS Beagle">22 chronometers</a> on board the ship to give the precise time of observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-admiralty_1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-admiralty_1-31">[31]</a></sup> They arrived at the island in the late evening of 19 February 1832, anchoring at midnight. On 20 February FizRoy landed a small party to take the observations, despite difficulties caused by heavy surf, then sailed on for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahia,_Brazil" title="Bahia, Brazil">Bahia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> that evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the day, the island was visited by the naturalist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, who was one of the Beagle's passengers. He took notes for his book on geology. He wrote about admiring the woods:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The whole island is one forest, &amp; this is so thickly intertwined that it requires great exertion to crawl along. — The scenery was very beautiful, &amp; large Magnolias &amp; Laurels &amp; trees covered with delicate flowers ought to have satisfied me. — But I am sure all the grandeur of the Tropics has not yet been seen by me. — We had no gaudy birds, No humming birds. No large flowers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>His experiences on Fernando de Noronha were recorded in his journal, later published as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle" title="The Voyage of the Beagle">The Voyage of the Beagle</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He also included a short description of the island in his 1844 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_Observations_on_the_Volcanic_Islands_visited_during_the_voyage_of_H.M.S._Beagle" title="Geological Observations on the Volcanic Islands visited during the voyage of H.M.S. Beagle">Geological Observations on the Volcanic Islands visited during the voyage of H.M.S. Beagle</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island was also used as a penal colony in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1900–present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1900.E2.80.93present">1900–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1900–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the late 18th century, the first prisoners were sent to Fernando de Noronha. A prison was built. In 1897 the government of the state of Pernambuco took possession of the prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Between 1938 and 1945, Fernando de Noronha was a political <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prison</a>. The former governor of Pernambuco, Miguel Arraes, was incarcerated there. In 1957 the prison was closed and the archipelago was visited by President <a href="/wiki/Juscelino_Kubitschek" title="Juscelino Kubitschek">Juscelino Kubitschek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the beginning of the 20th century, the British arrived to provide technical cooperation in <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraphy</a> (<i>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=South_American_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="South American Company (page does not exist)">South American Company</a></i>). Later the French came with the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=French_Cable&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="French Cable (page does not exist)">French Cable</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and the Italians with <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Italcable&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Italcable (page does not exist)">Italcable</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1942, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the archipelago was made a <a href="/wiki/Federal_territory" title="Federal territory">Federal territory</a>, which included <a href="/wiki/Rocas_Atoll" title="Rocas Atoll">Rocas Atoll</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Peter_and_Paul_Rocks" title="Saint Peter and Paul Rocks">Saint Peter and Paul Rocks</a>. The government‐sent political and ordinary prisoners to the local prison.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An airport was constructed in September 1942 by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Transport_Command_(United_States_Air_Force)" title="Air Transport Command (United States Air Force)">Air Transport Command</a> for the Natal-<a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a> air route. It provided a transoceanic link between Brazil and <a href="/wiki/French_West_Africa" title="French West Africa">French West Africa</a> for cargo, transiting aircraft and personnel during the Allies campaign in Africa. Brazil transferred the airport to the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> on 5 September 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> After the end of the war, the administration of the airport was transferred back to the Brazilian Government. <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha_Airport" title="Fernando de Noronha Airport">Fernando de Noronha Airport</a> is served by daily flights from <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natal,_Rio_Grande_do_Norte" title="Natal, Rio Grande do Norte">Natal</a> on the Brazilian coast.</p>
<p>In 1988, Brazil designated approximately 70% of the archipelago as a maritime <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national park</a>, with the goal of preserving the land and sea environment. On October 5, 1988, the Federal Territory was dissolved and added to the state of Pernambuco (except Rocas Atoll, which was added to the state of Rio Grande do Norte).</p>
<p>Today Fernando de Noronha's economy depends on tourism, restricted by the limitations of its delicate <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. In addition to the historical interest noted above, the archipelago has been the subject of the attention of various scientists dedicated to the study of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fauna_(animals)" title="Fauna (animals)">fauna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, etc. The jurisdiction is considered to be a separate "entity" by the <a href="/wiki/DX_Century_Club" title="DX Century Club">DX Century Club</a>, and so is visited rather often by <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> operators.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> declared Fernando de Noronha, with Rocas Atoll, a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. It cited the following reasons:</p>
<dl>
<dd>a) the island's importance as a feeding ground for several species, including <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billfish" title="Billfish">billfish</a>, cetaceans, sharks, and marine turtles,</dd>
<dd>b) a high population of resident <a href="/wiki/Spinner_dolphin" title="Spinner dolphin">spinner dolphins</a> and</dd>
<dd>c) protection for endangered species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hawksbill_sea_turtle" title="Hawksbill sea turtle">hawksbill sea turtle</a> (critically endangered) and various birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Air_France_Flight_447" title="Air France Flight 447">Air France Flight 447</a> disappeared off the northeast coast of Brazil. It was presumed to have crashed into the Atlantic Ocean off the coast of Fernando de Noronha. Rescue and recovery operations were launched from this island.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_and_environmental_threats">Conservation and environmental threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Conservation and environmental threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the original vegetation was cut down in the 19th century, when the island was used as a prison, to keep the prisoners from hiding and making rafts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Also, exotic species have been introduced:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linseed" title="Linseed">Linseed</a>, intended for use as cattle feed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tupinambis" title="Tupinambis">Tegu</a> lizards (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tupinambis_merianae" title="Tupinambis merianae">Tupinambis merianae</a></i>, locally known as <i>teju</i>) introduced in the 1950s to control a rat infestation. Ironically, that did not work out, because Tegus are diurnal and rats, nocturnal. Now the lizards themselves are considered a plague, feeding mostly on bird eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rock_cavy" title="Rock cavy">Rock cavies</a> (<i>Kerodon rupestris</i>, locally known as <i>mocó</i>) introduced by the military in the 1960s as hunting game for soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_cats" title="Domestic cats">Domestic cats</a>, introduced as pets, now they spread throughout the whole island and several acquired a feral status, surviving only by preying on native birds, rock cavies and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synanthropic" title="Synanthropic">synanthropic</a> rodents.</li>
</ul>
<p>From these, the domestic cat and the tegu lizard have become invasive.</p>
<p>The island is divided between the <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha_Marine_National_Park" title="Fernando de Noronha Marine National Park">Fernando de Noronha Marine National Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha_Environmental_Protection_Area" title="Fernando de Noronha Environmental Protection Area">Fernando de Noronha Environmental Protection Area</a>. The latter covers the urban, tourist area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tourism including <a href="/wiki/Whale_watching" title="Whale watching">dolphin watching</a>, diving and charter fishing comprise the majority of the island’s economy.</p>

<p>The archipelago of Fernando de Noronha in 2005 had a Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of R$22,802,000 and a per capita income of R$10,001. The Human Development Index (HDI) district's state was estimated at 0.862 (PNUD/2000). The only banking center in the archipelago is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Santander_Brasil" title="Santander Brasil">Banco Santander Brasil</a>. There are one or two additional ATMs around the main island.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_Noronha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The beaches of Fernando de Noronha are promoted for tourism and recreational diving. The most popular ones include Baía do Sancho, Pig Bay, Dolphins Bay, Sueste Bay and Praia do Leão. Due to the <a href="/wiki/South_Equatorial_Current" title="South Equatorial Current">South Equatorial Current</a> that pushes warm water from Africa to the island, diving to depths of 30 to 40 metres (98 to 131 ft) does not require a wetsuit. The visibility underwater can reach up to 50 metres (160 ft).</p>
<p>The part of the island facing the mainland has the beaches in the following order: Baía do Sancho, Baía dos Porcos, Praia dos Americanos, Praia do Boldró, Praia da Conceição, Praia do Meio and Praia do Cachorro. The part of the island facing the Atlantic ocean has only 03 beaches: Praia do Leão, Praia do Sueste and Praia do Atalaia. A great way to get to know the island is to walk from Praia dos Americanos, pass by Praia do Boldró, Praia da Conceição, Praia do Meio and finish the walking at Praia do Cachorro.</p>
<p>The island is served by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha_Airport" title="Fernando de Noronha Airport">Gov. Carlos Wilson Airport</a> with regular flights to <a href="/wiki/Natal,_Rio_Grande_do_Norte" title="Natal, Rio Grande do Norte">Natal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a>.</p>

See the English translation of the <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=boQKAQAAIAAJ&amp;pg=RA1-PA41#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">account</a> in <i>Letter to Soderini</i></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">An actual copy of this charter has never been found. Its contents and date, however, are summarized in a royal letter of March 3, 1522 confirming it, and yet another royal letter of May 20, 1559, identifying the location of <i>São João</i> precisely as Fernando de Noronha island. See Duarte Leite (1923: p.276–278) and Roukema (1963: p.21).</span></li>

<li id="cite_note-20"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-20">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">For an early reading of Quaresma as "Anaresma", see Henry Harrisse (1891) <i>The Discovery of North America</i> (1961 ed., p.319) and Orville Derby (1902) "Os mappas mais antigos do Brasil", <i>Revista do Instituto Historico e Geografico de São Paulo</i>, vol. 7, <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=i6MCAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA244#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">p.244</a>. This reading was insisted upon later by historian Marcondes de Sousa (1949) <i>Américo Vespúcio e suas viagens</i> (São Paulo). It provoked a brief and surprisingly bitter controversy with other historians, e.g. Damião Peres, Duarte Leite.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The inkblot theory was suggested by Duarte Leite (1923: p.275–278).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-22">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The 1501 mapping expedition is also known as the "Third Voyage" of Amerigo Vespucci (and his first under the Portuguese flag). Amerigo Vespucci relates the account of this expedition twice - first in a letter to Lorenzo Pietro Francesco di Medici, written in early 1503 (see (<a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=8wAuAAAAMAAJ&amp;pg=PA42#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">account</a>) in <i>Letter do Medici</i>), and then again in his letters to Piero Soderini, written 1504-05 ([account] in <i>Letter to Soderini</i>). In his account, Vespucci does not mention the name of the captain of this 1501 mapping expedition, and his identity has been widely speculated. The 16th-century chronicler <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Correia" title="Gaspar Correia">Gaspar Correia</a> suggests it was <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gon%C3%A7alves_(explorer)" title="André Gonçalves (explorer)">André Gonçalves</a> (<i>Lendas da India</i>, <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=YmVKAAAAYAAJ&amp;dq=editions%3ALCCN05022145&amp;as_brr=1&amp;pg=PA152#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">p.152</a>). Greenlee (1945) reviews various possible names – and settles on the conjecture that it might be Fernão de Loronha himself (a hypothesis also suggested by Duarte Leite (1923)). But this is strongly disputed by other authors, e.g. Roukema (1963). It would be highly unlikely a prominent and wealthy merchant like Loronha would absent his businesses to go personally command vessels himself. Loronha's support (if any) to the mapping expedition was probably limited to financing.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-23"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-23">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">This letter was written by Venetian emissary Pascualigo on October 12, 1502, and is quoted in the diary of <a href="/wiki/Marino_Sanuto_the_Younger" title="Marino Sanuto the Younger">Marino Sanuto</a>. See Greenlee (1945: p.11n) and Roukema (1963:p.19).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Roukema (1963) accepts the hypothesis of a unrecorded separate expedition in 1501 - and that this might be the one led by André Gonçalves. However, Greenlee (1945) rejects the unrecorded expedition theory, deeming it superfluous, and instead embraces the stray ship theory (and that this stray ship was precisely the flagship personally commanded by Fernão de Loronha).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Roukema (1963) rejects this theory.</span></li>
