<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Inflow (meteorology)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">Supercellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> image showing <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus</a> inflow bands</div>

<p><b>Inflow</b> is the flow of a fluid into a large collection of that fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Within <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, inflow normally refers to the influx of warmth and moisture from air within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> into <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> systems. Extratropical cyclones are fed by inflow focused along their <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm fronts</a>. Tropical cyclones require a large inflow of warmth and moisture from warm oceans in order to develop significantly, mainly within the lowest 1 kilometre (0.62 mi) of the atmosphere. Once the flow of warm and moist air is cut off from thunderstorms and their associated <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, normally by the thunderstorm's own rain-cooled <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow boundary</a>, the storms begin to dissipate. <a href="/wiki/Rear-inflow_jet" title="Rear-inflow jet">Rear inflow jets</a> behind <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> act to erode the broad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">rain</a> shield behind the squall line, and accelerate its forward motion.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunderstorms">Thunderstorms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inflow_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Thunderstorms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="965" data-file-width="2348" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg/220px-Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg/330px-Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg/440px-Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anvil_shaped_cumulus_panorama_edit_crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Thunderstorm in its mature stage over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swifts_Creek,_Victoria" title="Swifts Creek, Victoria">Swifts Creek, Victoria</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">Squall line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></div>
<p>Cool air, carried to the ground by thunderstorm downdraft, cuts off the inflow of the thunderstorm, destroying its updraft and causing its dissipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Tornadoes, which form within stronger thunderstorms, grow until they reach their mature stage. This is when the rear flank downdraft of the thunderstorm, fed by rain-cooled air, begins to wrap around the tornado, cutting off the inflow of warm air which previously fed the tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advanced_Spotter_Guide_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Advanced_Spotter_Guide-3">[3]</a></sup> When thunderstorms are able to organize into squall lines, a feature known as a rear inflow jet develops to the south of the mid-level circulation associated with its northern bookend vortex. This leads to an erosion of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> within the broad rain shield behind the squall line, and may lead to acceleration of the squall line itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">Tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inflow_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2007" data-file-width="3032" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Isabel_from_ISS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> (2003) as seen from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></div>
<p>While an initial warm core system, such as an organized thunderstorm complex, is necessary for the formation of a tropical cyclone, a large flux of energy is needed to lower <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> more than a few millibars (0.10 inch of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>). Inflow of warmth and moisture from the underlying ocean surface is critical for tropical cyclone strengthening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A significant amount of the inflow in the cyclone is in the lowest 1 kilometre (3,300 ft) of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extratropical_cyclones">Extratropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inflow_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extratropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uk-cyclone-2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="451" data-file-width="621" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Uk-cyclone-2.png/220px-Uk-cyclone-2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Uk-cyclone-2.png/330px-Uk-cyclone-2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Uk-cyclone-2.png/440px-Uk-cyclone-2.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uk-cyclone-2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather map</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> affecting Great Britain and Ireland. The "L" symbol denotes the center of the "low", and the occluded, cold, and warm <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">frontal boundaries</a> are depicted.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_cyclone_model" title="Norwegian cyclone model">Norwegian cyclone model</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">Weather fronts</a></div>
<p>Polar front theory is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Bjerknes" title="Jacob Bjerknes">Jacob Bjerknes</a>, and was derived from a coastal network of observation sites in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. This theory proposed that the main inflow into a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> was concentrated along two lines of convergence, one ahead (or east) of the low and another trailing equatorward (south in the Northern Hemisphere and north in the Southern Hemisphere) and behind (or west) of the low. The convergence line ahead of the low became known as either the steering line or the <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a>. The trailing convergence zone was referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>. Areas of clouds and rainfall appeared to be focused along these convergence zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OU_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OU-7">[7]</a></sup> A conveyor belt, also referred to as the warm conveyor belt, is a term describing the flow of a stream of warm moist air originating within the warm sector (or generally equatorward) of an extratropical cyclone in advance of the cold front which slopes up above and poleward (north in the Northern Hemisphere and south in the Southern Hemisphere) of the surface warm front. The concept of the conveyor belt originated in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLD_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-COLD-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The left edge of the conveyor belt is sharp due to higher density air moving in from the west forcing a sharp slope to the cold front. An area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratiform" title="Stratiform">stratiform</a> precipitation develops poleward of the warm front along the conveyor belt. Active precipitation poleward of the warm front implies potential for greater development of the cyclone. A portion of this conveyor belt turns to the right (left in the Southern Hemisphere), aligning with the upper level westerly flow. However, the western portion of this belt wraps around the northwest (southwest in the Southern Hemisphere) side of the cyclone, which can contain moderate to heavy precipitation. If the air mass is cold enough, the precipitation falls in the form of heavy snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OU_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-OU-7">[7]</a></sup> Theory from the 1980s talked about the presence of a cold conveyor belt originating north of the warm front and flowing along a clockwise path (in the northern hemisphere) into the main belt of the westerlies aloft, but there has been conflicting evidence as to whether or not this phenomenon actually exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COLD_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-COLD-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
