<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> is an area with an associated <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic</a> population which is harvested for its commercial value. Fisheries can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saltwater</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>. They can also be <b>wild</b> or <b>farmed</b>.</p>
<p>Wild fisheries are sometimes called <b>capture</b> fisheries. The aquatic life they support is not controlled in any meaningful way and needs to be "captured" or <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fished</a>. Wild fisheries exist primarily in the oceans, and particularly around <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coasts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" title="Continental shelves">continental shelves</a>. They also exist in <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>. Issues with wild fisheries are <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">pollution</a>. Significant wild fisheries have collapsed or are in danger of collapsing, due to overfishing and pollution. Overall, production from the world's wild fisheries has levelled out, and may be starting to decline.</p>
<p>As a contrast to wild fisheries, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">farmed fisheries</a> can operate in sheltered coastal waters, in rivers, lakes and <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, or in enclosed bodies of water such as tanks. Farmed fisheries are technological in nature, and revolve around developments in <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>. Farmed fisheries are expanding, and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> aquaculture in particular is making many advances. Nevertheless, the majority of fish consumed by humans continues to be sourced from wild fisheries. As of the early 21st century, fish is humanity's only significant wild food source.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_currents">Ocean currents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ocean currents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_surface_currents.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="927" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Ocean_surface_currents.jpg/300px-Ocean_surface_currents.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Ocean_surface_currents.jpg/450px-Ocean_surface_currents.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Ocean_surface_currents.jpg/600px-Ocean_surface_currents.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_surface_currents.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Major ocean surface currents. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> map.</div>

<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean current</a> is continuous, directed movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_water" title="Ocean water">ocean water</a>. Ocean currents are rivers of relatively warm or cold water within the ocean. The currents are generated from the forces acting upon the water like the planet rotation, the wind, the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> (hence <a href="/wiki/Isopycnal" title="Isopycnal">isopycnal</a>) differences and the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">gravitation of the moon</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depth_contour" title="Depth contour">depth contours</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shore" title="Shore">shoreline</a> and other currents influence the current's direction and strength.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">Ocean currents</a> can flow for thousands of kilometers. Surface ocean currents are generally wind driven and develop their typical clockwise spirals in the northern hemisphere and counter-clockwise rotation in the southern hemisphere because of the imposed wind stresses. In wind driven currents, the <a href="/wiki/Ekman_spiral" title="Ekman spiral">Ekman spiral</a> effect results in the currents flowing at an angle to the driving winds. The areas of surface ocean currents move somewhat with the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>; this is most notable in equatorial currents.</p>
<p>Deep ocean currents are driven by density and temperature gradients. <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Thermohaline circulation</a>, also known as the ocean's conveyor belt, refers to the deep ocean density-driven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">ocean basin</a> currents. These currents, which flow under the surface of the ocean and are thus hidden from immediate detection, are called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine_river&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Submarine river (page does not exist)">submarine rivers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">Upwelling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Downwelling" title="Downwelling">downwelling</a> areas in the oceans are areas where significant vertical movement of ocean water is observed.</p>
<p>Surface currents make up about 10% of all the water in the ocean. Surface currents are generally restricted to the upper 400 meters of the ocean. The movement of deep water in the ocean basins is by density driven forces and gravity. The density difference is a function of different temperatures and salinity. Deep waters sink into the deep ocean basins at high latitudes where the temperatures are cold enough to cause the density to increase. The main causes of currents are: solar heating, winds and gravity.</p>
<p>Ocean currents are also very important in the dispersal of many life forms. A dramatic example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eel_story" title="Eel story">life-cycle of the eel</a>. Currents also determine the disposition of <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">marine debris</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gyres_and_upwelling">Gyres and upwelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gyres and upwelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_gyre" title="Oceanic gyre">Oceanic gyres</a> are large-scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_currents" title="Ocean currents">ocean currents</a> caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>. Wind-driven surface currents interact with these gyres and the underwater topography, such as seamounts and the edge of continental shelves, to produce downwellings and <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwellings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These can transport nutrients and provide feeding grounds for plankton eating <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a>. This in turn draws larger fish that prey on the <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a>, and can result in productive fishing grounds. Most upwellings are coastal, and many of them support some of the most productive fisheries in the world, such as small pelagics (sardines, anchovies, etc.). Regions of upwelling include coastal <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, western <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> coast.</p>

<th>Prominent gyres</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>* The <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Current" title="Humboldt Current">Humboldt Current</a>. This gyre produces a cold, low-<a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> ocean current that flows north-westward along the west coast of South America from the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> to northern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. This results in the most prominent <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> system in the world, supporting an extraordinary abundance of marine life. Upwelling occurs off Peru year-round and off Chile during the spring and summer. Approximately 18-20% of the world’s fish catch comes from the Humboldt Current LME. The species are mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_mackerel" title="Jack mackerel">jack mackerel</a>. The LME’s high primary and secondary productivity supports other important fishery resources as well as marine mammals.
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/California_Current" title="California Current">California Current</a>.This is a <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">current</a> that moves south along the western coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, beginning off southern <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, and ending off southern <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>. The movement of northern waters southward makes the <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> waters cooler than coastal areas of comparable <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> on the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Extensive <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> of colder sub-surface waters occurs, caused by the prevailing northwesterly winds acting through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ekman_Effect" title="Ekman Effect">Ekman Effect</a>. The winds drive surface water to the right of the wind flow, that is offshore, which draws water up from below to replace it. The upwelling further cools the already cool California Current. The cold water is highly productive due to the upwelling, which brings to the surface nutrient-rich sediments, supporting large populations of <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> and important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a>. During <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events, the California Current is disrupted, leading to declines in <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>, resulting in cascading effects up the food chain, such as declines in fisheries, seabird breeding failures and marine mammal mortality. In 2005, a failure in the otherwise predictable upwelling events, unassociated with El Niño, caused a collapse in <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a> in the current, leading to similar effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>In the ocean, the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> typically follows the course:</p>
<ul>
<li>Phytoplankton → zooplankton → predatory zooplankton → <a href="/wiki/Filter_feeder" title="Filter feeder">filter feeders</a> → predatory fish</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">Phytoplankton</a> is usually the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_producer" title="Primary producer">primary producer</a> (the first level in the food chain or the first <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a>). Phytoplankton converts inorganic carbon into <a href="/wiki/Protoplasm" title="Protoplasm">protoplasm</a>. Phytoplankton is consumed by microscopic animals called <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>. These are the second level in the food chain, and include <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larva</a> of fish, squid, lobsters and crabs–as well as the small <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> called <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a>, and many other types. Zooplankton is consumed both by other, larger predatory zooplankters and by fish (the third level in the food chain). Fish that eat zooplankton could constitute the fourth trophic level, while seals consuming the fish are the fifth. Alternatively, for example, whales may consume zooplankton directly - leading to an environment with one less trophic level.</p>

<p>Global primary production can be estimated from <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> observations. Satellites scan the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NDVI" title="NDVI">normalised difference vegetation index</a> (NDVI) over terrestrial habitats, and scan sea-surface <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> levels over oceans. This results in 56.4 billion <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_carbon" title="Organic carbon">C</a>/yr (53.8%), for terrestrial primary production, and 48.5 billion tonnes C/yr for oceanic primary production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus, the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoautotrophic" title="Photoautotrophic">photoautotrophic</a> primary production for the Earth is about 104.9 billion tonnes C/yr. This translates to about 426 gC/m²/yr for land production (excluding areas with permanent ice cover), and 140 gC/m²/yr for the oceans.</p>
<p>However, there is a much more significant difference in <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">standing stocks</a> - while accounting for almost half of total annual production, oceanic <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> account for only about 0.2% of the total biomass.</p>
<p>The most successful <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> species, in terms of biomass, is probably the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_krill" title="Antarctic krill">Antarctic krill</a>, <i>Euphausia superba</i>, with a biomass of about 500 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NE97_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NE97-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, as a group, the small aquatic <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> called <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a> form the largest animal biomass on earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_waters">Coastal waters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coastal waters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Klamath_river_estuary.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="650" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Klamath_river_estuary.jpg/200px-Klamath_river_estuary.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Klamath_river_estuary.jpg/300px-Klamath_river_estuary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Klamath_river_estuary.jpg/400px-Klamath_river_estuary.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Klamath_river_estuary.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Estuary of <a href="/wiki/Klamath_River" title="Klamath River">Klamath River</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">Estuaries</a> are semi-enclosed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal" title="Coastal">coastal</a> bodies of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> with one or more <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a> flowing into them, and with a free connection to the open <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Estuaries are often associated with high rates of biological productivity. They are small, in demand, impacted by events far upstream or out at sea, and concentrate materials such as pollutants and sediments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagoons" title="Lagoons">Lagoons</a> are bodies of comparatively shallow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_water" title="Sea water">salt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a> separated from the deeper <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> by a shallow or exposed <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">sandbank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">coral reef</a>, or similar feature. <i>Lagoon</i> refers to both coastal lagoons formed by the build-up of sandbanks or reefs along shallow coastal waters, and the lagoons in atolls, formed by the growth of coral reefs on slowly sinking central islands. Lagoons that are fed by freshwater streams are estuaries.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a> (foreshore) is the area that is exposed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_tide" title="Low tide">low tide</a> and submerged at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_tide" title="High tide">high tide</a>, for example, the area between tide marks. This area can include many different types of habitats, including steep rocky cliffs, sandy beaches or vast <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mudflats</a>. The area can be a narrow strip, as in Pacific islands that have only a narrow tidal range, or can include many meters of shoreline where shallow beach slope interacts with high tidal excursion.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4182" data-file-width="2778" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg/160px-Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg/240px-Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg/320px-Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taiwan_2009_SuHua_Highway_Fixed_Net_Fishing_FRD_6865.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fixed-net fishing on the littoral zone along the <a href="/wiki/Suhua_Highway" title="Suhua Highway">Suhua Highway</a> on the East coast of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">littoral zone</a> is the part of the ocean closest to the shore. The word <i>littoral</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>litoralis</i>, which means <i>seashore</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The littoral zone extends from the high-water mark to near shore areas that are permanently submerged, and includes the intertidal zone. Definitions vary. Encyclopædia Britannica defines the littoral zone in a thoroughly vague way as the "marine ecological realm that experiences the effects of tidal and longshore currents and breaking waves to a depth of 5 to 10 metres (16 to 33 feet) below the low-tide level, depending on the intensity of storm waves".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The US Navy defines it as extending "from the shoreline to 600 feet (183 meters) out into the water"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Neritic_zone" title="Neritic zone">sublittoral zone</a> is the part of the ocean extending from the seaward edge of the littoral zone to the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It is sometimes called the <b>neritic</b> zone. Websters defines the neritic zone as the region of shallow water adjoining the seacoast. The word <i>neritic</i> perhaps comes from the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">new Latin</a> <i>nerita</i>, which refers to a genus of marine snails, 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The sublittoral zone is relatively shallow, extending to about 200 meters (100 fathoms), and generally has well-oxygenated water, low water pressure, and relatively stable temperature and <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> levels. These, combined with presence of light and the resulting photosynthetic life, such as <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> and floating <a href="/wiki/Sargassum" title="Sargassum">sargassum</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> make the sublittoral zone the location of the majority of sea life.</li>
<li>Voigt, Brian (1998) <a class="external text" href="http://www.csc.noaa.gov/text/glossary.html" rel="nofollow"><i>Glossary of Coastal Terminology</i></a> Washington State Department of Ecology, publication 98-105</li>
<li>Pawson, M G; Pickett, G D and Walker, P (2002) <a class="external text" href="http://www.cefas.co.uk/Publications/techrep/tech116.pdf" rel="nofollow"><i>The coastal fisheries of England and Wales, Part IV: A review of their status 1999–2001</i></a> Science Series, Technical Report 116.</li>
</ul>
</td>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_shelves">Continental shelves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Continental shelves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elevation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2160" data-file-width="4320" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Elevation.jpg/350px-Elevation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Elevation.jpg/525px-Elevation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Elevation.jpg/700px-Elevation.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elevation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #03FCDE; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> The global continental shelf, highlighted in cyan</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" title="Continental shelves">Continental shelves</a> are the extended perimeters of each <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Coastal_plain" title="Coastal plain">coastal plain</a>, which is covered during <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">interglacial periods</a> such as the current epoch by relatively shallow <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seas</a> (known as <b>shelf seas</b>) and <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">gulfs</a>.</p>
<p>The shelf usually ends at a point of decreasing slope (called the <b>shelf break</b>). The sea floor below the break is the <b>continental slope</b>. Below the slope is the <b>continental rise</b>, which finally merges into the deep ocean floor, the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a>. The continental shelf and the slope are part of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_margin" title="Continental margin">continental margin</a>.</p>
<p>Continental shelves are shallow (averaging 140 metres or 460 feet), and the sunlight available means they can teem with life. The shallowest parts of the continental shelf are called <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland" title="Grand Banks of Newfoundland">fishing banks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> There the sunlight penetrates to the seafloor and the <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>, on which fish feed, thrive.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<table class="wikitable collapsible collapsed">
<tr>
<th align="center" width="560px">Continental shelves : Details</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>The character of the shelf changes dramatically at the shelf break, where the continental slope begins. With a few exceptions, the shelf break is located at a remarkably uniform depth of roughly 140 m (460 ft); this is likely a hallmark of past ice ages, when sea level was lower than it is now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
<p>The width of the continental shelf varies considerably – it is not uncommon for an area to have virtually no shelf at all, particularly where the forward edge of an advancing <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic plate</a> dives beneath <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> in an offshore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">subduction zone</a> such as off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> or the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>. The largest shelf – the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Shelf" title="Siberian Shelf">Siberian Shelf</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> – stretches to 1500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilometer" title="Kilometer">kilometers</a> (930 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a>) in width. The <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> lies over another extensive area of continental shelf, the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Shelf" title="Sunda Shelf">Sunda Shelf</a>, which joins <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, Sumatra, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_(island)" title="Java (island)">Java</a> to the Asian mainland. Other familiar bodies of water that overlie continental shelves are the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. The average width of continental shelves is about 80 km (50 mi). The depth of the shelf also varies, but is generally limited to water shallower than 150 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a> (490 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Combined with the sunlight available in shallow waters, the continental shelves teem with life compared to the biotic desert of the oceans' <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> (water column) environment of the continental shelf constitutes the <a href="/wiki/Neritic_zone" title="Neritic zone">neritic zone</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> (sea floor) province of the shelf is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublittoral_zone" title="Sublittoral zone">sublittoral zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coral_reefs">Coral reefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Coral reefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coral_reef_locations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="640" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Coral_reef_locations.jpg/350px-Coral_reef_locations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Coral_reef_locations.jpg/525px-Coral_reef_locations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cb/Coral_reef_locations.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coral_reef_locations.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Locations of coral reefs.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reefs</a> are <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> structures produced by living organisms, found in shallow, tropical marine waters with little to no nutrients in the water. High nutrient levels such as those found in runoff from agricultural areas can harm the reef by encouraging the growth of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Although corals are found both in temperate and tropical waters, reefs are formed only in a zone extending at most from 30°N to 30°S of the equator.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<table class="wikitable collapsible collapsed">
<tr>
<th align="center" width="560px">Coral reefs : Details</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Coral reefs are estimated to cover 284,300 square kilometres, with the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific" title="Indo-Pacific">Indo-Pacific</a> region (including the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>) accounting for 91.9% of the total.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Southeast Asia accounts for 32.3% of that figure, while the Pacific including <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> accounts for 40.8%. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> coral reefs only account for 7.6% of the world total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalding_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spalding-25">[25]</a></sup>
<p>Coral reefs are either restricted or absent from the west coast of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, as well as the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. This is due primarily to <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> and strong cold coastal currents that reduce water temperatures in these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nybakken_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nybakken-26">[26]</a></sup> Corals are also restricted from off the coastline of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalding_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spalding-25">[25]</a></sup> They are also restricted along the coast around north-eastern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> due to the release of vast quantities of freshwater from the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> Rivers respectively.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><b>Famous coral reefs and reef areas of the world include:</b></p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> - largest coral reef system in the world, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland, Australia</a>;</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Belize_Barrier_Reef" title="Belize Barrier Reef">Belize Barrier Reef</a> - second largest in the world, stretching from southern Quintana Roo, Mexico and all along the coast of Belize down to the Bay Islands of Honduras.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> Coral Reef - located off the coast of Egypt and Saudi Arabia.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pulley_Ridge" title="Pulley Ridge">Pulley Ridge</a> - deepest photosynthetic coral reef, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a></li>
<li>Many of the numerous reefs found scattered over the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Caledonia_Barrier_Reef" title="New Caledonia Barrier Reef">New Caledonia Barrier Reef</a> - second longest double barrier reef in the world, with a length of about 1,500 km (932 mi).</li>
</ul>
<p>Coral reefs support an extraordinary <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>; although they are located in nutrient-poor tropical waters. The process of <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">nutrient cycling</a> between corals, zooxanthellae, and other reef organisms provides an explanation for why coral reefs flourish in these waters: recycling ensures that fewer nutrients are needed overall to support the community.</p>
<p>Coral reefs are home to a variety of tropical or reef <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, such as the colorful <a href="/wiki/Parrotfish" title="Parrotfish">parrotfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomacanthidae" title="Pomacanthidae">angelfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Damselfish" title="Damselfish">damselfish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Butterflyfish" title="Butterflyfish">butterflyfish</a>. Other fish groups found on coral reefs include <a href="/wiki/Grouper" title="Grouper">groupers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutjanidae" title="Lutjanidae">snappers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haemulidae" title="Haemulidae">grunts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wrasse" title="Wrasse">wrasses</a>. Over 4,000 species of fish inhabit coral reefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalding_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spalding-25">[25]</a></sup> It has been suggested that the high number of fish species that inhabit coral reefs are able to coexist in such high numbers because any free living space is rapidly inhabited by the first planktonic fish larvae that occupy it. These fish then inhabit the space for the rest of their life. The species that inhabit the free space is random and has therefore been termed 'a lottery for living space'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reefs are also home to a large variety of other organisms, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" title="Sea sponge">sponges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cnidaria" title="Cnidaria">Cnidarians</a> (which includes some types of corals and <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>), <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spiny_lobster" title="Spiny lobster">spiny lobsters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>), <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Starfish" title="Starfish">starfish</a>, sea urchins and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holothuroidea" title="Holothuroidea">sea cucumbers</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_squirt" title="Sea squirt">sea squirts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_snake" title="Sea snake">sea snakes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalding_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Spalding-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castro_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castro-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<i>Bioerosion</i> (coral damage) such as this may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></div>

<p>Human activity may represent the greatest threat to coral reefs living in Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and over-fishing are the most serious threats to these ecosystems. Physical destruction of reefs due to boat and shipping traffic is also a problem. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_food_fish_trade" title="Live food fish trade">live food fish trade</a> has been implicated as a driver of decline due to the use of <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a> and disaster for peoples living in the tropics. Hughes, et al., (2003), writes that "with increased <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">human population</a> and improved storage and transport systems, the scale of human impacts on reefs has grown exponentially. For example, markets for fishes and other <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> have become global, supplying demand for reef resources far removed from their tropical sources."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Currently researchers are working to determine the degree various factors impact the reef systems. The list of factors is long but includes the oceans acting as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">carbon dioxide sink</a>, changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, biological <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a>, impacts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dust_storms" title="Dust storms">dust storms</a> carrying agents to far flung reef systems, various pollutants, impacts of <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal blooms</a> and others. Reefs are threatened well beyond coastal areas and so the problem is broader than factors from land development and pollution though those are too causing considerable damage.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia_coral_reefs" title="Southeast Asia coral reefs">Southeast Asian coral reefs</a> are at risk from damaging <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> practices (such as <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_fishing" title="Cyanide fishing">cyanide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blast_fishing" title="Blast fishing">blast fishing</a>), <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, sedimentation, pollution and bleaching. A variety of activities, including education, regulation, and the establishment of marine protected areas are under way to protect these reefs. <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, for example has nearly 33,000 square miles (85,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of coral reefs. Its waters are home to a third of the world’s total corals and a quarter of its fish species. Indonesia's coral reefs are located in the heart of the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Triangle" title="Coral Triangle">Coral Triangle</a> and have been victim to destructive fishing, unregulated tourism, and bleaching due to climatic changes. Data from 414 reef monitoring stations throughout Indonesia in 2000 found that only 6% of Indonesia’s coral reefs are in excellent condition, while 24% are in good condition, and approximately 70% are in poor to fair condition (2003 <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">The Johns Hopkins University</a>).</p>
<p>General estimates show approximately 10% of the coral reefs around the world are already dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Problems range from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_fishing" title="Environmental effects of fishing">environmental effects of fishing</a> techniques, described above, to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">Coral bleaching</a> is another manifestation of the problem and is showing up in reefs across the planet.</p>
<p>NInhabitants of Ahus Island, <a href="/wiki/Manus_Province" title="Manus Province">Manus Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, have followed a generations-old practice of restricting fishing in six areas of their reef lagoon. While line fishing is permitted, net and spear fishing are restricted based on cultural traditions. The result is that both the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> and individual fish sizes are significantly larger in these areas than in places where fishing is completely unrestricted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> It is estimated that about 60% of the world’s reefs are at risk due to destructive, human-related activities. The threat to the health of reefs is particularly strong in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, where an enormous 80% of reefs are considered <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a>.</p>
<p>Organisations as <a class="external text" href="http://www.coralcay.org/expeditions/marine/fj1/" rel="nofollow">Coral Cay</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.counterpart.org/" rel="nofollow">Counterpart</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and the <a class="external text" href="http://www.fspi.org.fj/programs.htm" rel="nofollow">Foundation of the peoples of the South Pacific</a> are currently undertaking coral reef/atoll restoration projects. They are doing so using simple methods of <a href="/wiki/Plant_propagation" title="Plant propagation">plant propagation</a>. Other organisations as <a href="/wiki/Practical_Action" title="Practical Action">Practical Action</a> have released informational documents on how to set up coral reef restoration to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</td>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seamounts">Seamounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Seamounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:382px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seamount_Locations.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="1357" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Seamount_Locations.png/380px-Seamount_Locations.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Seamount_Locations.png/570px-Seamount_Locations.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Seamount_Locations.png/760px-Seamount_Locations.png 2x" width="380"/></a>

The locations of the world's major seamounts</div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamount</a> is an underwater <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a>, rising from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seafloor" title="Seafloor">seafloor</a> that does not reach to the water's surface (<a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>), and thus is not an <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a>. They are defined by <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanographers</a> as independent features that rise to at least 1,000 meters above the seafloor. Seamounts are common in the Pacific Ocean. Recent studies suggest there may be 30,000 seamounts in the Pacific, about 1,000 in the Atlantic Ocean and an unknown number in the Indian Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<table class="wikitable collapsible collapsed">
<tr>
<th align="center" width="560px">Seamounts : Details</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Seamounts often project upwards into shallower zones more hospitable to sea life, providing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitats</a> for marine species that are not found on or around the surrounding deeper ocean bottom. In addition to simply providing physical presence in this zone, the seamount itself may deflect deep currents and create <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>. This process can bring nutrients into the photosynthetic zone, producing an area of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/activity" title="wiktionary:activity">activity</a> in an otherwise desert-like open ocean. Seamounts may thus be vital stopping points for some migratory animals such as <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>. Some recent research indicates whales may use such features as navigational aids throughout their migration.
<p>Due to the larger populations of fish in these areas overexpoitation by the fishing industry has caused some seamount fauna populations to decrease considerably.</p>
<p>The primary productivity of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epipelagic" title="Epipelagic">epipelagic</a> waters above the submerged peak can often be enhanced by the <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">hydrographic</a> conditions of the seamount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This increases the densities of the <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> and leads to the high concentrations of fish in these areas. Another theory for this is that the fish are sustained on the diurnal migration of zooplankton being interrupted by the presence of the seamount, and causing the zooplankton to stay in the area. It is also possible that the high densities of fishes has more to do with the fish life histories and interaction with the benthic fauna of the seamount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The benthic fauna of the seamounts is dominated by suspension feeders, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" title="Sea sponge">sponges</a> and true <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>. For some seamounts that peaks at 200–300 metres below the surface benthic macroalgae is common. The sedimentary infauna is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaete</a> worms.</p>
<p>For a long time it has been surmised that many pelagic animals visit seamounts to gather food, but proof this of this aggregating effect has been lacking. The first demonstration of this conjecture has recently been published <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> started to look for new stocks of fish and began to trawl the seamounts. The majority of the invertebrates brought up are corals, and are mainly used for the jewelry trade. The two major fish species were the <a href="/wiki/Orange_roughy" title="Orange roughy">orange roughy</a> (<i>Hoplostethus atlanticus</i>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic_armourhead" title="Pelagic armourhead">pelagic armourhead</a> (<i>Pseudopentaceros wheeleri</i>), which were quickly overexploited due to lack of knowledge of the longevity of the fish, late maturity, low <a href="/wiki/Fecundity" title="Fecundity">fecundity</a>, small geographic range and recruitment to the fishery. As well as the fishes being overexploited the benthic communities were destroyed by the trawling gear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

</ul>
</td>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freshwater_fisheries">Freshwater fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Freshwater fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes">Lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lentic_ecosystems" title="Lentic ecosystems">Lentic ecosystems</a></div>
<p>Worldwide, freshwater lakes have an area of 1.5 million square kilometres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Saline inland seas add another 1.0 million square kilometres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> There are 28 freshwater lakes with an area greater than 5,000 square kilometres, totalling 1.18 million square kilometres or 79 percent of the total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivers">Rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lotic_ecosystems" title="Lotic ecosystems">Lotic ecosystems</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">Marine pollution</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">Pollution</a> is the introduction of contaminants into an environment. Wild fisheries flourish in oceans, lakes, and rivers, and the introduction of contaminants is an issue of concern, especially as regards plastics, pesticides, heavy metals, and other industrial and agricultural pollutants which do not disintegrate rapidly in the environment. Land run-off and industrial, agricultural, and domestic waste enter rivers and are discharged into the sea. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_pollution" title="Ship pollution">Pollution from ships</a> is also a problem.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_waste">Plastic waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Plastic waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">Marine debris</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">Marine debris</a> is human-created waste that ends up floating in the sea. Oceanic debris tends to accumulate at the centre of gyres and coastlines, frequently washing aground where it is known as beach litter. Eighty percent of all known marine debris is plastic - a component that has been rapidly accumulating since the end of World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weisman_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weisman-46">[46]</a></sup> Plastics accumulate because they don't <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegrade</a> as many other substances do; while they will <a href="/wiki/Photodegradation" title="Photodegradation">photodegrade</a> on exposure to the sun, they do so only under dry conditions, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> inhibits this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Discarded <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bag" title="Plastic bag">plastic bags</a>, <a href="/wiki/Six_pack_rings" title="Six pack rings">six pack rings</a> and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution" title="Plastic pollution">plastic waste</a> which finish up in the ocean present dangers to wildlife and fisheries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Aquatic life can be threatened through entanglement, suffocation, and ingestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plastic_particle_water_pollution" title="Plastic particle water pollution">Nurdles</a>, also known as mermaids' tears, are plastic pellets typically under five millimetres in diameter, and are a major contributor to marine debris. They are used as a raw material in plastics manufacturing, and are thought to enter the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a> after accidental spillages. Nurdles are also created through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_weathering" title="Physical weathering">physical weathering</a> of larger plastic debris. They strongly resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_eggs" title="Fish eggs">fish eggs</a>, only instead of finding a nutritious meal, any marine wildlife that ingests them will likely starve, be poisoned and die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many animals that live on or in the sea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feeding" title="Feeding">consume</a> flotsam by mistake, as it often looks similar to their natural prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Plastic debris, when bulky or tangled, is difficult to pass, and may become permanently lodged in the digestive tracts of these animals, blocking the passage of food and causing death through starvation or infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Tiny floating particles also resemble <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, which can lead <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_feeders" title="Filter feeders">filter feeders</a> to consume them and cause them to enter the ocean <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. In samples taken from the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Gyre" title="North Pacific Gyre">North Pacific Gyre</a> in 1999 by the Algalita Marine Research Foundation, the mass of plastic exceeded that of zooplankton by a factor of six.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weisman_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weisman-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlgalitaVid_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlgalitaVid-55">[55]</a></sup> More recently, reports have surfaced that there may now be 30 times more plastic than plankton, the most abundant form of life in the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Toxic additives used in the manufacture of plastic materials can <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leech" title="wikt:leech">leech</a> out into their surroundings when exposed to water. Waterborne <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> pollutants <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbtion" title="Adsorbtion">collect and magnify on the surface</a> of plastic debris,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Algalita_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Algalita-57">[57]</a></sup> thus making plastic far more deadly in the ocean than it would be on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weisman_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weisman-46">[46]</a></sup> Hydrophobic contaminants are also known to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioaccumulate" title="Bioaccumulate">bioaccumulate</a> in fatty tissues, <a href="/wiki/Biomagnification" title="Biomagnification">biomagnifying</a> up the food chain and putting great pressure on <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a>. Some plastic additives are known to disrupt the <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine system</a> when consumed, others can suppress the immune system or decrease reproductive rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlgalitaVid_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-AlgalitaVid-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxins">Toxins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Toxins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mercury_in_fish" title="Mercury in fish">Mercury in fish</a></div>
<p>Apart from plastics, there are particular problems with other toxins which do not disintegrate rapidly in the marine environment. Heavy metals are metallic chemical elements that have a relatively high density and are toxic or poisonous at low concentrations. Examples are <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>. Other persistent toxins are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCBs" title="PCBs">PCBs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds" title="Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds">dioxins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenols</a>.</p>
<p>Such toxins can accumulate in the tissues of many species of aquatic life in a process called <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a>. They are also known to accumulate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> environments, such as <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bay_mud" title="Bay mud">bay muds</a>: a geological record of human activities of the last century.</p>
<p>Some specific examples are</p>
<ul>
<li>Chinese and Russian industrial pollution such as <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenols</a> and heavy metals in the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" title="Amur River">Amur River</a> have devastated fish stocks and damaged its <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wabamun_Lake" title="Wabamun Lake">Wabamun Lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, once the best <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_whitefish" title="Freshwater whitefish">whitefish</a> lake in the area, now has unacceptable levels of heavy metals in its sediment and fish.</li>
<li>Acute and chronic <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> events have been shown to impact southern California kelp forests, though the intensity of the impact seems to depend on both the nature of the contaminants and duration of exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>Due to their high position in the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">accumulation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> from their diet, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> levels can be high in larger species such as bluefin and <a href="/wiki/Albacore" title="Albacore">albacore</a>. As a result, in March 2004 the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">FDA</a> issued guidelines recommending that pregnant women, nursing mothers and children limit their intake of tuna and other types of predatory fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>Some shellfish and crabs can survive polluted environments, accumulating heavy metals or toxins in their tissues. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitten_crab" title="Mitten crab">mitten crabs</a> have a remarkable ability to survive in highly modified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_habitat" title="Aquatic habitat">aquatic habitats</a>, including polluted waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issg.org_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-issg.org-65">[65]</a></sup> The farming and harvesting of such species needs careful management if they are to be used as a food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbcmc_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-mbcmc-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uctip_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-uctip-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a> has a poor environmental track record. For example, according to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>, mining has contaminated portions of the headwaters of over 40% of watersheds in the western continental US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Much of this pollution finishes up in the sea.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Heavy metals enter the environment through <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spills</a> - such as the <a href="/wiki/Prestige_oil_spill" title="Prestige oil spill">Prestige oil spill</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galician</a> coast - or from other natural or <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">anthropogenic sources</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eutrophication">Eutrophication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Eutrophication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">Eutrophication</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="955" data-file-width="1485" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg/220px-Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg/330px-Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg/440px-Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_eutrophication-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Effect of eutrophication on marine <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic life</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">Eutrophication</a> is an increase in chemical <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>, typically compounds containing <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, in an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. It can result in an increase in the ecosystem's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_productivity" title="Primary productivity">primary productivity</a> (excessive plant growth and decay), and further effects including lack of oxygen and severe reductions in water quality, fish, and other animal populations.</p>
<p>The biggest culprit are rivers that empty into the ocean, and with it the many chemicals used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a> in agriculture as well as waste from <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans">humans</a>. An excess of oxygen depleting chemicals in the water can lead to <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxia</a> and the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">dead zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surveys have shown that 54% of lakes in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutrophic" title="Eutrophic">eutrophic</a>; in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, 53%; in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, 48%; in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, 41%; and in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, 28%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ILEC_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-ILEC-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">Estuaries</a> also tend to be naturally eutrophic because land-derived nutrients are concentrated where run-off enters the marine environment in a confined channel. The <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a> has identified 375 hypoxic coastal zones around the world, concentrated in coastal areas in Western Europe, the Eastern and Southern coasts of the US, and East Asia, particularly in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> In the ocean, there are frequent <a href="/wiki/Red_tide" title="Red tide">red tide</a> algae blooms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> that kill fish and marine mammals and cause respiratory problems in humans and some domestic animals when the blooms reach close to shore.</p>
<p>In addition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_runoff" title="Land runoff">land runoff</a>, atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">fixed nitrogen</a> can enter the open ocean. A study in 2008 found that this could account for around one third of the ocean’s external (non-recycled) nitrogen supply and up to three per cent of the annual new marine biological production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> It has been suggested that accumulating reactive nitrogen in the environment may have consequences as serious as putting carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidification">Acidification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Acidification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a></div>
<p>The oceans are normally a natural <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink#Oceans" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a>, absorbing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Because the levels of atmospheric carbon dioxide are increasing, the oceans are becoming <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">more acidic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr05_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-orr05-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-key04_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-key04-77">[77]</a></sup> The potential consequences of ocean acidification are not fully understood, but there are concerns that structures made of calcium carbonate may become vulnerable to dissolution, affecting corals and the ability of shellfish to form shells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>A report from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> scientists published in the journal Science in May 2008 found that large amounts of relatively acidified water are upwelling to within four miles of the Pacific <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> area of North America. This area is a critical zone where most local marine life lives or is born. While the paper dealt only with areas from <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> to northern California, other continental shelf areas may be experiencing similar effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feeley08_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-feeley08-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_fishing">Effects of fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Effects of fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_destruction">Habitat destruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Habitat destruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_fishing" title="Environmental effects of fishing">Environmental effects of fishing</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">Fishing nets</a> that have been left or lost in the ocean by fishermen are called <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">ghost nets</a>, and can entangle <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dugong" title="Dugong">dugongs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>, and other creatures. Acting as designed, these nets restrict movement, causing starvation, laceration and infection, and—in those that need to return to the surface to breathe—suffocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overfishing">Overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a></div>
<p>Some specific examples of overfishing.</p>
<ul>
<li>On the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the availability of bay scallops has been greatly diminished by the overfishing of sharks in the area. A variety of sharks have, until recently, fed on <a href="/wiki/Batoidea" title="Batoidea">rays</a>, which are a main predator of bay scallops. With the shark population reduced, in some places almost totally, the rays have been free to dine on scallops to the point of greatly decreasing their numbers.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay's</a> once-flourishing <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> populations historically filtered the <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary's</a> entire water volume of excess nutrients every three or four days. Today that process takes almost a year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> and sediment, nutrients, and algae can cause problems in local waters. Oysters filter these pollutants, and either eat them or shape them into small packets that are deposited on the bottom where they are harmless.</li>
<li>The Australian government alleged in 2006 that <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> illegally overfished <a href="/wiki/Southern_bluefin_tuna" title="Southern bluefin tuna">southern bluefin tuna</a> by taking 12,000 to 20,000 tonnes per year instead of their agreed 6,000 tonnes; the value of such overfishing would be as much as US$2 billion. Such overfishing has resulted in severe damage to stocks. "Japan's huge appetite for tuna will take the most sought-after stocks to the brink of commercial extinction unless fisheries agree on more rigid quotas" stated the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Japan disputes this figure, but acknowledges that some overfishing has occurred in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li>Jackson, Jeremy B C et al. (2001) <a class="external text" href="http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/abstract/293/5530/629" rel="nofollow"><i>Historical overfishing and the recent collapse of coastal ecosystems</i></a> Science 293:629-638.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_biodiversity">Loss of biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Loss of biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a></div>
<p>Each <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> is affected by the other species in that ecosystem. There are very few single prey-single predator relationships. Most prey are consumed by more than one predator, and most predators have more than one prey. Their relationships are also influenced by other environmental factors. In most cases, if one species is removed from an ecosystem, other species will most likely be affected, up to the point of extinction.</p>
<p>Species <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> is a major contributor to the stability of ecosystems. When an organism exploits a wide range of resources, a decrease in biodiversity is less likely to have an impact. However, for an organism which exploit only limited resources, a decrease in biodiversity is more likely to have a strong effect.</p>
<p>Reduction of habitat, hunting and fishing of some species to <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> or near extinction, and pollution tend to tip the balance of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. For a systematic treatment of biodiversity within a <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Unified_neutral_theory_of_biodiversity" title="Unified neutral theory of biodiversity">unified neutral theory of biodiversity</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threatened_species">Threatened species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Threatened species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global standard for recording <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened</a> marine species is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> Red List of Threatened Species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> This list is the foundation for marine conservation priorities worldwide. A species is listed in the threatened category if it is considered to be <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">endangered</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>. Other categories are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_threatened" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_deficient" title="Data deficient">data deficient</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine">Marine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Marine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many marine species are under increasing risk of extinction and marine <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> is undergoing potentially irreversible loss due to threats such as <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> and coastal development.</p>
<p>By 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> had assessed about 3,000 marine species. This includes assessments of known species of shark, ray, chimaera, reef-building coral, grouper, marine turtle, seabird, and marine mammal. Almost one-quarter (22%) of these groups have been listed as threatened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_factsheet_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN_factsheet-86">[86]</a></sup></p>

<li><b>Sharks, rays, and chimaeras</b>: are deep water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> species, which makes them difficult to study in the wild. Not a lot is known about their ecology and population status. Much of what is currently known is from their capture in <a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">nets</a> from both targeted and accidental catch. Many of these slow growing species are not recovering from overfishing by shark fisheries around the world.</li>
<li><b>Groupers</b>: Major threats are overfishing, particularly the uncontrolled fishing of small juveniles and spawning adults.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reefs</a></b>: The primary threats to <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> are bleaching and disease which has been linked to an increase in sea temperatures. Other threats include coastal development, coral extraction, sedimentation and pollution. The <a href="/wiki/Coral_Triangle" title="Coral Triangle">coral triangle</a> (Indo-Malay-Philippine archipelago) region has the highest number of reef-building coral species in threatened category as well as the highest coral species diversity. The loss of coral reef ecosystems will have devastating effects on many marine species, as well as on people that depend on reef resources for their livelihoods.</li>
<li><b>Marine mammals</b>: include <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porpoise" title="Porpoise">porpoises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">sea lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walrus" title="Walrus">walruses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_otter" title="Sea otter">sea otter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_otter" title="Marine otter">marine otter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manatee" title="Manatee">manatees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dugong" title="Dugong">dugong</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a>. Major threats include entanglement in <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">ghost nets</a>, targeted harvesting, noise pollution from military and seismic sonar, and boat strikes. Other threats are water pollution, habitat loss from coastal development, loss of food sources due to the collapse of fisheries, and climate change.</li>
<li><b>Seabirds</b>: Major threats include <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline fisheries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spills</a>, and predation by rodents and cats in their breeding grounds. Other threats are habitat loss and degradation from coastal development, logging and pollution.</li>
<li><b>Marine turtles</b>: Marine turtles lay their eggs on beaches, and are subject to threats such as coastal development, sand mining, and predators, including humans who collect their eggs for food in many parts of the world. At sea, marine turtles can be targeted by small scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">subsistence fisheries</a>, or become <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> during <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trawling" title="Trawling">trawling</a> activities, or become entangled in <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">ghost nets</a> or struck by boats.</li>
</ul>
<p>An ambitious project, called the Global Marine Species Assessment, is under way to make IUCN Red List assessments for another 17,000 marine species by 2012. Groups targeted include the approximately 15,000 known marine fishes, and important habitat-forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_producer" title="Primary producer">primary producers</a> such <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seagrass" title="Seagrass">seagrasses</a>, certain <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweeds</a> and the remaining <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>; and important invertebrate groups including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_factsheet_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN_factsheet-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freshwater">Freshwater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Freshwater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freshwater fisheries have a disproportionately high diversity of species compared to other ecosystems. Although freshwater habitats cover less than 1% of the world’s surface, they provide a home for over 25% of known vertebrates, more than 126,000 known animal species, about 24,800 species of <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">freshwater fish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molluscs" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, and about 2,600 <a href="/wiki/Macrophyte" title="Macrophyte">macrophytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_factsheet_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN_factsheet-86">[86]</a></sup> Continuing industrial and agricultural developments place huge strain on these freshwater systems. Waters are polluted or extracted at high levels, wetlands are drained, rivers channelled, forests deforestated leading to sedimentation, invasive species are introduced, and over-harvesting occurs.</p>
<p>In the 2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> Red List, about 6,000 or 22% of the known freshwater species have been assessed at a global scale, leaving about 21,000 species still to be assessed. This makes clear that, worldwide, freshwater species are highly threatened, possibly more so than species in marine fisheries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> However, a significant proportion of freshwater species are listed as <a href="/wiki/Data_deficient" title="Data deficient">data deficient</a>, and more field surveys are needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_factsheet_86-3"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN_factsheet-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries_management">Fisheries management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_fisheries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Fisheries management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">Fisheries management</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishing" title="Sustainable fishing">Sustainable fishing</a></div>
<p>A recent paper published by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> of the USA warns that: "Synergistic effects of habitat destruction, overfishing, introduced species, warming, acidification, toxins, and massive runoff of nutrients are transforming once complex ecosystems like coral reefs and kelp forests into monotonous level bottoms, transforming clear and productive coastal seas into anoxic dead zones, and transforming complex food webs topped by big animals into simplified, microbially dominated ecosystems with boom and bust cycles of toxic dinoflagellate blooms, jellyfish, and disease".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
