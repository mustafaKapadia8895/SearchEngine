<p>The <b>Triassic–Jurassic extinction event</b> marks the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> periods, <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=201.3">201.3</a> million years ago</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is one of the major <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction events</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> eon, profoundly affecting life on land and in the oceans. In the seas, a whole <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> (<a href="/wiki/Conodont" title="Conodont">conodonts</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and 34% of marine genera disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> On land, all <a href="/wiki/Archosaur" title="Archosaur">archosaurs</a> other than <a href="/wiki/Crocodylomorpha" title="Crocodylomorpha">crocodylomorphs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sphenosuchia" title="Sphenosuchia">Sphenosuchia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crocodyliformes" title="Crocodyliformes">Crocodyliformes</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Avemetatarsalia" title="Avemetatarsalia">Avemetatarsalia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>), some remaining <a href="/wiki/Therapsid" title="Therapsid">therapsids</a>, and many of the large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibia" title="Amphibia">amphibians</a> became extinct.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="893" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg/350px-Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg/525px-Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg/700px-Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Triassic-jurassic_boundary.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ranges of families tetrapods through the Triassic and Early Jurassic</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At least half of the species now known to have been living on Earth at that time became extinct. This event vacated terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niches</a>, allowing the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> to assume the dominant roles in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period. This event happened in less than 10,000 years and occurred just before <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> started to break apart. In the area of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbingen" title="Tübingen">Tübingen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>), a Triassic-Jurassic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonebed" title="Bonebed">bonebed</a> can be found, which is characteristic for this boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Statistical analysis of marine losses at this time suggests that the decrease in diversity was caused more by a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> than by an increase in extinctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_theories">Current theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several explanations for this event have been suggested, but all have unanswered challenges:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gradual <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, sea-level fluctuations or a pulse of oceanic acidification<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> during the late <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> reached a <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping point</a>. However, this does not explain the suddenness of the extinctions in the marine realm.</li>
<li>Asteroid impact, but so far no impact crater of sufficient size has been dated to coincide with the Triassic–Jurassic boundary.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The eroded <a href="/wiki/Rochechouart_crater" title="Rochechouart crater">Rochechouart crater</a> in France has most recently been dated to 201 ±2 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmieder_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmieder-7">[7]</a></sup> but at 25 km across (possibly up to 50 km across originally), appears to be too small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSmith_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSmith-8">[8]</a></sup> The impact responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Annulus_(mathematics)" title="Annulus (mathematics)">annular</a> <a href="/wiki/Manicouagan_Reservoir" title="Manicouagan Reservoir">Manicouagan Reservoir</a> occurred about 12 million years before the extinction event - the Rochechouart crater is now thought to have been caused by part of the same fragmented impactor.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Massive volcanic eruptions, specifically the flood basalts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Atlantic_Magmatic_Province" title="Central Atlantic Magmatic Province">Central Atlantic Magmatic Province</a> (CAMP), would release <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>, which would cause either intense global warming (from the former) or cooling (from the latter).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blackburn2013_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-blackburn2013-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The isotopic composition of fossil soils of <a href="/wiki/Late_Triassic" title="Late Triassic">end Triassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Jurassic" title="Early Jurassic">Early Jurassic</a> has been tied to a large negative carbon isotope excursion (Whiteside et al. 2010). Carbon isotopes of lipids (<a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane"><i>n</i>-alkanes</a>) derived from leaf wax and <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, and total organic carbon from two sections of lake sediments interbedded with the CAMP in eastern North America have shown carbon isotope excursions similar to those found in the mostly marine <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=St._Audrie%E2%80%99s_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="St. Audrie’s Bay (page does not exist)">St. Audrie’s Bay</a> section, Somerset, England; the correlation suggests that the end-Triassic extinction event began at the same time in marine and terrestrial environments, slightly before the oldest basalts in eastern North America but simultaneous with the eruption of the oldest flows in Morocco (Also suggested by Deenen et al., 2010), with both a critical CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse and a marine biocalcification crisis.</dd>
<dd>Contemporaneous CAMP eruptions, mass extinction, and the carbon isotopic excursions are shown in the same places, making the case for a volcanic cause of a mass extinction. The catastrophic dissociation of <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">gas hydrates</a> (suggested as one possible cause of the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinction</a> of all time, the so-called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Great Dying</a>" at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> Period) may have exacerbated greenhouse conditions.</dd>
</dl>
