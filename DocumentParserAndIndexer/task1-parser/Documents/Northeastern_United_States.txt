<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Northeastern United States</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Northeastern United States</b>, also referred to as the <b>American Northeast</b> or simply <b>the Northeast</b>, is a geographical <a href="/wiki/List_of_regions_of_the_United_States" title="List of regions of the United States">region</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> bordered to the north by <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, to the east by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, to the south by the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a>, and to the west by the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a>. The Northeast is one of the four regions defined by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> for the collection and analysis of statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census-1">[1]</a></sup> The Census Bureau-defined region has a total area of 181,324 sq mi (469,630 km<sup>2</sup>) with 162,257 sq mi (420,240 km<sup>2</sup>) of that being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_mass" title="Land mass">land mass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although it lacks a unified <a href="/wiki/Cultural_identity" title="Cultural identity">cultural identity</a>, the Northeastern region is the nation's most <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economically developed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">densely populated</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_diversity" title="Cultural diversity">culturally diverse</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zelinksy1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zelinksy1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobbs_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hobbs-4">[4]</a></sup> Of the nation's four census regions, the Northeast is the second most urban, with 85 percent of its population residing in urban areas, led by the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">West</a> with 90 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Census Bureau has defined the Northeast region as comprising nine states: from northeast to southwest, they are <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Census-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[a]</a></sup> The region is subdivided into <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> (the six states east of New York) and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic states</a> (New York, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania).</p>
<p>This definition has been essentially unchanged since 1880 and is widely used as a standard for data tabulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-widely_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-widely-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The Census Bureau has acknowledged the limitations of this definition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> and the potential merits of a proposal created after the 1950 census that would include changing regional boundaries to include <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> with the Mid-Atlantic states, but ultimately decided that "the new system did not win enough overall acceptance among data users to warrant adoption as an official new set of general-purpose State groupings. The previous development of many series of statistics, arranged and issued over long periods of time on the basis of the existing State groupings, favored the retention of the summary units of the current regions and divisions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GARM_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GARM-13">[12]</a></sup> The Census Bureau confirmed in 1994 that it would continue to "review the components of the regions and divisions to ensure that they continue to represent the most useful combinations of States and State equivalents."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GARM_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-GARM-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many organizations and reference works follow the Census Bureau's definition for the region;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> however, other entities define the Northeastern United States in significantly different ways for various purposes. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_of_American_Geographers" title="Association of American Geographers">Association of American Geographers</a> divides the Northeast into two divisions- "New England", which consists of Maine, Vermont, New Hampshire, Massachusetts, Rhode Island and Connecticut, and the "Middle States", which consists of New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Delaware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_America" title="Geological Society of America">Geological Society of America</a> defines the Northeast as these same states but with the addition of Maryland and the District of Columbia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> The narrowest definitions include only the states of New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Other more restrictive definitions include New England and New York as part of the Northeast United States, but exclude Pennsylvania and New Jersey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> States beyond the Census Bureau definition that other entities include in the Northeast United States are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Delaware, Maryland, and Washington, D.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobbs_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hobbs-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McIlwraith_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-McIlwraith-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shelley_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shelley-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRC-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDA_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-USDA-26">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Delaware, Maryland, Washington, D.C., and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>Delaware, Maryland, Washington, D.C., West Virginia, and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>Delaware, and parts of Maryland, West Virginia, and Virginia (but excluding Washington, D.C. and surrounding area)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBoP_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FBoP-32">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>West Virginia, Ohio, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTDS_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-PTDS-33">[32]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_peoples">Indigenous peoples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indigenous peoples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Northeastern_Woodlands" title="Indigenous peoples of the Northeastern Woodlands">Indigenous peoples of the Northeastern Woodlands</a></div>
<p>Anthropologists recognize the "Northeastern Woodlands" as one of the <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Classification of indigenous peoples of the Americas">cultural regions</a> that existed in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a> at the time of <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">European colonists</a> in the 15th and later centuries. Most did not settle in North America until the 17th century. The cultural area, known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeastern_Woodland" title="Northeastern Woodland">Northeastern Woodlands</a>", in addition to covering the entire Northeast U.S., also covered much of what is now Canada and others regions of what is now the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">eastern United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Among the many tribes that inhabited this area were those that made up the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois nations</a> and the numerous <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian peoples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Native_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Native-35">[34]</a></sup> In the United States of the 21st century, 18 <a href="/wiki/List_of_federally_recognized_tribes_by_state" title="List of federally recognized tribes by state">federally recognized tribes</a> reside in the Northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wildlife_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-wildlife-36">[35]</a></sup> For the most part, the people of the Northeastern Woodlands, on whose lands European fishermen began camping to dry their codfish in the early 1600s, lived in villages, especially after being influenced by the agricultural traditions of the Ohio and Mississippi valley societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_history">Colonial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Colonial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All of the states making up the Northeastern region were among the original <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a>, though Maine, Vermont, and Delaware were part of other colonies before the United States became independent in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. The two cultural and geographic regions that form parts of the Northeastern region have distinct histories.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_England">New England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Landing-Bacon.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="368" data-file-width="559" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Landing-Bacon.PNG/220px-Landing-Bacon.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Landing-Bacon.PNG/330px-Landing-Bacon.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Landing-Bacon.PNG/440px-Landing-Bacon.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<i>The Landing of the Pilgrims</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_A._Bacon" title="Henry A. Bacon">Henry A. Bacon</a> (1877)</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_New_England" title="History of New England">History of New England</a></div>
<p>The first Europeans to settle New England were <a href="/wiki/Pilgrims_(Plymouth_Colony)" title="Pilgrims (Plymouth Colony)">Pilgrims</a> from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, who landed in present-day Massachusetts in 1620.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arenstam_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arenstam-38">[37]</a></sup> The Pilgrims arrived by the <i><a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a></i> ship and founded <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a> so they could practice religion freely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arenstam_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arenstam-38">[37]</a></sup> Ten years later, a larger group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritans</a> settled north of Plymouth Colony in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachusetts Bay Colony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> In 1636, colonists established <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Colony" title="Connecticut Colony">Connecticut Colony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Providence_Plantations" title="Providence Plantations">Providence Plantations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-41">[40]</a></sup> Providence was founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Williams_(theologian)" title="Roger Williams (theologian)">Roger Williams</a>, who was banished by Massachusetts for his beliefs in freedom of religion, and it was the first colony to guarantee all citizens freedom of worship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-41">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anne_Hutchinson" title="Anne Hutchinson">Anne Hutchinson</a>, who was also banished by Massachusetts, formed the town of <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Rhode_Island" title="Portsmouth, Rhode Island">Portsmouth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-41">[40]</a></sup> Providence, Portsmouth, and two other towns (<a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick</a>) consolidated to form the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Rhode_Island_and_Providence_Plantations" title="Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations">Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the first settlers of New England were motivated by religion, in more recent history, New England has become one of the least religious parts of the United States. In a 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gallup_Organization" title="The Gallup Organization">Gallup</a> survey, less than half of residents in Vermont, New Hampshire, Maine, and Massachusetts reported religion as an important part of their daily life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> In a 2010 Gallup survey, less than 30% of residents in Vermont, New Hampshire, Maine, and Massachusetts reported <a href="/wiki/Church_attendance" title="Church attendance">attending church</a> weekly, giving them the lowest church attendance among U.S. states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>New England played a prominent role in early American education. Starting in the 17th century, the larger towns in New England opened <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammar_schools" title="Grammar schools">grammar schools</a>, the forerunner of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The first public school in the English colonies was the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Latin_School" title="Boston Latin School">Boston Latin School</a>, founded in 1635.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> In 1636, the colonial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legislature_of_Massachusetts" title="Legislature of Massachusetts">legislature of Massachusetts</a> founded <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a>, the oldest <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">institution of higher learning</a> in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-Atlantic">Mid-Atlantic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mid-Atlantic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first European explorer known to have explored the Atlantic shoreline of the Northeast since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">Norse</a> was <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">Giovanni da Verrazzano</a> in 1524. His ship <i>La Dauphine</i> explored the coast from what is now known as Florida to New Brunswick. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hudson" title="Henry Hudson">Henry Hudson</a> explored the area of present-day New York in 1609 and claimed it for the Netherlands. His journey stimulated Dutch interest, and the area became known as <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>. In 1625, the city of <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a> (the location of present-day New York City) was designated the capital of the province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> The Dutch New Netherland settlement along the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> and, for a time, the <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> settlement along the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> divided the English settlements in the north and the south. In 1664, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a> formally annexed New Netherland and incorporated it into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_colonial_empire" title="English colonial empire">English colonial empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radford_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radford-47">[46]</a></sup> The territory became the colonies of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_New_York" title="Province of New York">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_New_Jersey" title="Province of New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radford_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Radford-47">[46]</a></sup> New Jersey was originally split into <a href="/wiki/East_Jersey" title="East Jersey">East Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Jersey" title="West Jersey">West Jersey</a> until the two were united as a royal colony in 1702.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radford_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Radford-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1681, <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a>, who wanted to give <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a> a land of religious freedom, founded <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and extended freedom of religion to all citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Penn strongly desired access to the sea for his <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania Province</a> and leased what then came to be known as the "Lower Counties on the Delaware"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hod_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-hod-49">[48]</a></sup> from the Duke.</p>
<p>Penn established representative government and briefly combined his two possessions under one General Assembly in 1682. However, by 1704 the Province of Pennsylvania had grown so large that their representatives wanted to make decisions without the assent of the Lower Counties and the two groups of representatives began meeting on their own, one at <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, and the other at New Castle. Penn and his heirs remained proprietors of both and always appointed the same person Governor for their Province of Pennsylvania and their territory of the Lower Counties. The fact that Delaware and Pennsylvania shared the same governor was not unique. From 1703 to 1738, New York and New Jersey shared a governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> Massachusetts and New Hampshire also shared a governor for some time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While most of the Northeastern United States lie in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_physiographic_region#Appalachian_Highlands" title="United States physiographic region">Appalachian Highlands</a> physiographic region, some are also part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_coastal_plain" title="Atlantic coastal plain">Atlantic coastal plain</a> which extends south to the southern tip of Florida. The coastal plain areas (including <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> in Massachusetts, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> in New York, most of New Jersey, Delaware, and the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> region of Maryland) are generally low and flat, with sandy soil and marshy land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobbs_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hobbs-4">[4]</a></sup> The highlands, including the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>, are generally heavily forested, ranging from rolling hills to summits greater than 5,000 feet (1,500 m), and pocked with many lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobbs_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hobbs-4">[4]</a></sup> The highest peak in the Northeast is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Washington_(New_Hampshire)" title="Mount Washington (New Hampshire)">Mount Washington (New Hampshire)</a>, at 6,288 feet (1,917 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use">Land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, forest-use covered approximately 60% of the Northeastern states (including Delaware, Maryland, and the District of Columbia), about twice the national average. About 12% was cropland and another 4% grassland pasture or range. There is also more urbanized land in the Northeast (11%) than any other region in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDA_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-USDA-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The climate of the Northeastern United States varies from northernmost New England in Maine to southernmost New Jersey and Pennsylvania. The climate of the region is created by the position of the general west to east flow of weather in the middle latitudes that much of the central and northern USA is controlled by. Summers are normally warm in northern areas to hot in southern areas, while in winter the subtropical high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_High" title="Bermuda High">Bermuda High</a> retreats eastward, and the prevailing westerly flow of air masses combines with the polar jet stream to bring cold and frequent storm systems to the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate-53">[52]</a></sup> Annual mean temperatures range from the low 50s F from northern Maryland to southern Connecticut, to the 40s F in most of New York State, New England, and northern Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Climate-53">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife">Wildlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wildlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Northeast has 72 <a href="/wiki/National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="National Wildlife Refuge">National Wildlife Refuges</a>, encompassing more than 500,000 acres (780 sq mi; 2,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of habitat, and designed to protect some of the 92 different threatened and <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a> living in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wildlife_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-wildlife-36">[35]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>As of the July 2013 U.S. Census Bureau estimate, the population of the region totaled 55,943,073.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopEstUS_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopEstUS-56">[55]</a></sup> With an average of 345.5 people per square mile, the Northeast is 2.5 times as densely populated as the second-most dense region, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a>. Since the last century, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._states_by_population_growth_rate" title="List of U.S. states by population growth rate">U.S. population has been shifting away</a> from the Northeast (and <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a>) toward the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">West</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two U.S. Census Bureau divisions in the Northeast (<a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic</a>) rank #1 and #2 among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._states_by_population_density" title="List of U.S. states by population density">9 divisions in population density</a> according to the 2013 population estimate. The <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_States" title="South Atlantic States">South Atlantic</a> region (233.1) was very close behind New England (233.2). Due to the faster growth of the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_States" title="South Atlantic States">South Atlantic</a> region, it will take over the #2 division rank in population density in the next estimate, dropping New England to 3rd position. New England is projected to retain the number 3 rank for many, many years, as the only other lower-ranked division with even half the population density of New England is the <a href="/wiki/East_North_Central_States" title="East North Central States">East North Central</a> division (192.1) and this region's population is projected to grow slowly. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-census.gov_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-census.gov-58">[57]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the Northeast accounts for approximately 23% of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a> oversees 34 nuclear reactors, eight for research or testing and 26 for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">power production</a> in the Northeastern United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NRC-25">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Prisons" title="Federal Bureau of Prisons">Federal Bureau of Prisons</a> maintains 17 federal prisons and two affiliated private facilities in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBoP_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-FBoP-32">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geographer <a href="/wiki/Wilbur_Zelinsky" title="Wilbur Zelinsky">Wilbur Zelinsky</a> asserts that the Northeast region lacks a unified cultural identity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zelinksy1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zelinksy1-3">[3]</a></sup> but has served as a "culture hearth" for the rest of the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[67]</a></sup> Several much smaller geographical regions within the Northeast do have distinct cultural identities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zelinksy1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zelinksy1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Almost half of the <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmarks</a> maintained by the National Park Service are located in the Northeastern United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a 2009 <a href="/wiki/Gallup_(company)" title="Gallup (company)">Gallup poll</a>, the Northeastern states differ from most of the rest of the U.S. in religious affiliation, generally reflecting the descendants of immigration patterns of the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with many Catholics arriving from Ireland, Italy, and eastern Europe. Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut, New York, and New Jersey are the only states in the nation where <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholics</a> outnumber <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestants</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">Christian denominations</a>. More than 20% of respondents in Maine, New Hampshire, and Vermont declared no religious identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[69]</a></sup> Compared to other U.S. regions, the Northeast has the lowest regular religious service attendance and the fewest number of people for whom religion is an important part of their daily lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Northeast region is home to several professional sports franchises in the <a href="/wiki/Major_professional_sports_leagues_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Major professional sports leagues in the United States and Canada">"Big Four" leagues</a> (NFL, NBA, NHL and MLB),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[71]</a></sup> with more than 100 championships collectively among them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[72]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>New York metropolitan area: <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants" title="New York Giants">Giants</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Jets" title="New York Jets">Jets</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">Yankees</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">Mets</a> (MLB), <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">Knicks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Nets" title="Brooklyn Nets">Nets</a> (NBA), <a href="/wiki/New_York_Rangers" title="New York Rangers">Rangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Islanders" title="New York Islanders">Islanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Devils" title="New Jersey Devils">Devils</a> (NHL)</li>
<li>Philadelphia: <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Eagles" title="Philadelphia Eagles">Eagles</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Phillies</a> (MLB), <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_76ers" title="Philadelphia 76ers">76ers</a> (NBA), <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Flyers" title="Philadelphia Flyers">Flyers</a> (NHL)</li>
<li>Washington, D.C.: <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Redskins</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Nationals</a> (MLB), <a href="/wiki/Washington_Wizards" title="Washington Wizards">Wizards</a> (NBA), <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Capitals</a> (NHL)</li>
<li>Boston: <a href="/wiki/New_England_Patriots" title="New England Patriots">Patriots</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Red Sox</a> (MLB), <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Celtics</a> (NBA), <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Bruins</a> (NHL)</li>
<li>Baltimore: <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Ravens" title="Baltimore Ravens">Ravens</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Orioles</a> (MLB)</li>
<li>Pittsburgh: <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Steelers" title="Pittsburgh Steelers">Steelers</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pirates</a> (MLB), <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Penguins</a> (NHL)</li>
<li>Buffalo: <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Bills</a> (NFL), <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Sabres" title="Buffalo Sabres">Sabres</a> (NHL)</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> features five Northeastern teams: <a href="/wiki/D.C._United" title="D.C. United">D.C. United</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_England_Revolution" title="New England Revolution">New England Revolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_FC" title="New York City FC">New York City FC</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Red_Bulls" title="New York Red Bulls">New York Red Bulls</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Union" title="Philadelphia Union">Philadelphia Union</a>. The region also has three <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_National_Basketball_Association" title="Women's National Basketball Association">WNBA</a> teams: <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Sun" title="Connecticut Sun">Connecticut Sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Liberty" title="New York Liberty">New York Liberty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Mystics" title="Washington Mystics">Washington Mystics</a>.</p>
<p>Notable golf tournaments in the Northeastern United States include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank_Championship" title="Deutsche Bank Championship">Deutsche Bank Championship</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Barclays" title="The Barclays">The Barclays</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quicken_Loans_National" title="Quicken Loans National">Quicken Loans National</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_City_LPGA_Classic" title="Atlantic City LPGA Classic">Atlantic City LPGA Classic</a>. The <a href="/wiki/US_Open_(tennis)" title="US Open (tennis)">US Open</a>, held at New York City, is one of the four Grand Slam tennis tournaments, whereas <a href="/wiki/Washington_Open_(tennis)" title="Washington Open (tennis)">Washington Open</a> is part of the <a href="/wiki/ATP_World_Tour_500_series" title="ATP World Tour 500 series">ATP World Tour 500 series</a>.</p>
<p>Notable Northeastern motorsports tracks include <a href="/wiki/Watkins_Glen_International" title="Watkins Glen International">Watkins Glen International</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dover_International_Speedway" title="Dover International Speedway">Dover International Speedway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pocono_Raceway" title="Pocono Raceway">Pocono Raceway</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_Motor_Speedway" title="New Hampshire Motor Speedway">New Hampshire Motor Speedway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lime_Rock_Park" title="Lime Rock Park">Lime Rock Park</a>, which have hosted <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IndyCar" title="IndyCar">IndyCar</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Motor_Sports_Association" title="International Motor Sports Association">International Motor Sports Association</a> races. Also, drag strips such as <a href="/wiki/Old_Bridge_Township_Raceway_Park" title="Old Bridge Township Raceway Park">Englishtown</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_England_Dragway" title="New England Dragway">Epping</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maple_Grove_Raceway" title="Maple Grove Raceway">Reading</a> have hosted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHRA" title="NHRA">NHRA</a> national events. <a href="/wiki/Pimlico_Race_Course" title="Pimlico Race Course">Pimlico Race Course</a> at Baltimore and <a href="/wiki/Belmont_Park" title="Belmont Park">Belmont Park</a> at New York host the <a href="/wiki/Preakness_Stakes" title="Preakness Stakes">Preakness Stakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belmont_Stakes" title="Belmont Stakes">Belmont Stakes</a> horse races, which are part of the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Crown_of_Thoroughbred_Racing_(United_States)" title="Triple Crown of Thoroughbred Racing (United States)">Triple Crown of Thoroughbred Racing</a>.</p>
<p>The region has also been noted for the relative prevalence of the traditionally Northeastern sports of <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lacrosse" title="Lacrosse">lacrosse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rate of potentially preventable hospitalizations in the Northeastern United States fell from 2005 to 2011 for overall conditions, acute conditions, and chronic conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_party_strength_in_U.S._states" title="Political party strength in U.S. states">Political party strength in U.S. states</a></div>
