<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">North American Plate</h1>

<p>The <b>North American Plate</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> covering most of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, extreme northeastern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">the Azores</a>. It extends eastward to the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> and westward to the <a href="/wiki/Chersky_Range" title="Chersky Range">Chersky Range</a> in eastern Siberia. The plate includes both <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>. The interior of the main continental landmass includes an extensive <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granitic</a> core called a <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">craton</a>. Along most of the edges of this craton are fragments of crustal material called <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a>, accreted to the craton by tectonic actions over the long span of geologic time. It is thought that much of North America west of the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> is composed of such terranes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries">Boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The southerly boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Plate" title="Cocos Plate">Cocos Plate</a> to the west and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Plate" title="Caribbean Plate">Caribbean Plate</a> to the east is a <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform fault</a>, represented by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cayman_Trench" title="Cayman Trench">Cayman Trench</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Motagua_Fault" title="Motagua Fault">Motagua Fault</a> through <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>. The parallel <a href="/wiki/Septentrional-Oriente_fault_zone" title="Septentrional-Oriente fault zone">Septentrional</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enriquillo-Plantain_Garden_fault_zone" title="Enriquillo-Plantain Garden fault zone">Enriquillo-Plantain Garden faults</a>, which run through the island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> and bound the <a href="/wiki/Gon%C3%A2ve_Microplate" title="Gonâve Microplate">Gonâve Microplate</a>, are also a part of the boundary. The rest of the southerly margin which extends east to the Mid Atlantic Ridge and marks the boundary between the North American Plate and the <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plate</a> is vague but located near the <a href="/wiki/Fifteen-Twenty_Fracture_Zone" title="Fifteen-Twenty Fracture Zone">Fifteen-Twenty Fracture Zone</a> around 16°N.</p>
<p>On the northerly boundary is a continuation of the Mid-Atlantic ridge called the <a href="/wiki/Gakkel_Ridge" title="Gakkel Ridge">Gakkel Ridge</a>. The rest of the boundary in the far northwestern part of the plate extends into <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. This boundary continues from the end of the Gakkel Ridge as the <a href="/wiki/Laptev_Sea_Rift" title="Laptev Sea Rift">Laptev Sea Rift</a>, on to a transitional deformation zone in the <a href="/wiki/Chersky_Range" title="Chersky Range">Chersky Range</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/Ulakhan_Fault" title="Ulakhan Fault">Ulakhan Fault</a> between it and the <a href="/wiki/Okhotsk_Plate" title="Okhotsk Plate">Okhotsk Plate</a>, and finally the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Trench" title="Aleutian Trench">Aleutian Trench</a> to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_Fault" title="Queen Charlotte Fault">Queen Charlotte Fault</a> system.</p>
<p>The westerly boundary is the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_Fault" title="Queen Charlotte Fault">Queen Charlotte Fault</a> running offshore along the coast of Alaska and the <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_subduction_zone" title="Cascadia subduction zone">Cascadia subduction zone</a> to the north, the <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault" title="San Andreas Fault">San Andreas Fault</a> through <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_Pacific_Rise" title="East Pacific Rise">East Pacific Rise</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_America_Trench" title="Middle America Trench">Middle America Trench</a> to the south.</p>
<p>On its western edge, the <a href="/wiki/Farallon_Plate" title="Farallon Plate">Farallon Plate</a> has been <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducting</a> under the North American Plate since the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> Period. The Farallon Plate has almost completely subducted beneath the western portion of the North American Plate leaving that part of the North American Plate in contact with the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> as the <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault" title="San Andreas Fault">San Andreas Fault</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Fuca_Plate" title="Juan de Fuca Plate">Juan de Fuca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Explorer_Plate" title="Explorer Plate">Explorer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorda_plate" title="Gorda plate">Gorda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rivera_Plate" title="Rivera Plate">Rivera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Plate" title="Cocos Plate">Cocos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Plate" title="Nazca Plate">Nazca</a> plates are remnants of the Farallon Plate.</p>
<p>The boundary along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> is complex. The Gulf is underlain by the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California_Rift_Zone" title="Gulf of California Rift Zone">Gulf of California Rift Zone</a>, a series of rift basins and transform fault segments between the northern end of the <a href="/wiki/East_Pacific_Rise" title="East Pacific Rise">East Pacific Rise</a> in the mouth of the gulf to the San Andreas Fault system in the vicinity of the <a href="/wiki/Salton_Sink" title="Salton Sink">Salton Trough</a> rift/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brawley_seismic_zone" title="Brawley seismic zone">Brawley seismic zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is generally accepted that a piece of the North American Plate was broken off and transported north as the East Pacific Rise propagated northward, creating the Gulf of California. However, it is as yet unclear whether the oceanic crust east of the Rise and west of the mainland coast of Mexico is actually a new plate beginning to converge with the North American Plate, consistent with the standard model of rift zone spreading centers generally.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hotspots">Hotspots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hotspots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A few <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a> are thought to exist below the North American Plate. The most notable hotspots are the <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_hotspot" title="Yellowstone hotspot">Yellowstone</a> (Wyoming), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raton_hotspot" title="Raton hotspot">Raton</a> (New Mexico), and <a href="/wiki/Anahim_hotspot" title="Anahim hotspot">Anahim</a> (British Columbia) hotspots. These are thought to be caused by a narrow stream of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">hot</a> <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convecting</a> up from the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core-mantle_boundary" title="Core-mantle boundary">core-mantle boundary</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GH_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GH-4">[4]</a></sup> although some geologists think that upper-mantle convection is a more likely cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Yellowstone and Anahim hotspots are thought to have first arrived during the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> period and are still geologically active, creating <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>. The Yellowstone hotspot is most notable for the <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_Caldera" title="Yellowstone Caldera">Yellowstone Caldera</a> and the many <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">calderas</a> that lie in the <a href="/wiki/Snake_River_Plain" title="Snake River Plain">Snake River Plain</a> while the Anahim hotspot is most notable for the <a href="/wiki/Anahim_Volcanic_Belt" title="Anahim Volcanic Belt">Anahim Volcanic Belt</a>, currently found in the <a href="/wiki/Nazko_Cone" title="Nazko Cone">Nazko Cone</a> area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plate_motion">Plate motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plate motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the most part, the North American Plate moves in roughly a southwest direction away from the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.</p>
<p>The motion of the plate cannot be driven by subduction as no part of the North American Plate is being subducted, except for a small section comprising part of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a>; thus other mechanisms continue to be investigated.</p>
<p>One recent study suggests that a mantle convective current is propelling the plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
