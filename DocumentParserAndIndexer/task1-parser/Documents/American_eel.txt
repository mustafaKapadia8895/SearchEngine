<p>The <b>American eel</b> (<i>Anguilla rostrata</i>) is a facultative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catadromous" title="Catadromous">catadromous</a> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> found on the eastern coast of North America. Eels (<i>Anguilla</i> spp.) are fish belonging to the elopomorph superorder, a group of phylogenetically ancient teleosts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The American eel has a slender snakelike body that is covered with a <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucous</a> layer, which makes the eel appear to be naked and slimy despite the presence of minute <a href="/wiki/Fish_scale" title="Fish scale">scales</a>. A long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsum_(anatomy)" title="Dorsum (anatomy)">dorsal</a> fin runs from the middle of the back and is continuous with a similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">ventral</a> fin. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic" title="Pelvic">Pelvic</a> fins are absent, and relatively small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral</a> fin can be found near the midline, followed by the head and gill-covers. Variations exist in coloration, from olive green, brown shading to greenish-yellow and light gray or white on the belly. Eels from clear water are often lighter than those from dark, tannic acid streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eel lives in <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> and estuaries and only leaves these habitats to enter the Atlantic Ocean to start its spawning migration to the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Spawning takes place far offshore where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> hatch. The female can lay up to 4 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buoyant" title="Buoyant">buoyant</a> eggs a year, and dies after egg-laying. After the eggs hatch and the early-stage larvae develop into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leptocephali" title="Leptocephali">leptocephali</a>, the young eels move toward North America where they metamorphose into glass eels and enter freshwater systems where they grow as yellow eels until they begin to mature.</p>
<p>The American eel is found along the Atlantic coast including <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> and as far north as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a> region. It is also present in the river systems of the eastern Gulf of Mexico and in some areas further south. Like all anguillid eels, American eels hunt predominantly at night, and during the day they hide in mud, sand or gravel very close to shore, at depths of roughly 5 to 6 feet. They feed on crustaceans, aquatic insects, small insects, and probably any aquatic organisms that they can find and eat. In the Lake Ontario system, they have been demonstrating dietary preferences towards the invasive round goby.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>American eels are economically important in various areas along the East Coast as bait for fishing for sport fishes such as the striped bass, or as a food fish in some areas. Their recruitment stage, the glass eel, are also caught and sold for use in aquaculture, although this is now restricted in most areas.</p>
<p>Eels were once an abundant species in rivers, and were an important fishery for aboriginal people. The construction of hydroelectric dams has blocked their migrations and locally extirpated eels in many watersheds. For example, in Canada, the vast numbers of eels in the St. Lawrence and Ottawa Rivers have dwindled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COSEWIC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-COSEWIC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American eel <i>Anguilla rostrata</i> was first described in 1817 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alexandre_Lesueur" title="Charles Alexandre Lesueur">Lesueur</a>. <i>Anguilla</i> is Latin for eel, and <i>rostrata</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word that can mean either "beaked or curved" or "long nose". <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Anguille d’Amérique</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Anguila americana</i></span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rostrata.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="390" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Rostrata.jpg/220px-Rostrata.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Rostrata.jpg/330px-Rostrata.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/Rostrata.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>American eels can grow to 1.22 m (4.0 ft) in length and to 7.5 kg (17 lb) in weight. Females are generally larger than males, lighter in color, with smaller eyes and higher fins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The body is elongate and snake-like. Its dorsal and anal fins are confluent with the rudimentary caudal fin. It lacks ventral fins but pectoral fins are present. The lateral line is well-developed and complete. The head is long and conical, with rather small, well-developed eyes. The mouth is terminal with jaws that are not particularly elongated. The teeth are small, pectinate or setiform in several series on the jaws and the vomer. Minute teeth also present on the <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharyngeal</a> bones, forming a patch on the upper pharyngeals. Tongue present with thick lips that are attached by a frenum in front. Nostrils are superior and well separated. Gill openings are partly below pectoral fins, relatively well-developed and well separated from one another. Inner gill slits are wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fahay_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fahay-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scales are small, rudimentary, cycloid, relatively well embedded below the epidermis and therefore often difficult to see without magnification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The scales are not arranged in overlapping rows as they often are in other fish species but are rather irregular, in some places distributed like “parquet flooring”. In general, one row of scales lies at right angle to the next, although the rows immediately above and below the lateral line lie at an angle of approximately 45°. Unlike other bony fishes, the first scales do not develop immediately after the larval stage but appear much later on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch2003_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch2003-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several morphological features distinguish the American eel from other eel species. Tesch (1977)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup> described three morphological characteristics which persist through all stages from larvae to maturing eels: the total number of vertebrae (mean 107.2), the number of myomeres (mean 108.2), and the distance between the origin of the dorsal fin to the anus (mean 9.1% of total length).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_natural_habitat">Distribution and natural habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and natural habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_range">Geographic range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geographic range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The distribution of the American eel encompasses all accessible freshwater (streams and lakes), estuaries and coastal marine waters across a latitudinal range of 5 to 62 N.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Their natural range includes the eastern North Atlantic Ocean coastline from Venezuela to Greenland and including Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott.2C_W.B_1973_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott.2C_W.B_1973-12">[12]</a></sup> Inland, this species extends into the Great Lakes and the Mississippi River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jessop.2C_B.M_2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jessop.2C_B.M_2006-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonindigenous occurrences of this species in the United States were recorded from Lake Mead on the Colorado River and on the Arizona border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minckley_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-minckley-14">[14]</a></sup> It was stocked on a few occasions in Sacramento and San Francisco bay, California, in the late 1800s. No apparent evidence of survival on these occasions was noted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was also stocked and unintentionally introduced in various states, including Illinois, Indiana,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Nebraska, Nevada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minckley_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-minckley-14">[14]</a></sup> North Carolina,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Ohio and Pennsylvania,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigler.2C_F._F._1963_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sigler.2C_F._F._1963-18">[18]</a></sup> and Wisconsin. Stocking of this species also occurred in Utah in the late 1800s, but soon disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigler.2C_F._F._1963_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sigler.2C_F._F._1963-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_habitat">Natural habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eels are bottom dwellers. They hide in burrows, tubes, snags, masses of plants, other types of shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fahay_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fahay-7">[7]</a></sup> They are found in a variety of habitats including streams, rivers, and muddy or silt-bottomed lakes during their freshwater stage, as well as oceanic waters, coastal bays and estuaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COSEWIC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-COSEWIC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott.2C_W.B_1973_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scott.2C_W.B_1973-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jessop.2C_B.M_2006_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jessop.2C_B.M_2006-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Individuals during the continental stage occasionally migrate between fresh, salt and brackish water habitats and have varying degrees of residence time in each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> During winter, eels burrow under the mud and enter a state of torpor (or complete inactivity) at temperatures below 5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> although they may occasionally be active during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COSEWIC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-COSEWIC-5">[5]</a></sup> Temperature requirements are suggested to be flexible. It has been found that American eels during elver stage can survive temperature as low as −0.8 °C. Barila and Stauffer (1980) reported a final mean temperature preference at 16.7 °C. Karlsson et al. (1984) disagreed with this interpretation and found the final temperature preference of 17.4 ± 2.0 °C with a 95% confidence interval.</p>
<p>Seasonal patterns described by Fletcher and Anderson (1972) generalize annual movements from freshwater to estuaries and coastal bays to feed during spring, then either a return during the fall to overwinter (juvenile and immature adults), or a southward migration to the spawning grounds (silver eels Continental phase eels appear highly plastic in habitat use. Eels are extremely mobile and may access habitats that appear unavailable to them, using small watercourses or moving through wet grasses. Small eels (&lt;100 mm total length) are able to climb and may succeed in passing over vertical barriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Habitat availability may be reduced by factors such as habitat deterioration, barriers to upstream migration (larger eels), and barriers (i.e. turbines) to downstream migration that can result in mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COSEWIC_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-COSEWIC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eel_life_history" title="Eel life history">Eel life history</a></div>
<p>The American eel’s complex life history begins far offshore in the Sargasso Sea in a <a href="/wiki/Semelparity_and_iteroparity#Semelparity" title="Semelparity and iteroparity">semelparous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panmixia" title="Panmixia">panmictic</a> reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfman_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Helfman-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> From there, young eels drift with ocean currents and then migrate inland into streams, rivers and lakes. This journey may take many years to complete with some eels travelling as far as 6,000 kilometers. After reaching these freshwater bodies they feed and mature for approximately 10 to 25 years before migrating back to the Sargasso Sea in order to complete their life cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COSEWIC_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-COSEWIC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Life stages are detailed below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>1. Eggs: The eggs hatch within a week of deposition in the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a>. McCleave et al. (1987) suggested that hatching peaks in February and may continue until April. Wang and Tzeng (2000) proposed, on the basis of otolith back-calculations, that hatching occurs from March to October and peaks in August. However, Cieri and McCleave (2000) argued that these back-calculated spawning dates do not match collection evidence and may be explained by resorption. Fecundity for many eels is between about 0.5 to 4.0 million eggs, with larger individuals releasing as many as 8.5 million eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wenner_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wenner-30">[30]</a></sup> The diameter of egg is about 1.1 mm. Fertilization is external, and adult eels are presumed to die after spawning. None has been reported to migrate up rivers.</p>
<p>2. <a href="/wiki/Leptocephalus" title="Leptocephalus">Leptocephali</a>: The leptocephalus is the larval form, a stage strikingly different from the adult form the eels will grow into. Leptocephali are transparent with a small pointed head and large teeth and are frequently described “leaf-like”. The laterally compressed larvae are passively transported west and north to the coastal waters on the eastern coast of North America, by the surface currents of the Gulf Stream system, a journey that will last between 7 and 12 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-27">[27]</a></sup> Vertical distribution is usually restricted to the upper 350 m of the ocean. Growth has been evaluated at about 0.21 to 0.38 mm per day.</p>
<p>3. Glass eel: As they enter the continental shelf, leptocephali metamorphose into glass eels (juveniles), which are transparent and possess the typical elongate and serpentine eel shape. The term glass eel refers to all developmental stages between the end of metamorphosis and full pigmentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch2003_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch2003-9">[9]</a></sup> Metamorphosis occurs when leptocephali are about 55 to 65 mm long. Mean age at this metamorphosis has been evaluated at 200 days and estuarine arrival at 255 days; giving 55 days between glass eel metamorphosis and estuarine arrival. Young eels use selective tidal stream transport to move up estuaries. As they enter coastal waters, the animals essentially transform from a pelagic oceanic organism to a benthic continental organism.</p>
<p>4. Elvers: Glass eels become progressively pigmented as they approach the shore; these eels are termed elvers. The melanic pigmentation process occurs when the young eels are in coastal waters. At this phase of the life cycle, the eel is still sexually undifferentiated. The elver stage lasts about three to twelve months. Elvers that enter fresh water may spend much of this period migrating upstream. Elver influx is linked to increased temperature and reduced flow early in the migration season, and to tidal cycle influence later on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>5. Yellow eels: This is the sexually immature adult stage of American eel. They begin to develop a yellow color and a creamy or yellowish belly. In this phase, the eels are still mainly nocturnal. Those remained in estuarine environment continue to go through their life cycle more quickly than those traveled into freshwater. Those in freshwater, however, tend to live longer and attain much larger sizes. Sexual differentiation occurs during the yellow stage and appears to be strongly influenced by environmental conditions. Krueger and Oliveira (1999) suggested that density was the primary environmental factor influencing the sex ratio of eels in a river, with high densities promoting the production of males. From life history traits of four rivers of Maine, Oliveira and McCleave (2000) evaluated that sexual differentiation was completed by 270 mm total length.</p>
<p>6. Silver eels: As the maturation process proceeds, the yellow eel metamorphoses into a silver eel. The silvering metamorphosis results in morphological and physiological modifications that prepare the animal to migrate back to the Sargasso Sea. The eel acquires a greyish colour with a whitish or cream coloration ventrally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott.2C_W.B_1973_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scott.2C_W.B_1973-12">[12]</a></sup> The digestive tract degenerates, the pectoral fins enlarge to improve swimming capacity, eye diameter expands and visual pigments in the retina adapt to the oceanic environment, the integument thickens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup> percentage of somatic lipids increases to supply energy for migrating and spawning, gonadosomatic index and oocyte diameter increase, gonadotropin hormone (GTH-II) production increases, and osmoregulatory physiology changes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eels are nocturnal and most of their feeding therefore occurs at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfman_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Helfman-26">[26]</a></sup> Having a keen sense of smell, eels most likely depend on scent to find food. The American eel is a generalist species which colonizes a wide range of habitats. Their diet is therefore extremely diverse and includes most of the aquatic animals sharing the same environment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leptocephalus">Leptocephalus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Leptocephalus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Little is known about the food habits of leptocephali. Recent studies on other eel species (Otake et al. 1993; Mochioka and Iwamizu 1996) suggest that leptocephali do not feed on zooplankton but rather consume detrital particles such as marine snow and fecal pellets or particles such as discarded houses of larvacean tunicates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_eel_and_elver">Glass eel and elver</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Glass eel and elver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based on laboratory experiments on European glass eels, Lecomte-Finiger (1983) reported that they were morphologically and physiologically unable to feed. However, Tesch (1977) found that elvers at a later stage of pigmentation, stage VIA4, were feeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup> Stomach examination of elvers caught during their upstream migration in the Petite Trinité River on the north shore of the Gulf of St. Lawrence revealed that elvers fed primarily on insect larvae.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yellow_eel">Yellow eel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Yellow eel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The yellow eel is essentially a nocturnal benthic omnivore. Prey includes fishes, molluscs, bivalves, crustaceans, insect larvae, surface-dwelling insects, worms, frogs and plants. The eel prefers small prey animals which can easily be attacked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup> Food type varies with body size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesch1977_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Tesch1977-10">[10]</a></sup> Stomachs of eels less than 40 cm and captured in streams contained mainly aquatic insect larvae, whereas larger eels fed predominantly on fishes and crayfishes. Insect abundance decreased in larger eels. The eel diet adapts to seasonal changes and the immediate environment. Feeding activity decreases or stops during the winter, and food intake ceases as eels physiologically prepare for the spawning migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wenner_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wenner-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predation">Predation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Predation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Little information about predation on eels has been published. It was reported that elvers and small yellow eels are prey of largemouth bass and striped bass, although they were not a major parts of these predators' diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Leptocephali, glass eels, elvers, and small yellow eels are likely to be eaten by various predatory fishes. Older eels are also known to eat incoming glass eels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> They also fall prey to other species of eels, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bald_eagles" title="Bald eagles">bald eagles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulls" title="Gulls">gulls</a>, as well as other fish-eating birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>The major outlet for US landings of yellow and silver eels is the EU market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fahay_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fahay-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1970s, the annual North Atlantic harvest averaged 125,418 kg, with an average value of $84,000. In 1977, the eel landings from Maine, New Hampshire, and Massachusetts were about 79,700, 2,700, and 143,300 kg, valued at $263,000, $5,000, and $170,000, respectively (US Department of Commerce 1984)</p>
<p>During the 1980s and early 1990s, the American eel was one of the top three species in commercial value to Ontario's fishing industry. At its peak, the eel harvest was valued at $600,000 and, in some years, eel accounted for almost half of the value of the entire commercial fish harvest from Lake Ontario. The commercial catch of American eel has declined from approximately 223,000 kilograms (kg) in the early 1980s to 11,000 kg in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>, the American eel is at very high risk of extinction in the wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Substantial decline in numbers and fishery landings of American eels over their range in eastern Canada and the US was noted, raising concerns over the status of this species. The number of juvenile eels in the Lake Ontario area decreased from 935,000 in 1985 to about 8,000 in 1993 and was approaching zero levels in 2001. Rapid declines were also recorded in Virginia, as well as in New Brunswick and Prince Edward Island in Canada.</p>
<p>Because of its complex life cycle, the species face a broad range of threats, some of which are specific to certain growth stage. Being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catadromous" title="Catadromous">catadromous</a>, the eels's reproductivity success depends heavily on free downstream passage for spawning migration. It also depends on the availability of diverse habitats for growth and maturation.</p>
<p>Sex ratio in the population can also be affected because males and females tend to utilize different habitats. Impacts on certain regions may greatly impact the number of either sex.</p>
<p>Despite being able to live in a wide range of temperatures and different levels of salinity, American eels are very sensitive to low dissolved oxygen level,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> which is typically found below dams. Contaminations of heavy metals, <a href="/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds" title="Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds">dioxins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlordane" title="Chlordane">chlordane</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyls" title="Polychlorinated biphenyls">polychlorinated biphenyls</a> as well as pollutants from <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source" title="Nonpoint source">nonpoint source</a> can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioaccumulate" title="Bioaccumulate">bioaccumulate</a> within the fat tissues of the eels, causing dangerous toxicity and reduced productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This problem is exacerbated due to the high fat content of eels.</p>
<p>Construction of dams and other irrigation facilities seriously decreases habitat availability and diversity for the eels. Dredging can affect migration, population distribution and prey availability. Overfishing or excessive harvesting of juveniles can also negatively impact local populations.</p>
<p>Other natural threats come from interspecific competition with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exotic_species" title="Exotic species">exotic species</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Flathead_catfish" title="Flathead catfish">flathead catfish</a> (<i>Pylodictis olivaris</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Blue_catfish" title="Blue catfish">blue catfish</a> (<i>Ictalurus furcatus)</i>, pathogens and parasites, and changes in oceanographic conditions that can alter currents-this potentially alter larval transport and migration of juveniles back to freshwater streams.</p>
<p>Management of the species had been virtually non-existent till very recently. Information on the species is still limited and much more efforts are needed for a longterm plan to monitor localized populations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_measures">Conservation measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Conservation measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service reviewed the status of the American eel both in 2007 and in 2015, finding both times that Endangered Species Act protection for the American eel is not warranted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Canadian province of Ontario has cancelled the commercial fishing quota since 2004. Eel sport fishery has been closed. Efforts have been made to improve the passage in which eels migrate across the hydroelectric dams on St. Lawrence River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_consumption">Sustainable consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_eel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sustainable consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Greenpeace International has added the American eel to its seafood red list. "The Greenpeace International seafood red list is a list of fish that are commonly sold in supermarkets around the world, and which have a very high risk of being sourced from unsustainable fisheries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
