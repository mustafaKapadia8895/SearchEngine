<p><b>Empirical evidence</b>, also known as <b>sensory experience</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> received by means of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senses" title="Senses">senses</a>, particularly by <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimentation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> word for experience, ἐμπειρία (<i>empeiría</i>).</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>, in philosophy, it is common to call the knowledge gained <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori"><i>a posteriori</i> knowledge</a></i> (in contrast to <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> knowledge).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning">Meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empirical_evidence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Empirical evidence is information that verifies the truth (that which accurately corresponds to reality) or falsity (inaccuracy) of a claim. "In the <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricist</a> view, one can claim to have knowledge only when based on empirical evidence", would not be a truthful statement about empiricist who believe that testable verifiable information is not the only way of gaining knowledge. This stands in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalist</a> view under which <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a> or reflection alone is considered evidence for the truth or falsity of some <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">propositions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdp-2">[2]</a></sup> Empirical evidence is information acquired by observation or experimentation. This data is recorded and analyzed by scientists. This is the primary source of empirical evidence. Secondary sources describe, discuss, interpret, comment upon, analyze, evaluate, summarize, and process primary sources. Secondary source materials can be articles in newspapers or popular magazines, book or movie reviews, or articles found in scholarly journals that discuss or evaluate someone else's original research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdp_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdp-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Empirical evidence may be synonymous with the outcome of an experiment. In this regard, an empirical result is a unified confirmation. In this context, the term <i>semi-empirical</i> is used for qualifying theoretical methods that use, in part, basic <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axioms</a> or postulated scientific laws and experimental results. Such methods are opposed to theoretical <i><a href="/wiki/Ab_initio" title="Ab initio">ab initio</a></i> methods, which are purely <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">deductive</a> and based on <a href="/wiki/First_principle" title="First principle">first principles</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, empirical evidence is required for a hypothesis to gain acceptance in the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_community" title="Scientific community">scientific community</a>. Normally, this validation is achieved by the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> of forming a <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design_of_experiments" title="Design of experiments">experimental design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_Method#Reproducibility" title="Scientific Method">reproduction of results</a>, conference presentation, and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">journal publication</a>. This requires rigorous communication of hypothesis (usually expressed in mathematics), experimental constraints and controls (expressed necessarily in terms of standard experimental apparatus), and a common understanding of measurement.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Statement_(logic)" title="Statement (logic)">Statements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argument" title="Argument">arguments</a> depending on empirical evidence are often referred to as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_posteriori" title="A posteriori">a posteriori</a></i> ("following experience") as distinguished from <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> (preceding it). <i>A priori</i> knowledge or justification is independent of experience (for example "All bachelors are unmarried"), whereas <i>a posteriori</i> knowledge or justification is dependent on experience or empirical evidence (for example "Some bachelors are very happy"). The notion that the distinction between <i>a posteriori</i> and <i>a priori</i> is tantamount to the distinction between empirical and non-empirical knowledge comes from <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Critique_of_Pure_Reason" title="Critique of Pure Reason">Critique of Pure Reason</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Craig2005_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Craig2005-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The standard <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">positivist</a> view of empirically acquired information has been that observation, experience, and experiment serve as neutral arbiters between competing theories. However, since the 1960s, a persistent critique most associated with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kuhn" title="Thomas Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2014)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> has argued that these methods are influenced by prior beliefs and experiences. Consequently, it cannot be expected that two scientists when observing, experiencing, or experimenting on the same event will make the same theory-neutral observations. The role of observation as a theory-neutral arbiter may not be possible. Theory-dependence of observation means that, even if there were agreed methods of inference and interpretation, scientists may still disagree on the nature of empirical data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
