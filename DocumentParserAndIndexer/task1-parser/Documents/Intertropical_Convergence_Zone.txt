<p>The <b>Intertropical Convergence Zone</b> (<b>ITCZ</b>), known by sailors as the <b><a href="/wiki/Doldrums" title="Doldrums">doldrums</a></b>, is the area encircling Earth near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>, where the northeast and southeast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a> converge.</p>
<p>The ITCZ was originally identified from the 1920s to the 1940s as the "Intertropical Front" ("ITF"), but after the recognition in the 1940s and 1950s of the significance of <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">wind field convergence</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> weather production, the term <i>ITCZ</i> was then applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When it lies near the Equator, it is called the <b>near-equatorial trough</b>. Where the ITCZ is drawn into and merges with a <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal</a> circulation, it is sometimes referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, a usage more common in Australia and parts of Asia. In the seamen's speech, the zone is referred to as the doldrums because of its erratic weather patterns with stagnant calms and violent <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>.</p>
<p>The ITCZ appears as a band of clouds, usually thunderstorms, that encircle the globe near the Equator. In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> move in a southwestward direction from the northeast, while in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, they move northwestward from the southeast. When the ITCZ is positioned north or south of the Equator, these directions change according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> imparted by <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">Earth's rotation</a>. For instance, when the ITCZ is situated north of the Equator, the southeast trade wind changes to a southwest wind as it crosses the Equator. The ITCZ is formed by vertical motion largely appearing as <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> activity of thunderstorms driven by solar heating, which effectively draw air in; these are the trade winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-2">[2]</a></sup> The ITCZ is effectively a tracer of the ascending branch of the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> and is wet. The dry descending branch is the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a>.</p>
<p>The location of the ITCZ gradually varies with the seasons. Over land, it moves back and forth across the Equator, following the Sun's <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">zenith point</a> at high <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">noon</a>. Over the oceans, where the convergence zone is better defined, the seasonal cycle is more subtle, as the convection is constrained by the distribution of ocean temperatures. Sometimes, a double ITCZ forms, with one located north and another south of the Equator, one of which is usually stronger than the other. When this occurs, a narrow ridge of high pressure forms between the two convergence zones.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Pacific_convergence_zone">South Pacific convergence zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intertropical_Convergence_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: South Pacific convergence zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_convergence_zone" title="South Pacific convergence zone">South Pacific convergence zone</a> (SPCZ) is a reverse-oriented, or west-northwest to east-southeast aligned, trough extending from the west Pacific warm pool southeastwards towards <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a>. It lies just south of the equator during the Southern Hemisphere warm season, but can be more extratropical in nature, especially east of the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a>. It is considered the largest and most important piece of the ITCZ, and has the least dependence upon heating from a nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_mass" title="Land mass">land mass</a> during the summer than any other portion of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The southern ITCZ in the southeast Pacific and southern Atlantic, known as the SITCZ, occurs during the Southern Hemisphere fall between <a href="/wiki/3rd_parallel_south" title="3rd parallel south">3°</a> and <a href="/wiki/10th_parallel_south" title="10th parallel south">10° south</a> of the equator east of the <a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140th meridian west</a> longitude during cool or neutral <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) patterns. When ENSO reaches its warm phase, otherwise known as El Niño, the tongue of lowered <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> due to upwelling off the South American continent disappears, which causes this convergence zone to vanish as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_weather">Effects on weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intertropical_Convergence_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects on weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ITCZ_january-july.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1253" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/ITCZ_january-july.png/220px-ITCZ_january-july.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/ITCZ_january-july.png/330px-ITCZ_january-july.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/ITCZ_january-july.png/440px-ITCZ_january-july.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ITCZ_january-july.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ITCZ moves farther away from the equator during the Northern summer than the Southern one due to the North-heavy arrangement of the continents.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Variation in the location of the intertropical convergence zone drastically affects rainfall in many equatorial nations, resulting in the wet and dry seasons of the tropics rather than the cold and warm seasons of higher latitudes. Longer term changes in the intertropical convergence zone can result in severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> or flooding in nearby areas.</p>
<p>In some cases, the ITCZ may become narrow, especially when it moves away from the equator; the ITCZ can then be interpreted as a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">front</a> along the leading edge of the equatorial air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> There appears to be a 15- to 25-day cycle in thunderstorm activity along the ITCZ, which is roughly half the wavelength of the <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden–Julian oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</a> (MJO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the ITCZ the average winds are slight, unlike the zones north and south of the equator where the trade winds feed. Early sailors named this belt of calm <i>the doldrums</i> because of the inactivity and stagnation they found themselves in after days of no wind. To find oneself becalmed in this region in a hot and muggy climate could mean death in an era when wind was the only effective way to propel ships across the ocean. Even today leisure and competitive sailors attempt to cross the zone as quickly as possible as the erratic weather and wind patterns may cause unexpected delays.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_tropical_cyclone_formation">Role in tropical cyclone formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intertropical_Convergence_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role in tropical cyclone formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="2784" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg/220px-ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg/330px-ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg/440px-ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ITCZ_jun_25_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical cyclones over the West Atlantic, Central America and East Pacific (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">Hurricane</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a> depends upon low-level <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> as one of its six requirements, and the ITCZ fills this role as it is a zone of wind change and speed, otherwise known as horizontal <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>. As the ITCZ migrates more than 500 kilometres (300 mi) from the equator during the respective hemisphere's summer season, increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis force</a> makes the formation of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> within this zone more possible. Surges of higher pressure from high latitudes can enhance tropical disturbances along its axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the north Atlantic and the northeastern Pacific oceans, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> move along the axis of the ITCZ causing an increase in thunderstorm activity, and under weak vertical wind shear, these clusters of thunderstorms can become tropical cyclones.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intertropical_Convergence_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thunderstorms along the Intertropical Convergence Zone played a role in the loss of <a href="/wiki/Air_France_Flight_447" title="Air France Flight 447">Air France Flight 447</a>, which left <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro%E2%80%93Gale%C3%A3o_International_Airport" title="Rio de Janeiro–Galeão International Airport">Rio de Janeiro–Galeão International Airport</a> on Sunday, May 31, 2009, at about 7:00 p.m. local time (6:00 p.m. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">EDT</a> or 10:00 p.m. <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>) and had been expected to land at <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" title="Charles de Gaulle Airport">Charles de Gaulle Airport</a> near <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> on Monday, June 1, 2009, at 11:15 a.m. (5:15 a.m. EDT or 9:15 a.m. UTC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The aircraft crashed with no survivors while flying through a series of large ITCZ thunderstorms, and ice forming rapidly on airspeed sensors was the precipitating cause for a cascade of human errors which ultimately doomed the flight. Most aircraft flying these routes are able to avoid the larger <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convective</a> cells without incident. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
