<p>The <b>blue whiting</b>, <i>Micromesistius poutassou</i>, one of the two species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Micromesistius" title="Micromesistius">Micromesistius</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Gadidae" title="Gadidae">cod family</a>, is common in the northeast Atlantic Ocean from Morocco to <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a>. It also occurs in the northern parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, where it may be locally abundant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailey_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bailey-1">[1]</a></sup> Blue whiting also occur in the northwest Atlantic Ocean between Canada and Greenland, but is considered rare. It has a long, narrow body and a silvery underbody. The fish can attain a length of more than 40 cm. The average length of blue whiting caught off the west shores of the UK is 31 cm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A related species, <a href="/wiki/Southern_blue_whiting" title="Southern blue whiting">southern blue whiting</a>, <i>Micromesistius australis</i>, occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_whiting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Exploitation of blue whiting only started in the 1970s. The species, in the last decades, has become increasingly important to the fishing industries of northern European countries, including Russia. Catches exceeded 1 million tonnes from 1998 to 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acom_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-acom-3">[3]</a></sup> According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations</a>, blue whiting was fifth most important capture fish species in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sofia_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sofia-4">[4]</a></sup> However, recruitment of the stock fell to a low level in 2006 and has been weak ever since, causing declining spawning stock and eventually triggering strong reductions in catch quotas. The reasons for low recruitment in recent years are poorly known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acom_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-acom-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The total quota for 2011 was set to 40,100 tonnes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quota_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-quota-6">[6]</a></sup> which is less than 2% of the record catch of 2.4 million tonnes in 2004. Catches in 2011 exceeded the quota by more than 100%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>For 2012, <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_the_Exploration_of_the_Sea" title="International Council for the Exploration of the Sea">ICES</a> advises the catches should be no more than 391,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-advice2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-advice2012-8">[8]</a></sup> This large increase relative to the quota in 2011 (but not to the catches in 1998-2008) is caused by a revision in the stock assessment; however, recruitment to the stock is still low and the stock is forecasted to decline. The coastal states set the total quota for 2012 to 391,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fish is usually not marketed fresh, but processed into fish meal and oil. However, in Russia and in southern Europe, blue whiting are sometimes sold as food fish.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_whiting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Faroe_stamp_423_blue_whiting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="473" data-file-width="347" height="354" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Faroe_stamp_423_blue_whiting.jpg/260px-Faroe_stamp_423_blue_whiting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Faroe_stamp_423_blue_whiting.jpg 1.5x" width="260"/></a>

Blue whiting on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faroese_islands" title="Faroese islands">Faroese</a> <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">stamp</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Blue whiting in the northeast Atlantic is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straddling_stock" title="Straddling stock">straddling stock</a>: it occupies the <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> (EEZ) of the Faroe Islands, the European Union, Iceland and Norway, as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_seas" title="High seas">high seas</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This means effective regulation calls for international co-operation.</p>
<p>Quota advice for blue whiting in the northeast Atlantic is provided by <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_the_Exploration_of_the_Sea" title="International Council for the Exploration of the Sea">ICES</a>. For a long period, blue whiting fisheries were mainly regulated through nationally set quotas because there was no international agreement about sharing the total quota.;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Standal2006_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Standal2006-11">[11]</a></sup> consequently, the total catch greatly exceeded the advised quotas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acom_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-acom-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the Coastal States (the Faroe Islands, the European Union, Iceland and Norway) reached an agreement in December 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CS_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CS-12">[12]</a></sup> ending the period of what was sometimes referred to as "Olympic fishing". Since 2006, the blue whiting fishery has been regulated under this agreement, which gives the greatest share to the European Union, but through quota swaps, Norway has been holding the largest annual quotas.</p>
