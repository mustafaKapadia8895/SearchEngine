<p>The <b>positron</b> or <b>antielectron</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> counterpart of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. The positron has an <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> of +1 <i><a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">e</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> of 1/2 (same as electron), and has the same <a href="/wiki/Electron_rest_mass" title="Electron rest mass">mass as an electron</a>. When a positron collides with an electron, <a href="/wiki/Annihilation" title="Annihilation">annihilation</a> occurs. If this collision occurs at low energies, it results in the production of two or more <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Electron%E2%80%93positron_annihilation" title="Electron–positron annihilation">electron–positron annihilation</a>).</p>
<p>Positrons may be generated by <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">positron emission</a> radioactive decay (through <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interactions</a>), or by <a href="/wiki/Pair_production" title="Pair production">pair production</a> from a sufficiently energetic <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> which is interacting with an atom in a material.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1928, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a> published a paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuantumElectron_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-QuantumElectron-2">[2]</a></sup> proposing that electrons can have both a positive and negative charge. This paper introduced the <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a>, a unification of quantum mechanics, <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>, and the then-new concept of electron <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> to explain the <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">Zeeman effect</a>. The paper did not explicitly predict a new particle but did allow for electrons having either positive or negative energy as solutions. Hermann Weyl then published a paper discussing the mathematical implications of the negative energy solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The positive-energy solution explained experimental results, but Dirac was puzzled by the equally valid negative-energy solution that the mathematical model allowed. Quantum mechanics did not allow the negative energy solution to simply be ignored, as classical mechanics often did in such equations; the dual solution implied the possibility of an electron spontaneously jumping between positive and negative energy states. However, no such transition had yet been observed experimentally. He referred to the issues raised by this conflict between theory and observation as "difficulties" that were "unresolved".</p>
<p>Dirac wrote a follow-up paper in December 1929<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElectronProton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ElectronProton-4">[4]</a></sup> that attempted to explain the unavoidable negative-energy solution for the relativistic electron. He argued that "... an electron with negative energy moves in an external [electromagnetic] field as though it carries a positive charge." He further asserted that all of space could be regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Dirac_sea" title="Dirac sea">"sea" of negative energy states</a> that were filled, so as to prevent electrons jumping between positive energy states (negative electric charge) and negative energy states (positive charge). The paper also explored the possibility of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> being an island in this sea, and that it might actually be a negative-energy electron. Dirac acknowledged that the proton having a much greater mass than the electron was a problem, but expressed "hope" that a future theory would resolve the issue.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Oppenheimer" title="Robert Oppenheimer">Robert Oppenheimer</a> argued strongly against the proton being the negative-energy electron solution to Dirac's equation. He asserted that if it were, the hydrogen atom would rapidly self-destruct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Persuaded by Oppenheimer's argument, Dirac published a paper in 1931 that predicted the existence of an as-yet-unobserved particle that he called an "anti-electron" that would have the same mass as an electron and that would mutually annihilate upon contact with an electron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feynman" title="Feynman">Feynman</a>, and earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernst_Carl_Gerlach_Stueckelberg" title="Ernst Carl Gerlach Stueckelberg">Stueckelberg</a>, proposed an interpretation of the positron as an electron moving backward in time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> reinterpreting the negative-energy solutions of the <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a>. Electrons moving backward in time would have a positive <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Archibald_Wheeler" title="John Archibald Wheeler">Wheeler</a> invoked this concept to explain the identical properties shared by all electrons, suggesting that <a href="/wiki/One-electron_universe" title="One-electron universe">"they are all the same electron"</a> with a complex, self-intersecting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldline" title="Worldline">worldline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yoichiro_Nambu" title="Yoichiro Nambu">Yoichiro Nambu</a> later applied it to all production and <a href="/wiki/Annihilation" title="Annihilation">annihilation</a> of particle-antiparticle pairs, stating that "the eventual creation and annihilation of pairs that may occur now and then is no creation or annihilation, but only a change of direction of moving particles, from the past to the future, or from the future to the past."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The backwards in time point of view is nowadays accepted as completely equivalent to other pictures, but it does not have anything to do with the macroscopic terms "cause" and "effect", which do not appear in a microscopic physical description.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Skobeltsyn" title="Dmitri Skobeltsyn">Dmitri Skobeltsyn</a> first observed the positron in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> While using a Wilson <a href="/wiki/Cloud_chamber" title="Cloud chamber">cloud chamber</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> to try to detect <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma radiation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>, Skobeltsyn detected particles that acted like electrons but curved in the opposite direction in an applied magnetic field.</p>
<p>Likewise, in 1929 <a href="/wiki/Chung-Yao_Chao" title="Chung-Yao Chao">Chung-Yao Chao</a>, a graduate student at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltech" title="Caltech">Caltech</a>, noticed some anomalous results that indicated particles behaving like electrons, but with a positive charge, though the results were inconclusive and the phenomenon was not pursued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MehraRechenberg_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MehraRechenberg-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carl_David_Anderson" title="Carl David Anderson">Carl David Anderson</a> discovered the positron on August 2, 1932,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> for which he won the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobel_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobel-14">[14]</a></sup> Anderson did not coin the term <i>positron</i>, but allowed it at the suggestion of the <a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a> journal editor to which he submitted his discovery paper in late 1932. The positron was the first evidence of <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> and was discovered when Anderson allowed cosmic rays to pass through a cloud chamber and a lead plate. A magnet surrounded this apparatus, causing particles to bend in different directions based on their electric charge. The ion trail left by each positron appeared on the photographic plate with a curvature matching the <a href="/wiki/Mass-to-charge_ratio" title="Mass-to-charge ratio">mass-to-charge ratio</a> of an electron, but in a direction that showed its charge was positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penny_Gilmer_6-19-11_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Penny_Gilmer_6-19-11-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson wrote in retrospect that the positron could have been discovered earlier based on Chung-Yao Chao's work, if only it had been followed up on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MehraRechenberg_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-MehraRechenberg-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Joliot-Curie" title="Frédéric Joliot-Curie">Frédéric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ir%C3%A8ne_Joliot-Curie" title="Irène Joliot-Curie">Irène Joliot-Curie</a> in Paris had evidence of positrons in old photographs when Anderson's results came out, but they had dismissed them as protons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penny_Gilmer_6-19-11_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Penny_Gilmer_6-19-11-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The positron had also been contemporaneously discovered by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Blackett" title="Patrick Blackett">Patrick Blackett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Occhialini" title="Giuseppe Occhialini">Giuseppe Occhialini</a> at the Cavendish Laboratory in 1932. Blackett and Occhialini had delayed publication to obtain more solid evidence, so Anderson was able to publish the discovery first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AM_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AM-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_production">Natural production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Natural production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">Positron emission</a></div>
<p>Positrons are produced naturally in β<sup>+</sup> decays of naturally occurring radioactive isotopes (for example, <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">potassium-40</a>) and in interactions of gamma quanta (emitted by radioactive nuclei) with matter. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antineutrino" title="Antineutrino">Antineutrinos</a> are another kind of antiparticle produced by natural radioactivity (β<sup>−</sup> decay). Many different kinds of antiparticles are also produced by (and contained in) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. Recent (as of January 2011) research by the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">American Astronomical Society</a> has discovered antimatter (positrons) originating above <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> clouds; positrons are produced in gamma-ray flashes created by electrons accelerated by strong electric fields in the clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Antiprotons have also been found to exist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_Belt" title="Van Allen Belt">Van Allen Belts</a> around the Earth by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_for_Antimatter_Matter_Exploration_and_Light-nuclei_Astrophysics#Results" title="Payload for Antimatter Matter Exploration and Light-nuclei Astrophysics">PAMELA module</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antiparticles, of which the most common are positrons due to their low mass, are also produced in any environment with a sufficiently high temperature (mean particle energy greater than the <a href="/wiki/Pair_production" title="Pair production">pair production</a> threshold). During the period of baryogenesis, when the universe was extremely hot and dense, matter and antimatter were continually produced and annihilated. The presence of remaining matter, and absence of detectable remaining antimatter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> also called <a href="/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry" title="Baryon asymmetry">baryon asymmetry</a>, is attributed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CP-violation" title="CP-violation">CP-violation</a>: a violation of the CP-symmetry relating matter to antimatter. The exact mechanism of this violation during baryogenesis remains a mystery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Positron production from radioactive <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>β<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span> decay</a> can be considered both artificial and natural production, as the generation of the radioisotope can be natural or artificial. Perhaps the best known naturally-occurring radioisotope which produces positrons is <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">potassium-40</a>, a long-lived isotope of potassium which occurs as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_isotope" title="Primordial isotope">primordial isotope</a> of potassium. Even though a small percent of potassium (0.0117%) it is the single most abundant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotope</a> in the human body. In a human body of 70 kg mass, about 4,400 nuclei of <sup>40</sup>K decay per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The activity of natural potassium is 31 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">Bq</a>/g.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> About 0.001% of these <sup>40</sup>K decays produce about 4000 natural positrons per day in the human body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engelkemeir_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Engelkemeir-23">[23]</a></sup> These positrons soon find an electron, undergo annihilation, and produce pairs of 511 keV gamma rays, in a process similar (but much lower intensity) to that which happens during a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PET_scan" title="PET scan">PET scan</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> procedure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Recent observations indicate black holes and neutron stars produce vast amounts of positron-electron plasma via the jets. Large clouds of positron-electron plasma have also been associated with neutron stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_in_cosmic_rays">Observation in cosmic rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Observation in cosmic rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">Cosmic ray</a></div>
<p>Satellite experiments have found evidence of positrons (as well as a few antiprotons) in primary cosmic rays, amounting to less than 1% of the particles in primary cosmic rays. These do not appear to be the products of large amounts of antimatter from the Big Bang, or indeed complex antimatter in the universe (evidence for which is lacking, see below). Rather, the antimatter in cosmic rays appear to consist of only these two elementary particles, probably made in energetic processes long after the Big Bang.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Preliminary results from the presently operating <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Magnetic_Spectrometer" title="Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer">Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer</a> (<i>AMS-02</i>) on board the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> show that positrons in the cosmic rays arrive with no directionality, and with energies that range from 10 <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gigaelectron_volt" title="wiktionary:gigaelectron volt">GeV</a> to 250 GeV. In September, 2014, new results with almost twice as much data were presented in a talk at CERN and published in Physical Review Letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A new measurement of positron fraction up to 500 GeV was reported, showing that positron fraction peaks at a maximum of about 16% of total electron+positron events, around an energy of 275 ± 32 GeV. At higher energies, up to 500 GeV, the ratio of positrons to electrons begins to fall again. The absolute flux of positrons also begins to fall before 500 GeV, but peaks at energies far higher than electron energies, which peak about 10 GeV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> These results on interpretation have been suggested to be due to positron production in annihilation events of massive <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physrevltrs413_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-physrevltrs413-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Positrons, like anti-protons, do not appear to originate from any hypothetical "antimatter" regions of the universe. On the contrary, there is no evidence of complex antimatter atomic nuclei, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihelium" title="Antihelium">antihelium</a> nuclei (i.e., anti-alpha particles), in cosmic rays. These are actively being searched for. A prototype of the <i>AMS-02</i> designated <i>AMS-01</i>, was flown into space aboard the <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery"><i>Discovery</i></a> on <a href="/wiki/STS-91" title="STS-91">STS-91</a> in June 1998. By not detecting any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihelium#Antihelium" title="Antihelium">antihelium</a> at all, the <i>AMS-01</i> established an upper limit of 1.1×10<sup>−6</sup> for the antihelium to helium <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_production">Artificial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Artificial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Physicists at the <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory</a> in California have used a short, ultra-intense laser to irradiate a millimeter-thick gold target and produce more than 100 billion positrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Presently significant lab production of 5 MeV positron-electron beams allows investigation of multiple characteristics such as how different elements react to 5 MeV positron interactions or impacts, how energy is transferred to particles, and the shock effect of GRBs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Certain kinds of <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> experiments involve colliding positrons and electrons at relativistic speeds. The high impact energy and the mutual annihilation of these matter/antimatter opposites create a fountain of diverse subatomic particles. Physicists study the results of these collisions to test theoretical predictions and to search for new kinds of particles.</p>
<p>Gamma rays, emitted indirectly by a positron-emitting radionuclide (tracer), are detected in <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> (PET) scanners used in hospitals. PET scanners create detailed three-dimensional images of metabolic activity within the human body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>An experimental tool called <a href="/wiki/Positron_annihilation_spectroscopy" title="Positron annihilation spectroscopy">positron annihilation spectroscopy</a> (PAS) is used in materials research to detect variations in density, defects, displacements, or even voids, within a solid material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
