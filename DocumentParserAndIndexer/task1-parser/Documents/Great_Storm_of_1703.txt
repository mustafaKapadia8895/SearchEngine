<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Great Storm of 1703</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Great Storm of 1703</b> was a destructive extratropical cyclone that struck central and southern England on 26 November 1703 (7 December 1703 in the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> in use today). High winds caused 2,000 chimney stacks to collapse in London and damaged the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a>, which lost 4,000 oaks. Ships were blown hundreds of miles off-course, and over 1,000 seamen died on the <a href="/wiki/Goodwin_Sands" title="Goodwin Sands">Goodwin Sands</a> alone. News bulletins of casualties and damage were sold all over England – a novelty at that time. The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> declared that the storm was God’s vengeance for the sins of the nation. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> thought it was a divine punishment for poor performance against Catholic armies in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Severity">Severity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Storm_of_1703&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Severity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Contemporary observers recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric" title="Barometric">barometric</a> readings as low as 973 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> (measured by <a href="/wiki/William_Derham" title="William Derham">William Derham</a> in south <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but it has been suggested that the storm deepened to 950 millibars over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Midlands" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Retrospective analysis conjectures that the storm was consistent with a Hurricane <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Category 2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage">Damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Storm_of_1703&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In London, approximately 2,000 massive chimney stacks were blown down. The lead roofing was blown off <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> had to shelter in a cellar at <a href="/wiki/St_James%27s_Palace" title="St James's Palace">St James's Palace</a> to avoid collapsing chimneys and part of the roof. On the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">Thames</a>, some 700 ships were heaped together in the <a href="/wiki/Pool_of_London" title="Pool of London">Pool of London</a>, the section downstream from <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a>. <a href="/wiki/HMS_Vanguard_(1678)" title="HMS Vanguard (1678)">HMS <i>Vanguard</i></a> was wrecked at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chatham,_Medway" title="Chatham, Medway">Chatham</a>. Admiral Sir <a href="/wiki/Cloudesley_Shovell" title="Cloudesley Shovell">Cloudesley Shovell</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Association" title="HMS Association">HMS <i>Association</i></a> was blown from <a href="/wiki/Harwich" title="Harwich">Harwich</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> before way could be made back to England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle" title="Pinnacle">Pinnacles</a> were blown from the top of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_Chapel,_Cambridge" title="King's College Chapel, Cambridge">King’s College Chapel, in Cambridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rennell_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rennell-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was extensive and prolonged flooding in the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a>, particularly around <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>. Hundreds of people drowned in flooding on the <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Levels" title="Somerset Levels">Somerset Levels</a>, along with thousands of sheep and cattle, and one ship was found 15 miles (24 km) inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Wells,_Somerset" title="Wells, Somerset">Wells</a>, Bishop <a href="/wiki/Richard_Kidder" title="Richard Kidder">Richard Kidder</a> and his wife were killed when two chimneystacks in the palace fell on them, asleep in bed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rennell_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-rennell-5">[5]</a></sup> This same storm blew in part of the great west window in <a href="/wiki/Wells_Cathedral" title="Wells Cathedral">Wells Cathedral</a>. Major damage occurred to the southwest tower of <a href="/wiki/Llandaff_Cathedral" title="Llandaff Cathedral">Llandaff Cathedral</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.</p>
<p>At sea, many ships were wrecked, some of which were returning from helping <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Archduke Charles</a>, the claimed King of Spain, fight the French in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>. These ships included <a href="/wiki/HMS_Stirling_Castle_(1679)" title="HMS Stirling Castle (1679)">HMS <i>Stirling Castle</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Northumberland_(1679)" title="HMS Northumberland (1679)">HMS <i>Northumberland</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Mary_(1650)" title="HMS Mary (1650)">HMS <i>Mary</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Restoration_(1678)" title="HMS Restoration (1678)">HMS <i>Restoration</i></a>, with about 1,500 seamen killed particularly on the <a href="/wiki/Goodwin_Sands#Great_Storm_of_1703" title="Goodwin Sands">Goodwin Sands</a>. Between 8,000 and 15,000 lives were lost overall.</p>

<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a> off <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> was destroyed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rennell_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-rennell-5">[5]</a></sup> on 27 November 1703 (Old Style), killing six occupants, including its builder <a href="/wiki/Henry_Winstanley" title="Henry Winstanley">Henry Winstanley</a>. (<a href="/wiki/John_Rudyard" title="John Rudyard">John Rudyard</a> was later contracted to build the second lighthouse on the site.) A ship torn from its moorings in the <a href="/wiki/Helford" title="Helford">Helford</a> River in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> was blown for 200 miles (320 km) before grounding eight hours later on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rennell_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-rennell-5">[5]</a></sup> The number of <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees lost in the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a> alone was 4,000.</p>
<p>The storm of 1703 caught a convoy of 130 merchant ships sheltering at <a href="/wiki/Milford_Haven" title="Milford Haven">Milford Haven</a>, along with their man of war escorts the <i>Dolphin</i>, the <i>Cumberland</i>, the <i>Coventry</i>, the <i>Looe</i>, the <i>Hastings</i> and the <i>Hector</i>. By 3:00pm the next afternoon, losses included 30 vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Storm_of_1703&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The storm was unprecedented in ferocity and duration and was generally reckoned by witnesses to represent the anger of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>, in recognition of the "crying sins of this nation". The government declared 19 January 1704 a day of <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a>, saying that it "loudly calls for the deepest and most solemn humiliation of our people". It remained a frequent topic of moralising in sermons well into the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary">Literary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Storm_of_1703&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Literary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Great Storm also coincided with the increase in English <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>, and was the first weather event to be a news story on a national scale. Special issue broadsheets were produced detailing damage to property and stories of people who had been killed.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> produced his full-length book <a href="/wiki/The_Storm_(Daniel_Defoe)" title="The Storm (Daniel Defoe)"><i>The Storm</i></a> (July 1704) in response to the calamity, calling it "the tempest that destroyed woods and forests all over England". He wrote: "No pen could describe it, nor tongue express it, nor thought conceive it unless by one in the extremity of it." Coastal towns such as <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> "looked as if the enemy had sackt them and were most miserably torn to pieces." Winds of up to 80 miles per hour (130 km/h) destroyed more than 400 windmills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-9">[9]</a></sup> Defoe reported that the sails in some turned so fast that the friction caused the wooden wheels to overheat and catch fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He thought that the destruction of the sovereign fleet was a punishment for their poor performance against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> armies of France and Spain during the first year of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKay_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKay-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_losses">Naval losses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Storm_of_1703&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Naval losses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, fierce winds and high seas swamped some vessels outright and drove others onto the <a href="/wiki/Goodwin_Sands" title="Goodwin Sands">Goodwin Sands</a>, an extensive sand bank off the southeast coast of England and the traditional anchorage for ships waiting either for passage up the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Estuary" title="Thames Estuary">Thames Estuary</a> to London or for favourable winds to take them out into the Channel and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahs_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mahs-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> was badly affected, losing thirteen ships including the entire Channel Squadron,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahs_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-mahs-12">[12]</a></sup> and upwards of 1,500 seamen drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheeler_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wheeler-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Third-rate" title="Third-rate">third-rate</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Restoration_(1678)" title="HMS Restoration (1678)">HMS <i>Restoration</i></a> was wrecked on the Goodwin Sands; of the ship's company of 387 not one was saved.</li>
<li>The third-rate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Northumberland_(1679)" title="HMS Northumberland (1679)">HMS <i>Northumberland</i></a> was lost on the Goodwin Sands; all 220 men, including 24 marines were killed.</li>
<li>The third-rate (battleship)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahs_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-mahs-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Stirling_Castle_(1679)" title="HMS Stirling Castle (1679)">HMS <i>Stirling Castle</i></a> was wrecked on the Goodwin Sands. Seventy men, including four marine officers, were saved, but 206 men were drowned.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fourth-rate" title="Fourth-rate">fourth-rate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Mary_(1650)" title="HMS Mary (1650)">HMS <i>Mary</i></a> was wrecked on the Goodwin Sands. The captain and the purser were ashore, but Rear Admiral Beaumont and 268 other men were drowned. Only one man, Thomas Atkins, was saved. His escape was remarkable – having first seen the rear admiral get onto a piece of her quarter-deck when the ship was breaking up, and then get washed off again, Atkins was tossed by a wave into the <i>Stirling Castle</i>, which sank soon after. From the <i>Stirling Castle</i> he was swept into a boat by a wave, and was rescued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fifth-rate" title="Fifth-rate">fifth-rate</a> <i>Mortar-bomb</i> was wrecked on the Goodwin Sands and her entire company of 65 lost.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sixth-rate" title="Sixth-rate">sixth-rate</a> advice boat <i>Eagle</i> was lost on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a>, but her ship's company of 45 were all saved.</li>
<li>The third-rate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Resolution_(1667)" title="HMS Resolution (1667)"><i>Resolution</i></a> was lost at <a href="/wiki/Pevensey" title="Pevensey">Pevensey</a> on the coast of Sussex; all her ship's company of 221 were saved.</li>
<li>The fifth-rate <i>Litchfield Prize</i> was wrecked on the coast of Sussex; all 108 on board were saved.</li>
<li>The fourth-rate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Newcastle_(1653)" title="HMS Newcastle (1653)"><i>Newcastle</i></a> was lost at <a href="/wiki/Spithead" title="Spithead">Spithead</a>. The carpenter and 39 men were saved, and the other 193 were drowned.</li>
<li>The fifth-rate fire-ship <i>Vesuvius</i> was lost at Spithead; all 48 of her ship's company were saved.</li>
<li>The fourth-rate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Reserve_(1701)" title="HMS Reserve (1701)"><i>Reserve</i></a> was lost by foundering off <a href="/wiki/Great_Yarmouth" title="Great Yarmouth">Yarmouth</a>. The captain, the surgeon, the clerk and 44 men were saved; the other 175 members of the crew were drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lavery.2C_SoLv1_p167_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lavery.2C_SoLv1_p167-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Second-rate" title="Second-rate">second-rate</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Vanguard_(1678)" title="HMS Vanguard (1678)"><i>Vanguard</i></a> was sunk in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chatham_harbour" title="Chatham harbour">Chatham harbour</a>. She was not manned and had no armament fitted; the following year she was raised for rebuilding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fourth-rate <a href="/wiki/HMS_York_(1654)" title="HMS York (1654)"><i>York</i></a> was lost at <a href="/wiki/Harwich" title="Harwich">Harwich</a>; all but four of her men were saved.</li>
</ul>
<p>Lamb (1991) claimed 10,000 seamen were lost in one night, a far higher figure, about one third of the seamen in the Royal Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lamb_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lamb-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Shrewsbury_(1695)" title="HMS Shrewsbury (1695)"><i>Shrewsbury</i></a> narrowly escaped a similar fate. More than 40 merchant ships were also lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jerrold_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jerrold-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
