<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Large igneous province</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

Only a few of the largest large igneous provinces appear (coloured dark purple) on this geological map, which depicts crustal geologic provinces as seen in <a href="/wiki/Seismic_refraction" title="Seismic refraction">seismic refraction</a> data.</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, a <b>large igneous province</b> (LIP) is an extremely large accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rocks</a>, including plutonic rocks (<a href="/wiki/Intrusive_rock" title="Intrusive rock">intrusive</a>) or volcanic rock formations (<a href="/wiki/Extrusive_rock" title="Extrusive rock">extrusive</a>), arising when hot magma extrudes from inside the Earth and flows out. The source of many or all LIPs is variously attributed to <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plumes</a> or to processes associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_tectonic" title="Plate tectonic">plate tectonics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foulger_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foulger-1">[1]</a></sup> Types of LIPs can include <i>large volcanic provinces</i> (LVP), created through <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalt</a>, and <i>large plutonic provinces</i> (LPP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheth_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheth-2">[2]</a></sup> Rampino and Stothers (1988) cite eleven distinct flood-basalt episodes - occurring in the past 250 million years - which created volcanic provinces and plateaus and coincided with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinctions" title="Mass extinctions">mass extinctions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, more flood basalts have been recognized, such as the large <a href="/wiki/Ontong_Java_Plateau" title="Ontong Java Plateau">Ontong Java Plateau</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Chilcotin_Group" title="Chilcotin Group">Chilcotin Group</a>, though the latter may be linked to the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River_Basalt_Group" title="Columbia River Basalt Group">Columbia River Basalt Group</a>. Formation depends on a range of factors, such as continental configuration, latitude, volume, rate, duration of eruption, style and setting (continental vs. oceanic), the pre-existing <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate state</a>, and the biota resilience to change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992 researchers first used the term <i>large igneous province</i> to describe very large accumulations—areas greater than 100,000 square kilometers (approximately the area of Iceland)—of <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> igneous rocks that were erupted or emplaced at depth within an extremely short <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_time" title="Geological time">geological time</a> interval: a few million years or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coffin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coffin-6">[6]</a></sup> Mafic, basalt sea floors and other geological products of 'normal' plate tectonics were not included in the definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The definition of <i>LIP</i> has been expanded and refined, and is still a work in progress. <i>LIP</i> is now frequently also used to describe voluminous areas of, not just mafic, but all types of igneous rocks. Sub-categorization of LIPs into <i>large volcanic provinces</i> (LVP) and <i>large plutonic provinces</i> (LPP), and including rocks produced by normal plate tectonic processes, has been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheth_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sheth-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some LIPs are now intact, such the basaltic <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Traps" title="Deccan Traps">Deccan Traps</a> in India, while others have been dismembered by plate tectonic motion, like the basaltic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Atlantic_Magmatic_Province" title="Central Atlantic Magmatic Province">Central Atlantic Magmatic Province</a> (CAMP)—parts of which are found in Brazil, eastern North America, and north-western Africa.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivations_for_study_of_LIPs">Motivations for study of LIPs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Motivations for study of LIPs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1152" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg/400px-Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg/600px-Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg/800px-Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flood_Basalt_Map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing the recognized continental large igneous provinces.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Large igneous provinces (LIPs) are created during short-lived igneous events resulting in relatively rapid and high-volume accumulations of volcanic and intrusive igneous rock. These events warrant study because:</p>
<ul>
<li>The study of LIPs has economic implications. Some workers associate them with trapped hydrocarbons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They are associated with economic concentrations of copper–nickel and iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eremin_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eremin-8">[8]</a></sup> They are also associated with formation of major mineral provinces including <a href="/wiki/Ore#Nickel-cobalt-platinum_deposits" title="Ore">Platinum-Group Element (PGE) Deposits</a>, and in the Silicic LIPs, silver and gold deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan-7">[7]</a></sup> Titanium and vanadium deposits are also found in association with LIPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Future LIP events may pose a hazard to human civilization. LIPs in the geological record have marked major changes in the hydrosphere and atmosphere, leading to major climate shifts and maybe mass extinctions of species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Plate tectonic theory explains topography using interactions between the tectonic plates, as influenced by viscous stresses created by flow within the underlying mantle. Since the mantle is extremely viscous, the mantle flow rate varies in pulses which are reflected in the lithosphere by small amplitude, long wavelength undulations. Understanding how the interaction between mantle flow and lithosphere elevation influences formation of LIPs is important to gaining insights into past mantle dynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braun-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>LIPs have played a major role in continental breakup, continental formation, new crustal additions from the upper mantle, and supercontinent cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Braun-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Large_igneous_province_formation">Large igneous province formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Large igneous province formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Earth has an outer shell made of a number of discrete, moving tectonic plates floating on a solid convective mantle above a liquid core. The mantle's flow is driven by the descent of cold tectonic plates during subduction and the complementary ascent of plumes of hot material from lower levels. The surface of the Earth reflects stretching, thickening and bending of the tectonic plates as they interact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-allen-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ocean-plate creation at upwellings, spreading and subduction are well accepted fundamentals of plate tectonics, with the upwelling of hot mantle materials and the sinking of the cooler ocean plates driving the mantle convection. In this model, tectonic plates diverge at mid-ocean ridges, where hot mantle rock flows upward to fill the space. Plate-tectonic processes account for the vast majority of Earth's volcanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hump_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-hump-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beyond the effects of convectively driven motion, deep processes have other influences on the surface topography. The convective circulation drives up-wellings and down-wellings in Earth's mantle that are reflected in local surface levels. Hot mantle materials rising up in a plume can spread out radially beneath the tectonic plate causing regions of uplift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-allen-11">[11]</a></sup> These ascending plumes play an important role in LIP formation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_characteristics">Formation characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Formation characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When created, LIPs often have an areal extent of a few million km² and volumes on the order of 1 million km³. In most cases, the majority of a basaltic LIP's volume is emplaced in less than 1 million years. One of the conundra of such LIPs' origins is to understand how enormous volumes of basaltic magma are formed and erupted over such short time scales, with effusion rates up to an order of magnitude greater than mid-ocean ridge basalts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_theories">Formation theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The source of many or all LIPs are variously attributed to mantle plumes, to processes associated with plate tectonics or to meteorite impacts.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plume_formation_of_LIPs">Plume formation of LIPs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Plume formation of LIPs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although most of volcanic activity on Earth is associated with subduction zones or mid-oceanic ridges, there are significant regions of long-lived, extensive volcanism, known as hot spots, which are only indirectly related to plate tectonics. The Emperor Sea Mount chain, located on the Pacific plate, is one example, tracing millions of years of relative motion as the plate moves over the Hawaiian hot spot. Numerous hot spots of varying size and age have been identified across the world. These hot spots move slowly with respect to one another, but move an order of magnitude more quickly with respect to tectonic plates, providing evidence that they are not directly linked to tectonic plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hump_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-hump-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The origin of hotspots remains controversial. Hotspots that reach the Earth’s surface may have three distinct origins. The deepest probably originate from the boundary between the lower mantle and the core; roughly 15–20% have characteristics such as presence of a linear chain of sea mounts with increasing ages, LIPs at the point of origin of the track, low shear wave velocity indicating high temperatures below the current location of the track, and ratios of He<sup>3</sup> to He<sup>4</sup> which are judged consistent with a deep origin. Others such as the Pitcairn, Samoan and Tahitian hotspots appear to originate at the top of large, transient, hot lava domes (termed superswells) in the mantle. The remainder appear to originate in the upper mantle and have been suggested to result from the breakup of subducting lithosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cour_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-cour-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent imaging of the region below known hot spots (e.g., Yellowstone and Hawaii) using seismic-wave <a href="/wiki/Tomography" title="Tomography">tomography</a> has produced mounting evidence that supports relatively narrow, deep-origin, convective plumes that are limited in region compared to the large-scale plate tectonic circulation in which they are imbedded. Images reveal continuous, but torturous vertical paths with varying quantities of elevated temperature material, particularly at elevations where crystallographic transformations are predicted to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plate-related_stress_formation_of_LIPs">Plate-related stress formation of LIPs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Plate-related stress formation of LIPs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A major alternative to the plume model is a model in which ruptures are caused by plate-related stresses that fractured the lithosphere, allowing melt to reach the surface from shallow heterogeneous sources. The high volumes of molten material that form the LIPs is postulated to be caused by convection in the upper mantle, which is secondary to the convection driving tectonic plate motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hagstrum_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hagstrum-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_formed_reservoir_outpourings">Early formed reservoir outpourings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early formed reservoir outpourings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>It has been proposed that geochemical evidence supports an early-formed reservoir that survived in the Earth's mantle for about 4.5 billion years. Molten material is postulated to have originated from this reservoir, contributing the Baffin Island flood basalt ~60 million years ago. Basalts from the Ontong Java plateau show similar isotopic and trace element signatures proposed for the early-Earth reservoir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorite-induced_formation">Meteorite-induced formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Meteorite-induced formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Seven pairs of hotspots and LIPs located on opposite sides of the earth have been noted; analyses indicate this coincident antipodal location is highly unlikely to be random. The hotspot pairs include a large igneous province with continental volcanism opposite an oceanic hotspot. Oceanic impacts of large meteorites are expected to have high efficiency in converting energy into seismic waves. These waves would propagate around the world and reconverge close to the antipodal position; small variations are expected as the seismic velocity varies depending upon the route characteristics along which the waves propagate. As the waves focus on the antipodal position, they put the crust at the focal point under significant stress and are proposed to rupture it, creating antipodal pairs. When the meteorite impacts a continent, the lower efficiency of kinetic energy conversion into seismic energy is not expected to create an antipodal hotspot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hagstrum_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hagstrum-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second impact-related model of hotspot and LIP formation has been suggested in which minor hotspot volcanism was generated at large-body impact sites and flood basalt volcanism was triggered antipodally by focused seismic energy [13,14]. This model has been challenged because impacts are generally considered seismically too inefficient [15], and the Deccan Traps of India were not antipodal to, and began erupting several Myr before, the end-Cretaceous Chicxulub impact in Mexico. In addition, no clear example of impact-induced volcanism, unrelated to melt sheets, has been confirmed at any known terrestrial crater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hagstrum_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hagstrum-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992, Coffin and Eldholm initially defined the term "large igneous province" (LIP) as representing a variety of mafic igneous provinces with areal extent &gt;1 x 10<sup>5</sup> km<sup>2</sup> that represented "massive crustal emplacements of predominantly mafic (Mg- and Fe-rich) extrusive and intrusive rock, and originated via processes other than 'normal' seafloor spreading."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> That original definition included continental <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_plateau" title="Oceanic plateau">oceanic plateaus</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Dike_swarm" title="Dike swarm">dike swarms</a> (the eroded roots of a volcanic province), and <a href="/wiki/Passive_margin" title="Passive margin">volcanic rifted margins</a>. Most of these LIPs consist of basalt, but some contain large volumes of associated <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolite</a> (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River_Basalt_Group" title="Columbia River Basalt Group">Columbia River Basalt Group</a> in the western United States); the rhyolite is typically very dry compared to island arc rhyolites, with much higher eruption temperatures (850 °C to 1000 °C) than normal rhyolites.</p>
<p>Since 1992 the definition of 'LIP' has been expanded and refined, and remains a work in progress. Some new definitions of the term 'LIP' include large granitic provinces such as those found in the Andes Mountains of South America and in western North America. Comprehensive taxonomies have been developed to focus technical discussions.</p>
<p>In 2008, Bryan and Ernst refined the definition to narrow it somewhat: "Large Igneous Provinces are magmatic provinces with areal extents &gt;1 x 10<sup>5</sup> km<sup>2</sup>, igneous volumes &gt;1 x 10<sup>5</sup> km<sup>3</sup> and maximum lifespans of ∼50 Myr that have intraplate tectonic settings or geochemical affinities, and are characterised by igneous pulse(s) of short duration (∼1–5 Myr), during which a large proportion (&gt;75%) of the total igneous volume has been emplaced. They are dominantly mafic, but also can have significant ultramafic and silicic components, and some are dominated by silicic magmatism." This definition places emphasis on the high magma emplacement rate characteristics of the LIP event and excludes seamounts, seamount groups, submarine ridges and anomalous seafloor crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan_2-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>'LIP' is now frequently used to also describe voluminous areas of, not just mafic, but all types of igneous rocks. Sub-categorization of LIPs into Large Volcanic Provinces (LVP) and Large Plutonic Provinces (LPP), and including rocks produced by 'normal' plate tectonic processes, has been proposed. Further, the threshold to be included as a LIP has been lowered to &gt;5x10<sup>4</sup> km<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheth_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sheth-2">[2]</a></sup> The working taxonomy, focused heavily on geochemistry, which will be used to structure examples below, is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Large igneous provinces (LIP)
<ul>
<li>Large volcanic provinces (LVP)
<ul>
<li>Large rhyolitic provinces (LRPs)</li>
<li>Large andesitic provinces (LAPs)</li>
<li>Large basaltic provinces (LBPs): oceanic, or continental flood basalts</li>
<li>Large basaltic–rhyolitic provinces (LBRPs)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Large plutonic provinces (LPP)
<ul>
<li>Large granitic provinces (LGP)</li>
<li>Large mafic plutonic provinces</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Aerally extensive <a href="/wiki/Dike_swarm" title="Dike swarm">dike swarms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sill</a> provinces, and large layered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> intrusions are indicators of LIPs, even when other evidence is not now observable. The upper basalt layers of older LIPs may have been removed by erosion or deformed by tectonic plate collisions occurring after the layer is formed. The is especially likely for earlier periods such as the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_2_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan_2-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Giant dyke swarms having lengths &gt;300 km<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstButler97_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstButler97-21">[21]</a></sup> are a common record of severely eroded LIPs. Both radial and linear dyke swarm configurations exist. Radial swarms with an areal extent &gt;2000 km and linear swarms extending &gt;1000 km are known. The linear dyke swarms often have a high proportion of dykes relative to country rocks, particularly when the width of the linear field is less than 100 km. The dykes have a typical width of 20–100 meters, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> dykes with widths greater than 1000 meters have been reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_2_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan_2-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dykes are typically sub-vertical to vertical. When upward flowing (dyke-forming) magma encounters horizontal boundaries or weaknesses, such as between layers in a sedimentary deposit, the magma can flow horizontally creating a sill. Some sill provinces have areal extents &gt;1000 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_2_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan_2-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Correlations_with_LIP_formation">Correlations with LIP formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Correlations with LIP formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Correlation_with_hot-spots">Correlation with hot-spots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Correlation with hot-spots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The early volcanic activity of major <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a>, postulated to result from deep mantle plumes, is frequently accompanied by flood basalts. These flood basalt eruptions have resulted in large accumulations of basaltic lavas emplaced at a rate greatly exceeding that seen in contemporary volcanic processes. Continental rifting commonly follows flood basalt volcanism. Flood basalt provinces may also occur as a consequence of the initial hot-spot activity in ocean basins as well as on continents. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_extinction_events">Relationship to extinction events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Relationship to extinction events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eruptions or emplacements of LIPs appear to have, in some cases, occurred simultaneously with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_anoxic_event" title="Oceanic anoxic event">oceanic anoxic events</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction events</a>. The most important examples are the <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Traps" title="Deccan Traps">Deccan Traps</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Karoo-Ferrar" title="Karoo-Ferrar">Karoo-Ferrar</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pliensbachian-Toarcian_extinction" title="Pliensbachian-Toarcian extinction">Pliensbachian-Toarcian extinction</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Atlantic_Magmatic_Province" title="Central Atlantic Magmatic Province">Central Atlantic Magmatic Province</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triassic-Jurassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic-Jurassic extinction event">Triassic-Jurassic extinction event</a>), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_traps" title="Siberian traps">Siberian traps</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Permian-Triassic extinction event</a>).</p>
<p>Several mechanisms are proposed to explain the association of LIPs with extinction events. The eruption of basaltic LIPs onto the earth's surface releases large volumes of sulfate gas, which forms sulfuric acid in the atmosphere; this absorbs heat and causes substantial cooling (e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laki_(volcano)" title="Laki (volcano)">Laki</a> eruption in Iceland, 1783). Oceanic LIPs can reduce oxygen in seawater by either direct oxidation reactions with metals in hydrothermal fluids or by causing algal blooms that consume large amounts of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_rifted_margins">Volcanic rifted margins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Volcanic rifted margins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mantleplume.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="577" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e3/Mantleplume.png/240px-Mantleplume.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e3/Mantleplume.png/360px-Mantleplume.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e3/Mantleplume.png/480px-Mantleplume.png 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mantleplume.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<br/>
Extension thins the crust. Magma reaches the surface through radiating sills and dikes, forming basalt flows, as well as deep and shallow magma chambers below the surface. The crust gradually thins due to thermal subsidence, and originally horizontal basalt flows are rotated to become seaward dipping reflectors.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volcanic_passive_margin" title="Volcanic passive margin">Volcanic rifted margins</a> are found on the boundary of large igneous provinces. Volcanic margins form when rifting is accompanied by significant mantle melting, with volcanism occurring before and/or during continental breakup. Volcanic rifted margins are characterized by: a transitional crust composed of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rocks</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> flows, <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dikes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbros</a>, high volume basalt flows, seaward-dipping reflector sequences (SDRS) of basalt flows that were rotated during the early stages of breakup, limited passive-margin subsidence during and after breakup, and the presence of a lower crust with anomalously high seismic P-wave velocities in lower crustal bodies (LCBs), indicative of lower temperature, dense media.</p>
<p>Example of volcanic margins include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Yemen margin</li>
<li>The East Australian margin</li>
<li>The West Indian margin</li>
<li>The Hatton–Rockal margin</li>
<li>The US East Coast</li>
<li>The mid-Norwegian margin</li>
<li>The Brazilian margins</li>
<li>The Namibian margin</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dike_swarms">Dike swarms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Dike swarms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="384" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png/220px-Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png/330px-Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mackenzie_dike_swarm.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Mackenzie dike swarm in Canada</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A dike swarm is a large geological structure consisting of a major group of parallel, linear, or radially oriented dikes intruded within continental crust. They consist of several to hundreds of dikes emplaced more or less contemporaneously during a single intrusive event, and are magmatic and stratigraphic. Such dike swarms are the roots of a volcanic province. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_dike_swarm" title="Mackenzie dike swarm">Mackenzie dike swarm</a> (Canadian Shield)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Long_Range_dikes" title="Long Range dikes">Long Range dikes</a> (Newfoundland and Labrador, Canada)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mistassini_dike_swarm" title="Mistassini dike swarm">Mistassini dike swarm</a> (western Quebec, Canada)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matachewan_dike_swarm" title="Matachewan dike swarm">Matachewan dike swarm</a> (northern Ontario, Canada)</li>
<li>Sorachi Plateau and Belt (Hokkaido, Japan)</li>
<li>Uralian dike swarm (Russia)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Puchkov2016_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Puchkov2016-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sills">Sills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Large_igneous_province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A series of related sills that were formed essentially contemporaneously (within several million years) from related dikes comprise a LIP if their area is sufficiently large. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Winagami_sill_complex" title="Winagami sill complex">Winagami sill complex</a> (northwestern Alberta, Canada)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bushveld_Igneous_Complex" title="Bushveld Igneous Complex">Bushveld Igneous Complex</a> (South Africa) with an area of over 66,000 km<sup>2</sup> (25,000 sq mi), and a thickness reaching 9 kilometres (5.6 mi) thick.</li>
</ul>
