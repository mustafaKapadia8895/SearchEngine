<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of off-season Atlantic hurricanes</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>An <b>off-season Atlantic hurricane</b> is a recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">tropical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> that existed in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic basin</a> outside of the official Atlantic hurricane season. The <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> currently defines the season as occurring between June 1 and November 30 each calendar year, which is when 97% of all Atlantic tropical cyclones occur. Peak activity is known to be between August and October. In the off-season, storms are most likely to occur in May, with approximately 60% of such storms occurring during that month. Occasionally, however, storms develop in or persist until December. As of 2017, there have been 81 off-season cyclones in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons" title="List of Atlantic hurricane seasons">Atlantic hurricane database</a>, which began in <a href="/wiki/1851_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1851 Atlantic hurricane season">1851</a>. In addition, there were six storms before 1851, and one hurricane in 1863 that is not part of the official database.</p>
<p>Off-season cyclones are most likely to occur in the central to western Atlantic Ocean, and most do not make <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfall</a>. Of the storms that did strike land, most affected areas surrounding the Caribbean Sea. Cumulatively, at least 398 deaths occurred due to the storms, primarily on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>; a <a href="/wiki/1948_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_One" title="1948 Atlantic hurricane season">tropical storm in May 1948</a> struck the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> to become the deadliest off-season storm. However, an unofficial hurricane in 1863 killed 110 people, in a shipwreck off Florida and on land. The same storm was estimated to have reached winds of 105 mph (165 km/h), making it the strongest hurricane between December to May; the strongest currently in the official database was a March hurricane in 1908 that reached winds of 100 mph (160 km/h). In addition, the strongest off-season cyclone to make landfall in the United States was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Beryl_(2012)" title="Tropical Storm Beryl (2012)">Tropical Storm Beryl</a> in <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012</a>, which made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Beach,_Florida" title="Jacksonville Beach, Florida">Jacksonville Beach, Florida</a> with 65 mph (100 km/h) winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beryl_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beryl-1">[1]</a></sup> The most recent off-season storm was <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Arlene" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Arlene</a> in April 2017.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_off-season_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1938, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> began issuing <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical cyclone warnings</a> as a collaborative observation network for cities along the U.S. coastline, and the season was defined between June 15 and November 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1964, the season was extended to begin on June 1 and end on November 30,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-upi-3">[3]</a></sup> which remains the official length of the season. About 97% of all tropical cyclones form within this time span, and activity usually peaks between August and October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-season_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-season-4">[4]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2015)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2015)">Tropical Storm Ana</a> formed in May 2015, former <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> president Marshall Shepherd used <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> to question whether the season should begin earlier. <a href="/wiki/James_Franklin_(meteorologist)" title="James Franklin (meteorologist)">James Franklin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> believed there was little advantage to changing it because of the rarity of off-season storms, noting that May storms only formed on average once every six years since the advent of satellite imagery. Franklin opined that the season could begin on May 15 with little difficulty, coinciding with the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane_season" title="Pacific hurricane season">Pacific hurricane season</a>, but an earlier start would be costlier and interfere with off-season work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronology">Chronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_off-season_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tropical cyclones have been named in the Atlantic since the <a href="/wiki/1947_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1947 Atlantic hurricane season">1947 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, and subtropical cyclones have been recognized in <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">HURDAT</a> since 1968. The National Hurricane Center issues names for tropical and subtropical cyclones once their winds reach 39 mph (63 km/h). Before 1950, storms are numbered based on their appearance in the Atlantic hurricane database, although tropical depressions are unnumbered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-META_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-META-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL_Book_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATL_Book-8">[8]</a></sup> Storms before 1851 are unofficial and are not part of the official Atlantic hurricane best track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenoweth_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-chenoweth-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, a hurricane from May 1863, labeled <a href="/wiki/1863_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Other_storms" title="1863 Atlantic hurricane season">"Amanda"</a>, is included after being rediscovered in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amanda_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-amanda-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wind speeds listed are <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum one-minute average sustained winds</a>, and the pressure is the minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a>; tropical cyclones listed with N/A under pressure indicates there is no known estimated pressure. For deaths, "None" indicates that there were no reports of fatalities; death tolls listed as "several" mean there were fatalities reported, but an exact total is unavailable. For both deaths and damage, N/A refers to no known total, although such storms may have impacted land. The damage totals are the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a> of the year of the storm.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records_and_statistics">Records and statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_off-season_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records and statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TS_Odette.jpg"><img alt="Satellite image of a developing Tropical storm in the central Caribbean Sea. Clouds extend northeast toward Hispaniola and southeast toward northern South America." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="642" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TS_Odette.jpg/220px-TS_Odette.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TS_Odette.jpg/330px-TS_Odette.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TS_Odette.jpg/440px-TS_Odette.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TS_Odette.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Odette (2003)">Tropical Storm Odette</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> in December 2003</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the official Atlantic hurricane database, which dates back to 1851, the first storm to occur outside of the current season was in 1865 in the Caribbean Sea. In the database, 81 <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a> have existed between December and May, most recently <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Arlene" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Arlene</a> in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-20"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-META_7-20"><a href="#cite_note-META-7">[7]</a></sup> In addition, there were at least five storms in May and another in December before the start of the official database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenoweth_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-chenoweth-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Storms were most likely to occur in May, followed by December. Out of all recorded storms in the database, only one cyclone was reported in the month of March; the <a href="/wiki/1908_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_One" title="1908 Atlantic hurricane season">1908 March hurricane</a>, as well as one tropical storm in February, which was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1952_Groundhog_Day_Storm" title="1952 Groundhog Day Storm">1952 Groundhog Day Storm</a>. In addition, only three tropical or subtropical cyclones formed in April – a <a href="/wiki/1992_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Subtropical_Storm_One" title="1992 Atlantic hurricane season">subtropical storm in 1992</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2003)">Tropical Storm Ana</a> of 2003, and <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Arlene" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Arlene</a> of 2017. A <a href="/wiki/1938_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_One" title="1938 Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane in 1938</a>, a <a href="/wiki/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_One" title="1951 Atlantic hurricane season">tropical storm in 1951</a>, a <a href="/wiki/1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Subtropical_Storm_One" title="1978 Atlantic hurricane season">subtropical storm in 1978</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Hurricane Alex</a> of 2016 occurred in January. Of all cyclones during the off-season, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lili_(1984)" title="Hurricane Lili (1984)">Hurricane Lili</a> in 1984 lasted the longest, for a total of 12 days. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Epsilon" title="Hurricane Epsilon">Hurricane Epsilon</a>, which formed in November, maintained hurricane status for five days in December 2005, longer than any other storm in December; the previous record was two and a half days, set by Hurricane Lili in 1984. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> in 1954–1955 and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> in 2005–2006 were the only recorded cyclones to have spanned two calendar years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-21"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-META_7-21"><a href="#cite_note-META-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the off-season storms that struck land, portions of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> were affected most.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-22"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-META_7-22"><a href="#cite_note-META-7">[7]</a></sup> Hurricane Alice was the only of the cyclones to strike land as a hurricane, doing so to islands in the northern <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>; it caused locally heavy rainfall and moderate damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1955mwr_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-1955mwr-25">[25]</a></sup> No hurricanes have ever made landfall in the United States during December, although at one point a storm in 1925 was believed to have done this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-23"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> One century earlier, a hurricane formed in the western Caribbean and struck Florida on or before June 3,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> which was the earliest date for a United States hurricane landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> However, there is an unofficial hurricane in 1863 that struck the Florida panhandle, killing 110 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amanda_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-amanda-10">[10]</a></sup> The deadliest official off-season storm was a tropical storm in May 1948, which killed 80 people in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadliest_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-deadliest-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The year with the most off-season storms was <a href="/wiki/1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1887 Atlantic hurricane season">1887</a>, with a total of five existing in the off-season. The <a href="/wiki/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1951 Atlantic hurricane season">1951</a> season had four, one of which a depression. Several others had three tropical cyclones, of which only <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a> had three tropical storms. The <a href="/wiki/1908_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1908 Atlantic hurricane season">1908</a> and 1951 seasons were the only ones with two hurricanes forming in the off-season. In seven seasons, there were storms both prior to the start of the season as well as after the season ended, those being 1887, <a href="/wiki/1911_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1911 Atlantic hurricane season">1911</a>, 1951, <a href="/wiki/1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1953 Atlantic hurricane season">1953</a>, 1954, 2003, and <a href="/wiki/2007_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2007 Atlantic hurricane season">2007</a>; all but 1911 had tropical cyclones of at least tropical storm status before and after the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-24"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-META_7-23"><a href="#cite_note-META-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
