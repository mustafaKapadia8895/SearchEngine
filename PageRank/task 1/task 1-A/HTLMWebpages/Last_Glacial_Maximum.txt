<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Last Glacial Maximum</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <b>Last Glacial Maximum</b> (<b>LGM</b>) was the last period in the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s climate history during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> when <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> were at their greatest extension. Growth of the ice sheets reached their maximum positions at about 26.5 k<a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>. Deglaciation commenced in the Northern Hemisphere at approximately 20 kBP and in Antarctica approximately at 14.5 kBP, which is consistent with evidence that it was the primary source for an abrupt rise in the <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> at about 14.5 kBP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The LGM is referred to in Britain as the <b>Dimlington Stadial</b>, and dated by Nick Ashton between 31,000 and 16,000 BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vast ice sheets covered much of North America, northern Europe, and Asia. The ice sheets profoundly affected Earth's climate by causing drought, desertification, and a dramatic drop in sea levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Late_Glacial" title="Late Glacial">Late Glacial</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacial_climate">Glacial climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Glacial climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="761" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png/450px-Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png/675px-Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png 2x" width="450"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epica-vostok-grip-40kyr.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Temperature proxies for the last 40,000 years.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="782" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png/450px-Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png/675px-Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png 2x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Last_glacial_vegetation_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map of vegetation patterns during the last glacial maximum.</div>
</div>

<p>The formation of an ice sheet or <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice cap</a> requires both prolonged cold and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>). Hence, despite having temperatures similar to those of glaciated areas in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> remained unglaciated except at higher elevations. This difference was because the ice sheets in Europe produced extensive <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclones</a> above them.</p>
<p>These anticyclones generated <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air masses</a> that were so dry on reaching <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> that precipitation sufficient for the formation of glaciers could never occur (except in <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka</a> where these westerly winds lifted moisture from the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a>). The relative warmth of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> due to the shutting down of the <a href="/wiki/Oyashio_Current" title="Oyashio Current">Oyashio Current</a> and the presence of large 'east-west' mountain ranges were secondary factors preventing continental glaciation in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.</p>
<p>All over the world, climates at the Last Glacial Maximum were cooler and almost everywhere drier. In extreme cases, such as <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>, rainfall could be diminished by up to 90% from present, with florae diminished to almost the same degree as in glaciated areas of Europe and North America. Even in less affected regions, <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> cover was greatly diminished, especially in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> where a few <i>refugia</i> were surrounded by tropical <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> was split into two large blocks by extensive <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, and the tropical rainforests of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> probably were similarly affected, with deciduous forests expanding in their place except on the east and west extremities of the <a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a> shelf. Only in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Choc%C3%B3" title="Department of Chocó">Chocó</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> did tropical rainforests remain substantially intact – probably due to the extraordinarily heavy rainfall of these regions.</p>
<p>Most of the world's deserts expanded. Exceptions were in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">western United States</a>, where changes in the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> brought heavy rain to areas that are now desert and large <a href="/wiki/Pluvial_lake" title="Pluvial lake">pluvial lakes</a> formed, the best known being <a href="/wiki/Lake_Bonneville" title="Lake Bonneville">Lake Bonneville</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>. This also occurred in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, where a major lake formed in the <a href="/wiki/Dasht-e_Kavir" title="Dasht-e Kavir">Dasht-e Kavir</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, shifting sand dunes covered half the continent, whilst the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Chaco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pampas" title="Pampas">Pampas</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> became similarly dry. Present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_climate" title="Subtropical climate">subtropical</a> regions also lost most of their forest cover, notably in eastern Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Forest" title="Atlantic Forest">Atlantic Forest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, where open <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> became dominant due to drier conditions. In northern China – unglaciated despite its cold climate – a mixture of grassland and <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> prevailed, and even here, the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">northern limit of tree growth</a> was at least 20° farther south than today.</p>
<p>In the period before the Last Glacial Maximum, many areas that became completely barren desert were wetter than they are today, notably in southern Australia, where <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Aboriginal</a> occupation is believed to coincide with a wet period between 40,000 and 60,000 years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">Before Present</a> (BP, a formal measurement of uncalibrated <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon years</a>, counted from 1950 CE).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_impact">World impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the Last Glacial Maximum, much of the world was cold, dry, and inhospitable, with frequent storms and a dust-laden atmosphere. The dustiness of the atmosphere is a prominent feature in ice cores; dust levels were as much as 20 to 25 times greater than now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csmonitor.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-csmonitor.com-4">[4]</a></sup> This was probably due to a number of factors: reduced vegetation, stronger global winds, and less precipitation to clear dust from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_Atmosphere" title="Earth's Atmosphere">the atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csmonitor.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-csmonitor.com-4">[4]</a></sup> The massive sheets of ice locked away water, lowering the sea level, exposing <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelves</a>, joining land masses together, and creating extensive coastal <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithen_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mithen-5">[5]</a></sup> During the last glacial maximum, 21,000 years ago, the sea level was about 125 meters (about 410 feet) lower than it is today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="534" data-file-width="1046" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg/500px-LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg/750px-LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg/1000px-LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LGM-Mangerud-2003.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The extent of the Last Glacial Maximum in Eurasia, Mangerud <i>et al.</i> 2004 (fig 4).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mangerud04_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mangerud04-7">[7]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> was largely covered by ice, the southern boundary of the ice sheets passing through Germany and Poland. This ice extended northward to cover <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Land" title="Franz Josef Land">Franz Josef Land</a> and northeastward to occupy the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kara_Sea" title="Kara Sea">Kara Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a>, ending at the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mangerud04_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mangerud04-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In northwestern Russia the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fennoscandian_Ice_Sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fennoscandian Ice Sheet (page does not exist)">Fennoscandian Ice Sheet</a> reached its LGM extent 17 ka BP, five thousand years later than in Denmark, Germany and Western Poland. Outside the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Shield" title="Baltic Shield">Baltic Shield</a>, and in Russia in particular, the LGM ice margin of the Fennoscandian Ice Sheet was highly lobate. The main LGM lobes of Russia followed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Dvina" title="Northern Dvina">Dvina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vologda_River" title="Vologda River">Vologda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rybinsk" title="Rybinsk">Rybinsk</a> basins respectively. Lobes originated as result of ice following shallow topographic depressions filled with a <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">soft sediment</a> substrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a> covered Europe south of the ice sheet down to present-day <a href="/wiki/Szeged" title="Szeged">Szeged</a> in Southern Hungary. Ice covered the whole of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and almost all of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> but <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">southern England</a>. Britain was no more than a peninsula of Europe, its north capped in ice, and its south a <a href="/wiki/Polar_desert" title="Polar desert">polar desert</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were ice sheets in modern <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> (although scientists continue to debate the extent to which the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> was covered with ice) as well as in <a href="/wiki/Baltistan" title="Baltistan">Baltistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, many smaller mountain glaciers formed, and permafrost covered Asia as far south as <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>. Because of lowered sea levels, many of today's islands were joined to the continents: the Indonesian islands as far east as <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a> were connected to the Asian continent in a landmass called <a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a>. <a href="/wiki/Palawan" title="Palawan">Palawan</a> was also part of Sundaland, while the rest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Islands" title="Philippine Islands">Philippine Islands</a> formed one large island separated from the continent only by the <a href="/wiki/Sibutu_Passage" title="Sibutu Passage">Sibutu Passage</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mindoro_Strait" title="Mindoro Strait">Mindoro Strait</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FieldMuseum_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FieldMuseum-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa_and_the_Middle_East">Africa and the Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Africa and the Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Africa and the Middle East, many smaller mountain glaciers formed, and the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> and other sandy deserts were greatly expanded in extent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithen_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mithen-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> averages about 35 metres in depth and the seabed between <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a> is even shallower, being mostly less than 15 metres deep. For thousands of years the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ur-Shatt" title="Ur-Shatt">Ur-Shatt</a> (a confluence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigris_River" title="Tigris River">Tigris</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphrates_River" title="Euphrates River">Euphrates Rivers</a>) provided fresh water to the Gulf, as it flowed through the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz" title="Strait of Hormuz">Strait of Hormuz</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman" title="Gulf of Oman">Gulf of Oman</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">Bathymetric</a> data suggests there were two palaeo-basins in the Persian Gulf. The central basin may have approached an area of 20,000 km², comparable at its fullest extent to lakes such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Malawi" title="Lake Malawi">Lake Malawi</a> in Africa. Between 12,000 and 9000 years ago much of the Gulf floor would have remained exposed, only being flooded by the sea after 8,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is estimated that annual average temperatures in Southern Africa were 6° C lower than at present during the Last Glacial Maximum. This alone would however not have been enough to create a widespread <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg Mountains</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Lesotho_Highlands" title="Lesotho Highlands">Lesotho Highlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millsetal2017_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Millsetal2017-11">[11]</a></sup> Seasonal freezing of the ground in the Lesotho Highlands might have reached depths of 2 meter or more below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A few small glaciers did however develop during the Last Glacial Maximum, in particular in south-facing slopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millsetal2017_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Millsetal2017-11">[11]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Hex_River_Mountains" title="Hex River Mountains">Hex River Mountains</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape" title="Western Cape">Western Cape</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Block_stream&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Block stream (page does not exist)">block streams</a> and terraces found near the summit of Matroosberg evidences past <a href="/wiki/Periglaciation" title="Periglaciation">periglacial activity</a> which likely occurred during the Last Glacial Maximum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australasia">Australasia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Australasia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Australian mainland, New Guinea, Tasmania and many smaller islands comprised a single land mass. This continent is now referred to sometimes as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahul_continent" title="Sahul continent">Sahul</a>.</p>
<p>Between Sahul and <a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a> – a peninsula of South East Asia that comprised present-day Malaysia and western and northern Indonesia – there remained an archipelago of islands known as <a href="/wiki/Wallacea" title="Wallacea">Wallacea</a>. The water gaps between these islands, Sahul and Sundaland were considerably narrower and fewer in number.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Glaciation" title="Wisconsin Glaciation">Wisconsin Glaciation</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cordilleran_Ice_Sheet" title="Cordilleran Ice Sheet">Cordilleran Ice Sheet</a></div>
<p>In North America, the ice covered essentially all of Canada and extended roughly to the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio Rivers</a>, and eastward to <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. In addition to the large Cordilleran Ice Sheet in Canada and <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_glacier" title="Alpine glacier">alpine glaciers</a> advanced and (in some locations) ice caps covered much of the Rocky Mountains further south. Latitudinal gradients were so sharp that permafrost did not reach far south of the ice sheets except at high elevations. Glaciers forced the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_history_of_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Genetic history of indigenous peoples of the Americas">early human populations</a> who had originally migrated from northeast Siberia into <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">refugia</a>, reshaping their <a href="/wiki/Genetic_variation" title="Genetic variation">genetic variation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">drift</a>. This phenomenon established the older <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup" title="Haplogroup">haplogroups</a> found among <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a>, and later migrations are responsible for northern North American haplogroups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_of_Hawaii" title="Island of Hawaii">Island of Hawaii</a>, geologists have long recognized deposits formed by glaciers on <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">Mauna Kea</a> during recent ice ages. The latest work indicates that deposits of three glacial episodes since 150,000 to 200,000 years ago are preserved on the volcano. Glacial moraines on the volcano formed about 70,000 years ago and from about 40,000 to 13,000 years ago. If glacial deposits were formed on <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a>, they have long since been buried by younger lava flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Last_Glacial_Maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Llanquihue_glaciation" title="Llanquihue glaciation">Llanquihue glaciation</a></div>
<p>During the Last Glacial Maximum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valley_glacier" title="Valley glacier">valley glaciers</a> in the southern Andes (38–43° S) merged and descended from the Andes occupying lacustrine and marine basins where they spread out forming large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmont_glacier" title="Piedmont glacier">piedmont glacier lobes</a>. Glaciers extended about 7 km west of the modern <a href="/wiki/Llanquihue_Lake" title="Llanquihue Lake">Llanquihue Lake</a> but not more than 2 to 3 km south of it. <a href="/wiki/Nahuel_Huapi_Lake" title="Nahuel Huapi Lake">Nahuel Huapi Lake</a> in Argentina was also glaciated by the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heusser2004-25-29_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heusser2004-25-29-16">[16]</a></sup> Over most <a href="/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Archipelago" title="Chiloé Archipelago">Chiloé</a> glacier advance peaked in 26,000 yrs BP forming a long north-south <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraine</a> system along the eastern coast of <a href="/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Island" title="Chiloé Island">Chiloé Island</a> (31.5–43° S). By that time the glaciation at the latitude of Chiloé was of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> type contrasting to the valley glaciation found further north in Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garcia2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia2012-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite glacier advances much of the area west of Llanquihue Lake was still ice-free during the Last Glacial Maximum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowelletal1995_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lowelletal1995-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morenoetal2015_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morenoetal2015-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowelletal1995_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lowelletal1995-18">[18]</a></sup> During the coldest period of the Last Glacial Maximum vegetation at this location was dominated by Alpine herbs in wide open surfaces. The global warming that followed caused a slow change in vegetation towards a sparsely distributed vegetation dominated by <i><a href="/wiki/Nothofagus" title="Nothofagus">Nothofagus</a></i> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowelletal1995_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lowelletal1995-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morenoetal2015_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morenoetal2015-19">[19]</a></sup> Within this parkland vegetation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magellanic_moorland" title="Magellanic moorland">Magellanic moorland</a> alternated with <i>Nothofagus</i> forest, and as warming progressed even warm-climate trees begun to grow in the area. It is estimated that the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> was depressed about 1000 m relative to present day elevations during the coldest period, but it rose gradually until 19,300 yr BP. At that time a cold reversal caused a replacement of much of the arboreal vegetation with Magellanic moorland and Alpine species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morenoetal2015_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Morenoetal2015-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Little is known about the extent of glaciers during Last Glacial Maximum north of the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Lake_District" title="Chilean Lake District">Chilean Lake District</a>. To the north, in the <a href="/wiki/Dry_Andes" title="Dry Andes">dry Andes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Central_Chile" title="Central Chile">Central</a> and the Last Glacial Maximum is associated with increased humidity and the verified advance of at least some mountain glaciers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison2004-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Southern Hemisphere, the <a href="/wiki/Patagonian_Ice_Sheet" title="Patagonian Ice Sheet">Patagonian Ice Sheet</a> covered the whole southern third of Chile and adjacent areas of Argentina. On the western side of the Andes the ice sheet reached sea level as far north as in the <a href="/wiki/41st_parallel_south" title="41st parallel south">41 degrees south</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chacao_Channel" title="Chacao Channel">Chacao Channel</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The western coast of <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> was largely glaciated, but some authors have pointed out the possible existence of ice-free refugia for some plant species. On the eastern side of the Andes, glacier lobes occupied the depressions of <a href="/wiki/Seno_Skyring" title="Seno Skyring">Seno Skyring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seno_Otway" title="Seno Otway">Seno Otway</a>, <a href="/wiki/In%C3%BAtil_Bay" title="Inútil Bay">Inútil Bay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel" title="Beagle Channel">Beagle Channel</a>. On the Straits of Magellan, ice reached as far as <a href="/wiki/Segunda_Angostura" title="Segunda Angostura">Segunda Angostura</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
