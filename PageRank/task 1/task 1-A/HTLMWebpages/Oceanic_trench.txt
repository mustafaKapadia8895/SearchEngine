<p><b>Oceanic trenches</b> are <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographic</a> depressions of the sea floor, relatively narrow in width, but very long. These <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanographic</a> features are the deepest parts of the ocean floor. Oceanic trenches are a distinctive morphological feature of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">convergent plate boundaries</a>, along which <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plates</a> move towards each other at rates that vary from a few millimeters to over ten centimeters per year. A trench marks the position at which the flexed, <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducting</a> <a href="/wiki/Slab_(geology)" title="Slab (geology)">slab</a> begins to descend beneath another lithospheric slab. Trenches are generally parallel to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_island" title="Volcanic island">volcanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arc</a>, and about 200 km (120 mi) from a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_arc" title="Volcanic arc">volcanic arc</a>. Oceanic trenches typically extend 3 to 4 km (1.9 to 2.5 mi) below the level of the surrounding oceanic floor. The greatest ocean depth measured is in the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>, at a depth of 11,034 m (36,201 ft) below sea level. Oceanic lithosphere moves into trenches at a global rate of about 3 km<sup>2</sup>/yr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>There are about 50,000 km (31,000 mi) of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">convergent plate margins</a>, mostly around the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>—the reason for the reference “Pacific-type” margin—but they are also in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, with relatively short convergent margin segments in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. Globally, there are over 50 major ocean trenches covering an area of 1.9 million km<sup>2</sup> or about 0.5% of the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Trenches that are partially infilled are known as "troughs" and sometimes they are completely buried and lack bathymetric expression, but the fundamental <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> structures that these represent mean that the great name should also be applied here. This applies to the <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_subduction_zone" title="Cascadia subduction zone">Cascadia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makran" title="Makran">Makran</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabrian trenches</a>. Trenches along with <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_arc" title="Volcanic arc">volcanic arcs</a> and zones of <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> that dip under the volcanic arc as deeply as 700 km (430 mi) are diagnostic of convergent plate boundaries and their deeper manifestations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zones" title="Subduction zones">subduction zones</a>. Trenches are related to but distinguished from continental collision zones (such as that between India and Asia forming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a>), where <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> enters a subduction zone. When buoyant continental crust enters a trench, subduction eventually stops and the area becomes a zone of continental collision. Features analogous to trenches are associated with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Collisions_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Collisions zone (page does not exist)">collisions zones</a>, including sediment-filled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foredeep" title="Foredeep">foredeeps</a>, such as those the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganges_River" title="Ganges River">Ganges River</a> and Tigris-Euphrates rivers flow along.</p>
<h2><span id='History_of_the_term_"trench"'></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_term_.22trench.22">History of the term "trench"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: History of the term "trench"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trenches were not clearly defined until the late 1940s and 1950s. The <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetry</a> of the ocean was of little interest until the late 19th and early 20th centuries<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="lack of ability/technology to survey is not necessarily the same as lack of interest (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, when the <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">Transatlantic telegraph cables</a> on the seafloor between the continents were first laid. The elongated bathymetric expression of trenches was not recognized until well into the 20th century. The term “trench” does not appear in <a href="/wiki/John_Murray_(oceanographer)" title="John Murray (oceanographer)">Murray</a> and Hjort’s (1912) classic <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> book. Instead they applied the term “deep“ for the deepest parts of the ocean, such as <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>. Experiences from <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> battlefields emblazoned the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench</a> as an elongate depression defining an important boundary, perhaps leading to the term “trench” being used to describe natural features in the early 1920s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The term was first used in a geologic context by Scofield two years after the war ended to describe a structurally controlled depression in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>. Johnstone, in his 1923 textbook <i>An Introduction to Oceanography</i>, first used the term in its modern sense for any marked, elongate depression of the sea bottom.</p>
<p>During the 1920s and 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Andries_Vening_Meinesz" title="Felix Andries Vening Meinesz">Felix Andries Vening Meinesz</a> developed a unique <a href="/wiki/Gravimeter" title="Gravimeter">gravimeter</a> that could measure <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> aboard a submarine and used it to measure gravity over trenches. His measurements revealed that trenches are sites of <a href="/wiki/Downwelling" title="Downwelling">downwelling</a> in the solid Earth. The concept of downwelling at trenches was characterized by Griggs in 1939 as the tectogene hypothesis, for which he developed an analogue model using a pair of rotating drums. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> in the Pacific led to great improvements of bathymetry, particularly in the western Pacific, and the linear nature of these deeps became clear. The rapid growth of deep sea research efforts, especially the widespread use of echosounders in the 1950s and 1960s confirmed the morphological utility of the term. Important trenches were identified, sampled, and their greatest depths sonically plumbed. The early phase of trench exploration culminated in the 1960 descent of the <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe" title="Bathyscaphe">Bathyscaphe</a> <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe_Trieste" title="Bathyscaphe Trieste"><i>Trieste</i></a>, which set an unbeatable world record by diving to the bottom of the Challenger Deep. Following <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Dietz" title="Robert S. Dietz">Robert S. Dietz</a>’ and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Hammond_Hess" title="Harry Hammond Hess">Harry Hess</a>’ articulation of the seafloor spreading hypothesis in the early 1960s and the plate tectonic revolution in the late 1960s the term “trench“ has been redefined with <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonic</a> as well as bathymetric connotations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphologic_expression">Morphologic expression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphologic expression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

It runs along an oceanic-continental boundary, where the oceanic <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Plate" title="Nazca Plate">Nazca Plate</a> subducts beneath the continental <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plate</a></div>

<p>Trenches are centerpieces of the distinctive physiography of a convergent plate margin. Transects across trenches yield asymmetric profiles, with relatively gentle (~5°) outer (seaward) slopes and a steeper (~10–16°) inner (landward) slopes. This asymmetry is due to the fact that the outer slope is defined by the top of the downgoing plate, which must bend as it starts its descent. The great thickness of the lithosphere requires that this bending be gentle. As the subducting plate approaches the trench, it first bends upwards to form the <a href="/wiki/Outer_trench_swell" title="Outer trench swell">outer trench swell</a>, then descends to form the outer trench slope. The outer trench slope is typically disrupted by a set of sub-parallel normal <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faults</a> that 'staircase' the seafloor down to the trench. The plate boundary is defined by the trench axis itself. Beneath the inner trench wall, the two plates slide past each other along the subduction <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decollement" title="Decollement">decollement</a>, the seafloor intersection of which defines the trench location. The overriding plate typically contains a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_arc" title="Volcanic arc">volcanic arc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forearc" title="Forearc">forearc</a> region. The volcanic arc is caused by physical and chemical interactions between the subducted plate at depth and <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenospheric mantle</a> associated with the overriding plate. The forearc lies between the trench and the volcanic arc. Globally, forearcs have the lowest heatflow from the interior Earth because there is no <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> (convecting mantle) between the forearc lithosphere and the cold subducting plate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The inner trench wall marks the edge of the overriding plate and the outermost forearc. The forearc consists of <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> crust, and this crust may act as buttress to a growing <a href="/wiki/Accretionary_wedge" title="Accretionary wedge">accretionary wedge</a> (formed from sediments scraped off the top of the downgoing plate). If the flux of sediments is high, material transfers from the subducting plate to the overriding plate. In this case an accretionary prism grows and the location of the trench migrates progressively away from the volcanic arc over the life of the convergent margin. Convergent margins with growing accretionary prisms are called accretionary margins and make up nearly half of all convergent margins. If the incoming sediment flux is low, material is scraped from the overriding plate by the subducting plate in a process called subduction erosion. This material is then carried down into the subduction zone. In this case, the location of the trench migrates towards the magmatic arc over the life of the convergent margin. Convergent margins experiencing subduction erosion are called non-accretionary or erosive margins and comprise more than half of convergent plate boundaries. This is an oversimplification, because the same section of margin may experience both sediment accretion and subduction erosion throughout its active time span.</p>
<p>The asymmetric profile across a trench reflects fundamental differences in materials and tectonic evolution. The outer trench wall and outer swell comprise seafloor that takes several million years to move from where subduction-related deformation begins to sinking beneath the overriding plate. In contrast, the inner trench wall is deformed by plate interactions for the entire life of the convergent margin. The forearc is continuously subjected to subduction-related deformation and <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>. This protracted deformation and shaking ensures that the inner trench slope is controlled by the angle of repose of whatever material comprises it. Because the inner slope of non-accretionary trenches are composed of igneous and metamorphic rocks instead of deformed sediments, these trenches have steeper inner walls than accretionary trenches.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filled_trenches">Filled trenches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filled trenches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The composition of the inner trench slope and a first-order control on trench morphology is determined by <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> supply. Active accretionary prisms are common in trenches near <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> where <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> supply great volumes of sediment to the trench. These filled trenches may lack the <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetric expression</a> of a trench. The <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_subduction_zone" title="Cascadia subduction zone">Cascadia</a> margin of the northwest USA is a filled trench, the result of sedimentation by the rivers of the western United States and Canada.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> convergent margin demonstrates the importance of proximity to sediment sources for trench morphology. In the south, near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> River, there is no morphological trench and the forearc (including the accretionary prism) is almost 500 km (310 mi) wide. The large accretionary prism reaches above sea level to form the islands of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>. Northward, the forearc narrows, the accretionary prism disappears, and, north of ~17°N, the morphology of a trench dominates. Further north, far from major sediment sources, the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a> is over 8,600 m (28,200 ft) deep and there is no active accretionary prism.</p>
<p>A similar relationship between proximity to rivers, forearc width, and trench morphology can be observed from east to west along the <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaskan</a>-<a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Trench" title="Aleutian Trench">Aleutian</a> convergent margin. The convergent plate boundary offshore Alaska changes along its strike from a filled trench with broad forearc in the east (near the coastal rivers of Alaska) to a deep trench with narrow forearc in the west (offshore the Aleutian islands). Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Makran" title="Makran">Makran</a> convergent margin offshore Pakistan and Iran, which is a trench filled by sediments from the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a>-<a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus</a> rivers. Thick accumulations of <a href="/wiki/Turbidite" title="Turbidite">turbidites</a> along a trench can be supplied by down-axis transport of sediments that enter the trench 1,000–2,000 km (620–1,240 mi) away, as is found for the <a href="/wiki/Peru%E2%80%93Chile_Trench" title="Peru–Chile Trench">Peru–Chile Trench</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Valpara%C3%ADso" title="Valparaíso">Valparaíso</a> and for the Aleutian Trench.</p>
<p>Convergence rate can also be important for controlling trench depth—especially for trenches near continents—because slow convergence makes the convergent margin capacity insufficient to dispose of sediment. An evolution in trench morphology can be expected, as oceans close and continents converge. While the ocean is wide, the trench may be far from continental sources of sediment and so may be deep. As continents approach each other, the trench can fill with continental sediments and become shallower. A simple way to approximate when the transition from subduction to collision has occurred is when the plate boundary previously marked by a trench is filled enough to rise above sea level.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accretionary_prisms_and_sediment_transport">Accretionary prisms and sediment transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accretionary prisms and sediment transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subduction_accretionary2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Accretionary prisms grow in two ways: by frontal accretion, whereby sediments are scraped off the downgoing plate, <a href="/wiki/Bulldozer" title="Bulldozer">bulldozer</a>-fashion, near the trench, and by <a href="/wiki/Underplating" title="Underplating">underplating</a> of subducted sediments (and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>) along the shallow parts of the subduction decollement. Frontal accretion over the life of a convergent margin results in younger sediments defining the outermost part of the accretionary prism and the oldest sediments defining the innermost portion. Older (inner) parts of the accretionary prism are more lithified and have steeper structures than the younger (outer) parts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="the prism slope is usually steeper in the younger/outer portion (May 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Underplating is difficult to detect in modern subduction zones but may be recorded in ancient accretionary prisms such as the Franciscan Group of California in the form of tectonic mélanges and duplex structures.</p>
<p>Different modes of accretion are reflected in the morphology of the inner slope of the trench, which generally shows three morphological provinces. The lower slope comprises imbricate thrust slices that form ridges. The mid slope may comprise a bench or terraces. The upper slope is smoother but may be cut by <a href="/wiki/Submarine_canyon" title="Submarine canyon">submarine canyons</a>. Because accretionary convergent margins have high relief, are continuously deforming, and accommodate a large flux of sediments, they are vigorous systems of sediment dispersal and accumulation. <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport" title="Sediment transport">Sediment transport</a> is controlled by submarine <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>, debris flows, <a href="/wiki/Turbidity_current" title="Turbidity current">turbidity currents</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Contourite" title="Contourite">contourites</a>. Submarine canyons transport sediment from <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a> and rivers down the upper slope. These canyons form by channelized turbidites and generally lose definition with depth because continuous faulting disrupts the submarine channels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="observation is easily verified, but reason needs credible source (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sediments move down the inner trench wall via channels and a series of fault-controlled basins. The trench itself serves as an axis of sediment transport. If enough sediment moves to the trench, it may be completely filled so that turbidity currents are able to carry sediments well beyond the trench and may even surmount the outer swell, as in the eastern Gulf of Alaska. Sediments from the rivers of North America spill over the filled Cascadia trench and cross the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_de_Fuca_plate" title="Juan de Fuca plate">Juan de Fuca plate</a> to reach the spreading ridge several hundred kilometres to the west.</p>
<p>The slope of the inner trench slope of an accretionary convergent margin reflects continuous adjustments to the thickness and width of the accretionary prism. The prism maintains a ‘<a href="/wiki/Critical_taper" title="Critical taper">critical taper</a>’, established in conformance with <a href="/wiki/Mohr%E2%80%93Coulomb_theory" title="Mohr–Coulomb theory">Mohr–Coulomb theory</a>, with slope determined by the material properties of the sediments. A package of sediments scraped off the downgoing lithospheric plate deforms until it and the accretionary prism that it has been added to attain the maximum slope supported by the sediments. Once this critical taper is attained, the wedge slides stably along its basal <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9collement" title="Décollement">décollement</a>. Strain rate and hydrologic properties also influence the strength of the accretionary prism and the angle of critical taper. Fluid pore pressures modify rock strength. Low permeability and rapid convergence may result in pore pressures that exceed lithostatic pressure and thus a relatively weak accretionary prism with a shallowly tapered geometry, whereas high permeability and slow convergence result in lower pore pressure, stronger prisms, and steeper geometry.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_Trench" title="Hellenic Trench">Hellenic Trench</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_arc" title="Hellenic arc">Hellenic arc</a> system is unusual because this convergent margin subducts <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporites</a>. The slope of the surface of the southern flank of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Ridge" title="Mediterranean Ridge">Mediterranean Ridge</a> (its accretionary prism) is low, about 1°, indicating very low shear stress on the decollement at the base of the wedge. Evaporites control the shallow taper of the accretionary complex both because their mechanical properties differ from those of siliciclastic sediments, and because of their effect on fluid flow and fluid pressure, which in turn control <a href="/wiki/Effective_stress" title="Effective stress">effective stress</a>. In the 1970s, the linear deeps of the Hellenic trench south of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> were thought to be similar to trenches at other subduction zones. However, with the realization that the Mediterranean Ridge is an accretionary complex, it became apparent that the Hellenic trench is actually a starved forearc basin, and that the plate boundary lies south of the Mediterranean Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cita_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cita-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Trenches distant from an influx of continental sediments lack an accretionary prism, and the inner slope of such trenches is commonly composed of igneous or metamorphic rocks. Non-accretionary convergent margins are characteristic of (but not limited to) primitive arc systems. Primitive arc systems are those built on oceanic lithosphere, such as the Izu-Bonin-Mariana, Tonga-Kermadec, and Scotia (South Sandwich) arc systems. The inner trench slope of these convergent margins exposes the crust of the forearc, including basalt, gabbro, and serpentinized mantle peridotite. These exposures allow easy access to study the lower oceanic crust and upper mantle in place and provide a unique opportunity to study the magmatic products associated with the initiation of subduction zones. Most ophiolites probably originate in a forearc environment during the initiation of subduction, and this setting favors ophiolite emplacement during collision with blocks of thickened crust. Not all non-accretionary convergent margins are associated with primitive arcs. Trenches adjacent to continents where there is little influx of sediments carried by rivers, such as the central part of the Peru–Chile Trench, may also lack an accretionary prism.</p>
<p>Igneous basement of a nonaccretionary forearc may be continuously exposed by subduction erosion. This transfers material from the forearc to the subducting plate and can be accomplished by frontal erosion or basal erosion. Frontal erosion is most active in the wake of seamounts being subducted beneath the forearc. Subduction of large edifices (seamount tunneling) oversteepens the forearc, causing mass failures that carry debris towards and ultimately into the trench. This debris may be deposited in graben of the downgoing plate and subducted with it. In contrast, structures resulting from subduction erosion of the base of the forearc are difficult to recognize from seismic reflection profiles, so the possibility of basal erosion is difficult to confirm. Subduction erosion may also diminish a once-robust accretionary prism if the flux of sediments to the trench diminishes.</p>
<p>Nonaccretionary forearcs may also be the site of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine</a> <a href="/wiki/Mud_volcano" title="Mud volcano">mud volcanoes</a>. These form where fluids released from the downgoing plate percolate upwards and interact with cold mantle lithosphere of the forearc. Mantle <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> is hydrated into <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a>, which is much less dense than peridotite and so rises diapirically when it can. Some nonaccretionary forearcs are subjected to strong extensional stresses, for example the Marianas, and this allows buoyant serpentinite to rise to the seafloor where they form serpentinite mud volcanoes. Chemosynthetic communities are also found on non-accretionary margins such as the Marianas, where they thrive on vents associated with serpentinite mud volcanoes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trench_rollback">Trench rollback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Trench rollback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trenches seem positionally stable over time, but scientists believe that some trenches—particularly those associated with subduction zones where two oceanic plates converge—move backward into the subducting plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This is called <b>trench rollback</b> or <b>hinge retreat</b> (also <b>hinge rollback</b>) and is one explanation for the existence of <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">back-arc basins</a>.</p>
<p>Slab rollback occurs during the <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> of two tectonic plates, and results in seaward motion of the trench. Forces perpendicular to the slab at depth (the portion of the subducting plate within the mantle) are responsible for steepening of the slab in the mantle and ultimately the movement of the hinge and trench at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schellart_2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schellart_2013-6">[6]</a></sup> The driving force for rollback is the negative buoyancy of the slab with respect to the underlying mantle <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schellart_2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schellart_2006-7">[7]</a></sup> modified by the geometry of the slab itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakakuki_2013_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nakakuki_2013-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back-arc_basins" title="Back-arc basins">Back-arc basins</a> are often associated with slab rollback due to extension in the overriding plate as a response to the subsequent subhorizontal mantle flow from the displacement of the slab at depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flower_2003_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flower_2003-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes_involved">Processes involved</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Processes involved">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several forces are involved in the process of slab rollback. Two forces acting against each other at the interface of the two subducting plates exert forces against one another. The subducting plate exerts a bending force (FPB) that supplies pressure during subduction, while the overriding plate exerts a force against the subducting plate (FTS). The slab pull force (FSP) is caused by the negative buoyancy of the plate driving the plate to greater depths. The resisisting force from the surrounding mantle opposes the slab pull forces. Interactions with the 660-km discontinuity cause a deflection due to the buoyancy at the phase transition (F660).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakakuki_2013_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nakakuki_2013-8">[8]</a></sup> The unique interplay of these forces is what generates slab rollback. When the deep slab section obstructs the down-going motion of the shallow slab section, slab rollback occurs. The subducting slab undergoes backward sinking due to the negative buoyancy forces causing a retrogradation of the trench hinge along the surface. Upwelling of the mantle around the slab can create favorable conditions for the formation of a back-arc basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flower_2003_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flower_2003-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seismic_tomography" title="Seismic tomography">Seismic tomography</a> provides evidence for slab rollback. Results demonstrate high temperature anomalies within the mantle suggesting subducted material is present in the mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Ophiolites are viewed as evidence for such mechanisms as high pressure and temperature rocks are rapidly brought to the surface through the processes of slab rollback, which provides space for the exhumation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophiolites" title="Ophiolites">ophiolites</a>.</p>
<p>Slab rollback is not always a continuous process suggesting an episodic nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schellart_2006_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schellart_2006-7">[7]</a></sup> The episodic nature of the rollback is explained by a change in the density of the subducting plate, such as the arrival of buoyant lithosphere (a continent, arc, ridge, or plateau), a change in the subduction dynamics, or a change in the plate kinematics. The age of the subducting plates does not have any effect on slab rollback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakakuki_2013_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nakakuki_2013-8">[8]</a></sup> Nearby continental collisions have an effect on slab rollback. Continental collisions induce mantle flow and extrusion of mantle material, which causes stretching and arc-trench rollback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flower_2003_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Flower_2003-9">[9]</a></sup> In the area of the Southeast Pacific, there have been several rollback events resulting in the formation of numerous back-arc basins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schellart_2006_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schellart_2006-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mantle_interactions">Mantle interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mantle interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interactions with the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> discontinuities play a significant role in slab rollback. Stagnation at the 660-km discontinuity causes retrograde slab motion due to the suction forces acting at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakakuki_2013_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nakakuki_2013-8">[8]</a></sup> Slab rollback induces mantle return flow, which causes extension from the shear stresses at the base of the overriding plate. As slab rollback velocities increase, circular mantle flow velocities also increase, accelerating extension rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schellart_2013_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schellart_2013-6">[6]</a></sup> Extension rates are altered when the slab interacts with the discontinuities within the mantle at 410 km and 660 km depth. Slabs can either penetrate directly into the lower mantle, or can be retarded due to the phase transition at 660 km depth creating a difference in buoyancy. An increase in retrograde trench migration (slab rollback) (2–4 cm/yr) is a result of flattened slabs at the 660-km discontinuity where the slab does not penetrate into the lower mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen_1996_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen_1996-11">[11]</a></sup> This is the case for the Japan, Java and Izu-Bonin trenches. These flattened slabs are only temporarily arrested in the transition zone. The subsequent displacement into the lower mantle is caused by slab pull forces, or the destabilization of the slab from warming and broadening due to thermal diffusion. Slabs that penetrate directly into the lower mantle result in slower slab rollback rates (~1–3 cm/yr) such as the Mariana arc, Tonga arcs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen_1996_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen_1996-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_and_biosphere">Water and biosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Water and biosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The volume of water escaping from within and beneath the <a href="/wiki/Forearc" title="Forearc">forearc</a> results in some of Earth’s most dynamic and complex interactions between aqueous fluids and rocks. Most of this water is trapped in pores and fractures in the upper lithosphere and sediments of the subducting plate. The average forearc is underrun by a solid volume of oceanic sediment that is 400 m (1,300 ft) thick. This sediment enters the trench with 50–60% <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a>. These sediments are progressively squeezed as they are subducted, reducing void space and forcing fluids out along the decollement and up into the overlying forearc, which may or may not have an accretionary prism. Sediments accreted to the forearc are another source of fluids. Water is also bound in hydrous minerals, especially <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opal" title="Opal">opal</a>. Increasing pressure and temperature experienced by subducted materials converts the hydrous minerals to denser phases that contain progressively less structurally bound water. Water released by dehydration accompanying phase transitions is another source of fluids introduced to the base of the overriding plate. These fluids may travel through the accretionary prism diffusely, via interconnected pore spaces in sediments, or may follow discrete channels along faults. Sites of venting may take the form of mud volcanoes or seeps and are often associated with chemosynthetic communities. Fluids escaping from the shallowest parts of a subduction zone may also escape along the plate boundary but have rarely been observed draining along the trench axis. All of these fluids are dominated by water but also contain dissolved ions and organic molecules, especially <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. Methane is often sequestered in an ice-like form (<a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a>, also called gas hydrate) in the forearc. These are a potential energy source and can rapidly break down. Destabilization of gas hydrates has contributed to global warming in the past and will likely do so in the future.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">Chemosynthetic</a> communities thrive where cold fluids seep out of the forearc. Cold seep communities have been discovered in inner trench slopes down to depths of 7000 m in the western Pacific, especially around Japan, in the Eastern Pacific along North, Central and South America coasts from the Aleutian to the Peru–Chile trenches, on the Barbados prism, in the Mediterranean, and in the Indian Ocean along the Makran and Sunda convergent margins. These communities receive much less attention than the chemosynthetic communities associated with <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a>. Chemosynthetic communities are located in a variety of geological settings: above over-pressured sediments in accretionary prisms where fluids are expelled through mud volcanoes or ridges (Barbados, Nankai and Cascadia); along active erosive margins with faults; and along escarpments caused by debris slides (Japan trench, Peruvian margin). Surface seeps may be linked to massive hydrate deposits and destabilization (e.g. Cascadia margin). High concentrations of methane and <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> in the fluids escaping from the seafloor are the principal energy sources for chemosynthesis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_affecting_trench_depth">Factors affecting trench depth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oceanic_trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Factors affecting trench depth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlantic-trench.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1076" data-file-width="1500" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Atlantic-trench.JPG/310px-Atlantic-trench.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Atlantic-trench.JPG/465px-Atlantic-trench.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Atlantic-trench.JPG/620px-Atlantic-trench.JPG 2x" width="310"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlantic-trench.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are several factors that control the depth of trenches. The most important control is the supply of sediment, which fills the trench so that there is no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathymetric" title="Bathymetric">bathymetric</a> expression. It is therefore not surprising that the deepest trenches (deeper than 8,000 m (26,000 ft)) are all nonaccretionary. In contrast, all trenches with growing accretionary prisms are shallower than 8,000 m (26,000 ft). A second order control on trench depth is the age of the lithosphere at the time of subduction. Because <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic lithosphere</a> cools and thickens as it ages, it subsides. The older the seafloor, the deeper it lies, and this determines the minimum depth from which the seafloor begins to descend. This obvious correlation can be removed by looking at the relative depth, the difference between regional seafloor depth and maximum trench depth. Relative depth may be controlled by the age of the lithosphere at the trench, the convergence rate, and the dip of the subducted slab at intermediate depths. Finally, narrow slabs can sink and roll back more rapidly than broad plates, because it is easier for underlying <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> to flow around the edges of the sinking plate. Such slabs may have steep dips at relatively shallow depths and so may be associated with unusually deep trenches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>.</p>
