<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-Admiral" title="Vice-Admiral">Vice-Admiral</a> <b>Robert FitzRoy</b> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">RN</a> (5 July 1805 – 30 April 1865) was an English officer of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and a scientist. He achieved lasting fame as the captain of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS <i>Beagle</i></a> during <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>'s famous voyage, FitzRoy's second expedition to <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Cone" title="Southern Cone">Southern Cone</a>.</p>
<p>FitzRoy was a pioneering <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorologist</a> who made accurate <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">daily weather predictions</a>, which he called by a new name of his own invention: "forecasts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1854 he established what would later be called the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>, and created systems to get weather information to sailors and fishermen for their safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup> He was an able surveyor and <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">hydrographer</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_New_Zealand" title="Governor-General of New Zealand">Governor</a> of New Zealand, serving from 1843 to 1845, he tried to protect the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Maori</a> from illegal land sales claimed by British settlers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robert FitzRoy was born at <a href="/wiki/Ampton_Hall" title="Ampton Hall">Ampton Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ampton" title="Ampton">Ampton</a>, Suffolk, England, into the upper echelons of the British <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a> and a tradition of public service. Through his father, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Charles_FitzRoy_(British_Army_officer)" title="Lord Charles FitzRoy (British Army officer)">General Lord Charles FitzRoy</a>, Robert was a fourth great-grandson of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a>; his paternal grandfather was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Henry_FitzRoy,_3rd_Duke_of_Grafton" title="Augustus Henry FitzRoy, 3rd Duke of Grafton">Augustus Henry FitzRoy, 3rd Duke of Grafton</a>. His mother, Lady Frances Stewart, was the daughter of the first <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stewart,_1st_Marquess_of_Londonderry" title="Robert Stewart, 1st Marquess of Londonderry">Marquess of Londonderry</a> and the half-sister of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stewart,_Viscount_Castlereagh" title="Robert Stewart, Viscount Castlereagh">Viscount Castlereagh</a>, who became <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Foreign Secretary</a>. From the age of four, Robert FitzRoy lived with his family at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wakefield_Lodge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wakefield Lodge (page does not exist)">Wakefield Lodge</a>, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladian" title="Palladian">Palladian</a> mansion in Northamptonshire.</p>
<p>Robert's half-brother <a href="/wiki/Charles_Augustus_FitzRoy" title="Charles Augustus FitzRoy">Sir Charles FitzRoy</a> later served as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_South_Wales" title="Governor of New South Wales">Governor of New South Wales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Prince_Edward_Island" title="Governor of Prince Edward Island">Governor of Prince Edward Island</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Antigua" title="Governor of Antigua">Governor of Antigua</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 1818 at the age of 12, FitzRoy entered the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Academy" title="Royal Naval Academy">Royal Naval College, Portsmouth</a>, and in the following year he entered the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. At the age of 14, he embarked as a voluntary student aboard the <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Owen_Glendower_(1808)" title="HMS Owen Glendower (1808)">HMS <i>Owen Glendower</i></a>, which sailed to South America in the middle of 1820, and returned in January 1822. He was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Midshipman" title="Midshipman">midshipman</a> while on the vessel. FitzRoy served on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Hind_(1814)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HMS Hind (1814) (page does not exist)">HMS <i>Hind</i></a> as a midshipman.</p>
<p>He completed his course with distinction and was promoted lieutenant on 7 September 1824, having passed the examination with 'full numbers' (100%), the first to achieve this result. After serving on <a href="/wiki/HMS_Thetis_(1817)" title="HMS Thetis (1817)">HMS <i>Thetis</i></a>, in 1828 he was appointed flag lieutenant to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear-Admiral" title="Rear-Admiral">Rear-Admiral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Waller_Otway" title="Robert Waller Otway">Sir Robert Waller Otway</a>, commander-in-chief of the South American station, aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ganges_(1821)" title="HMS Ganges (1821)">HMS <i>Ganges</i></a>.</p>
<p>At that time <i>Beagle</i>, under Captain <a href="/wiki/Pringle_Stokes" title="Pringle Stokes">Pringle Stokes</a>, was carrying out a hydrographic survey of <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, under the overall command of Captain <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Parker_King" title="Phillip Parker King">Phillip Parker King</a> in <a href="/wiki/HMS_Aid_(1809)" title="HMS Aid (1809)">HMS <i>Adventure</i></a>. Pringle Stokes became severely depressed and fatally shot himself. Under Lieutenant Skyring, the ship sailed to <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, where Otway appointed FitzRoy as (temporary) captain of the <i>Beagle</i> on 15 December 1828. By the ship's return to England on 14 October 1830, FitzRoy had established his reputation as a surveyor and commander.</p>
<p>During the survey, some of his men were camping onshore when a group of Fuegian natives made off with their boat. His ship gave chase and, after a scuffle, the culprits' families were brought on board as hostages. Eventually FitzRoy held two boys, a girl and two men (one man escaped.) As it was not possible to put them ashore conveniently, he decided to "civilise the savages", teaching them "English ... the plainer truths of Christianity ... and the use of common tools" before returning them as missionaries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The sailors gave them names: the girl was called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuegia_Basket" title="Fuegia Basket">Fuegia Basket</a> (so named because the replacement for the stolen boat was an improvised <a href="/wiki/Coracle" title="Coracle">coracle</a> that resembled a basket), the boy <a href="/wiki/Jemmy_Button" title="Jemmy Button">Jemmy Button</a> (FitzRoy purchased him with buttons), and the man he named York Minster (after the large rock near which he was captured). The second boy was called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boat_Memory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boat Memory (page does not exist)">Boat Memory</a>. FitzRoy brought the four back with the ship to England. Boat Memory died following a <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> vaccination. The others were cared for and taught by the trainee missionary Richard Matthews; they were considered civilised enough to be presented at Court to King William IV and Queen Adelaide in the summer of 1831.</p>
<h3><span id="HMS_Beagle's_second_voyage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="HMS_Beagle.27s_second_voyage">HMS <i>Beagle</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s second voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: HMS Beagle's second voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle" title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle">Second voyage of HMS Beagle</a></div>
<p>In early May 1831 FitzRoy stood as <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a> candidate for <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a> in the General Election, but was defeated. His hopes of obtaining a new posting and organising a missionary project to Tierra del Fuego appeared to be failing. He was arranging for the charter of a ship at his own expense to return the Fuegians with Matthews when his friend <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrographer_of_the_Navy" title="Hydrographer of the Navy">Hydrographer</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Admiralty" title="British Admiralty">British Admiralty</a>, and his "kind uncle", the <a href="/wiki/George_FitzRoy,_4th_Duke_of_Grafton" title="George FitzRoy, 4th Duke of Grafton">Duke of Grafton</a>, interceded on his behalf at the Admiralty. On 25 June 1831 FitzRoy was re-appointed commander of the <i>Beagle</i>. He spared no expense in fitting out the ship.</p>
<p>He was conscious of the stressful loneliness of command. He knew of the suicides both of Captain Stokes and of his uncle Viscount Castlereagh, who had cut his own throat in 1822 while in government office. FitzRoy talked to Beaufort in August 1831, asking him to find a suitable gentleman companion for the voyage. Such a companion should share his scientific tastes, make good use of the expedition's opportunities for researching natural history, dine with him as an equal, and provide a semblance of normal human friendship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-browne_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-browne-2">[2]</a></sup> While those Beaufort first approached (including Professor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._S._Henslow" title="J. S. Henslow">J. S. Henslow</a> of the University of Cambridge) turned the opportunity down, FitzRoy eventually approved <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> for the position. Before they left England, FitzRoy gave Darwin a copy of the first volume of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">Charles Lyell</a>'s <i>Principles of Geology</i>, a book the captain had read that explained terrestrial features as the outcome of a gradual process taking place over extremely long periods. FitzRoy took a request from Lyell to record observations on geological features, such as erratic boulders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-intro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>FitzRoy and Darwin got on well together, but there were inevitable strains during the five-year survey voyage. The captain had a violent temper, his outbursts had gained him the nickname "Hot Coffee",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dm_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dm-4">[4]</a></sup> which resulted in quarrels sometimes "bordering on insanity", as Darwin later recalled. On a memorable occasion in March 1832 at <a href="/wiki/Bahia" title="Bahia">Bahia</a>, Brazil, Darwin was horrified at tales of the treatment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaves" title="Slaves">slaves</a>. FitzRoy, while not endorsing brutality, recounted how an <i>estancia</i> owner once asked his slaves if they wished to be free and was told they did not. Darwin asked FitzRoy if he thought slaves could answer such a question honestly when it was posed by their master, at which the captain lost his temper and, before storming out, told Darwin that if he doubted his word they could no longer live together; effectively he banished Darwin from his table. Before nightfall FitzRoy's temper cooled and he sent an apology, with the request that Darwin "continue to live with him." They avoided the subject of slavery from that time on. None of their quarrels were over religious or doctrinal issues; such disagreements came after the voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-browne_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-browne-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the island of "Buttons Land" in <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> they set up a mission post, but when they returned nine days later, the possessions had been looted. Matthews gave up, rejoining the ship. He left the three "westernised" Fuegians to continue the missionary work.</p>
<p>While in the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, FitzRoy bought a <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a> out of his own funds to assist with the surveying tasks he had been asked to complete. He had it refitted and renamed <i>Adventure</i>, hoping that the cost would be reimbursed by the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a>. They returned to the mission post but found only <a href="/wiki/Jemmy_Button" title="Jemmy Button">Jemmy Button</a>. He had returned to native ways and refused the offer to go with them back to England.</p>
<p>At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valparaiso" title="Valparaiso">Valparaiso</a> in 1834, while Charles Darwin was away from the ship exploring the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, the Admiralty reprimanded FitzRoy for buying the <i>Adventure</i>. He took the criticism badly, selling the schooner and announcing they would go back to recheck his survey, then resigning his command with doubts about his sanity. The ship's officers persuaded him to withdraw his resignation and continue as planned once Darwin returned to the ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dm_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-dm-4">[4]</a></sup> FitzRoy continued his voyage, sailing on to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galapagos" title="Galapagos">Galapagos</a>, Tahiti, New Zealand, Australia and South Africa. He detoured to <a href="/wiki/Bahia" title="Bahia">Bahia</a> in Brazil on the return voyage so that he could carry out an additional check, to ensure the accuracy of his longitude measurements before returning to England.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_from_the_voyage">Return from the voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Return from the voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after the <i>Beagle'</i>s return on 2 October 1836, FitzRoy married Mary Henrietta O'Brien, a young woman to whom he had long been engaged. Darwin was amazed, as not once during the entire five years of the trip had FitzRoy spoken about being engaged.</p>
<p>FitzRoy was awarded a gold medal by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Geographical_Society" title="Royal Geographical Society">Royal Geographical Society</a> in 1837. Extracts from his diary read to the society on 8 May 1837 included the observation:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Is it not extraordinary, that sea-worn, <i>rolled</i>, shingle-stones, and alluvial accumulations, compose the greater portion of these plains? How vast, and of what immense <i>duration</i>, must have been the actions of these waters which smoothed the shingle-stones now buried in the deserts of Patagonia!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>FitzRoy wrote his account of the voyage, including editing the notes of the previous captain of the <i>Beagle.</i> It was published in May 1839 as the <i>Narrative of the Surveying Voyages of H.M.S. Adventure and Beagle,</i> in four volumes, including Darwin's <i>Journal and Remarks, 1832—1836</i> as the third volume. FitzRoy's account includes a section of <i>Remarks with reference to the Deluge</i> in which he admits that, having read works "by geologists who contradict, by implication, if not in plain terms, the authenticity of the Scriptures" and "while led away by sceptical ideas," he had remarked to a friend that the vast plain of sedimentary material they were crossing "could never have been effected by a forty days' flood." He wrote that in his "turn of mind and ignorance of scripture," he was willing to disbelieve the Biblical account. Concerned that such ideas might "reach the eyes of young sailors," he explains in detail his renewed commitment to a literal reading of the Bible, with arguments that rock layers high in the mountains containing sea shells are proof of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Flood" title="Noah's Flood">Noah's Flood</a> and that the six days of creation could not have extended over aeons because the grass, herbs and trees would have died out during the long nights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deluge_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-deluge-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD_diary_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CD_diary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>R.D. Keynes, in his introduction to the 2001 edition of Darwin's diary, suggests that FitzRoy had undergone a religious conversion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD_diary_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-CD_diary-7">[7]</a></sup> He was dissociating himself from the new ideas of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">Charles Lyell</a>, which he had accepted during the voyage, and from Darwin's account which embraced these ideas. Under the influence of his very religious wife, he asserted a new commitment to the doctrine of the established <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dm_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-dm-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1841 FitzRoy was elected the Tory Member of Parliament for <a href="/wiki/City_of_Durham_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="City of Durham (UK Parliament constituency)">Durham</a>. He was appointed <a href="/wiki/Acting_Conservator_of_the_River_Mersey" title="Acting Conservator of the River Mersey">Acting Conservator of the River Mersey</a> in 1842.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor_of_New_Zealand">Governor of New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Governor of New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first Governor of New Zealand, Captain <a href="/wiki/William_Hobson" title="William Hobson">William Hobson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">R.N.</a>, died in late 1842. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_Missionary_Society" title="Church Missionary Society">Church Missionary Society</a>, which had a strong New Zealand presence, suggested FitzRoy as his successor and he was appointed by the government. He took up his new task in December 1843. On the journey to New Zealand, he met <a href="/wiki/William_John_Warburton_Hamilton" title="William John Warburton Hamilton">William John Warburton Hamilton</a> and made him his private secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScholefield1940349_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScholefield1940349-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>His instructions were to maintain order and protect the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a>, while satisfying the land hunger of the settlers pouring into the country. He was given very few military resources. Government revenue, mainly from customs duties, was woefully inadequate for his responsibilities.</p>
<p>One of his first tasks was to enquire into the circumstances surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Wairau_Affray" title="Wairau Affray">Wairau Affray</a>, in which there had been violent conflict between settlers and the Maori. He found the actions of the colonists to have been illegal and declined to take any action against <a href="/wiki/Te_Rauparaha" title="Te Rauparaha">Te Rauparaha</a>. He did not have the troops to meet him on anything like equal terms. But the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Company" title="New Zealand Company">New Zealand Company</a> and the settlers felt betrayed and angry. He appointed a Government Superintendent for the area, to establish a ruling presence. Fitzroy also insisted that the New Zealand Company pay the Māori a realistic price for the land they claimed to have purchased. These moves made him very unpopular.</p>
<p>Land sales were a continuing vexatious issue. The settlers were eager to buy land and some Māori were willing to sell, but under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi" title="Treaty of Waitangi">Treaty of Waitangi</a>, land sales required the Government as an intermediary, and were thus extremely slow. FitzRoy changed the rules to allow settlers to purchase Māori land directly, subject to a duty of ten shillings per acre. But land sales proved slower than expected.</p>
<p>To meet the financial shortfall, FitzRoy raised the customs duties, then replaced them with <a href="/wiki/Property_tax" title="Property tax">property</a> and <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income taxes</a>. All these expedients failed. Before long the Colony was faced with bankruptcy, and FitzRoy was forced to begin issuing <a href="/wiki/Promissory_note" title="Promissory note">promissory notes</a>, paper money without backing.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, the Māori in the far North, around the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Islands" title="Bay of Islands">Bay of Islands</a>, who had been the first to sign the Treaty of Waitangi, were feeling increasingly sidelined and resentful of the changes that had taken place in New Zealand. To signal their resentment, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hone_Heke" title="Hone Heke">Hone Heke</a> cut down the flagpole at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kororareka" title="Kororareka">Kororareka</a>. Rather than address the problems, FitzRoy had the flagpole re-erected. Hone Heke cut it down again, four times altogether; by the fourth occasion the First New Zealand War, sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff_War" title="Flagstaff War">Flagstaff War</a> or the Northern War, was well under way.</p>
<p>FitzRoy quickly realized that he did not have the resources to bring about a quick end to the war. Meanwhile, the spokesmen for the New Zealand Company were active back in the United Kingdom, lobbying against FitzRoy's Governorship, which they presented to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> in a very poor light. As a result, he was shortly afterwards recalled and replaced by <a href="/wiki/George_Grey" title="George Grey">George Grey</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_South_Australia" title="Governor of South Australia">Governor of South Australia</a>. Grey was given the backing and financial support that FitzRoy had needed but was denied.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_meteorology">Later life and meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life and meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FitzRoy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1076" data-file-width="647" height="366" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/FitzRoy.jpg/220px-FitzRoy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/FitzRoy.jpg/330px-FitzRoy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/FitzRoy.jpg/440px-FitzRoy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FitzRoy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
FitzRoy circa 1855</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>FitzRoy was not disgraced. After his return to Britain, in September 1848, he was made superintendent of the Royal Naval Dockyards at <a href="/wiki/Woolwich" title="Woolwich">Woolwich</a>. In March 1849 he was given his final sea command, the screw frigate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Arrogant_(1848)" title="HMS Arrogant (1848)">HMS <i>Arrogant</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1850, FitzRoy retired from active service, partly due to ill health. The following year, in 1851, he was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> with the support of 13 fellows, including Charles Darwin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the protégé of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a>, in 1854, FitzRoy was appointed, on the recommendation of the President of the Royal Society, as chief of a new department to deal with the collection of weather data at sea. His title was <i>Meteorological Statist to the Board of Trade</i>, and he had a staff of three. This was the forerunner of the modern <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Meteorological Office</a>. He arranged for captains of ships to provide information, with tested instruments being loaned for this purpose, and for computation of the data collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>FitzRoy soon began to work on strategies to make weather information more widely available for the safety of shipping and fishermen. He directed the design and distribution of a type of <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a> which, on his recommendation, was fixed at every port to be available to crews for consultation before setting out to sea. Stone housings for such barometers are still visible at many fishing harbours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The invention of several different types of <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometers</a> was attributed to him. These became popular and continued in production into the 20th century, characteristically engraved with <i>Admiral FitzRoy's special remarks</i> on interpretation, such as: "When rising: In winter the rise of the barometer presages frost."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_(ship).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1000" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/220px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/330px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/440px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_(ship).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Royal Charter</i> sinking in 1859 storm.</div>
</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_Storm" title="Royal Charter Storm">A terrible storm in 1859</a>, which caused the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_(ship)" title="Royal Charter (ship)"><i>Royal Charter</i></a>, inspired FitzRoy to develop charts to allow predictions to be made, which he called "forecasting the weather", thus coining the term "weather forecast".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968-11">[11]</a></sup> Fifteen land stations were established to use the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> to transmit to him daily reports of weather at set times. The first daily weather forecasts were published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> in 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup> The 1859 storm resulted in the Crown distributing <a href="/wiki/Storm_glass" title="Storm glass">storm glasses</a>, then known as "FitzRoy's storm barometers", to many small fishing communities around the British Isles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1860, the following year, FitzRoy introduced a system of hoisting storm warning cones at the principal ports when a gale was expected. He ordered fleets to stay in port under these conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kington_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kington-15">[15]</a></sup> The <i>Weather Book,</i> which he published in 1863, was far in advance of the scientific opinion of the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> once sent messengers to FitzRoy’s home requesting a weather forecast for a crossing she was about to make to the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many fishing fleet owners objected to the posting of gale warnings, which required that fleets not leave the ports. Under this pressure, FitzRoy's system was abandoned for a short time after his death. The fishing fleet owners reckoned without the pressure of the fishermen, for whom FitzRoy had been a hero, responsible for saving many lives. In 1874, the system was resumed in a simplified form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kington_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kington-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>FitzRoy debunked Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Martin_Saxby" title="Stephen Martin Saxby">Stephen Martin Saxby</a>'s lunar weather forecasting method as pseudoscience. Saxby tried to counter FitzRoy's arguments in the second edition of his book <i>Saxby Weather System</i> (1864).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Origin_of_Species"><i>The Origin of Species</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Origin of Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/1860_Oxford_evolution_debate" title="1860 Oxford evolution debate">1860 Oxford evolution debate</a></div>
<p>When <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Origin_of_Species" title="The Origin of Species">The Origin of Species</a></i> was published FitzRoy was dismayed and apparently felt guilty for his part in the theory's development. He was in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> on 30 June 1860 to present a paper on storms and attended the meeting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association for the Advancement of Science</a> at which <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Wilberforce" title="Samuel Wilberforce">Samuel Wilberforce</a> attacked Darwin's theory. During the debate FitzRoy, seen as "a grey haired Roman nosed elderly gentleman", stood in the centre of the audience and "lifting an immense Bible first with both and afterwards with one hand over his head, solemnly implored the audience to believe God rather than man". As he admitted that <i>The Origin of Species</i> had given him "acutest pain", the crowd shouted him down.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robert FitzRoy was married twice. In 1836 he married Mary Henrietta O'Brien. They had four children: Emily-Unah, Fanny, Katherine and <a href="/wiki/Robert_O%27Brien_FitzRoy" title="Robert O'Brien FitzRoy">Robert O'Brien</a>. In 1854, after the death of his first wife, he married Maria Isabella Smyth in London. They had one daughter, Laura Elizabeth.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Robert_FitzRoy%27s_Grave.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>In 1863 FitzRoy was promoted to Vice-Admiral due to seniority, but in the coming years internal and external troubles at the Meteorological Office, financial concerns as well as failing health, and his struggle with <a href="/wiki/Mood_disorder" title="Mood disorder">depression</a> took their toll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 30 April 1865, Vice-Admiral FitzRoy committed suicide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> by cutting his throat with a razor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> FitzRoy died having exhausted his entire fortune (£6,000, the equivalent of £400,000 today) on public expenditure.</p>
<p>When this came to light, in order to prevent his wife and daughter living in destitution, his friend and colleague <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Sulivan" title="Bartholomew Sulivan">Bartholomew Sulivan</a> began an Admiral FitzRoy Testimonial Fund, which succeeded in getting the government to pay back £3,000 of this sum<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> contributed £100<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letter_no._4908_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Letter_no._4908-20">[20]</a></sup>).<a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> gave the special favour of allowing his widow and daughter the use of <a href="/wiki/Grace_and_favour" title="Grace and favour">grace and favour</a> apartments at <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Court_Palace" title="Hampton Court Palace">Hampton Court Palace</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>FitzRoy is buried in the front churchyard of All Saints' Church in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Norwood" title="Upper Norwood">Upper Norwood</a>, south London. His memorial was restored by the Meteorological Office in 1981.</p>
<p>FitzRoy's book <i>Sailing Directions for South America</i> led Chilean hydrographer <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Hudson" title="Francisco Hudson">Francisco Hudson</a> to investigate the possible existence of a sailing route through internal waters from the <a href="/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Archipelago" title="Chiloé Archipelago">Chiloé Archipelago</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Magellan" title="Straits of Magellan">Straits of Magellan</a>. Hudson was the first to realise that the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Ofqui" title="Isthmus of Ofqui">Isthmus of Ofqui</a> made this route impossible.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A memorial to FitzRoy is erected atop a metamorphic outcrop beside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahia_Wulaia" title="Bahia Wulaia">Bahia Wulaia</a> dome <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middens" title="Middens">middens</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_Navarino" title="Isla Navarino">Isla Navarino</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> part of <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> archipelago, South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It was presented in his bicentenary (2005) and commemorates his 23 January 1833 landing on Wulaia Cove. Another memorial, presented also in FitzRoy's bicentenary, commemorates his <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a> landing on 19 April 1830.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Fitz_Roy" title="Mount Fitz Roy">Mount Fitz Roy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>–<a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, at the extreme south of the continent), was named after him by the Argentine scientist and explorer <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Moreno" title="Francisco Moreno">Francisco Moreno</a>. It is 3,440 m (11,286 ft) high. The aboriginals had not named it, and used the word <i>chaltén</i> (meaning smoking mountain) for this and other peaks. <a href="/wiki/Fitzroy_River_(Western_Australia)" title="Fitzroy River (Western Australia)">Fitzroy River</a>, in northern Western Australia, was named after him by Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/John_Lort_Stokes" title="John Lort Stokes">John Lort Stokes</a> who, at the time, commanded HMS <i>Beagle</i> (previously commanded by FitzRoy). The South American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a> <a href="/wiki/Fitzroya" title="Fitzroya"><i>Fitzroya cupressoides</i></a> is named after him, as well as the <i>Delphinus fitzroyi</i>, a species of dolphin discovered by Darwin during his voyage aboard the <i>Beagle</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fitzroy,_Falkland_Islands" title="Fitzroy, Falkland Islands">Fitzroy, Falkland Islands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Fitzroy" title="Port Fitzroy">Port Fitzroy</a>, New Zealand are also named after him.</p>
<p>The World War II <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_class_frigate" title="Captain class frigate">Captain class</a> frigate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Fitzroy_(K553)" title="HMS Fitzroy (K553)">HMS <i>Fitzroy</i> (K553)</a> was named after him, as was the weather ship <i>Admiral Fitzroy</i> (formerly <a href="/wiki/HMS_Amberley_Castle_(K386)" title="HMS Amberley Castle (K386)">HMS <i>Amberley Castle</i></a>). In 2010 New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Water_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research">National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research</a> (NIWA) named its new <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputer</a> "FitzRoy" in honour of him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 4 February 2002, when the <a href="/wiki/Shipping_Forecast" title="Shipping Forecast">shipping forecast sea area</a> Finisterre was renamed to avoid confusion with the (smaller) French and Spanish forecast area of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Finisterre" title="Cape Finisterre">same name</a>, the new name chosen by the UK's Meteorological Office was "FitzRoy", in honour of their founder.</p>
<p>FitzRoy has been commemorated by the Fitzroy Building at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Plymouth" title="University of Plymouth">University of Plymouth</a>, used by the School of Earth, Ocean and Environmental Science.</p>
<p>Vice-Admiral Robert FitzRoy was commemorated on two stamps issued by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Helena" title="St Helena">St Helena</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_FitzRoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTA</a> award winning television series in 1978 tiled <a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_Charles_Darwin" title="The Voyage of Charles Darwin">The Voyage of Charles Darwin</a> where Captain Robert Fitzroy was played by actor <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Burt" title="Andrew Burt">Andrew Burt</a> with <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Stoddard" title="Malcolm Stoddard">Malcolm Stoddard</a> as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> with a storyline that followed the historic interaction between Darwin and FizRoy before and after their time together on the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS Beagle</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, the play <i>FitzRoy</i> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juliet_Aykroyd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juliet Aykroyd (page does not exist)">Juliet Aykroyd</a> was first performed at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Reading" title="University of Reading">University of Reading</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It has since been performed under the title <i>The Ostrich and the Dolphin</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> - alluding to <a href="/wiki/Darwin%27s_rhea" title="Darwin's rhea">Darwin's rhea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dusky_dolphin" title="Dusky dolphin">Dusky dolphin</a>, named <i>Delphinus fitzroyi</i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> - before being published as <i>Darwin &amp; FitzRoy</i> in October 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>A novel entitled <i><a href="/wiki/This_Thing_of_Darkness" title="This Thing of Darkness">This Thing of Darkness</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Thompson" title="Harry Thompson">Harry Thompson</a> was published in 2005. The novel's plot followed the lives of FitzRoy, Darwin and others connected with the Beagle expeditions, following them between the years of 1828 and 1865. It was a nominee on the <a href="/wiki/Short_list#Literary_awards" title="Short list">long list</a> for the 2005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" title="Man Booker Prize">Man Booker Prize</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> (although Thompson died in November 2005).</p>
<p>The novel <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Darwin%27s_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Darwin's Dreams (page does not exist)">Darwin's Dreams</a></i> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Hoade&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sean Hoade (page does not exist)">Sean Hoade</a> was published in 2008 and republished in a new edition in 2016. The novel begins with the first meeting of Darwin and FitzRoy and ends with Darwin receiving notice of FitzRoy's suicide. The plot is interlaced with fictional ″dreams″ that imagine how the world would be if the ideas of evolutionary thinkers over the millennia had been literally true. The dreams also show how Darwin′s subconscious dealt with major themes in his life such as the death of his beloved daughter Annie and his lifelong friendship and rivalry with FitzRoy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The play <i>Darwins Kapten</i> was published by <a href="/wiki/Henning_Mankell" title="Henning Mankell">Henning Mankell</a> in 2009, and had its world premiere in 2010 at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dramatic_Theatre" title="Royal Dramatic Theatre">Royal Dramatic Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>. It is about Charles Darwin and his journey with the <i>Beagle</i>: many years after the five-year long voyage, Darwin receives a message that his captain on the ship, FitzRoy, has committed suicide. The play portrays the reception of Darwin’s discoveries, as well as the consequences of taking a stand against existing ideas in a world that is built on belief in God as the only creator of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
