<p><b>Storm chasing</b> is broadly defined as the pursuit of any <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a> condition, regardless of motive, which can be curiosity, adventure, scientific investigation, or for news or media coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A person who chases storms is known as a <b>storm chaser</b>, or simply a <b>chaser</b>. While witnessing a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> is the single biggest objective for most chasers, many chase <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> and delight in viewing <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus</a> and related <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> structures, watching a barrage of <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, and seeing what skyscapes unfold. There are also a smaller number of storm chasers who intercept <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_FAQ_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ST_FAQ-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_and_motivations_for_chasing">Nature of and motivations for chasing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nature of and motivations for chasing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Storm chasing is chiefly a recreational endeavor, with motives usually given toward photographing or videoing the storm and for multivariate personal reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These can include the beauty of views afforded by the sky and land, the mystery of not knowing precisely what will unfold and the quest to undetermined destination on the open road, intangible experiences such as feeling one with a much larger and powerful natural world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the challenge of correctly <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">forecasting</a> and intercepting storms with the optimal vantage points,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and pure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrill_seeking" title="Thrill seeking">thrill seeking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Pecuniary interests and competition may also be components; in contrast, camaraderie is common.</p>
<p>Although scientific work is sometimes cited as a goal, direct participation in such work is almost always impractical except for those collaborating in an organized university or government project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recreational_chasing_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-recreational_chasing-7">[7]</a></sup> Many chasers also are <a href="/wiki/Storm_spotting" title="Storm spotting">storm spotters</a>, reporting their observations of hazardous weather to relevant authorities. These reports greatly benefit real-time warnings with <a href="/wiki/Ground_truth" title="Ground truth">ground truth</a> information as well as science by increasing the reliability of severe storm databases used in <a href="/wiki/Tornado_climatology" title="Tornado climatology">climatology</a> and other research (which ultimately boosts forecast and warning skill).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Additionally, many recreational chasers submit photos and videos to researchers as well as to the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS) for spotter training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spotting_history_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-spotting_history-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Storm chasers are not generally paid to chase, with the exception of television media crews in certain television market areas, video stringers and photographers, and researchers such as a handful of graduate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologists" title="Meteorologists">meteorologists</a> and professors. An increasing number do sell storm videos and pictures and manage to make a profit. A few operate "chase tour" services, making storm chasing a recently developed <a href="/wiki/List_of_adjectival_tourisms" title="List of adjectival tourisms">niche tourism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Financial returns usually are relatively meager given the expenses with most chasers spending more than they take in and very few making a living solely from chasing.</p>
<p>No degree or certification is required to be a storm chaser. Local National Weather Service offices do hold storm spotter training classes, usually early in the spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Some offices collaborate to produce <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a> workshops oriented toward operational meteorologists.</p>
<p>Storm chasers come from a wide variety of occupational and socioeconomic backgrounds. A fair number are meteorologists, however, most storm chasers may be from any number of occupational fields that have little or nothing to do with meteorology. A large majority of chasers are male and white but a minority are female or of Asian or other descents. The average age is probably around 35 but chasers could theoretically be of any age. A relatively high proportion possess college degrees and a large number live in the central and southern U.S. Many are lovers of <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> with interests that also include <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">aurora</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">eclipses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_FAQ_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ST_FAQ-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first recognized storm chaser is <a href="/wiki/David_K._Hoadley" title="David K. Hoadley">David Hoadley</a> (1938– ), who began chasing <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> storms in 1956, systematically using data from area weather offices and airports. He is widely considered the pioneer storm chaser<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_FAQ_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ST_FAQ-2">[2]</a></sup> and was the founder of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_Track_(magazine)" title="Storm Track (magazine)">Storm Track</a></i> magazine.</p>
<p>Bringing research chasing to the forefront was <a href="/wiki/Neil_B._Ward" title="Neil B. Ward">Neil B. Ward</a> (1914–1972) who in the 1950s and 1960s enlisted the help of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Highway_Patrol" title="Oklahoma Highway Patrol">Oklahoma Highway Patrol</a> to study storms. His work pioneered modern storm spotting and made institutional chasing a reality.</p>
<p>In 1972 the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a> (OU) in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/National_Severe_Storms_Laboratory" title="National Severe Storms Laboratory">National Severe Storms Laboratory</a> (NSSL) began the Tornado Intercept Project, with the first outing taking place on 19 April of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grazulis_Tornado_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grazulis_Tornado-13">[13]</a></sup> This was the first large scale chase activity sponsored by an institution. It culminated in a brilliant success in 1973 with the <a href="/wiki/Union_City,_Oklahoma" title="Union City, Oklahoma">Union City, Oklahoma</a> tornado providing a foundation for tornado and supercell morphology that proved the efficacy of storm chasing field research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-field_programs_history_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-field_programs_history-14">[14]</a></sup> The project produced the first legion of veteran storm chasers, with Hoadley's <i>Storm Track</i> magazine bringing the community together in 1977.</p>
<p>Storm chasing then reached popular culture in three major spurts: in 1978 with the broadcast of an episode of the television program <i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of..._(TV_series)" title="In Search of... (TV series)">In Search of...</a></i>; in 1985 with a documentary on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">Nova</a></i>; and in May 1996 with the theatrical release of <i><a href="/wiki/Twister_(1996_film)" title="Twister (1996 film)">Twister</a></i> which provided an action-packed but distorted glimpse at the hobby. Further early exposure to storm chasing encouraging some in the weather community resulted from several articles beginning in the late 1970s in <i><a href="/wiki/Weatherwise" title="Weatherwise">Weatherwise</a></i> magazine.</p>
<p>Various television programs and increased coverage of severe weather by the media, especially since the initial video revolution in which VHS ownership became widespread by the early 1990s, substantially elevated awareness of and interest in storms and storm chasing. The advent of the Internet, in particular, contributed to a significant growth in number of storm chasers since the mid-late 1990s. A sharp increase in the general public impulsively wandering in their local area searching for tornadoes similarly is largely attributable to these factors. The 2007-2011 Discovery Channel reality series <i><a href="/wiki/Storm_Chasers_(TV_series)" title="Storm Chasers (TV series)">Storm Chasers</a></i> produced another surge in activity. Over the years the nature of chasing and the characteristics of chasers shifted.</p>
<p>From their advent in the 1970s until the mid-1990s, scientific <a href="/wiki/Field_experiment" title="Field experiment">field projects</a> were occasionally conducted during spring in the Great Plains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-field_programs_history_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-field_programs_history-14">[14]</a></sup> Then, the first of the seminal <a href="/wiki/VORTEX_projects" title="VORTEX projects">VORTEX projects</a> occurred in 1994-95<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VORTEX_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-VORTEX-15">[15]</a></sup> and this was soon followed by various field experiments each spring, with another large project, <a class="external text" href="http://vortex2.org/" rel="nofollow">VORTEX2</a>, in 2009-10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VORTEX2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-VORTEX2-16">[16]</a></sup> Since the mid-1990s, most storm chasing science, with the notable exception of large field projects, consists of mobile <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">Doppler radar</a> intercepts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_storm_chase">Typical storm chase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typical storm chase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chasing often involves driving thousands of miles in order to witness the relatively short window of time of active severe thunderstorms. It is not uncommon for a chaser to end up empty handed on any particular day. Storm chasers' degrees of involvement, competencies, philosophies, and techniques vary widely, but many chasers spend a significant amount of time forecasting, both before going on the road as well as during the chase, utilizing various sources for weather data. Most storm chasers are not meteorologists, and many chasers expend significant time and effort in learning meteorology and the intricacies of severe convective storm prediction through both study and experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides the copious driving to, from, and during chases, storm chasing is punctuated with contrasting periods of long waiting and ceaseless action. Downtime can consist of sitting under sun-baked skies for hours, playing pickup sports, evaluating data, or visiting landmarks whilst awaiting convective initiation. During an inactive pattern, this down time can persist for days. When storms are going there is often little or no time to eat or relieve oneself and finding fuel can cause frustrating delays and detours. Navigating obstacles such as rivers and areas with inadequate road networks is a paramount concern. Only a handful of chasers decide to chase <a href="/wiki/Dixie_Alley" title="Dixie Alley">Dixie Alley</a>, an area of the Southern United States in which trees and road networks heavily obscure the often large tornadoes. The combination of driving and waiting has been likened to "extreme sitting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A "bust" occurs when storms do not fire, sometimes referred to as "severe clear", when storms fire but are missed, when storms fire but are meager, or when storms fire after dusk.</p>
<p>Most chasing is accomplished by driving, however, a few individuals occasionally fly planes and television stations in some markets use helicopters. Research projects sometimes employ aircraft, as well.</p>
<h2><span id="Geographical,_seasonal,_and_diurnal_activity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical.2C_seasonal.2C_and_diurnal_activity">Geographical, seasonal, and diurnal activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geographical, seasonal, and diurnal activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Storm chasers are most active in the spring and early summer, particularly May and June, across the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (extending into <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>) in an area colloquially known as <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>, with many hundred individuals active on some days during this period. This coincides with the most consistent tornado days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in the most desirable topography of the Great Plains. Not only are the most intense <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a> common here, but due to the moisture profile of the atmosphere the storms tend to be more visible than locations farther east where there are also frequent severe thunderstorms. There is a tendency for chases earlier in the year to be farther south, shifting farther north with the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> as the season progresses. Storms occurring later in the year tend to be more isolated and slower moving, both of which are also desirable to chasers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chasers may operate whenever significant thunderstorm activity is occurring, whatever the date. This most commonly includes more sporadic activity occurring in warmer months of the year bounding the spring maximum, such as the active month of April and to a lesser extent March. The focus in the summer months is the Central or Northern Plains states and the Prairie Provinces, the Upper Midwest, or on to just east of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorado_Front_Range" title="Colorado Front Range">Colorado Front Range</a>. An annually inconsistent and substantially smaller peak of severe thunderstorm and tornado activity also arises in the transitional months of autumn, particularly October and November. This follows a pattern somewhat the reverse of the spring pattern with the focus beginning in the north then dropping south and with an overall eastward shift. In the area with the most consistent <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_tornado_terms#S" title="Glossary of tornado terms">significant</a> tornado activity, the Southern Plains, the tornado season is intense but is relatively brief whereas central to northern and eastern areas experience less intense and consistent activity that is diffused over a longer span of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_FAQ_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ST_FAQ-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Advancing technology since the mid-2000s led to chasers more commonly targeting less amenable areas (i.e. hilly or forested) that were previously eschewed when continuous wide visibility was critical. These advancements, particularly in-vehicle weather data such as radar, also led to an increase in chasing after nightfall. Most chasing remains during daylight hours with active storm intercepting peaking from mid-late afternoon through early-to-mid evening. This is dictated by a chaser's schedule (availability to chase) and by when storms form, which usually is around peak heating during the mid-to-late afternoon but on some days occurs in early afternoon or even in the morning. An additional advantage of later season storms is that days are considerably longer than in early spring. Morning or early afternoon storms tend to be associated with stronger <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and thus most often happen earlier in the spring season or later during the fall season.</p>
<p>Some organized chasing efforts have also begun in the <a href="/wiki/Top_End" title="Top End">Top End</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> and in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> with the biggest successes in November and December. A handful of individuals are also known to be chasing in other countries, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>; although many people trek to the Great Plains of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> from these and other countries around the world (especially from the UK). The number of chasers and number countries where chasers are active expanded at an accelerating pace in Europe from the 1990s-2010s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">Dangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dangers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are inherent dangers involved in pursuing hazardous weather. These range from lightning, tornadoes, large hail, flooding, hazardous road conditions (rain or hail-covered roadways), animals on the roadway, downed power lines (and occasionally other debris), reduced visibility from heavy rain (often wind blown), blowing <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hail_fog" title="Hail fog">hail fog</a>. Most directly weather-related hazards such as from a tornado are minimized if the storm chaser is knowledgeable and cautious. In some situations severe <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a> winds may push automobiles around, especially high-profile vehicles. Tornadoes affect a relatively small area and are predictable enough to be avoided if sustaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situational_awareness" title="Situational awareness">situational awareness</a> and following strategies including always having an open escape route, maintaining a safe distance, and avoiding placement in the direction of travel of a tornado (in most cases in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> this is to the north and to the east of a tornado). Lightning, however, is an unavoidable hazard. "Core punching", storm chaser slang for driving through a heavy precipitation core to intercept the area of interest within a storm, is recognized as hazardous due to reduced visibility and because many tornadoes are rain-wrapped. The "bear's cage" refers to the area under a rotating <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a> (and any attendant tornadoes), which is the "bear", and to the blinding precipitation (which can include window-shatteringly large hail) surrounding some or all sides of a tornado, which is the "cage". Similarly, chasing at night heightens risk due to darkness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storm_Talk_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storm_Talk-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In reality, the most significant hazard is <a href="/wiki/Driving" title="Driving">driving</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storm_Talk_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Storm_Talk-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doswell_ethics_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doswell_ethics-23">[23]</a></sup> which is made more dangerous by the severe weather. Adding still more to this hazard are the multiple <a href="/wiki/Distracted_driving" title="Distracted driving">distractions</a> which can compete for a chaser's attention, such as driving, communicating with chase partners and others with a phone and/or radio, navigating, watching the sky, checking weather data, and shooting photos or video. Again here, <a href="/wiki/Defensive_driving" title="Defensive driving">prudence</a> is key to <a href="/wiki/Road_traffic_safety" title="Road traffic safety">minimizing</a> the risk. Chasers ideally work to prevent the driver from <a href="/wiki/Human_multitasking" title="Human multitasking">multitasking</a> either by chase partners covering the other aspects or by the driver pulling over to do these other things if he or she is chasing alone. Falling <a href="/wiki/Sleep-deprived_driving" title="Sleep-deprived driving">asleep</a> while driving is a chase hazard, especially on long trips back. This also is exacerbated by nocturnal darkness and by the <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(safety)" title="Fatigue (safety)">defatigating demands</a> of driving through precipitation and on slick roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manual_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-manual-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For nearly 60 years, the only known chaser deaths were driving-related. The first was Christopher Phillips, a University of Oklahoma undergraduate student, killed in a <a href="/wiki/Aquaplaning" title="Aquaplaning">hydroplaning</a> accident when swerving to miss a rabbit in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Three other incidents occurred when <a class="external text" href="http://www.theperksofchasing.com/" rel="nofollow">Jeff Wear</a> was driving home from a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">hurricane</a> chase in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> when Fabian Guerra swerved to miss a deer while driving to a chase in 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and when a wrong-way driver resulted in a head-on collision that killed Andy Gabrielson returning from a chase in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On 31 May 2013, an extreme event led to the first known chaser deaths inflicted directly by weather when the <a href="/wiki/2013_El_Reno_tornado" title="2013 El Reno tornado">widest tornado</a> ever recorded struck near El Reno, Oklahoma. Engineer <a href="/wiki/Tim_Samaras" title="Tim Samaras">Tim Samaras</a>, his photographer son Paul, and meteorologist Carl Young were killed doing in situ probe and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrasonic" title="Infrasonic">infrasonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Field_research" title="Field research">field research</a> by an exceptional combination of events in which an already large and rain-obscured tornado swelled within less than a minute to 2.6 miles (4.2 km) wide simultaneously as it changed direction and accelerated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Several other chasers were also struck and some injured by this tornado and its parent supercell's <a href="/wiki/Rear_flank_downdraft" title="Rear flank downdraft">rear flank downdraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Three chasers were killed, two in one vehicle and one in another, when their vehicles collided in West Texas in 2017, bringing the total number of known traffic related fatalities to 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> There are other incidents in which chasers were injured by automobile accidents, lightning strikes, and tornado impacts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While chasing a <a href="/wiki/March_1990_Central_United_States_tornado_outbreak" title="March 1990 Central United States tornado outbreak">tornado outbreak</a> on 13 March 1990, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KWTV" title="KWTV">KWTV</a> television photographer Bill Merickel was shot and injured near <a href="/wiki/Lindsay,_Oklahoma" title="Lindsay, Oklahoma">Lindsay, Oklahoma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Storm chasers vary with regards to the amount of equipment used, some prefer a minimalist approach; for example, where only basic photographic equipment is taken on a chase, while others use everything from satellite-based tracking systems and live data feeds to vehicle-mounted <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> and hail guards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic">Historic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Historically, storm chasing relied on either in-field analysis or in some cases nowcasts from trained observers and forecasters. The first in-field technology consisted of radio gear for communication. Much of this equipment could also be adapted to receive <a href="/wiki/Radiofax" title="Radiofax">radiofax</a> data which was useful for receiving basic observational and analysis data. The primary users of such technology were university or government research groups who often had larger budgets than individual chasers.</p>
<p>Radio scanners were also heavily used to listen in on <a href="/wiki/Emergency_service" title="Emergency service">emergency services</a> and storm spotters so as to determine where the most active or dangerous weather was located. A number of chasers were also <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_operator" title="Amateur radio operator">radio amateurs</a>, and used mobile (or portable) <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> to communicate directly with spotters and other chasers, allowing them to keep abreast of what they could not themselves see.</p>
<p>It was not until the mid- to late 1980s that the evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptop</a> computer would begin to revolutionize storm chasing. Early on, some chasers carried <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_coupler" title="Acoustic coupler">acoustic couplers</a> to download batches of raw surface and upper air data from payphones. The technology was too slow for graphical imagery such as <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">radar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellite</a> data; and during the first years this wasn't available on any connection over telephone lines, anyway. Some raw data could be downloaded and plotted by software, such as <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface weather observations</a> using <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.weathergraphics.com/dl/" rel="nofollow">WeatherGraphix</a></i> (predecessor to <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.weathergraphics.com/da/" rel="nofollow">Digital Atmosphere</a></i>) and similar software or for upper air soundings using <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.weathergraphics.com/dl/" rel="nofollow">SHARP</a></i>, <i><a class="external text" href="http://raob.com/" rel="nofollow">RAOB</a></i>, and similar software.</p>
<p>Most meteorological data was acquired all at once early in the morning, and the rest of day's chasing was based on analysis and forecast gleaned from this; as well as on visual clues that presented themselves in the field throughout the day. Plotted <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather maps</a> were often analyzed by hand for manual diagnosis of meteorological patterns. Occasionally chasers would make stops at rural airstrips or NWS offices for an update on weather conditions. <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Weather_Radio" title="NOAA Weather Radio">NOAA Weather Radio</a> (NWR) could provide information in the vehicle, without stopping, such as <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather_terminology_(United_States)" title="Severe weather terminology (United States)">weather watches and warnings</a>, surface weather conditions, <a href="/wiki/Storm_Prediction_Center#Convective_outlooks" title="Storm Prediction Center">convective outlooks</a>, and NWS radar summaries. Nowadays, storm chasers may use high-speed Internet access available in any library, even in small towns in the US. This data is available throughout the day, but one must find and stop at a location offering Internet access.</p>
<p>With the development of the mobile computers, the first computer mapping software became feasible, at about the same time as the popular adoption of the <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> <a href="/wiki/Camcorder" title="Camcorder">camcorder</a> began a rapid growth phase. Prior to the mid to late 1980s most motion picture equipment consisted of <a href="/wiki/8_mm_film" title="8 mm film">8 mm film</a> cameras. While the quality of the first VHS consumer cameras was quite poor (and the size somewhat cumbersome) when compared to traditional film formats, the amount of <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">video</a> which could be shot with a minimal amount of resources was much greater than any <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">film format</a> at the time.</p>
<p>In the 1980s and 1990s <a href="/wiki/The_Weather_Channel" title="The Weather Channel">The Weather Channel</a> (TWC) and <i><a href="/wiki/A.M._Weather" title="A.M. Weather">A.M. Weather</a></i> were popular with chasers, in the morning preceding a chase for the latter and both before and during a chase for the former. Commercial radio sometimes also provides weather and damage information. The 1990s brought technological leaps and bounds. With the swift development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-state_(electronics)" title="Solid-state (electronics)">solid state</a> technology, television sets for example could be installed with ease in most vehicles allowing storm chasers to actively view local TV stations. Mobile phones became popular making group coordination easier when traditional radio communications methods were not ideal or for those possessing radios. The development of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> (WWW) in 1993 hastened adoption of the Internet and led to <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">FTP</a> access to some of the first university weather sites.</p>
<p>The mid-1990s marked the development of smaller more efficient marine radars. While such marine radars are illegal if used in land-mobile situations, a number of chasers were quick to adopt them in an effort to have mobile radar. These radars have been found to interfere with research radars, such as the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_on_Wheels" title="Doppler on Wheels">Doppler on Wheels</a> (DOW) utilized in field projects. The first personal <a href="/wiki/Lightning_detection" title="Lightning detection">lightning detection</a> and mapping devices also became available<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and the first online radar data was offered by private corporations or, at first with delays, with free services. A popular data vendor by the end of the 1990s was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=WeatherTAP&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="WeatherTAP (page does not exist)">WeatherTAP</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current">Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Chasers used paper <a href="/wiki/Road_map" title="Road map">maps</a> for navigation and some of those now using GPS still use these as a backup or for strategizing with other chasers. Foldable state maps can be used but are cumbersome due to the multitude of states needed and only show major roads. National atlases allow more detail and all states are contained in a single book, with <a href="/wiki/American_Automobile_Association" title="American Automobile Association">AAA</a> favored and <a href="/wiki/Rand_McNally" title="Rand McNally">Rand McNally</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Michelin_Guide" title="Michelin Guide">Michelin</a> also used. The preferred atlases due to great detail in rural areas are the "<i>Roads of...</i>" series originally by Shearer Publishing, which first included Texas but expanded to other states such as Oklahoma and Colorado. Covering every state of the union are the <a href="/wiki/DeLorme" title="DeLorme">DeLorme</a> "Atlas and Gazetteer" series. DeLorme also produced early GPS receivers that connected to laptops and continues to be one of two major mapping software creators. <i>DeLorme Street Atlas USA</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Streets_%26_Trips" title="Microsoft Streets &amp; Trips">Microsoft Streets &amp; Trips</a></i> are used by most chasers.</p>
<p>A major turning point was the advent of civilian <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> in 1996. At first, GPS units were very costly and only offered basic functions, but that would soon change. Towards the late 1990s the Internet was awash in weather data and free weather software, the first true cellular Internet modems for consumer use also emerged providing chasers access to data in the field without having to rely on a nowcaster. The NWS also released the first free, up-to-date <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">NEXRAD</a> Level 3 radar data. In conjunction with all of this, GPS units now had the ability to connect with computers, granting greater ease when navigating.</p>
<p>2001 marked the next great technological leap for storm chasers as the first <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> units began to emerge offering wireless broadband service in many cases for free. Some places (restaurants, motels, libraries, etc.) were known to reliably offer wireless access and <a href="/wiki/Wardriving" title="Wardriving">wardriving</a> located other availabilities. In 2002 the first <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a>-based package to combine GPS positioning and Doppler radar appeared called SWIFT WX.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> SWIFT WX allowed storm chasers to seamlessly position themselves accurately relative to tornadic storms.</p>
<p>In 2004 two more storm chaser tools emerged. The first, WxWorx, was a new <a href="/wiki/XM_Satellite_Radio" title="XM Satellite Radio">XM Satellite Radio</a> based system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> utilizing a special receiver and <a href="/wiki/Baron_Services" title="Baron Services">Baron Services</a> weather software. Unlike preexisting cellular based services there was no risk of dead spots, and that meant that even in the most remote areas storm chasers still had a live data feed. The second tool was a new piece of software called GRLevel3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/GRLevelX" title="GRLevelX">GRLevel3</a> utilized both free and subscription based raw radar files, displaying the data in a true <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">vector</a> format with <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">GIS</a> layering abilities. Since 2006 a growing number of chasers are using <a href="/wiki/Spotter_Network" title="Spotter Network">Spotter Network</a> (SN), which uses GPS data to plot real time position of participating spotters and chasers, and allows observers to report significant weather as well as GIS layering for navigation maps, weather products, and the like.</p>
<p>The most common chaser communications device is the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">cellular phone</a>. These are used for both voice and data connections. External antennas and amplifiers may be used to boost signal transception. It is not uncommon that chasers travel in small groups of cars, and they may use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens_Band_radio" title="Citizens Band radio">CB radio</a> (declining in use) or inexpensive <a href="/wiki/General_Mobile_Radio_Service" title="General Mobile Radio Service">GMRS</a> / <a href="/wiki/Family_Radio_Service" title="Family Radio Service">FRS</a> hand-held <a href="/wiki/Transceiver" title="Transceiver">transceivers</a> for inter-vehicle communication. More commonly, many chasers are also ham radio operators and use the <a href="/wiki/2-meter_band" title="2-meter band">2 meters</a> <a href="/wiki/Very_high_frequency" title="Very high frequency">VHF</a> and, less often, <a href="/wiki/70-centimeter_band" title="70-centimeter band">70 cm</a> <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> bands to communicate between vehicles or with <a href="/wiki/Skywarn" title="Skywarn">Skywarn</a> / <a href="/wiki/Canwarn" title="Canwarn">Canwarn</a> spotter networks. <a href="/wiki/Scanner_(radio)" title="Scanner (radio)">Scanners</a> are often used to monitor spotter, sometimes public safety communications, and can double as <a href="/wiki/Weather_radio" title="Weather radio">weather radios</a>. Since the mid-2000s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_networking_services" title="Social networking services">social networking services</a> may also be used, with <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> most used for ongoing events, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> for sharing images and discussing chase reports, and <a href="/wiki/Google%2B" title="Google+">Google+</a> trailing in adoption. Social networking services largely (but not completely) replace forums and email lists, which complemented and eventually supplanted <i>Stormtrack</i> magazine, for conversing about storms.</p>
<p>In-field environmental data is still popular among some storm chasers, especially <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, moisture, and <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">direction</a> data. Many have chosen to mount weather stations atop their vehicles. Others use handheld <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometers</a>. Rulers or baseballs may be brought along for measuring hail and for showing as a comparison object. Vehicle mounted cameras, such as on the roof or more commonly on the dash, provide continuous visual recording capability.</p>
<p>Chasers heavily utilize still <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a> since the beginning. <a href="/wiki/Videography" title="Videography">Videography</a> gained prominence by the 1990s into the early 2000s but a resurgence of photography occurred with the advent of affordable and versatile <a href="/wiki/Digital_single-lens_reflex_camera" title="Digital single-lens reflex camera">digital SLR</a> (DSLR) <a href="/wiki/Still_camera" title="Still camera">cameras</a>. Prior to this, <a href="/wiki/35_mm_film" title="35 mm film">35 mm</a> <a href="/wiki/Single-lens_reflex_camera" title="Single-lens reflex camera">SLR</a> <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">print</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">slide</a> <a href="/wiki/Film_format" title="Film format">film formats</a> were mostly used, along with some <a href="/wiki/Medium_format_(film)" title="Medium format (film)">medium format</a> cameras. In the late 2000s, mobile phone <a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">3G</a> data networks became fast enough to allow live streaming video from chasers using webcams. This live imagery is frequently used by the media, as well as NWS meteorologists, emergency managers, and the general public for direct ground truth information, and it provides video sales opportunities to chasers. Also by this time, camcorders using <a href="/wiki/Memory_card" title="Memory card">memory cards</a> to record video began to be adopted. <a href="/wiki/DV" title="DV">Digital video</a> had been around for years but was recorded on <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">tape</a>, whereas solid-state is <a href="/wiki/Random_access" title="Random access">random access</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Sequential_access" title="Sequential access">sequential access</a> (linear) and has no moving parts. Late in the 2000s <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">HD video</a> began to overtake <a href="/wiki/Standard-definition_television" title="Standard-definition television">SD</a> (which had been <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a> in North America) in usage as prices came down and performance increased (initially there were low-light and sporadic aliasing problems due to chip and sensor limitations). By the mid-2010s <a href="/wiki/4K_resolution" title="4K resolution">4K</a> cameras were increasingly in use. <a href="/wiki/Tripod_(photography)" title="Tripod (photography)">Tripods</a> are used by those seeking crisp professional photo and video imagery and also enable chasers to tend to other activities.</p>
<p>Late in the 2000s <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a> increased in usage, with radar viewing applications frequently used. Particularly, <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.basevelocity.com/" rel="nofollow">RadarScope</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> platform and <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.pykl3radar.com/" rel="nofollow">Pkyl3</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> are favored. Other apps may be used as are browsers for viewing meteorological data and accessing social networking services. Some handsets can be used as <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(Wi-Fi)" title="Hotspot (Wi-Fi)">WiFi hotspots</a> and wireless cards may also be used to avoid committing a handset to <a href="/wiki/Tethering" title="Tethering">tethering</a> or operating as a hotspot. Some hotspots operate as <a href="/wiki/Mobile_broadband" title="Mobile broadband">mobile broadband</a> <a href="/wiki/Mobile_virtual_network_operator" title="Mobile virtual network operator">MNVO</a> devices using any radio spectrum that is both available and is in contract with a service provider. Such devices may expand mobile data range beyond a single carrier's service area and typically can work on month-to-month contracts. Adoption of <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a> is expanding as of the early 2010s. <a href="/wiki/LTE_(telecommunication)" title="LTE (telecommunication)">4G LTE</a> has been adopted when available and can be especially useful for uploading HD video. A gradual uptick of those selecting <a href="/wiki/Mirrorless_interchangeable-lens_camera" title="Mirrorless interchangeable-lens camera">mirrorless interchangeable-lens cameras</a> (MILCs) began in the mid-2010s although DSLR remains heavily favored. Usage of DSLR for video capture, called HDSLR, is common, although HD camcorders remain popular due to their greater functionality (many chasers still shoot both).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics">Ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_chasing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A growing number of experienced storm chasers advocate the adoption of a code of <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> in storm chasing featuring safety, courtesy, and objectivity as the backbone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doswell_ethics_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doswell_ethics-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Storm chasing is a highly visible recreational activity (which is also associated with <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>) that is vulnerable to sensationalist media promotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Veteran storm chasers <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Doswell_III" title="Charles A. Doswell III">Chuck Doswell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Edwards_(meteorologist)" title="Roger Edwards (meteorologist)">Roger Edwards</a> have deemed reckless storm chasers as "yahoos".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Doswell and Edwards believe poor chasing ethics at TV news stations add to the growth of "yahoo" storm chasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Edwards and Rich Thompson, among others, also expressed concern about pernicious effects of media profiteering<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> with Matt Crowther, among others, agreeing in principle but viewing sales as not inherently corrupting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Self-policing is seen as the means to mold the hobby. There is occasional discussion among chasers that at some point government regulation may be imposed due to increasing numbers of chasers and because of poor behavior by some individuals; however, many chasers do not expect this eventuality and almost all oppose regulations -—as do some formal studies of dangerous leisure activities which advocate deliberative <a href="/wiki/Industry_self-regulation" title="Industry self-regulation">self-policing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>As there is for storm chaser conduct, there is concern about chaser responsibility. Since some chasers are trained in <a href="/wiki/First_aid" title="First aid">first aid</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Certified_first_responder" title="Certified first responder">first responder</a> procedures, it is not uncommon for tornado chasers to be first on a scene and tending to storm victims or treating injuries at the site of a disaster in advance of emergency personnel and other outside aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aside from questions concerning their ethical values and conduct, many have been accredited for giving back to the community in several ways. Just before the Joplin tornado a storm chaser provided advanced warning to local law enforcement, prompting them to activate the emergency sirens. Though lives were lost, many who survived accredited their survival to the siren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> After a storm has passed storm chasers are often the first to arrive on the scene to help assist in the aftermath. An unexpected and yet increasingly more common result of storm chasers is the data they provide to storm research from their videos, social video posts and documentation of storms they encounter. After the El Reno tornado in 2013 portals were created for chasers to submit their information to help in the research of the deadly storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
