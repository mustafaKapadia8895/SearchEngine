<p>The <b>1926 Miami hurricane</b>, commonly called the <b>"Great Miami" hurricane</b>, was a large and intense <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that devastated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Miami" title="Greater Miami">Greater Miami</a> area and caused extensive damage in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">U.S. Gulf Coast</a> in September <a href="/wiki/1926" title="1926">1926</a>. The toll for the storm was <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>105 million (equivalent to $7.2 billion in 2016).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWS1-1">[1]</a></sup> As a result of the destruction in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, the hurricane represented an early start to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> in the aftermath of the state's <a href="/wiki/Florida_land_boom_of_the_1920s" title="Florida land boom of the 1920s">1920s land boom</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Your explanation here (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The tropical cyclone is believed to have <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">formed</a> in the central Atlantic Ocean on September 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UTC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-UTC-5">[nb 1]</a></sup> Steadily strengthening as it tracked west-northwestward, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> reached hurricane intensity the next day. As a result of scattered observations at open sea, however, no ship encountered the storm until September 15, by which time the cyclone had reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" title="Major hurricane">major hurricane</a> intensity north of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>. Strengthening continued up until the following day, when the storm reached peak intensity with a strength equivalent to a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4 hurricane</a>. This intensity was maintained as the storm tracked across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos" title="Turks and Caicos">Turks and Caicos</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> on September 18.</p>
<p>The cyclone caused immense destruction throughout the islands and across southern Florida. The storm destroyed hundreds of structures in its path over the islands, leaving thousands of residents homeless. At least 17 deaths occurred on the islands, though many others—some related only indirectly to the storm—were reported in the aftermath. Upon striking South Florida, the cyclone generated hurricane-force winds over a broad swath of the region, causing widespread and severe structural damage from both wind and water. Most of the deaths occurred near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Okeechobee" title="Lake Okeechobee">Lake Okeechobee</a>, when a large <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> breached muck <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">dikes</a> and drowned hundreds of people.</p>
<p>The hurricane quickly traversed the Florida peninsula before emerging into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> near <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers,_Florida" title="Fort Myers, Florida">Fort Myers</a>. The storm flooded surrounding communities and <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier islands</a>, while strong winds downed trees and disrupted electrical service. The storm later made two landfalls with weaker intensities on <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> on September 20 and 21, respectively. The storm caused additional but less severe damage in those states, primarily from heavy rains and storm surge. Land interaction caused the cyclone to deteriorate and later dissipate on September 22.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png/220px-1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png/330px-1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png/440px-1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1926_Miami_hurricane_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to the sparseness of available observations in the central Atlantic, the specific <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">origins</a> of the 1926 Miami hurricane remain unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">[5]</a></sup> Operationally, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">United States Weather Bureau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, did not begin issuing <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#North_Atlantic_systems" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">advisories</a> on the cyclone until September 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[6]</a></sup> However, the tropical cyclone is first listed in <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">HURDAT</a>—the official <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> database—as having begun as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 65 miles per hour (100 km/h) roughly 1,100 mi (1,770 km) east of the island of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> on September 11. Tracking west-northwestward, the storm gradually intensified and reached hurricane intensity on September 12 while still east of the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup> The observation of low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressures</a> and winds suggesting <a href="/wiki/Cyclonic_rotation" title="Cyclonic rotation">cyclonic rotation</a> at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts" title="Saint Kitts">Saint Kitts</a> on the evening of September 14 was the first to suggest that a hurricane had developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[6]</a></sup> The following day, the <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamship</a> <i>Matura</i> encountered the strengthening tropical cyclone and documented a minimum pressure of 28.82 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inches of mercury</a> (976 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mb</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">[5]</a></sup> By 06:00 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Time_Coordinate" title="Universal Time Coordinate">UTC</a> on September 15, the storm had strengthened further to major hurricane intensity north of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strengthening continued into September 16 as the hurricane reached a strength equivalent to that of a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4</a> on the modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>. Although no official minimum pressure readings were taken in the area at the time, the tropical cyclone peaked in wind-based intensity at 18:00 UTC on September 16 with sustained winds of 150 mph (241 km/h), near the uppermost limit of the modern-day ranking <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup> With this strength the hurricane passed near the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>, though its intensity at the time was based on the extent of damage there as any measurement device was knocked out by the damaging winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR-Turk_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-Turk-9">[8]</a></sup> Shortly afterward, the cyclone struck the Bahamian island of <a href="/wiki/Mayaguana" title="Mayaguana">Mayaguana</a> at its peak intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup> After passing the island, the hurricane slightly weakened but maintained formidable strength as it accelerated through the southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, passing near <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a> on September 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[6]</a></sup> The storm then made a second landfall on <a href="/wiki/Andros,_Bahamas" title="Andros, Bahamas">Andros Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mangrove_Cay" title="Mangrove Cay">Mangrove Cay</a> district early on September 18. Thereafter, the hurricane crossed Andros Island and passed over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> en route to Florida. This trajectory brought the storm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">ashore</a> on the coast of South Florida near <a href="/wiki/Perrine,_Florida" title="Perrine, Florida">Perrine</a>, located just 15 mi (24 km) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downtown_Miami" title="Downtown Miami">Downtown</a> <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, before 12:00 UTC on September 18 with winds of 145 mph (233 km/h) and a minimum pressure estimated at 930 mb (27.46 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_hurricanes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_hurricanes-10">[9]</a></sup> At the time, the hurricane was very large in size, with a <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_outermost_closed_isobar" title="Radius of outermost closed isobar">radius of outermost closed isobar</a> 375 miles (604 km) across;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate-11">[10]</a></sup> hurricane-force winds were reported from the upper <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> to near <a href="/wiki/St._Lucie_County,_Florida" title="St. Lucie County, Florida">St. Lucie County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WindsLocale_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-WindsLocale-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank1926_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frank1926-13">[12]</a></sup> Around 20:30 UTC, the eye of the hurricane passed into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> near <a href="/wiki/Punta_Rassa,_Florida" title="Punta Rassa, Florida">Punta Rassa</a>; though by that time the pressure in the eye had only risen to 28.05 inHg (950 mb),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[6]</a></sup> the winds in the eye wall had decreased to 105 mph (169 km/h). The hurricane had weakened over <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a> as a result of land interaction, but re-strengthened after emerging into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> off <a href="/wiki/Punta_Rassa,_Florida" title="Punta Rassa, Florida">Punta Rassa</a> six hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The warm waters of the Gulf of Mexico allowed for the tropical cyclone to reach a secondary peak intensity with winds of 125 mph (201 km/h)on September 20, equivalent to that of a modern-day high-end Category 3 hurricane. Although the storm had taken a more northwesterly course through the gulf, the hurricane later began paralleling the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> and thus slowly curved westward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup> As a result, the major hurricane, now weakening quickly, made its second landfall near <a href="/wiki/Perdido_Beach,_Alabama" title="Perdido Beach, Alabama">Perdido Beach, Alabama</a>, at around 21:30 UTC that day with winds of 115 mph (185 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_hurricanes_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-US_hurricanes-10">[9]</a></sup> After landfall, the storm quickly weakened and meandered off of Alabama's barrier islands, eventually moving ashore for the last time on September 21 near <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">Gulfport, Mississippi</a>, as a tropical storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">[5]</a></sup> The cyclone continued its decay inland, degenerating into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a> on the following day, before dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 16, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">United States Weather Bureau</a> advised caution to ships tracking in Bahamian waters and the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Florida" title="Straits of Florida">Florida Strait</a>. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">tropical cyclone warning</a> associated with the storm was a northeast storm warning issued on September 17 for the Florida coast from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_Inlet" title="Jupiter Inlet">Jupiter Inlet</a> to <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West, Florida</a>. Warnings along the United States <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Eastern Seaboard</a> eventually stretched as far north as <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, upon the storm's first landfall. Additional warnings were posted for the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">United States Gulf Coast</a> on September 19 and covered coastal areas from <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">Apalachicola, Florida</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Burrwood,_Louisiana" title="Burrwood, Louisiana">Burrwood, Louisiana</a>. Information on the storm as ascertained by the U.S. Weather Bureau was relayed by various radio and local press services, though the bureau specifically acknowledged the <a href="/wiki/Press-Register" title="Press-Register">Mobile Register</a> for their efforts in disseminating storm details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-7">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turks_and_Caicos_and_Bahamas">Turks and Caicos and Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Turks and Caicos and Bahamas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although no fatalities were reported, the hurricane wrought extensive property damage to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk Island</a>. Rain gauges recorded 10 <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inches</a> (254 <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">mm</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> during the storm, and high surf left knee-deep sand drifts on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR-Turk_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-Turk-9">[8]</a></sup> The ocean covered the land up to .75 mi (1.2 km) inland, and winds unroofed buildings at the weather station. Reportedly, the winds even ripped spines from <a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">prickly pear</a> cacti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR-Turk_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-Turk-9">[8]</a></sup> Nearly all <a href="/wiki/Lighter_(barge)" title="Lighter (barge)">lighters</a> at port were lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EnormousDamage_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-EnormousDamage-17">[16]</a></sup> The storm left 4,000 people homeless on three of the islands in the Turks and Caicos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1-18">[17]</a></sup> Due to hampered communication, the extent of damage in the Bahamas was initially unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweeps_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sweeps-19">[18]</a></sup> In the Bahamas, the storm flattened hundreds of structures and killed at least 17 people, mostly on <a href="/wiki/Bimini" title="Bimini">Bimini</a>, where seven people died and the greatest property damage occurred. The hurricane also leveled many structures on Andros, including churches and large buildings, and downed trees and other homes on New Providence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-2-3">[3]</a></sup> On parts of Andros, the storm snapped or felled almost all of the coconut palms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neely-1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neely-1-20">[19]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Exuma" title="Exuma">Exuma</a> district a large storm surge ruined many crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neely-2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neely-2-21">[20]</a></sup> The storm also destroyed 60% of the homes on the north island of Bimini, left water up to 7 feet (2.1 m) deep in some areas, and was widely considered the worst storm on record in Bimini to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neely-3_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neely-3-22">[21]</a></sup> Some sources say 25 people died on Bimini, but these may have been indirect deaths, as many people reportedly perished after drinking contaminated well water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neely-3_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neely-3-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida_peninsula">Florida peninsula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Florida peninsula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1926 hurricane is known primarily for its impacts and lasting aftermath in <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>, particularly in the Miami area. Effects were concentrated around Florida's southeastern coast and south-central Florida. Damage figures from the storm in the state alone reached US$75 million and accounted for most of the damage that the tropical cyclone produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">[5]</a></sup> Newspapers initially reported 500 killed. Homes and office buildings were used to serve as refugee camps for the approximately 38,000 people displaced by the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Miami_metropolitan_area">Miami metropolitan area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Miami metropolitan area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The storm surge in South Florida was not as high as it would have been had the hurricane struck another area, owing to the deep offshore <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>, which reduced the energy needed to sustain a large surge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank1926_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frank1926-13">[12]</a></sup> However, along <a href="/wiki/Biscayne_Bay" title="Biscayne Bay">Biscayne Bay</a>, the hurricane produced a substantial storm surge; visual estimates suggested a peak height of 14–15 ft (4.3–4.6 m) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coconut_Grove,_Miami,_Florida" title="Coconut Grove, Miami, Florida">Coconut Grove</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downtown_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Downtown-24">[23]</a></sup> and a value of 13.2 feet (4.0 m) occurred at <a href="/wiki/Dinner_Key" title="Dinner Key">Dinner Key</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinner_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dinner-25">[24]</a></sup> though the value was somewhat lower at 11.7 feet (3.6 m) along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_1_in_Florida#Miami-Dade_County" title="U.S. Route 1 in Florida">Biscayne Boulevard</a> in Downtown Miami.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downtown_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Downtown-24">[23]</a></sup> In fact, the storm surge measured in the 1926 hurricane was the highest ever officially documented on the east coast of South Florida until observers recorded a height of 16.89 ft (5.1 m) at the <a href="/wiki/Burger_King#Structure_and_operations" title="Burger King">Burger King International Headquarters</a> near <a href="/wiki/Cutler,_Florida" title="Cutler, Florida">Cutler</a> in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Dade County</a> during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> in <a href="/wiki/1992_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1992 Atlantic hurricane season">1992</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duedall2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duedall2-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrew1992tcr_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andrew1992tcr-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hurricane's high <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> swept into Miami and <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach,_Florida" title="Miami Beach, Florida">Miami Beach</a>, flooding city streets with knee-deep water. Yachts and large vessels were carried by the intense wind and waves onto shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/MacArthur_Causeway" title="MacArthur Causeway">MacArthur Causeway</a> connecting Miami and Miami Beach was submerged under 6 ft (1.8 m) of water, and communication between the two locales as well as the rest of the United States was cut after all local telecommunications and power lines were blown down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweeps_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sweeps-19">[18]</a></sup> Due to their susceptibility to strong winds, most wooden buildings in Miami were either blown down or lost their roofs. Concrete and steel buildings were warped at their bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_buildings_in_Miami" title="List of tallest buildings in Miami">skyscrapers</a> mostly sustained minor damage, the 18-story <a href="/wiki/Meyer-Kiser_Building" title="Meyer-Kiser Building">Meyer-Kiser Building</a> bore considerable damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Highlights_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Highlights-28">[27]</a></sup> The structure reportedly swayed and vibrated precipitously during the storm; eyewitnesses likened it to the <a href="/wiki/Charleston_(dance)" title="Charleston (dance)">Charleston dance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casinos_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Casinos-29">[28]</a></sup> Many of the injuries in the city were due to ballistic fragments of broken roofing including iron sheeting.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #ACE1AF;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div><i>Waves several feet high were rolling up <a href="/wiki/Las_Olas_Boulevard" title="Las Olas Boulevard">Las Olas Boulevard</a>, which had the appearance of a river rather than a street. ... Practically the entire town was covered with three feet of water.</i></div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">M. A. Hortt, former mayor of Fort Lauderdale<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hortt2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hortt2-30">[29]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Other structures across the region sustained significant damage. Strong winds leveled "hundreds" of working-class homes in <a href="/wiki/Hialeah,_Florida" title="Hialeah, Florida">Hialeah</a> and severely damaged 70% of the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hialeah-1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hialeah-1-31">[30]</a></sup> Winds destroyed the interiors of buildings in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a>, the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">seat</a> of Broward County, and ripped the roof from the Broward County courthouse. Despite having only 12,000 inhabitants, the town sustained severe damage to 3,500 of its buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreHaven-1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MooreHaven-1-4">[4]</a></sup> Nearby, the storm severely damaged the abandoned <a href="/wiki/Houses_of_Refuge_in_Florida#Locations" title="Houses of Refuge in Florida">New River House of Refuge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewRiver_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewRiver-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delray2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delray2-33">[32]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood</a> beach, waves smashed windows and invaded the interior of the Hollywood Beach Hotel. People on the second floor found sand drifts reaching "half way to the ceiling."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell2-34">[33]</a></sup> The storm also ravaged entertainment venues. The storm flattened the <a href="/wiki/Fulford%E2%80%93Miami_Speedway" title="Fulford–Miami Speedway">Fulford–Miami Speedway</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Miami_Beach,_Florida" title="North Miami Beach, Florida">North Miami Beach</a>, which then ceased operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulford1_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fulford1-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulford2_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fulford2-36">[35]</a></sup> Winds peeled into pieces the roof of the <a href="/wiki/Grandstand" title="Grandstand">grandstand</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hialeah_Park_Race_Track" title="Hialeah Park Race Track">Hialeah Race Track</a> and destroyed the kennels, allowing <a href="/wiki/Greyhound_racing" title="Greyhound racing">racing</a> <a href="/wiki/Greyhound" title="Greyhound">greyhounds</a> to escape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hialeah-1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hialeah-1-31">[30]</a></sup> The storm wrecked prominent restaurants and tourist attractions on Miami Beach, including the Million Dollar <a href="/wiki/Pier" title="Pier">Pier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casinos_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Casinos-29">[28]</a></sup> Many historic structures throughout South Florida sustained significant damage, including <a href="/wiki/The_Barnacle_Historic_State_Park" title="The Barnacle Historic State Park">the Barnacle</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villa_Vizcaya" title="Villa Vizcaya">Villa Vizcaya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnacle-1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnacle-1-37">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vizcaya-1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vizcaya-1-38">[37]</a></sup> The storm damaged the main residence at the <a href="/wiki/Bonnet_House" title="Bonnet House">Bonnet House</a>—the only hurricane to do so since it was first built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BonnetHouse_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-BonnetHouse-39">[38]</a></sup> No other storm since 1926 caused a similar level of destruction to the property until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BonnetHouse_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-BonnetHouse-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:30em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #ACE1AF;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div><i>We had never been through a hurricane in 1926, when we experienced our first one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell1-40">[39]</a></sup> ... We didn't know that all windows should be covered in a hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell2-34">[33]</a></sup> ... I was watching as railroad cars were being knocked off the tracks and telegraph poles were snapped like toothpicks. ...[Immediately] almost all the windows on the top floor were broken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell1-40">[39]</a></sup></i></div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Floy Cooke Mitchell, wife of former mayor of Boca Raton J. C. Mitchell</cite></div>
<p>Cities as far north as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Park,_Florida" title="Lake Park, Florida">Lake Park</a> (then called Kelsey City) and <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach</a> in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_County,_Florida" title="Palm Beach County, Florida">Palm Beach County</a> reported many roofs blown off, numerous small buildings destroyed, walls blown down, windows shattered, and trees, shrubs, and other objects torn apart or uprooted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delray2_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Delray2-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PalmPost-1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-PalmPost-1-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PalmPost-2_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-PalmPost-2-42">[41]</a></sup> The worst destruction occurred in the poorer, mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_(people)" title="Black (people)">black</a> sections of the towns, where many homes were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delray2_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Delray2-33">[32]</a></sup> The waters of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Worth_Lagoon" title="Lake Worth Lagoon">Lake Worth Lagoon</a> overflowed their banks, submerging nearby streets, parks, and golf courses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeWorth-1_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-LakeWorth-1-43">[42]</a></sup> High tides piled debris on the streets of <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">Palm Beach</a>, caused a beachfront boardwalk to collapse, and exacerbated previous damage from the <a href="/wiki/1926_Nassau_hurricane" title="1926 Nassau hurricane">July hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PalmPost-1_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-PalmPost-1-41">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>Along the east coast of South Florida, the storm caused widespread, significant beach erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erosion_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erosion-44">[43]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Hillsboro_Inlet_Light" title="Hillsboro Inlet Light">Hillsboro Inlet Light</a>, high tides removed 20 feet (6.1 m) of sand beneath the lighthouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillsboro1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hillsboro1-45">[44]</a></sup> The hurricane swept away much of State Road A1A in Broward and Miami-Dade Counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hortt1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hortt1-46">[45]</a></sup> The combined force of waves and storm surge undermined coastal structures that collapsed, including multi-story casinos on <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach,_Florida" title="Miami Beach, Florida">Miami Beach</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casinos_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Casinos-29">[28]</a></sup> and washed out the coastal bridge on <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_Road_A1A" title="Florida State Road A1A">Florida State Road A1A</a> at <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_Haulover_Inlet" title="Baker's Haulover Inlet">Baker's Haulover Inlet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWSMiamipage-1_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWSMiamipage-1-47">[46]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Boca_Raton,_Florida" title="Boca Raton, Florida">Boca Raton</a>, waves were so large that they rose to the top of the high ridge on the <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier island</a>, though they did not overtop it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delray1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delray1-48">[47]</a></sup> High surf also destroyed a casino at the <a href="/wiki/Boca_Raton,_Florida#Water" title="Boca Raton, Florida">Boca Raton Inlet</a>. "Knee-deep" water east of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_1_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 1 in Florida">U.S. Route 1</a> (Federal Highway) in Boca Raton blocked beach access, but residents waded through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delray1_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Delray1-48">[47]</a></sup> Meanwhile, large waves left much debris and sand drifts several feet deep on State Road A1A in <a href="/wiki/Delray_Beach,_Florida" title="Delray Beach, Florida">Delray Beach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delray2_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-Delray2-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm ravaged cultivated areas throughout South Florida. The storm flooded the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> crop and agricultural fields south of Miami, particularly near <a href="/wiki/Homestead,_Florida" title="Homestead, Florida">Homestead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florida_City,_Florida" title="Florida City, Florida">Florida City</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homestead_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homestead-49">[48]</a></sup> destroying half of the citrus-bearing trees in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> Much of the citrus crop in <a href="/wiki/Dania_Beach,_Florida" title="Dania Beach, Florida">Dania</a> was a total loss as floodwaters submerged the area to depths of 6 ft (1.8 m); flooding lingered for more than a week after the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloodDania_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FloodDania-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Everglades_and_Lake_Okeechobee">Everglades and Lake Okeechobee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Everglades and Lake Okeechobee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A storm surge from Lake Okeechobee entirely inundated <a href="/wiki/Clewiston,_Florida" title="Clewiston, Florida">Clewiston</a>, reportedly leaving numerous bodies along the road connecting the city with Miami. Further inland, the surge burst through frail, earthen, 6-foot (1.8 m) tall muck dikes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleinberg-1_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kleinberg-1-51">[50]</a></sup> submerging <a href="/wiki/Moore_Haven,_Florida" title="Moore Haven, Florida">Moore Haven</a> under 13–15 ft (4.0–4.6 m) of water. Residents scrambled, often unsuccessfully, to safety on rooftops but were swept away by the winds and storm surge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreHaven-1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-MooreHaven-1-4">[4]</a></sup> A nearby drainage dam was destroyed, causing additional flooding of the countryside. Most of the city's buildings were swept off of their original foundations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> Reports by the Red Cross and local authorities indicated that 150 human corpses were found in Moore Haven alone;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreHaven-1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-MooreHaven-1-4">[4]</a></sup> their estimates were incomplete as many bodies were never found, reportedly having been swept deep into the Everglades. Estimates of the dead near Lake Okeechobee ranged as high as 300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreHaven-1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-MooreHaven-1-4">[4]</a></sup> Two years later, another <a href="/wiki/1928_Okeechobee_hurricane#Florida" title="1928 Okeechobee hurricane">Category 4 hurricane</a> killed at least 2,500 people along Lake Okeechobee, but mostly affected the eastern shore, leaving Moore Haven largely unscathed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreHaven-2_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-MooreHaven-2-52">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWSMiamipage-2_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWSMiamipage-2-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gulf_Coast">Gulf Coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gulf Coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Gulf Coast of the Florida peninsula saw comparatively less damage compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Miami" title="Greater Miami">Greater Miami</a> but still suffered significant impacts. A peak storm tide of 11 to 12 ft (3.4 to 3.7 m) affected <a href="/wiki/Punta_Rassa,_Florida" title="Punta Rassa, Florida">Punta Rassa</a> and the islands of <a href="/wiki/Captiva_Island" title="Captiva Island">Captiva</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanibel_Island" title="Sanibel Island">Sanibel</a>, causing $3,000,000 in flood damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edic1_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edic1-54">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1-55">[54]</a></sup> The storm opened <a href="/wiki/Redfish_Pass" title="Redfish Pass">Redfish Pass</a> between Captiva and <a href="/wiki/North_Captiva_Island" title="North Captiva Island">North Captiva</a> islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edic1_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edic1-54">[53]</a></sup> Between <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naples,_Florida" title="Naples, Florida">Naples</a>, strong winds destroyed windows and felled trees and power poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TampaNaples_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-TampaNaples-56">[55]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers,_Florida" title="Fort Myers, Florida">Fort Myers</a>, citrus crops sustained some damage and public utilities were put out of commission. Strong winds uprooted trees in <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg</a>, while heavy rainfall caused flooding in the outlying districts of nearby Tampa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> South of the eye, a storm tide of 8 ft (2.4 m) submerged the streets of <a href="/wiki/Everglades_City,_Florida" title="Everglades City, Florida">Everglades City</a>, forcing people into the upper stories of buildings. Homes that were not secured to their foundations floated away on the tide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TampaNaples_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-TampaNaples-56">[55]</a></sup> Tides reached 4 to 5 ft (1.2 to 1.5 m) as far south as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flamingo,_Florida" title="Flamingo, Florida">Flamingo</a>, sending seaweed, fish, and mud into dwellings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flamingo_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flamingo-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida_Panhandle_and_upper_Gulf_Coast">Florida Panhandle and upper Gulf Coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Florida Panhandle and upper Gulf Coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the hurricane weakened before striking the upper Gulf Coast, its slow movement produced substantial effects to coastal regions between Mobile and Pensacola; these areas experienced heavy damage from wind, rain, and storm surge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TampaNaples_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-TampaNaples-56">[55]</a></sup> Wind records at Pensacola indicate that the city encountered sustained winds of hurricane force for more than 20 hours, including winds above 100 mph (161 km/h) for five hours.. The storm tide destroyed nearly all waterfront structures on Pensacola Bay and peaked at 14 ft (4.3 m) near <a href="/wiki/Bagdad,_Florida" title="Bagdad, Florida">Bagdad, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TampaNaples_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-TampaNaples-56">[55]</a></sup> Rainfall maximized at <a href="/wiki/Bay_Minette,_Alabama" title="Bay Minette, Alabama">Bay Minette, Alabama</a>, where 18.5 inches (470 mm) fell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlabamaRains_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlabamaRains-58">[57]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_Miami_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The disarray in Miami following the hurricane's passage led a breakout of <a href="/wiki/Looting" title="Looting">looting</a> in the city's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Residential_segregation" title="Residential segregation">African American districts</a> that resulted in seven arrests. This unrest prompted the declaration of <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> with the swearing-in of 300 special policemen for voluntary duty. Similarly, 200 policemen were placed on duty in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood, Florida</a>. After a survey indicated that the available food and water supplies would only last 30 days, <a href="/wiki/Hoarding" title="Hoarding">hoarding</a> was banned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-7"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Soup_kitchen" title="Soup kitchen">Soup kitchens</a> were set up in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downtown_Miami#Central_business_district_.28.22Downtown.22.29" title="Downtown Miami">Miami's business district</a> in order to serve food to the recently displaced and as a source for clean drinking water that was contaminated in other areas. The first aid arriving from outside the impacted areas was a relief train guarded by state militiamen that carted medical staff, medicine, potable water, and other relief supplies into Miami immediately following the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breadlines_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Breadlines-59">[58]</a></sup> Afterwards, then-<a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. president</a> <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a> placed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a> on standby should relief efforts necessitate their presence in Florida and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RushAid_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-RushAid-60">[59]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Red_Cross" title="United States Red Cross">Red Cross</a> offered its facilities and the <a href="/wiki/Pullman_Company" title="Pullman Company">Pullman Company</a> offered its resources for use in relief efforts. The <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_of_the_United_States" title="National Guard of the United States">National Guard of the United States</a> dispatched several companies of guardsmen to disaster areas following urgent appeals from then-<a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Florida" title="List of Governors of Florida">Florida governor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_W._Martin" title="John W. Martin">John W. Martin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500Dead_23-8"><a href="#cite_note-500Dead-23">[22]</a></sup> In response to the widespread destruction of buildings on Miami Beach, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_J._Farrey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John J. Farrey (page does not exist)">John J. Farrey</a> was appointed chief building, plumbing and electrical inspector. He initiated and enforced the first building code in the United States, which more than 5,000 US cities duplicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>, the storm caused 372 fatalities, including 114 from the city of Miami, but these totals apparently do not include deaths outside the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pfost1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pfost1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deaths_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deaths-62">[61]</a></sup> Prior to 2003, the National Weather Service had long accepted 243 as the number of deaths, but historical research indicated that this total was far too low. The NWS then updated its totals to reflect the new findings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC-6_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC-6-63">[62]</a></sup> Even the estimates for the United States are uncertain and vary, since there were many people, especially transients and colored migrants in South Florida, listed as "missing". About 43,000 people were left homeless, mostly in the Miami area.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>, located in Coral Gables, had been founded in 1925 and opened its doors for the first time just days after the hurricane passed. The university's athletic teams were <a href="/wiki/Athletic_nickname" title="Athletic nickname">nicknamed</a> the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Hurricanes" title="Miami Hurricanes">Hurricanes</a> in memory of this catastrophe. The school's mascot is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebastian_the_ibis" title="Sebastian the ibis">Sebastian</a>, an <a href="/wiki/American_white_ibis" title="American white ibis">ibis</a>. The ibis is a small white bird that can be seen around South Florida, including on the UM campus. An ibis was selected to represent the Hurricanes because of <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> in which it is typically the last bird to leave before a hurricane strikes and the first to return once it's gone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several events, including the sinking of a ship in the Miami harbor and an embargo by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_East_Coast_Railroad" title="Florida East Coast Railroad">Florida East Coast Railroad</a> before the storm, weakened the <a href="/wiki/Florida_land_boom_of_the_1920s" title="Florida land boom of the 1920s">Florida land boom of the 1920s</a> in South Florida. However, the storm is considered the final blow to end the boom locally. Thousands of newcomers to Florida left the state and cleared their bank accounts, leaving many banks to the brink of bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gannon2012_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gannon2012-65">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:295</sup> As a result, the Great Depression of 1929 did not make a great impact to Florida unlike the rest of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gannon2012_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gannon2012-65">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:298</sup> Many planned developments, which had fallen into deadlock due to insufficient resources, were abandoned due to the economic effects of the hurricane. In Boca Raton, for instance, one planned community by <a href="/wiki/Addison_Mizner" title="Addison Mizner">Addison Mizner</a>, called Villa Rica, was destroyed by the hurricane and never rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gillis1_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gillis1-66">[65]</a></sup> South Florida did not achieve full economic recovery until the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gannon2012_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gannon2012-65">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:324</sup></p>
