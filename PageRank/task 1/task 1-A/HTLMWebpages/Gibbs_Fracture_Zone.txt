<p><b>Charlie-Gibbs Fracture Zone</b> is a system of two parallel <a href="/wiki/Fracture_zone" title="Fracture zone">fracture zones</a>. It is the most prominent interruption of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. It can be traced over more than 2000 kilometers, all the way from north-east of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> to south-west of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. It took 90 million years for the fault to grow to this length.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform fault</a> of the southern fracture zone displaces the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, coming from the <a href="/wiki/Azores_Triple_Junction" title="Azores Triple Junction">Azores Triple Junction</a>, to the west over a distance of 120 km. At <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> 31.75W a south to north <a href="/wiki/Seismicity" title="Seismicity">seismically active</a> <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> with a length of 40 km connects the western end of the southern transform to the eastern end of the northern transform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The northern transform fault displaces the spreading ridge over another 230 km to the west before it connects to the northern part of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge going to Iceland. Thus the total offset of the system is 350 kilometers.</p>
<p>Both transform faults continue eastward and westward as inactive fracture zones.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1963 the existence of a transform fault near <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> 53N was first postulated on the basis of earthquake <a href="/wiki/Epicenter" title="Epicenter">epicenter</a> data by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruce_Heezen" title="Bruce Heezen">Bruce Heezen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ewing" title="Maurice Ewing">Maurice Ewing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Also the study of <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">ocean</a> currents indicated that there should be a deep passage through the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. In 1966 the area was investigated by <a href="/wiki/USCGC_Spar_(WLB-403)" title="USCGC Spar (WLB-403)">USCGC <i>Spar</i> (WLB-403)</a> on its return from an <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrographic_survey" title="Hydrographic survey">survey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The fault was named Charlie Fracture Zone after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USCG" title="USCG">USCG</a> Ocean Weather Station Charlie at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;params=52_45_N_35_30_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">52°45′N</span> <span class="longitude">35°30′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">52.750°N 35.500°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">52.750; -35.500</span></span></span></a></span>, athwart the fault. In July 1968 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USNS_Josiah_Willard_Gibbs_(T-AGOR-1)" title="USNS Josiah Willard Gibbs (T-AGOR-1)">USNS <i>Josiah Willard Gibbs</i> (T-AGOR-1)</a> conducted a more extended survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was proposed that the fracture zone be renamed Gibbs Fracture Zone, as fracture zones are generally named for research vessels. The proposal was accepted only in part, and currently the official name is Charlie-Gibbs Fracture Zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gebco_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-gebco-5">[5]</a></sup> Note that the double name refers to the two parallel fracture zones together. The individual fracture zones have to be referred to as Charlie-Gibbs North and South.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seamounts">Seamounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seamounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The transform area contains two named seamounts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Minia Seamount at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;params=53_01_N_34_58_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">53°01′N</span> <span class="longitude">34°58′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">53.017°N 34.967°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">53.017; -34.967</span></span></span></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gebco_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-gebco-5">[5]</a></sup> It is located inside the corner of the northern branch of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge and the northern transform fault. This seamount is named after the ship Minia (1866-1922)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-American_Telegraph_Company" title="Anglo-American Telegraph Company">Anglo-American Telegraph Company</a>. The Minia is known for recovering bodies and artifacts from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic#Retrieval_and_burial_of_the_dead" title="Titanic">Titanic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Hecate Seamount at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;params=52_17_N_31_00_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">52°17′N</span> <span class="longitude">31°00′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">52.283°N 31.000°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">52.283; -31.000</span></span></span></a></span>, named after <a href="/wiki/HMS_Hecate_(A137)" title="HMS Hecate (A137)">HMS <i>Hecate</i> (A137)</a>. It is located on the northern wall of the southern transform fault east of the short spreading ridge.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_area">Protected area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlie-Gibbs_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protected area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>Charlie-Gibbs Marine Protected Area</b> is a conservation area in the Charlie-Gibbs fracture zone in North Atlantic international waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
