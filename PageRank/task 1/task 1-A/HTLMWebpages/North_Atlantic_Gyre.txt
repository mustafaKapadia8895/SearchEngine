<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">North Atlantic Gyre</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>North Atlantic Gyre</b>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, is one of the five major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_gyre" title="Oceanic gyre">oceanic gyres</a>. It is a circular system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_currents" title="Ocean currents">ocean currents</a> that stretches across the North Atlantic from near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> almost to <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and from the east coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> to the west coasts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.</p>
<p>The currents that compose the North Atlantic Gyre include the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> in the west, the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> in the north, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Current" title="Canary Current">Canary Current</a> in the east, and the Atlantic <a href="/wiki/North_Equatorial_Current" title="North Equatorial Current">North Equatorial Current</a> in the south. This gyre is particularly important for the central role it plays in the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, bringing salty water west from the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and then north to form the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>.</p>
<p>This gyre is similar to the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Gyre" title="North Pacific Gyre">North Pacific Gyre</a> in the way it traps man-made <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">marine debris</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Garbage_Patch" title="North Atlantic Garbage Patch">North Atlantic Garbage Patch</a>, similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch" title="Great Pacific Garbage Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a> in the North <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lavlaw_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lavlaw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The North Atlantic Gyre forms the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a>, noted for its still waters and dense seaweed accumulations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_variability">Seasonal variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_Gyre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Seasonal variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with many oceanographic patterns, the North Atlantic Gyre experiences seasonal changes. Stramma and Siedler (1988) determined that the gyre expands and contracts with a seasonal variance; however, the magnitude of volume transport does not seem to change significantly. During the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> season, the gyre follows a more zonal pattern; that is, it expands in the east-west direction and thins in the north-south direction. As the seasons move from winter to summer, the gyre shifts south by a few degrees latitude. This occurs concurrently with the displacement of the northeastern part of the gyre. It has been concluded that <a href="/wiki/Zonal_and_meridional" title="Zonal and meridional">zonal</a> deviations within the gyre remain small while north and south of the gyre they are large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Data collected in the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a> region in the western part of the North Atlantic Gyre has led to analytical evidence that the variability of this gyre is linked to wintertime <a href="/wiki/Convective_mixing" title="Convective mixing">convective mixing</a>. According to Bates (2001), a seasonal variation of 8-10 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_temperature" title="Surface temperature">surface temperature</a> occurs alongside a fluctuation in the <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed layer</a> depth between the Northern Hemisphere winter and summer seasons. The depth rises from 200 meters in winter to about 10 meters in summer. Nutrients remain below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphotic_zone" title="Euphotic zone">euphotic zone</a> for most of the year, resulting in low <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a>. Yet during winter convective mixing, nutrients penetrate the euphotic zone, causing a short-lived <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> bloom in the spring. This then lifts the mixed-layer depth to 10 meters.</p>
<p>The changes in oceanic <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and vertical mixing between winter and summer in the North Atlantic Gyre seasonally alter the total amount of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>. Interannual trends have established that carbon dioxide concentrations within this gyre are increasing at a similar rate to that occurring in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. This discovery concurs with that made in the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Gyre" title="North Pacific Gyre">North Pacific Gyre</a>. The North Atlantic Gyre also undergoes <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> changes via atmospheric wave patterns. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a> (NAO) is one such pattern. During its positive phase, the gyre warms. This is due to a weakening of the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerly winds</a>, resulting in reduced <a href="/wiki/Wind_stress" title="Wind stress">wind stress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat exchange</a>, providing a greater period of time for the gyre water temperatures to rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lead_contamination">Lead contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_Gyre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lead contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Collections of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">marine</a> particles, and ocean water in the North Atlantic Gyre from 1990-1992 have led to measurements in <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> ratios. These measurements exceed those associated with atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> carried from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a>, implying that the majority of lead <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a> in the gyre in the early 1990s was primarily caused by American emissions. The surface layers of the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a> were used to estimate the lead isotopic concentrations. 42-57% of the lead isotope concentrations result from American <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive" title="Automotive">automotive</a> contamination, despite the reduction in the production and use of leaded <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> in the United States. However, lead concentrations from measurements made after 1992 show a decrease in the amount of lead contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
