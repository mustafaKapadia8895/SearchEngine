<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Labrador Sea Water</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Labrador Sea Water (LSW)</b> is an intermediate water mass characterized by cold water, relatively low salinity compared to other intermediate water masses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and high concentrations of both oxygen and anthropogenic tracers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickart_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pickart-2">[2]</a></sup> It is formed by convective processes in the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea" title="Labrador Sea">Labrador Sea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yash_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yash-3">[3]</a></sup> located between <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and the northeast coast of the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Peninsula" title="Labrador Peninsula">Labrador Peninsula</a>. Deep convection in the Labrador Sea allows colder water to sink forming this water mass, which is a contributor to the upper layer of <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>. North Atlantic Deep Water flowing southward is integral to the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Labrador Sea experiences a net heat loss to the atmosphere annually.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">Convection</a> in the Labrador Sea is the result of a combination of cyclonic oceanographic circulation of the sea currents and cyclonic atmospheric forcing. At the southern tip of Greenland, water enters the <a href="/wiki/West_Greenland_Current" title="West Greenland Current">West Greenland Current</a> from the <a href="/wiki/East_Greenland_Current" title="East Greenland Current">East Greenland Current</a>, continues to flow northwest around the Baffin Bay, and then southeast into the Baffin Island Current continuing in the same direction in the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Current" title="Labrador Current">Labrador Current</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">Sea ice</a> in the winter months inhibits surface flow into Baffin Bay. The Labrador Current and the WGC flow in opposite directions resulting in a cyclonic <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddy</a>. During winter months low pressure dominates in this region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in years with a positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a> deeper convection is observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spreading">Spreading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spreading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>LSW spreads through the North Atlantic Ocean by three routes: northeast directly into the Irminger Sea, into the eastern North Atlantic by means of the deep North Atlantic current, and meridionally via the Deep Western Boundary Current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickart_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pickart-2">[2]</a></sup> Oceanagrapher Robert Pickart, in a paper published in 2002, presented data that suggests that the Labrador Sea is not the only formation site for LSW. They observed similar convective processes in the Irminger Sea and noted that transit times for LSW into Irminger Sea were unusually fast, suggesting that there is another source for LSW in the Irminger Sea.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variability">Variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labrador_Sea_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>LSW properties experience seasonal and interannual variations. In late spring and summer, large amounts of cold freshwater accumulate from melting ice and are mixed downward during convection. The source for heat in the Labrador Sea is modified North Atlantic Current water after circulating the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_gyres&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ocean gyres (page does not exist)">subpolar gyre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In winter the sea becomes more saline as freshwater freezes to form sea ice. The greatest seasonal variability is largely confined to the surface waters, however an annual cycle of convective mixing and re-stratification is observed throughout the water column. Warming and increased salinity in the lower level and freshening at the surface is associated with re-stratification (May–December), whereas a convective mixing period (January–April) leads to cooling and a decrease in salt content in intermediate and deep waters and an increase in salt content at the surface. Interannual variations in the intermediate LSW are due largely to changes in convectivity throughout these periods. Weak convective periods are associated with more heat in the water column and deep convective periods are characterized by cold water. In the early 1990s, several consecutive severe winters contributed towards deep convection in the Labrador Sea. These winters were also associated with strong positive fluctuations in the North Atlantic Oscillation. LSW became very cold, fresh, and dense during this period, and the layer extended to depths of 2300m in the spring of 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yash_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yash-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to weakened convection, LSW began warming significantly and increased in salinity over the following decade. This trend continued through 2010 and 2011 when weak convection was observed in relation with negative NAO. Deep convection was observed again in 2012 with the LSW reaching 1400m, corresponding with a positive NAO similar to those seen in the early 1990s.</p>
