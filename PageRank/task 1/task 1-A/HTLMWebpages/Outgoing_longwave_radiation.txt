<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Outgoing longwave radiation</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Outgoing Longwave Radiation</b> (<b>OLR</b>) is the energy radiating from the Earth as infrared radiation at low <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> to Space.</p>
<p>OLR is <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> emitted from Earth and its atmosphere out to space in the form of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Radiant_flux" title="Radiant flux">flux</a> of energy transported by outgoing longwave radiation is measured in <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">W/m²</a>.</p>
<p>Over 99% of outgoing longwave radiation has wavelengths between 4 µm and 100 µm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in the thermal <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>. Contributions with wavelengths larger than 40 µm are small, therefore often only wavelengths up to 50 µm are considered . In the wavelength range between 4 µm and 10 µm the spectrum of outgoing longwave radiation overlaps that of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">solar radiation</a>, and for various applications different cut-off wavelengths between the two may be chosen.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">Radiative cooling</a> by outgoing longwave radiation is the primary way the Earth System loses energy. The balance between this loss and the energy gained by radiative heating from incoming solar <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_radiation" title="Shortwave radiation">shortwave radiation</a> determines global heating or cooling of the Earth system (<a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Energy budget of Earth’s climate</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Local differences between radiative heating and cooling provide the energy that drives atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_fluid_dynamics" title="Geophysical fluid dynamics">dynamics</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_energy_radiation">Atmospheric energy radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outgoing_longwave_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Atmospheric energy radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OLR is a critical component of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Earth's energy budget</a>, and represents the total radiation going to space emitted by the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTRS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTRS-3">[3]</a></sup> Earth's radiation balance is quite closely achieved since the OLR very nearly equals the Shortwave Absorbed Radiation received at high energy from the sun. Thus, the Earth's average temperature is very nearly stable. The OLR is affected by clouds and dust in the atmosphere, which tend to reduce it to below clear sky values.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_greenhouse_effect">Role in greenhouse effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outgoing_longwave_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role in greenhouse effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">Greenhouse gases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O), <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), absorb certain wavelengths of OLR adding heat to the atmosphere, which in turn causes the respective absorbing layer of the atmosphere to emit more radiation. Some of this radiation is directed back towards the Earth, increasing the average temperature of the Earth's surface. Therefore, an increase in the concentration of a greenhouse gas would contribute to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> by increasing the amount of radiation that is absorbed and emitted by these atmospheric constituents.</p>
<p>The OLR is dependent on the temperature of the radiating body. It is affected by the Earth's skin temperature, skin surface emissivity, atmospheric temperature, water vapor profile, and cloud cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTRS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NTRS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OLR_measurements">OLR measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outgoing_longwave_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: OLR measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Longwave radiation typically refers to radiation in the spectral region from 3 to 100 microns. In the Earth’s climate system, longwave radiation involves processes of absorption, scattering, and emissions from atmospheric gases, aerosols, clouds and the surface. Measuring outgoing longwave radiation at the top of atmosphere and downwelling longwave radiation at the surface are important for understanding how much radiative energy is kept in our climate system, how much reaches and warms the surface, and how the energy in the atmosphere is distributed to affect developments of clouds.</p>
<p>Outgoing longwave radiation (OLR) has been monitored globally since 1975 by a number of successful and valuable satellite missions. These missions include broadband measurements from the Earth Radiation Balance (ERB) instrument on the Nimbus-6 and Nimbus-7 satellites;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Radiation_Budget_Experiment" title="Earth Radiation Budget Experiment">Earth Radiation Budget Experiment (ERBE)</a> scanner and the ERBE non scanner on NOAA-9, NOAA-10 and NASA Earth Radiation Budget Satellite (ERBS); <a href="/wiki/Clouds_and_the_Earth%27s_Radiant_Energy_System" title="Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System">The Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System (CERES)</a> instrument aboard NASA's Aqua and Terra satellites; and <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_Earth_Radiation_Budget" title="Geostationary Earth Radiation Budget">Geostationary Earth Radiation Budget instrument (GERB)</a> instrument on the Meteosat Second Generation (MSG) satellite.</p>
<p>Downwelling longwave radiation at the surface is mainly measured by <a href="/wiki/Pyrgeometer" title="Pyrgeometer">Pyrgeometer</a>. A most notable ground-based network for monitoring surface longwave radiation is <a href="/wiki/Baseline_Surface_Radiation_Network" title="Baseline Surface Radiation Network">Baseline Surface Radiation Network (BSRN)</a>, which provides crucial well-calibrated measurements for studying <a href="/wiki/Global_dimming" title="Global dimming">global dimming</a> and brightening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OLR_calculation_and_simulation">OLR calculation and simulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outgoing_longwave_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: OLR calculation and simulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many applications call for calculation of longwave radiation quantities: the balance of global incoming shortwave to outgoing longwave <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative flux</a> determines the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Energy budget of Earth’s climate</a>; local <a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">radiative cooling</a> by outgoing longwave radiation (and heating by shortwave radiation) drive the temperature and dynamics of different parts of the atmosphere; from the <a href="/wiki/Radiance" title="Radiance">radiance</a> from a particular direction measured by an instrument, atmospheric properties (like <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> or <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> can be <a href="/wiki/Inverse_problem" title="Inverse problem">retrieved</a>, etc. Calculations of these quantities solve the <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative transfer equations</a> that describe radiation in the atmosphere. Usually the solution is done numerically by an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_radiative_transfer_codes" title="Atmospheric radiative transfer codes">Atmospheric radiative transfer code</a> adapted to the specific problem.</p>
