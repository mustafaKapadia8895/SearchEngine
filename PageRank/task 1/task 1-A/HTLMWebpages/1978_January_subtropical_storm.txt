<p>The <b>1978 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was the last <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> to use an all-female naming list. The hurricane season officially began on June 1, and ended on November 30. It was an above average season due to a subsiding <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>. The first storm, a subtropical storm, developed unusually early – on January 18 – and dissipated five days later without causing any damage. At the end of July and early August, short-lived <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Amelia_(1978)" title="Tropical Storm Amelia (1978)">Tropical Storm Amelia</a> caused extensive flooding in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> after dropping as much as 48 in (1,200 mm) of rain. There were 33 deaths and $110 million (1978 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) in damage. Tropical Storm Bess and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cora_(1978)" title="Hurricane Cora (1978)">Hurricane Cora</a> resulted in only minor land impacts, while the latter was attributed to one fatality.</p>
<p>Later in August, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Debra_(1978)" title="Tropical Storm Debra (1978)">Tropical Storm Debra</a> produced widespread effects, though damage was also relatively minor. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ella_(1978)" title="Hurricane Ella (1978)">Hurricane Ella</a> became the northernmost Category 4 hurricane while located at <a href="/wiki/38th_parallel_north" title="38th parallel north">38°N</a>, and lashed the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> with gusty winds and rip currents. Hurricanes Flossie and Kendra as well as Tropical Storms Hope, Irma, and Juliet caused minimal land impacts as a tropical cyclone. However, the precursor to Hurricane Kendra caused flooding in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Greta%E2%80%93Olivia" title="Hurricane Greta–Olivia">Hurricane Greta</a> brought strong winds, high tides, and flooding to Central America, particularly <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. Greta resulted in about $25 million in damage and at least five fatalities. Overall, the storms of this season collectively caused $191 million in damage and 42 fatalities. Hurricane Greta crossed into the eastern pacific and was renamed Olivia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_summary">Season summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Season summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<center>

<p>The Atlantic hurricane season officially began on June 1, 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seasondates_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-seasondates-1">[1]</a></sup> Although 24 tropical cyclones developed, only twelve of them reached tropical storm intensity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> which is slightly above the 1966-2009 average of 11.3 named storms per season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-climo-3">[3]</a></sup> Of the twelve tropical storms, five of them strengthened into a hurricane,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> which is slightly below the 1966-2009 average of 6.2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-climo-3">[3]</a></sup> Two of the five hurricane became major hurricanes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> which is Category 3 or greater on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sshws_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sshws-4">[4]</a></sup> Three tropical storms and two hurricanes made landfall during the season and caused at least 41 fatalities and $135 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Additionally, the precursor to Hurricane Kendra brought flooding to Puerto Rico, with $6 million in damage and one death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-6">[6]</a></sup> The season officially ended on November 30, 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seasondates_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-seasondates-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a> began very early, with the development of a subtropical storm on January 18. It dissipated about five days later. However, the next tropical cyclone, an unnumbered depression, did not develop until June 21. In July, there were two systems, including an unnumbered tropical depression and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Amelia_(1978)" title="Tropical Storm Amelia (1978)">Tropical Storm Amelia</a>. Seven tropical cyclones formed in August, including Tropical Depression Four and tropical storms Bess and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Debra_(1978)" title="Tropical Storm Debra (1978)">Debra</a> and hurricanes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cora_(1978)" title="Hurricane Cora (1978)">Cora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ella_(1978)" title="Hurricane Ella (1978)">Ella</a>. There were also seven system in September – tropical depressions Eight, Nine, and Twelve, Tropical Storm Hope, and hurricanes Flossie and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Greta%E2%80%93Olivia" title="Hurricane Greta–Olivia">Greta</a>. During the month of October, there were five tropical cyclones, with two unnumbered tropical depressions, tropical storms Irma and Juliet, and Hurricane Kendra. There was another unnumbered tropical depression in November, which dissipated on November 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season's activity was reflected with an <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 63.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRDat_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRDat-7">[7]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. It is only calculated for full advisories on tropical systems at or exceeding 39 mph (63 km/h), which is tropical storm strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the middle of January, an upper-level trough in the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> spawned a surface <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> to the east-northeast of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> to the south of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>. Isolated from the detrimental effects of the westerlies, it was initially <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">non-tropical</a> in nature and intensified through a <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> energy source, or one that derives energy from the interaction of cold and warm air. Convection increased slightly despite cool sea surface temperatures of around 75 °F (24 °C). At 1200 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on January 18, it organized into a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical depression</a> about 1,725 mi (2,776 km) east-northeast of Puerto Rico while moving in a general westward track, which it would maintain for much of its duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) initiated <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak classifications</a> on the cyclone at 0000 UTC on January 19, assessing a Dvorak number of T2.5, suggesting both tropical and subtropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On the same day, the pressure gradient between the storm and the ridge produced gale-force winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-6">[6]</a></sup> By early on January 20, the storm maintained minimal convection near its center, with its primary <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainband</a> revolved cyclonically around its well-defined center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Later that day, the storm strengthened into a subtropical storm and attained peak winds of 45 mph (72 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> supported by both ship and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late on January 21, the outer rainbands to the south and east of the center began diminishing, which began a weakening trend after the storm maintained peak winds for about 36 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> At around that time, the cyclone was moving west-southwestward, and within 72 hours was forecast by one <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">hurricane forecast model</a> to be located over <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By midday on January 22, the winds decreased to below gale force after the convection dissipated near the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Subsequently, it turned more to the west away from land, and by January 23 the circulation degenerated into a remnant trough about 185 mi (298 km) north of the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> The storm was one of six tropical or subtropical cyclones on record in the month of January, and one of four to have formed in the month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Amelia_(1978)" title="Tropical Storm Amelia (1978)">Tropical Storm Amelia (1978)</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> emerged into the Atlantic Ocean from the west coast of Africa on July 19. The wave did not develop significantly while crossing the Atlantic Ocean and Caribbean Sea. The disturbance then entered an area of the Gulf of Mexico that was conducive to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> and became a tropical depression while located about 30 mi (48 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville, Texas</a> on July 30. Despite its proximity to land, the depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Amelia on July 31 and peaked with winds of 50 mph (80 km/h). Around that time, Amelia made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Port_Isabel,_Texas" title="Port Isabel, Texas">Port Isabel, Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atcr1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-atcr1-16">[16]</a></sup> Later on July 31, the storm weakened to a tropical depression and dissipated early the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amelia affected the Texas coast for two days, causing several shipping incidents and minor damage in Corpus Christi and South Padre Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atcr2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-atcr2-17">[17]</a></sup> While active, there were no deaths linked to the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toledo_Blade_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toledo_Blade-18">[18]</a></sup> However, the biggest impact from the storm followed its dissipation, when its remnants contributed to record rainfall totals over the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-19">[19]</a></sup> The state, already suffering from a previous drought, believed that the rain would help alleviate the conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, the dry ground aided the flooding from the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times-Union_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times-Union-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaver_County_Times_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beaver_County_Times-22">[22]</a></sup> The rainfall caused several rivers and creeks to flood, especially around the Texas Hill Country and northern Texas, leading to severe damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atcr2_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-atcr2-17">[17]</a></sup> Overall, Amelia caused 33 fatalities with an estimated $110 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texashurricanes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-texashurricanes-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Evening_Independent_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Evening_Independent-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>A low-pressure area developed in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> along a dissipating <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> on August 1. The system detached from the cold front and drifted southwestward, reaching northeastern Gulf of Mexico on August 3. Satellite imagery, buoys, and reconnaissance aircraft flights indicated that by August 5, the system likely acquired a closed circulation. Thus, the National Hurricane Center estimated that a tropical depression developed in the central Gulf of Mexico at 1200 UTC that day. On August 6, a reconnaissance flight into the depression resulted in an upgrade to Tropical Storm Bess, while located about 250 mi (400 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville, Texas</a>. The storm had initially headed west-southwestward at 8 mph (13 km/h), before moving southwestward at nearly the same speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btcr_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-btcr-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 1200 UTC on August 7, Bess attained its minimum barometric pressure of 1,005 mbar (29.7 inHg). Thereafter, Bess began to turn nearly due southward under the influence of a high-pressure area over southern Texas. Later on August 7, the storm reached its maximum sustained winds of 50 mph (80 km/h). Early on August 8, Bess made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Nautla,_Veracruz" title="Nautla, Veracruz">Nautla, Veracruz</a>, at the same intensity and then rapidly dissipated inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btcr_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-btcr-25">[25]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuxpan,_Veracruz" title="Tuxpan, Veracruz">Tuxpan, Veracruz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a>, sustained winds reached only 29 mph (47 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btcr2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-btcr2-26">[26]</a></sup> The storm also produced heavy rainfall, peaking at 12.04 in (306 mm) in La Estrella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However, no flooding occurred and no damage or fatalities were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btcr2_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-btcr2-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cora" title="Hurricane Cora">Hurricane Cora</a></div>
<p>A disturbance exited the west coast of Africa on August 4 and developed into a tropical depression about three days later, while located well east of the Lesser Antilles. On August 8, the depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Cora. The storm moved at an unusually high forward speed for a cyclone in the Atlantic Ocean in August, and intensified into a hurricane later that day. The hurricane was upgraded into a hurricane based solely on satellite photography, the second time this occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctcr1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctcr1-28">[28]</a></sup> Early on August 9, it peaked with winds of 90 mph (140 km/h). Around 0000 UTC the next day, Cora weakened to a tropical storm while moving west-southwestward. The storm made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> on August 11, while weakening to a tropical depression. Cora weakened further to a tropical depression, before losing its circulation and degenerating into a tropical wave on August 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctcr4_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctcr4-29">[29]</a></sup> The remnant crossed over Central America into the Pacific Ocean, where it regenerated into <a href="/wiki/1978_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Kristy" title="1978 Pacific hurricane season">Hurricane Kristy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropicalsystems_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-tropicalsystems-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cora was an unusual cyclone, maintaining an unusually low latitude in the Atlantic in August at high speeds. While passing through the Lesser Antilles, gusty winds and light rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctcr1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ctcr1-28">[28]</a></sup> In the latter, a person died after stepping on a high tension power line that was downed in <a href="/wiki/Castries" title="Castries">Castries</a>. Approximately two to four percent of trees on the island were toppled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Virgin_Islands_Daily_News_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Virgin_Islands_Daily_News-31">[31]</a></sup> Cora was also responsible for altering weather conditions in <a href="/wiki/Presque_Isle,_Maine" title="Presque Isle, Maine">Presque Isle, Maine</a>, allowing for a takeoff of the historical flight of the Double Eagle II hot air balloon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Mexican_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Mexican-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> that developed over southeast <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and an area of convection near the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> merged and resulted in the formation of a tropical depression over the southern Gulf of Mexico on August 26. Tacking west-northwestward around a high-pressure ridge, the depression gradually intensified began and was upgraded to Tropical Storm Debra on August 28. Debra turned to the north and reached its peak intensity of 60 mph (97 km/h) on August 29, shortly before making landfall in southwestern Louisiana. It was a disorganized storm, with most of the convection located to the east of the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-33">[33]</a></sup> The system weakened rapidly and dissipated over Arkansas on August 29, though its remnants continued into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Valley" title="Ohio Valley">Ohio Valley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>One person died while attempting to evacuate an oil rig to the south of <a href="/wiki/Cameron,_Louisiana" title="Cameron, Louisiana">Cameron, Louisiana</a>. Damage caused by Debra was considered minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-33">[33]</a></sup> In Louisiana, rainfall peaked at 10.81 in (275 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_Bayou,_Louisiana" title="Freshwater Bayou, Louisiana">Freshwater Bayou</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-19">[19]</a></sup> However, no flooding was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-33">[33]</a></sup> Wind impacts were light and mainly limited to down trees and damage to roofs in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Charles,_Louisiana" title="Lake Charles, Louisiana">Lake Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spokane_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-spokane-35">[35]</a></sup> The storm spawned several tornadoes in Arkansas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ths-4_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ths-4-36">[36]</a></sup> Mississippi, Louisiana,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-34">[34]</a></sup> Tennessee,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ths-5_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ths-5-37">[37]</a></sup> and Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc2_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-34">[34]</a></sup> In Mississippi, a tornado in <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Springs,_Mississippi" title="Crystal Springs, Mississippi">Crystal Springs</a> destroyed three mobile homes and a house, killing one person and seriously injuring another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ella_(1978)" title="Hurricane Ella (1978)">Hurricane Ella (1978)</a></div>
<p>A cold front spawned a tropical disturbance near Bermuda, which became a tropical depression on August 30. The depression strengthened, and by early on August 31, it was upgraded to Tropical Storm Ella. Ship reports indicated that Ella became a hurricane later that day. Further significant intensification occurred, and the storm reached a preliminary peak intensity of 125 mph (201 km/h). A <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">short-wave trough</a> over the Eastern United States caused Ella to decelerate and turn north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-etcr1-39">[39]</a></sup> Simultaneously, dry air diminished convection on September 2, which in turn resulted in weakening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr2_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-etcr2-41">[41]</a></sup> Eventually, another trough forced Ella to re-curve northeastward, thereby remaining well offshore the East Coast of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr2_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-etcr2-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm then re-intensified and by 1200 UTC on September 4, Ella peaked as a Category 4 hurricane with winds of 140 mph (230 km/h). Thereafter, Ella rapidly weakened as it passed offshore Atlantic Canada, before being absorbed by an extratropical storm while located more than 700 mi (1,100 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador">St. John's</a> on September 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr2_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-etcr2-41">[41]</a></sup> Ella threatened to pass within 50 mi (80 km) of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Because of this, a hurricane watch was issued for the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of North Carolina during <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day Weekend</a>, resulting in a significant drop in tourism occurred. However, because the storm veered northeastward,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr2_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-etcr2-41">[41]</a></sup> little effects other than 5 to 9 ft (1.5 to 2.7 m) waves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> minor beach erosion, and light winds in coastal portions of North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In Newfoundland, Ella produced rainfall amounts reaching 2.39 in (61 mm) and wind gusts up to 71 mph (114 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical wave passed westward across <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> on August 31 and entered the Atlantic Ocean later that day. Convection markedly increased over the next few days and by 0000 UTC on September 4, the wave developed into a tropical depression while located about midway between Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. Later that day, the <i>Hong Kong Merchant</i> reported tropical storm force winds, thus the depression was upgraded to Tropical Storm Flossie. It initially tracked northwestward at 23 mph (37 km/h) and minimal strengthening occurred, possibly due to rapid forward speeds. On September 5, the storm curved westward, until turning north on September 7. A high-pressure area transitioned into a trough, causing Flossie to re-curve northeastward and generating strong upper-level winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr1-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 8, Flossie was downgraded to a tropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr1_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr1-46">[46]</a></sup> After the trough began weakening, favorable conditions returned, allowing Flossie to re-strengthen into a tropical storm on September 10. Flossie then decelerated and became nearly stationary on September 12. Around that time, the storm was upgraded to a hurricane. Further intensification continued, and Flossie peaked with winds of 100 mph (160 km/h) early on September 13. The storm began turned nearly due northward and began weakening. Flossie accelerated to the northeast and eventually transitioned into an extratropical cyclone while 700 miles (1,100 km) north of the Azores on September 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr2-47">[47]</a></sup> The strong <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> brought winds as high as 104 mph (167 km/h) to <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle" title="Fair Isle">Fair Isle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical wave developed into a tropical depression near <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> on September 13. By the following day, the depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Greta. It headed westward to west-northwestward across the Caribbean Sea and slowly intensified, becoming a hurricane on September 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr1_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr1-49">[49]</a></sup> The rate of intensification increased as Greta was approaching the northwestern Caribbean Sea. Greta briefly peaked as a Category 4 hurricane with maximum sustained winds of 130 mph (210 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 947 mbar (28.0 inHg), while brushing northeastern <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. Although the storm remained offshore, land interaction caused significant weakening. On September 19, Greta made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Stann_Creek_District" title="Stann Creek District">Stann Creek District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> with winds of 110 mph (180 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr2_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr2-50">[50]</a></sup> The storm rapidly weakened inland over <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, but survived its passage and eventually became Hurricane Olivia in the Eastern Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr3_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr3-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early in its duration, Greta produced heavy rainfall in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr1_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr1-49">[49]</a></sup> With a similar path to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fifi-Orlene" title="Hurricane Fifi-Orlene">Hurricane Fifi</a> four years prior, Greta threatened to reproduce the devastating effects of the catastrophic storm;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> however, damage and loss of life was significantly less than feared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr3_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr3-51">[51]</a></sup> In Honduras, about 1,200 homes were damaged, about half of which in towns along the coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-west-53">[53]</a></sup> The storm damaged about 75% of the houses on <a href="/wiki/Roat%C3%A1n" title="Roatán">Roatán</a> along the offshore <a href="/wiki/Bay_Islands_Department" title="Bay Islands Department">Bay Islands</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and there was one death in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr3_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr3-51">[51]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Belize_Barrier_Reef" title="Belize Barrier Reef">Belize Barrier Reef</a>, Greta downed trees and produced high waves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> while on the mainland, there was minimal flooding despite a high <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr3_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr3-51">[51]</a></sup> In Dangriga where it made landfall, the hurricane damaged or destroyed 125 houses and the primary hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw6_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-hw6-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw7_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-hw7-57">[57]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Belize_City" title="Belize City">Belize City</a>, a tornado flipped over a truck and damaged four houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Damage in Belize was estimated at $25 million, and there were four deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gtcr3_51-4"><a href="#cite_note-gtcr3-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>A mid-tropospheric low-pressure area developed over the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a> on September 10. The system developed into a subtropical depression early on September 12, while located about 75 mi (121 km) east of <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine,_Florida" title="St. Augustine, Florida">St. Augustine, Florida</a>. Over the next few days, the depression tracked east-northeastward to eastward. While strengthening into a subtropical storm on September 15, it passed just north of Bermuda,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-htcr1_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-htcr1-59">[59]</a></sup> but produced only 1.07 in (27 mm) of rain on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-19">[19]</a></sup> The storm then made a brief dip to the east-southeast, before resuming its east-northeastward course on September 16. Beginning on the following day, satellite imagery indicated that the system was acquiring tropical characteristics. As a result, it was reclassified as Tropical Storm Hope at 0600 UTC on September 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-htcr1_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-htcr1-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because Hope remained out of range of reconnaissance aircraft flights, the National Hurricane Center relied on ships and satellite estimates. After becoming a tropical cyclone, Hope began to accelerate while slowly intensifying. Satellite estimates at 1200 UTC on September 19 indicated that the storm attained its peak intensity with maximum sustained winds of 65 mph (105 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 987 mbar (29.1 inHg), recorded by the <i>S.S. Banglar Mann</i>. While located hundreds of miles north of the Azores on September 20, the storm turned northward<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-htcr1_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-htcr1-59">[59]</a></sup> and began crossing into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a> of 68 °F (20 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hdisc_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-hdisc-60">[60]</a></sup> By 1200 UTC on September 21, Hope transitioned into an extratropical cyclone<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-htcr1_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-htcr1-59">[59]</a></sup> and was absorbed by another extratropical storm while situated about 220 mi (350 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hdisc_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-hdisc-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the 2017 storm of the same name, see <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a>.</div>
<p>The origins of Tropical Storm Irma were from a subtropical depression that formed about 500 mi (800 km) south of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> on October 2. During the next two days, thunderstorm activity gradually increased around the circulation center as the storm drifted northward. On October 2, the storm had taken the appearance of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> on satellite photographs, and upper-level <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a> flow over the center of the storm was evident on satellite time-lapse movies. By the afternoon of October 4, the system had acquired the characteristics of a tropical storm and was named Irma; gale-force winds extended 150 mi (240 km) from the center of circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADV1_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADV1-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Six hours after being named, Irma reached its peak intensity of 50 mph (80 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTK_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-BTK-63">[63]</a></sup> On October 5, Irma turned towards the north-northeast and passed about midway between the central and western <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-61">[61]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, Irma became less organized,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADV5_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADV5-64">[64]</a></sup> and that evening was absorbed into an approaching <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, about 450 mi (720 km) northeast of the Azores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADV_6_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADV_6-65">[65]</a></sup> Although Irma passed near parts of the western and central Azores with gale-force winds in some areas, no reports of damage or casualties caused by Irma were received. Several nearby ships reported winds around 46 mph (74 km/h). It was noted that heavy rains may have occurred on some of the mountainous islands as Irma passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<p>A weak tropical wave emerged into the Atlantic Ocean from the west coast of Africa on September 30. The wave moved west-northwestward and was centered well east of the Leeward Islands on October 6, when satellite imagery indicated that deep convection became much more concentrated. The following day, ship reports noted that a closed circulation was developing. The system was classified as a tropical depression beginning at 1800 UTC on October 7, while located about 600 mi (970 km) east of Puerto Rico. Around midday on October 8, the depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Juliet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtcr_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-jtcr-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>After peaking with maximum sustained winds of 50 mph (80 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 1,006 mbar (29.7 inHg) early on October 9, Juliet passed north of Puerto Rico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtcr_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-jtcr-66">[66]</a></sup> The storm brought light rainfall to the island, peaking at 4.51 in (115 mm) at Toro Negro Plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-19">[19]</a></sup> The storm then accelerated and curved northwestward, northward, and then northeastward. On October 11, Juliet merged with a frontal zone, while located west-southwest of Bermuda. Later that day, the remnants moved across the island and produced up to 3 in (76 mm) of rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtcr_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-jtcr-66">[66]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Kendra">Hurricane Kendra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hurricane Kendra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In late October, a tropical wave and an area of disturbed weather combined in the northwestern Caribbean, before crossing Puerto Rico. The system moved northwest and by late on October 28, it became a tropical depression while located about 80 miles (130 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Mayaguana" title="Mayaguana">Mayaguana</a> in The Bahamas. Early on the following day, it strengthened into Tropical Storm Kendra. The storm quickly intensified while moving either north or north-northwestward and became a hurricane late on October 29. After peaking with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h) on October 30, Kendra weakened significantly to a 50 mph (80 km/h) tropical storm in only 12 hours. Kendra continued north-northeastward or northeastward, before being absorbed by an extratropical cyclone while located west-northwest of Bermuda early on November 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The precursor system dropped rainfall across much of southern Puerto Rico was at least 7 in (180 mm), with a peak at 20.43 in (519 mm) in Pico del Este.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-19">[19]</a></sup> Mudslides and flooding from the heavy precipitation left many roads impassable, washed out or collapsed several bridges, and caused considerable damage to agriculture, especially livestock. Additionally, one fatality occurred and 1,710 families fled their homes for shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd11_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-sd11-67">[67]</a></sup> Damage in Puerto Rico reached $6 million. A <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> and Kendra combined produced strong winds and abnormally high tides along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>, though no damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the 12 other tropical cyclones, there were several tropical depressions that developed during the season. The first of which formed over the central Gulf of Mexico on June 21. The depression moved northeastward toward Florida and strengthened slightly. It dissipated by late on June 22. Another tropical depression developed about 175 mi (282 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Porto_Novo,_Cape_Verde" title="Porto Novo, Cape Verde">Porto Novo, Cape Verde</a> on July 10. The system moved generally westward and intensified into a strong tropical depression, before dissipating two days later. Tropical Depression Four formed about 465 mi (748 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> on August 7. It tracked westward without significantly intensifying, and passed through the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> over <a href="/wiki/Bequia" title="Bequia">Bequia</a> on the following day. The depression continued westward and passed near <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a> on August 9. It eventually traversed the Caribbean Sea, and made landfall to the south of <a href="/wiki/Bluefields" title="Bluefields">Bluefields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on August 11. The depression dissipated shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A tropical depression formed in the central Gulf of Mexico on August 9. The storm moved northward and struck southeastern <a href="/wiki/Plaquemines_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Plaquemines Parish, Louisiana">Plaquemines Parish, Louisiana</a> before dissipating the next day. By August 30, another depression developed in the north-central Gulf of Mexico. The system tracked generally eastward and avoided landfall. It dissipated around midday on September 1. Tropical Depression Eight developed over western <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> around 1200 UTC on September 3. The depression initially headed west-southwestward and soon entered the Atlantic Ocean. Between late on September 4 and early on September 5, the system passed south of Cape Verde. Later that day, the storm began curving west-northwestward. By early on September 7, it was heading northwestward and then turned to the north-northwest the next day. The depression moved northward between September 9 and September 10, before re-curving to the northeast. It dissipated about 440 mi (710 km) of <a href="/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores)" title="Flores Island (Azores)">Flores Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> around midday on September 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 1200 UTC on September 8, Tropical Depression Nine developed over the west-central Gulf of Mexico. Moving generally westward, the depression made landfall south of <a href="/wiki/La_Pesca" title="La Pesca">La Pesca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a>, around midday on September 10. The system rapidly weakened inland and dissipated later that day. Another tropical depression formed over western Senegal on September 18. It moved westward across the Atlantic Ocean before curving west-northwestward about three days later. On September 25, the depression moved northwestward and then northward by September 28. It dissipated about 550 mi (890 km) east-northeast of Bermuda at 1200 UTC the following day. The next tropical depression developed in the Gulf of Mexico just offshore <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a> on September 21. Moving west-northwestward, the depression made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a> on September 23, shortly before dissipating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A tropical depression formed at 1200 UTC on October 13, while located about 55 mi (89 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Corvo_Island" title="Corvo Island">Corvo Island</a> in the Azores. The depression initially moved south-southwestward, before curving southwestward by the following day. It then turned west-northwestward on October 15. Late the next day, the depression turned abruptly northward. The system dissipated about 500 mi (800 km) west-northwest of Flores Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> The next tropical depression developed at 1200 UTC on October 26, while located about 490 mi (790 km) south-southwest of the southernmost islands of Cape Verde. It moved generally westward and dissipated about halfway between the Lesser Antilles and the west coast of Africa on October 29. The final tropical depression of the season formed about 265 mi (426 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/North_Abaco" title="North Abaco">North Abaco</a> in the Bahamas on November 3. Moving north-northeastward, the depression turned northeastward by the next day. It dissipated about 275 mi (443 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Virginia Beach, Virginia">Virginia Beach, Virginia</a> on November 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_names">Storm names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Storm names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>The following names were used for named storms that formed in the north Atlantic basin in 1978. Storms were named Amelia, Bess, Cora, Flossie, Hope, Irma and Juliet for the first time in 1978. This was the last year that only female names were used for Atlantic hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The name Greta was retired after the 1978 season. Names that were not assigned are marked in <span style="color:#666666">gray</span>.</p>
