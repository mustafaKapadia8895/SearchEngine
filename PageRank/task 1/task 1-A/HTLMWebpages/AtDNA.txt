<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Autosome</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=AtDNA&amp;redirect=no" title="AtDNA">AtDNA</a>)</span></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about a type of chromosome. For the ancestral discovery method using autosomal DNA, see <a href="/wiki/Genealogical_DNA_test#Geographic_origin_tests" title="Genealogical DNA test">Genealogical DNA test § Geographic origin tests</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>autosome</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosome</a> that is not an <a href="/wiki/Allosome" title="Allosome">allosome</a> (a sex chromosome).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The members of an autosome pair in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> cell have the same morphology unlike those in allosome pairs which may have different structure. The <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> in autosomes is collectively known as <b>atDNA</b> or <b>auDNA</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">genome</a> that usually contains 22 pairs of autosomes and one <a href="/wiki/Allosome" title="Allosome">allosome</a> pair (46 chromosomes total). The autosome pairs are labeled with numbers (1–22 in humans) roughly in order of their sizes in base pairs, while allosomes are labelled with their letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By contrast, the allosome pair consists of two <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosomes</a> in females or one X and one <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> in males. (Unusual combinations of <a href="/wiki/XYY_syndrome" title="XYY syndrome">XYY</a>, <a href="/wiki/Klinefelter_syndrome" title="Klinefelter syndrome">XXY</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triple_X_syndrome" title="Triple X syndrome">XXX</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XXXX_syndrome" title="XXXX syndrome">XXXX</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XXXXX_syndrome" title="XXXXX syndrome">XXXXX</a> or <a href="/wiki/XXYY_syndrome" title="XXYY syndrome">XXYY</a>, among <a href="/wiki/Aneuploidy" title="Aneuploidy">other allosome combinations</a>, are known to occur and usually cause developmental abnormalities.)</p>
<p>Autosomes still contain sexual determination genes even though they are not sex chromosomes. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SRY" title="SRY">SRY</a> gene on the Y chromosome encodes the transcription factor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testis_determining_factor" title="Testis determining factor">TDF</a> and is vital for male sex determination during development. TDF functions by activating the <a href="/wiki/SOX9" title="SOX9">SOX9</a> gene on <a href="/wiki/Chromosome_17_(human)" title="Chromosome 17 (human)">chromosome 17</a>, so mutations of the <a href="/wiki/SOX9" title="SOX9">SOX9</a> gene can cause humans with a Y chromosome to develop as females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>All human autosomes have been identified and mapped by extracting the chromosomes from a cell arrested in <a href="/wiki/Metaphase" title="Metaphase">metaphase</a> or <a href="/wiki/Prometaphase" title="Prometaphase">prometaphase</a> and then staining them with some sort of dye (most commonly, <a href="/wiki/Giemsa_stain" title="Giemsa stain">Giemsa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These chromosomes are typically viewed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karyogram" title="Karyogram">karyograms</a> for easy comparison. Clinical geneticists can compare the karyogram of an individual to a reference karyogram to discover the cytogenetic basis of certain <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypes</a>. For example, the karyogram of someone with <a href="/wiki/Patau_syndrome" title="Patau syndrome">Patau Syndrome</a> would show that they possess three copies of <a href="/wiki/Chromosome_13_(human)" title="Chromosome 13 (human)">chromosome 13</a>. Karyograms and staining techniques can only detect large-scale disruptions to chromosomes—chromosomal aberrations smaller than a few million base pairs generally cannot be seen on a karyogram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autosomal_genetic_disorders">Autosomal genetic disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Autosomal genetic disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Autosomal_recessive_inheritance.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="218" data-file-width="206" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a0/Autosomal_recessive_inheritance.gif" width="206"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Autosomal_recessive_inheritance.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An illustration of the inheritance pattern and phenotypic effects of an autosomal recessive gene.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Autosomal genetic disorders can arise due to a number of causes, some of the most common being <a href="/wiki/Nondisjunction" title="Nondisjunction">nondisjunction</a> in parental germ cells or <a href="/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance" title="Mendelian inheritance">Mendelian inheritance</a> of deleterious alleles from parents. Autosomal genetic disorders which exhibit Mendelian inheritance can be inherited either in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomal_dominant" title="Autosomal dominant">autosomal dominant</a> or recessive fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> These disorders manifest in and are passed on by either sex with equal frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Autosomal dominant disorders are often present in both parent and child, as the child needs to inherit only one copy of the deleterious <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">allele</a> to manifest the disease. Autosomal recessive diseases, however, require two copies of the deleterious allele for the disease to manifest. Because it is possible to possess one copy of a deleterious allele without presenting a disease phenotype, two phenotypically normal parents can have a child with the disease if both parents are carriers (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterozygote" title="Heterozygote">heterozygotes</a>) for the condition.</p>
<p>Autosomal <a href="/wiki/Aneuploidy" title="Aneuploidy">aneuploidy</a> can also result in disease conditions. Aneuploidy of autosomes is not well tolerated and usually results in miscarriage of the developing fetus. Fetuses with aneuploidy of gene-rich chromosomes—such as <a href="/wiki/Chromosome_1_(human)" title="Chromosome 1 (human)">chromosome 1</a>—never survive to term,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> and fetuses with aneuploidy of gene-poor chromosomes—such as <a href="/wiki/Chromosome_21_(human)" title="Chromosome 21 (human)">chromosome 21</a>— are still miscarried over 23% of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Possessing a single copy of an autosome (known as a monosomy) is nearly always incompatible with life, though very rarely some monosomies can survive past birth. Having three copies of an autosome (known as a trisomy) is far more compatible with life, however. A common example is <a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down syndrome</a>, which is caused by possessing three copies of <a href="/wiki/Chromosome_21_(human)" title="Chromosome 21 (human)">chromosome 21</a> instead of the usual two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Partial aneuploidy can also occur as a result of <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_translocation" title="Chromosomal translocation">unbalanced translocations</a> during meiosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Deletions of part of a chromosome cause partial monosomies, while duplications can cause partial trisomies. If the duplication or deletion is large enough, it can be discovered by analyzing a karyogram of the individual. Autosomal translocations can be responsible for a number of diseases, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> to <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Unlike single gene disorders, diseases caused by aneuploidy are the result of improper <a href="/wiki/Gene_dosage" title="Gene dosage">gene dosage</a>, not nonfunctional gene product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
