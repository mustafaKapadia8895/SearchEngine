<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sverdrup</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Sverdrup_(disambiguation)" title="Sverdrup (disambiguation)">Sverdrup (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>sverdrup</b> (symbol: <b>Sv</b>) is a non-<a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_measure" title="Unit of measure">unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> transport. It is used almost exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> to measure the volumetric rate of transport of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>. It is named after <a href="/wiki/Harald_Sverdrup_(oceanographer)" title="Harald Sverdrup (oceanographer)">Harald Sverdrup</a>.</p>
<p>It should not be confused with SI unit <a href="/wiki/Sievert" title="Sievert">sievert</a> or the non-SI <a href="/wiki/Svedberg" title="Svedberg">svedberg</a>, which use the same symbol.</p>
<p>It is equivalent to 1 million cubic metres per second (264,000,000 USgal/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The entire global input of <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> from rivers to the ocean is equal to about 1.2 sverdrup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The water transport in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> gradually increases from 30 Sv in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Current" title="Florida Current">Florida Current</a> to a maximum of 150 Sv south of Newfoundland at 55°W <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The heat carried within this volume equals roughly that transported through the atmosphere to make the relatively milder <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> of north-western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a>, at approximately 125 Sverdrups, is the largest ocean current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
