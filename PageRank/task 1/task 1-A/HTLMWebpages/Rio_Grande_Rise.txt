<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rio Grande Rise</h1>

The Rio Grande Rise separates the Brazil (north) and Argentine Basins (south) and is separated from the Vema Sill and Santos Plain (west) by the Vema Channel and from the Mid-Atlantic Ridge by the Hunter Channel (east).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Rio Grande Rise</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aseismic" title="Aseismic">aseismic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_ridge" title="Ocean ridge">ocean ridge</a> in the southern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. Together with the <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Ridge" title="Walvis Ridge">Walvis Ridge</a> off Africa, the Rio Grande Rise forms a V-shaped structure of mirrored hotspot tracks or seamount chains across the northern South Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2013, Brazilian scientists announced that they found granite boulders on the Rio Grande Rise and speculated that it could be the remains of a submerged continent, which they called the "Brazilian Atlantis". Other researchers, however, noted that such boulders can end-up on the ocean floor by less speculative means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rio_Grande_Rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Rio Grande Rise separates the <a href="/wiki/Santos_Basin" title="Santos Basin">Santos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelotas_Basin" title="Pelotas Basin">Pelotas Basins</a> and is composed of western and eastern areas, which have different geological backgrounds. The western area has numerous <a href="/wiki/Guyot" title="Guyot">guyots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a> and a basement dated to <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=80–87">80 to 87</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>. The eastern area is covered by <a href="/wiki/Fracture_zone" title="Fracture zone">fracture zones</a> and may represent an abandoned spreading centre. In the western area, volcanic <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccia</a> and layers of ash indicate widespread volcanism during the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>, which coincides with the formation of volcanic rocks onshore. During this period, parts of the western plateau were uplifted over sea level and short-lived volcanic islands formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sager-Motiv_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sager-Motiv-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>When West <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> (i.e. South America) broke away from Africa during the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a> (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=146–100">146 to 100</a> Ma</span>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" title="South Atlantic">South Atlantic</a> opened up from its southern to its northern end. In this process, the voluminous <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_and_Etendeka_traps" title="Paraná and Etendeka traps">Paraná and Etendeka</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_flood_basalt" title="Continental flood basalt">continental flood basalts</a> formed in what is now Brazil and <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>. This event is linked to the <a href="/wiki/Tristan_hotspot" title="Tristan hotspot">Tristan-Gough hotspot</a>, now located near the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>, close to <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gough_Island" title="Gough Island">Gough Islands</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=60">60</a> Ma</span>), the orientation of spreading changed, which is still visible on the African side, and volcanism ended on the American side. This process resulted in the Tristan-Gough seamount chains on either side of the Tristan-Gough hotspot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sager-Motiv_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sager-Motiv-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeoclimatic_role">Palaeoclimatic role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rio_Grande_Rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Palaeoclimatic role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Brazilian-Japanese expedition in 2013 recovered <i>in situ</i> granitic and metamorphic rocks on the Rio Grande Rise. This can possibly indicate that the plateau includes fragments of continental crust — possible remains of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro-continent" title="Micro-continent">micro-continents</a> similar to those found on and around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerguelen" title="Kerguelen">Kerguelen</a> in the Indian Ocean and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> in the Arctic Ocean. The existence of such microcontinents is speculative, however, since their remains tend to be covered by younger layers of lava and sediments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sager-Motiv_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sager-Motiv-4">[4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, transoceanic dispersals are hinted at by the fossil record of, for example, flightless birds such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lavocatavis" title="Lavocatavis">Lavocatavis</a></i>, indicating that several islands between Africa and South America made <a href="/wiki/Island_hopping" title="Island hopping">island hopping</a> possible across the Atlantic during the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=66–2.58">66 to 2.58</a> Ma</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the beginning of the Maastrichtian, the characteristics of water masses differed north and south of the Rio Grande Rice-Walvis Ridge complex. The disappearance of these differences during the Maastritchtian indicates a reorganisation of oceanic circulation patterns that lead to a global homogenisation of intermediate and deep waters. This process seems to have been triggered by the breaching of the Rio Grande Rise-Walvis Ridge complex and the disappearance of epicontinental seaways such as the <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Ocean</a>. The process resulted in the deterioration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudist" title="Rudist">rudist</a>-dominated tropical habitats and consequently the extinction of benthic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inoceramid" title="Inoceramid">inoceramid</a> bivalves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The origin of modern circulation of cold, deep water — known as the "Big Flush" — is associated with Early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=55–40">55 to 40</a> Ma</span>) geological events; tectonism that resulted in the opening of the north-east Atlantic and fracture zones that developed in the subsiding Rio Grande Rise, which allowed cold water from the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Weddell_Sea" title="Weddell Sea">Weddell Sea</a> to flow northward into the North Atlantic. <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=40">40</a> Ma</span>, the generation of cold bottom water in the Antarctic resulted in the formation of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/psychrosphere" title="wikt:psychrosphere">psychrospheric</a> fauna, which today live in temperatures below 10 °C (50 °F), in the Atlantic and Tethys. This global distribution suggests that the Rio Grande Rise had been breached by this time, allowing cold, dense water to move north-south through a corridor enhancing the transition from a latitudinal <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermospheric circulation</a> to a meridional <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
