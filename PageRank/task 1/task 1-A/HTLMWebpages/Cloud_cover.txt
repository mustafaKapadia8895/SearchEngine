<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cloud cover</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MODIS_Map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2700" data-file-width="5400" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/MODIS_Map.jpg/220px-MODIS_Map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/MODIS_Map.jpg/330px-MODIS_Map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/MODIS_Map.jpg/440px-MODIS_Map.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<p><b>Cloud cover</b> (also known as <b>cloudiness</b>, <b>cloudage</b>, or <b>cloud amount</b>) refers to the fraction of the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> obscured by <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> when observed from a particular location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Okta" title="Okta">Okta</a> is the usual unit of measurement of the cloud cover. The cloud cover is correlated to the <a href="/wiki/Sunshine_duration" title="Sunshine duration">sunshine duration</a> as the least cloudy locales are the sunniest ones while the cloudiest areas are the least sunny places.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1504" data-file-width="2133" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG/220px-Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG/330px-Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG/440px-Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cloud_cover_over_the_North_Atlantic_Ocean_3.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Partial cloud cover over the North Atlantic Ocean.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The global cloud cover averages around 0.68 when analyzing clouds with <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depth</a> larger than 0.1. This value is lower (0.56) when considering clouds with an optical depth larger than 2, and higher when counting subvisible <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus clouds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013stubenrauch_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013stubenrauch-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_climate_system">Role in the climate system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_cover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role in the climate system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Worldclouds_2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="720" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Worldclouds_2009.jpg/220px-Worldclouds_2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Worldclouds_2009.jpg/330px-Worldclouds_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Worldclouds_2009.jpg/440px-Worldclouds_2009.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Worldclouds_2009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Global cloud cover, averaged over the month of October, 2009. The image shows that the outlines of the continents can often be traced through observations of clouds alone, with the sharpest outlines where very dry land is surrounded by ocean.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Clouds play multiple critical roles in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_system" title="Climate system">climate system</a>. In particular, being bright objects in the visible part of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar</a> <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a>, they efficiently reflect light to space and thus contribute to the cooling of the planet. Cloud cover thus plays an important role in the energetic balance of the atmosphere and a variation of it is a consequence of and to the <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> expected by recent studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variability">Variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_cover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MODAL2_M_CLD_FR.ogv" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
These maps display the fraction of Earth's area that was cloudy on average during each month from January 2005 to August 2013. The measurements were collected by the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) on NASA’s Terra satellite. Colors range from blue (no clouds) to white (totally cloudy). Like a digital camera, MODIS collects information in gridded boxes, or pixels. Cloud fraction is the portion of each pixel that is covered by clouds. Colors range from blue (no clouds) to white (totally cloudy).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (<i>click for more detail</i>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cloud cover values only vary by 0.03 from year to year, whereas the local, day to day variability in cloud amount typically rises to 0.3 over the globe. Most data sets agree on the fact that the land is covered by 0.10-0.15 less cloud than the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013stubenrauch_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-2013stubenrauch-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lastly, there is a latitudinal variation in the cloud cover, such that around 20°N there are regions with 0.10 less cloudiness than the global mean. Almost the same variation (0.15 instead of 0.10) is found 20°S. On the other hand, in the storm regions of the Southern Hemisphere midlatitudes were found to have with 0.15-0.25 more cloudiness than the global mean at 60°S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013stubenrauch_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-2013stubenrauch-2">[2]</a></sup> On average, about 52% of Earth is cloud-covered at any moment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some regions are almost always cloudy such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a> and some others are almost always clear such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>.</p>
