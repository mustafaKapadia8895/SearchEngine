<p>The <b>Sacramento Valley</b> is the area of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a> of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> that lies north of the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento%E2%80%93San_Joaquin_River_Delta" title="Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta">Sacramento–San Joaquin River Delta</a> and is drained by the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River" title="Sacramento River">Sacramento River</a>. It encompasses all or parts of ten <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a> counties. Although many areas of the Sacramento Valley are rural, it contains several urban areas, including the state capital, <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramento_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River" title="Sacramento River">Sacramento River</a> and its tributaries are a huge part of the geography of the Sacramento Valley. Rising in the various mountain ranges (the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Coast_Ranges" title="Northern Coast Ranges">Northern Coast Ranges</a> to the west, the southern <a href="/wiki/Siskiyou_Mountains" title="Siskiyou Mountains">Siskiyou Mountains</a> to the north, and the northern <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> to the east) that define the shape of the valley, they provide water for agricultural, industrial, residential, and recreation uses. Most of the rivers are heavily dammed and diverted.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lassens_Butte.tiff"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="388" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Lassens_Butte.tiff/lossless-page1-220px-Lassens_Butte.tiff.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Lassens_Butte.tiff/lossless-page1-330px-Lassens_Butte.tiff.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Lassens_Butte.tiff/lossless-page1-440px-Lassens_Butte.tiff.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lassens_Butte.tiff" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
19th century etching shows Sutter Buttes in the Sacramento Valley</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The terrain of the Sacramento Valley is primarily flat <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> that become lusher as one moves east from the <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> of the Coast Ranges toward the Sierras. Unlike the San Joaquin Valley, which in its pre-irrigation state was a vegetation-hostile desert, the somewhat less arid Sacramento Valley had significant tracts of forest prior to the arrival of settlers of European ancestry. Most of it was cut down during the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a> and the ensuing wave of American settlement, although there are still some heavily tree-populated areas, such as the greater Sacramento area.</p>
<p>Foothills become more common from just south of <a href="/wiki/Corning,_California" title="Corning, California">Corning</a> to <a href="/wiki/Shasta_Lake,_California" title="Shasta Lake, California">Shasta Lake City</a>. These are known as the Valley Hills and begin south of the Tehama-Glenn County line near Corning. There are also a few hills in <a href="/wiki/Red_Bluff,_California" title="Red Bluff, California">Red Bluff</a> and Corning. There is one major range of foothills between Cottonwood and Red Bluff known as the Cottonwood Hills (a.k.a. 9-mile Hill), and there is the Cottonwood Ridge between Anderson and Cottonwood. There are some hills in Redding, a few more than Red Bluff, and after Redding it is mainly foothills.</p>
<p>One distinctive geographic feature of the Sacramento Valley is the <a href="/wiki/Sutter_Buttes" title="Sutter Buttes">Sutter Buttes</a>. Nicknamed the smallest mountain range in the world, it consists of the remnants of an extinct <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> and is located just outside <a href="/wiki/Yuba_City,_California" title="Yuba City, California">Yuba City</a>, 44 miles north of Sacramento.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramento_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">Citrus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nut</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchards" title="Orchards">orchards</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_ranch" title="Cattle ranch">cattle ranches</a> are common to both halves of the Central Valley. The Sacramento Valley's agricultural mix also resembles that of the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> to the south. Nuts, such as <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnuts</a>, are of greater importance north of the Delta, and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, nonviable in the drier San Joaquin valley, is a major crop. The town of <a href="/wiki/Corning,_California" title="Corning, California">Corning</a> produces olives for oil extraction and for consumption as fruit. The <a href="/wiki/Sunsweet_Growers" title="Sunsweet Growers">Sunsweet Growers Incorporated</a> headquarters are in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuba_City" title="Yuba City">Yuba City</a>. The valley controls more than two-thirds of the worldwide prune market through the over 400 growers in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramento_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Weather patterns in the Sacramento Valley are very similar to those in the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> to the south, although the humidity and precipitation tends to be a bit higher. Summers are the dry season, with average daytime temperatures in the upper 80s to mid 90s but triple digits are a common occurrence, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Chico,_California" title="Chico, California">Chico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redding,_California" title="Redding, California">Redding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Bluff,_California" title="Red Bluff, California">Red Bluff</a> area. The "breeze", which comes in from the Bay area, brings cooler temperatures and higher humidity. At times the delta breeze is gusty with wind speed to up to 30 mph in valley and up to 45 mph in the delta region which is always breezy. This breeze can also bring morning low clouds at times into the region but the clouds generally burn off quickly and temperatures stay cool. Summer-like conditions continue into early to mid September but weather starts to change to cooler, wetter, foggier weather during October which gives trees vibrant autumn foliage. Winters, also known as the rainy season, are generally mild to cool, foggy and wet with highs averaging in the mid 40s F to low 10s F, colder in the northern part of the valley and colder still in the foothills and frost can occur almost anywhere. The rainy season runs from October to April but it's not unusual for rain to occur in September or May. During the rainy season, the Sacramento Valley is prone to strong thunderstorms and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, mostly of EF0 or EF1 intensity, especially in <a href="/wiki/Colusa_County,_California" title="Colusa County, California">Colusa County</a> and areas around Corning and Orland. Flooding does occur at times during wetter periods, usually November to March. Snow in the valley is rare, although <a href="/wiki/Redding,_California" title="Redding, California">Redding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Bluff,_California" title="Red Bluff, California">Red Bluff</a>, being at the north end of the valley, often experience a light dusting or two per year. <a href="/wiki/Chico,_California" title="Chico, California">Chico</a> may get a rain-snow mix every few years, but, on the average, only snows about every 5 years. Farther south in Sacramento, snow falls about once every 10 years or so. During the autumn and winter months the entire Central Valley is susceptible to dense <a href="/wiki/Tule_fog" title="Tule fog">tule fog</a> that makes driving hazardous, especially at night and especially south of Corning. The fog can last for weeks depending on how weak the wind is.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramento_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_5_(California)" title="Interstate 5 (California)">Interstate 5</a> is the primary route through the Sacramento Valley, traveling north-south roughly along the valley's western edge. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_80_(California)" title="Interstate 80 (California)">Interstate 80</a> cuts a northeast-to-southwest swath through the southern end of the valley, mostly through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_County" title="Sacramento County">Sacramento</a> and Yolo Counties, and ends at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco-Oakland_Bay_Bridge" title="San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge">San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge</a>. Several secondary routes connect the two roads, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_505_(California)" title="Interstate 505 (California)">Interstate 505</a> and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_113" title="California State Route 113">State Route 113</a>. The Sacramento area has a web of urban freeways.</p>
<p>Other principal routes in the region include <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_99" title="California State Route 99">State Route 99</a>, which runs along the valley's eastern edge, roughly parallel to I-5, from Sacramento until its northern terminus in <a href="/wiki/Red_Bluff,_California" title="Red Bluff, California">Red Bluff</a>; <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_20" title="California State Route 20">State Route 20</a>, which traverses the valley from west to east on its route from <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_1" title="California State Route 1">State Route 1</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mendocino_County,_California" title="Mendocino County, California">Mendocino County</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Donner_Pass" title="Donner Pass">Donner Pass</a>; <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_49" title="California State Route 49">State Route 49</a>, named in honor of the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a> and running through many old mining towns in the foothills of the valley; and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_45" title="California State Route 45">State Route 45</a>, which runs along the course of the Sacramento River roughly ten miles (20 km) east of I-5.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific Railroad</a> serves the valley, with its principal north-south line from <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>, California to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>, via <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marysville,_California" title="Marysville, California">Marysville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chico,_California" title="Chico, California">Chico</a>, and Redding. This is also the route of <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_Starlight" title="Coast Starlight">Coast Starlight</a></i> passenger train. The Union Pacific also has two east-west lines, through <a href="/wiki/Donner_Pass" title="Donner Pass">Donner Pass</a> (the former <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Railroad" title="Central Pacific Railroad">Central Pacific Railroad</a>), and through the <a href="/wiki/Feather_River" title="Feather River">Feather River</a> gorge (the former <a href="/wiki/Western_Pacific_Railroad" title="Western Pacific Railroad">Western Pacific Railroad</a>). Amtrak's <i><a href="/wiki/California_Zephyr" title="California Zephyr">California Zephyr</a></i> uses the Donner Pass route. The <a href="/wiki/BNSF_Railway" title="BNSF Railway">BNSF Railway</a> has a line from <a href="/wiki/Klamath_Falls,_Oregon" title="Klamath Falls, Oregon">Klamath Falls, Oregon</a>, to a junction with the Union Pacific Feather River line at <a href="/wiki/Keddie,_California" title="Keddie, California">Keddie</a>. The BNSF has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trackage_rights" title="Trackage rights">trackage rights</a> on both the UP east-west routes. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/California_Northern_Railroad" title="California Northern Railroad">California Northern Railroad</a> operates the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Railroad" title="Southern Pacific Railroad">Southern Pacific Railroad</a> line on the west side of the valley from <a href="/wiki/Davis,_California" title="Davis, California">Davis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tehama,_California" title="Tehama, California">Tehama</a> (near Red Bluff).</p>
