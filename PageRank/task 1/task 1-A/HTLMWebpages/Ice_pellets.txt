<p><b>Ice pellets</b> are a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> consisting of small, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent">translucent</a> balls of ice. Ice pellets are smaller than <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hailstones</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which form in <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> rather than in winter, and are different from <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">graupel</a> ("soft hail") which is made of frosty white <a href="/wiki/Hard_rime" title="Hard rime">rime</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Rain_and_snow_mixed" title="Rain and snow mixed">a mixture of rain and snow</a> which is a <a href="/wiki/Slush" title="Slush">slushy</a> liquid or semisolid. Ice pellets often bounce when they hit the ground or other solid objects, and make a higher-pitched "tap" when striking objects like <a href="/wiki/Jacket" title="Jacket">jackets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windshield" title="Windshield">windshields</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_litter" title="Leaf litter">dried leaves</a>, compared to the dull splat of liquid raindrops. Pellets generally do not freeze into a solid mass unless mixed with <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a>. The <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> code for ice pellets is <b>PL</b> (<b>PE</b> before November 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pellets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ice pellets are known to Americans as <b>sleet</b>, the official term used by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the term <b>sleet</b> refers to a <a href="/wiki/Rain_and_snow_mixed" title="Rain and snow mixed">mixture of rain and snow</a> in most Commonwealth countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of this, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a> never uses the term <i>sleet</i>, and uses the terms "ice pellets" or "wet snow" instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pellets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SleetSounding.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="719" data-file-width="643" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/SleetSounding.svg/220px-SleetSounding.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/SleetSounding.svg/330px-SleetSounding.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/SleetSounding.svg/440px-SleetSounding.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SleetSounding.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>

<p>Ice pellets form when a layer of above-freezing air is located between 1,500 and 3,000 meters (5,000 and 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>) above the ground, with sub-freezing air both above and below it. This causes the partial or complete melting of any <a href="/wiki/Snowflake" title="Snowflake">snowflakes</a> falling through the warm layer (the French term for sleet, <i>neige fondue</i>, literally means "melted snow" because of this). As they fall back into the sub-freezing layer closer to the surface, they re-freeze into ice pellets. However, if the sub-freezing layer beneath the warm layer is too small, the precipitation will not have time to re-freeze before hitting the surface, so it will become <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a>. A temperature profile showing a warm layer above the ground is most likely to be found in advance of a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> during the cold season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but can occasionally be found behind a passing <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, and often with a <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary front</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pellets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most parts of the world, ice pellets only occur for brief periods and do not accumulate a significant amount. However, across the eastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and southeastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, warm air flowing north from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> ahead of a strong <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic-scale</a> storm system can overrun cold, dense air at the surface for many hundreds of miles for an extended period of time. In these areas, ice pellet accumulations of <span class="nowrap">2–5 cm</span> <span class="nowrap">(0.8–2.0 in)</span> are not unheard of. The effects of a significant accumulation of ice pellets are not unlike an accumulation of snow. One significant difference is that for the same volume of snow, an equal volume of ice pellets is significantly heavier and thus more difficult to clear away. Additionally, a volume of ice pellets takes significantly longer to melt compared to an equal volume of fresh snowfall.</p>
