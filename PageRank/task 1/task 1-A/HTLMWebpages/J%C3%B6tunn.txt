<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Jötunn</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Frost Giant" redirects here. For the music album, see <a href="/wiki/Frost_Giant_(album)" title="Frost Giant (album)">Frost Giant (album)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, a <b>jötunn</b> (plural <b>jötnar</b>) is a type of entity contrasted with gods and other figures, such as dwarfs and elves. The entities are themselves ambiguously defined, variously referred to by a several other terms, including <b>risi</b>, <b>thurs</b>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Troll" title="Troll">troll</a></i>.</p>
<p>Although the term <i>giant</i> is used to gloss the word <i>jötunn</i> and its apparent synonyms in some translations and academic texts, jötnar are not necessarily notably large and may be described as exceedingly beautiful or alarmingly grotesque. Some deities, such as <a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skaði">Skaði</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ger%C3%B0r" title="Gerðr">Gerðr</a>, are themselves described as jötnar, and various well-attested deities, such as <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>, are descendants of the jötnar. Several jötnar like <a href="/wiki/Hr%C3%A6svelgr" title="Hræsvelgr">Hræsvelgr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fenrir" title="Fenrir">Fenrir</a> are animal shaped. While most are described as being roughly human sized, some are indeed portrayed as huge, such as frost giants (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hr%C3%ADm%C3%BEursar" title="Hrímþursar">hrímþursar</a></i>), fire giants (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eldj%C3%B6tnar" title="Eldjötnar">eldjötnar</a></i>), and mountain giants (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bergrisar" title="Bergrisar">bergrisar</a></i>).</p>
<p><br/>
Norse mythology traces the origin of the jötnar to the proto-being <a href="/wiki/Ymir" title="Ymir">Ymir</a>, a result of growth of asexual reproduction from the entity's body. Ymir is later killed, his body dismembered to create the world, and the jötnar survive this event by way of sailing through a flood of Ymir’s blood. The jötnar dwell in <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunheimr" title="Jötunheimr">Jötunheimr</a>. In later <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_folklore" title="Scandinavian folklore">Scandinavian folklore</a>, the ambiguity surrounding the entities gives way to negative portrayals.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%B6tunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1193" data-file-width="1128" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg/250px-Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg/375px-Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg/500px-Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <i>bergrisi</i> - the traditional Protector of Southwestern Iceland - appears as a <a href="/wiki/Supporter" title="Supporter">supporter</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Iceland" title="Coat of arms of Iceland">coat of arms of Iceland</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Old Norse <i>jötunn</i> and Old English <i>eóten</i> developed from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> masculine noun *<i>etunaz</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL-86_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OREL-86-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">Philologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Orel" title="Vladimir Orel">Vladimir Orel</a> says that semantic connections between *<i>etunaz</i> with Proto-Germanic *<i>etanan</i> makes a relation between the two nouns likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL-86_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-OREL-86-1">[1]</a></sup> Proto-Germanic *<i>etanan</i> is reconstructed from Old Norse <i>etall</i> 'consuming', Old English <i>etol</i> 'voracious, gluttonous', and Old High German <i>filu-ezzal</i> 'greedy'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL-86_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-OREL-86-1">[1]</a></sup> Old Norse <i>risi</i> and Old High German <i>riso</i> derive from the Proto-Germanic masculine noun *<i>wrisjon</i>. Orel observes that the Old Saxon adjective <i>wrisi-līke</i> 'enormous' is likely also connected. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL-472_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-OREL-472-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Old Norse <i>þurs</i>, Old English <i>ðyrs</i>, and Old High German <i>duris</i> 'devil, evil spirit' derive from the Proto-Germanic masculine noun *<i>þur(i)saz</i>, itself derived form Proto-Germanic *<i>þurēnan</i>, which is etymologically connected to <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>turá</i>- 'strong, powerful, rich'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL429-430_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OREL429-430-3">[3]</a></sup> For discussion regarding Old Norse <i>troll</i> and its development, see <a href="/wiki/Troll" title="Troll">troll</a>. Several terms are used specifically to refer to female entities that fall into this category, including <i>íviðja</i> (plural <i>íviðjur</i>) and <i>gýgr</i> (plural <i>gýgjar</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attestations">Attestations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%B6tunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attestations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The jötnar are frequently attested throughout the Old Norse record. For example, in a stanza of <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1_hin_skamma" title="Völuspá hin skamma">Völuspá hin skamma</a></i> (found in the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Hyndlulj%C3%B3%C3%B0" title="Hyndluljóð">Hyndluljóð</a></i>), a variety of origins are provided: völvas are descended from Viðòlfr, all seers from Vilmeiðr, all charm-workers from Svarthöfði, and all jötnar descend from Ymir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HYNDLULJOD_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HYNDLULJOD-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
