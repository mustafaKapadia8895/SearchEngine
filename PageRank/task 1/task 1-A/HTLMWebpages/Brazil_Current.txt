<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Brazil Current</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Brazil Current</b> is a warm water current that flows south along the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> south coast to the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">Río de la Plata</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This current is caused by diversion of a portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/South_Equatorial_Current" title="South Equatorial Current">South Equatorial Current</a> from where that current meets the <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> continent. The Brazil Current begins at about 10-15˚S where the South Equatorial Current (SEC) splits near Cabo de Sao Roque, Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The current reaches a depth of 700m and the estimated transport at 12˚S is 2.5 Sv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The transport increases as the current goes further south with 4 Sv at 15˚S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> On the offshore side of the current an anticyclonic recirculation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyre" title="Gyre">gyre</a> at about 30˚S causes an increase of the Brazil Current’s transport. The estimated southward transport at 27˚S, 31˚S, 34˚S, and 36˚S are 11 Sv, 17 Sv, 22 Sv, and 41 Sv. The total transport can be from 70-80 Sv by 36˚S with half of it being in the recirculation gyre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> The Brazil Current is a part of the South Atlantic subtropical gyre. The southern side of the gyre consists of the eastward flowing South Atlantic Current (SAC). The eastern boundary is the Benguela Current System (BCS). The north side consists of the SEC which flows west and then splits with the north water becoming the North Brazil Current (NBC) The southern flowing waters become the Brazil Current which makes up the western boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_boundary_current" title="Western boundary current">western boundary current</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>, and is indeed its southern counterpart; however, it is considerably shallower and weaker. It flows south from the equator to the west wind drift. It joins the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Current" title="Falkland Current">Falkland Current</a> (Malvinas Current) at the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Sea" title="Argentine Sea">Argentine Sea</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazil%E2%80%93Falkland_Confluence" title="Brazil–Falkland Confluence">Brazil–Falkland Confluence</a>), making it a temperate sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eco_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eco-4">[4]</a></sup> The Brazil-Falkland Confluence is where the Brazil Current begins to separate from the coast at about 36˚S and is when the saltier subtropical water of the Brazil Current meets the fresher subantarctic water of the Falkland Current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> The main transport of the current leaves the continental shelf at about 38˚S and the sea surface temperature at this latitude is estimated to be about 16-18˚C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Although the latitude where the current separates from the coast has been thought to be farther north from July to September than from January to March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> If the Falkland Current has low transport then the path of the Brazil Current is dominated by wind stress curl. However, if the Falkland Current transport is increased then the Brazil Current separates from the coast at the observed 38˚S latitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The range of sea surface temperature and the salinity for the Brazil-Falkland Confluence is 7-18˚C and 33.6-36 psu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> The Brazil Current region also contains six major water masses within the system: Upper Circumpolar Water (UCPW), Lower Circumpolar Water (LCPW), Central Water (CW), Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW), Antarctic Bottom Water (AABW), and North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
