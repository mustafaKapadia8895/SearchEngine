<p>The <b>Hecatoncheires</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[note 1]</a></sup> (In <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stress</a> on the fourth syllable;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> singular: <i>Hecatoncheir</i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἑκατόγχειρες</span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>Hekatonkheires</i></span> "<b>Hundred-Handed Ones</b>"), also called the <b>Centimanes</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Centimani</i></span>) or <b>Hundred-Handers</b>, were figures in an archaic stage<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">giants</a> of incredible strength and ferocity that surpassed all of the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titans</a>, whom they helped overthrow. Their name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἑκατόν</span> (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">hekaton</span></i>, "hundred") and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">χείρ</span> (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">cheir</span></i>, "hand"), "each of them having a hundred hands and fifty heads" (<i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Bibliotheca</a></i> 1.1). <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>'s <i>Theogony</i> (624, 639, 714, 734–35) reports that the three Hecatoncheires became the guards of the gates of <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a>. The Hundred-Handed-Ones are "giants" of great storms and hurricanes.</p>
<p>In Virgil's <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i> (10.566–67), in which Aeneas is likened to one of them (Briareos, known here as Aegaeon), they fought on the side of the Titans rather than the Olympians; in this, Virgil was following the lost Corinthian epic <i><a href="/wiki/Titanomachy_(epic_poem)" title="Titanomachy (epic poem)">Titanomachy</a></i> rather than the more familiar account in Hesiod.</p>
<p>Other accounts make Briareos (or Aegaeon) one of the assailants of Olympus. After his defeat, he was buried under Mount Aetna (Callimachus, <i>Hymn to Delos</i>, 141).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hecatoncheires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hesiod">Hesiod</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hecatoncheires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hesiod">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Hesiod, the Hecatoncheires were children of <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a> (Earth) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranus_(god)" title="Uranus (god)">Uranus</a> (sky).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> They played no known part in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_(religion)" title="Cult (religion)">cult</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> They were:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Briareos</b> (Βριάρεως) "Strong",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> also called <b>Aegaeon</b> (Αἰγαίων), spelled in Latin as "Briareus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Kottos</b> (Κόττος) "Strike, punch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Gyges</b> (Γύγης) or <b>Gyes</b> (Γύης), possibly "Limb" or "Curved".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Titanomachy" title="Titanomachy">War of the Titans</a>, the Hecatoncheires fought against the Titans, throwing rocks as big as mountains, one hundred at a time, and overwhelming them. After this, the Hecatoncheires became the guards of Tartarus. Briareos became the son-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a>, who gave him "Kymopoliea his daughter to wed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pausanias">Pausanias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hecatoncheires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pausanias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Corinth" title="Corinth">Corinthian</a> myth related in the second century CE to <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, Briareos was the arbitrator in a dispute between Poseidon and <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">Helios</a>, between sea and sun: he adjudged the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Corinth" title="Isthmus of Corinth">Isthmus of Corinth</a> to belong to Poseidon and the acropolis of Corinth (<a href="/wiki/Acrocorinth" title="Acrocorinth">Acrocorinth</a>) sacred to Helios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hecatoncheires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scholia" title="Scholia">Scholia</a> (manuscript notes) on <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Rhodes" title="Apollonius of Rhodes">Apollonius of Rhodes</a> represent Aegaeon as a son of Gaea and <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(mythology)" title="Pontus (mythology)">Pontos</a>, the Sea, ruling the fabulous Aegaea in Euboea, an enemy of Poseidon and the inventor of warships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> In Ovid's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphoses_(poem)" title="Metamorphoses (poem)">Metamorphoses</a></i> and in Philostratus' <i>Life of <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana" title="Apollonius of Tyana">Apollonius of Tyana</a></i> he is a marine deity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hecatoncheires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In the first book of Homer's <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> (c. 700 BC), Achilles asks his mother to appeal to Zeus on account of a previous interaction with Briareus, or Aegaeon.</li>
<li>Briareus is mentioned twice in Dante Alighieri's <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i> (completed 1320); he is first found as a giant inhabiting the ninth circle of Hell<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> and then again as an example of pride, carved into the pavement of the first terrace of Purgatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Rabelais' five-part novel <i><a href="/wiki/Gargantua_and_Pantagruel" title="Gargantua and Pantagruel">Gargantua and Pantagruel</a></i> (finished 1564) it is mentioned that a waiter needs as many hands as "Briareus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Briareus is also mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Cervantes'</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quixote</a></i> (1615), in the famous episode of the windmills.</li>
<li>Briareus is mentioned in Book I of <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Paradise_Lost" title="Paradise Lost">Paradise Lost</a></i> (1667) alongside Typhon as an analogue to the fallen Satan.</li>
<li>Briareus is mentioned in <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_Tom_Jones,_a_Foundling" title="The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling">The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling</a></i> (1749), by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fielding" title="Henry Fielding">Henry Fielding</a>, in a conversation between Tom Jones and Mr. Partridge (Book 8, Chapter IX).</li>
<li>Kottos, styled as 'Cottus' is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>' fragment <i><a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(poem)" title="Hyperion (poem)">Hyperion</a></i> (1819) as a brooding deity in the aftermath of the Titanomachy.</li>
<li>In canto VI of his satiric poem <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_(Byron)" title="Don Juan (Byron)">Don Juan</a></i> (completed in 1824), <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Byron</a> makes a slightly crude joke, musing whether "enviable Briareus […] with thy hands and heads […] hads't all things multiplied in proportion" (this thought arising from Byron's assertion of his love of all womankind in the previous canto).</li>
</ul>
