<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, a <b>placer deposit</b> or <b>placer</b> is an accumulation of valuable minerals formed by gravity separation during sedimentary processes. The name is from the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> word <i>placer</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvial</a> sand". <a href="/wiki/Placer_mining" title="Placer mining">Placer mining</a> is an important source of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, and was the main technique used in the early years of many gold rushes, including the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a>. Types of placer deposits include <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eluvium" title="Eluvium">eluvium</a>, beach placers, and <a href="/wiki/Palaeochannel#Economic_importance" title="Palaeochannel">paleoplacers</a>.</p>
<p>Placer materials must be both dense and resistant to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> processes. To accumulate in placers, mineral particles must be significantly denser than quartz (whose <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> is 2.65), as quartz is usually the largest component of sand or gravel. Placer environments typically contain <a href="/wiki/Black_sand" title="Black sand">black sand</a>, a conspicuous shiny black mixture of iron oxides, mostly <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> with variable amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a>. Valuable mineral components often occurring with black sands are <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromite" title="Chromite">chromite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cassiterite" title="Cassiterite">cassiterite</a>.</p>

<p>Alluvial placers are those formed in river or stream sediments. Typical locations for alluvial gold placer deposits are on the inside bends of <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">creeks</a>; in natural hollows; at the break of slope on a stream; the base of an <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfall</a> or other barrier.</p>
<p>Alluvial placers are formed by the deposition of dense particles at a site where water velocity remains below that required to transport them further.</p>
<p>To form a placer deposit, the particles desired must show a marked density contrast with the <a href="/wiki/Gangue" title="Gangue">gangue</a> material, which is able to be transported away from the trap site. Only if the deposit is winnowed in this way can the minerals be concentrated to economic levels.</p>
