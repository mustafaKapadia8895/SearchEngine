<p>The <b>spiny dogfish</b>, <b>spurdog</b>, <b>mud shark</b>, or <b>piked dogfish</b> (<i>Squalus acanthias</i>) is one of the best known <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Squalidae" title="Squalidae">Squalidae</a> (dogfish) <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, which is part of the <a href="/wiki/Squaliformes" title="Squaliformes">Squaliformes</a> order. While these common names may apply to several species, <i>Squalus acanthias</i> is distinguished by having two spines (one <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anterior" title="wikt:anterior">anterior</a> to each <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a>) and lacks an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a>. It is found mostly in shallow waters and further offshore in most parts of the world, especially in temperate waters. Spiny dogfish in the northern Pacific Ocean have recently been reevaluated and found to constitute a separate species, now known as "<a href="/wiki/Pacific_spiny_dogfish" title="Pacific spiny dogfish">Pacific spiny dogfish</a>", <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squalus_suckleyi" title="Squalus suckleyi">Squalus suckleyi</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert2010-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_behavior">Description and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_dogfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spiny dogfish has dorsal spines, no anal fin, and white spots along its back. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_(anatomical_term)" title="Caudal (anatomical term)">caudal</a> fin has asymmetrical lobes, forming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterocercal" title="Heterocercal">heterocercal</a> tail. The species name <i>acanthias</i> refers to the shark's two spines. These are used defensively. If captured, the shark can arch its back to pierce its captor. Glands at the base of the spines secrete a mild venom.</p>
<p>Males mature at around 11 years of age, growing to 80â100Â cm (2.6â3.3Â ft) in length; females mature in 18â21 years and are slightly larger than males, reaching 98.5â159Â cm (3.23â5.22Â ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Animal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Animal-3">[3]</a></sup> Both sexes are greyish brown in color and are <a href="/wiki/Countershading" title="Countershading">countershaded</a>. Males are identified by a pair of pelvic fins modified as sperm-transfer organs, or "claspers". The male inserts one clasper into the female cloaca during copulation.</p>
<p>Reproduction is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aplacental_viviparous" title="Aplacental viviparous">aplacental viviparous</a>, which was before called <a href="/wiki/Ovoviviparity" title="Ovoviviparity">ovoviviparity</a>. Fertilization is internal. The male inserts one clasper into the female oviduct orifice and injects sperm along a groove on the clasper's dorsal section. Immediately following fertilization, the eggs are surrounded by thin shells called "candles" with one candle usually surrounding several eggs. Mating takes place in the winter months with gestation lasting 22â24 months. Litters range between 2 and 11 but average 6 or 7.</p>
<p>Spiny dogfish are bottom-dwellers They are commonly found at depths of around 50â149Â m (164â489Â ft), but have been found deeper than 700Â m (2,300Â ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Life span is estimated to be up to 100 years and their gestation period is 18 to 24 months, which may be the longest of any known animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lifespan-sharksmou_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-lifespan-sharksmou-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_dogfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spiny dogfish are fished for food in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. The meat is primarily consumed in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux</a> countries and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The fins and tails are processed into fin needles and are used in less expensive versions of shark fin soup in Chinese cuisine. In England this and other dogfish are sold in fish and chip shops as "huss", and it was historically sold as "rock salmon" until this term was outlawed by consumer legislation. In France it is sold as "small salmon" (<i>saumonette</i>) and in Belgium and Germany it is sold as "sea eel" (<i>zeepaling</i> and <i>Seeaal</i>, respectively). It is also used as fertilizer, liver oil, and pet food, and, because of its availability, cartilaginous skull, and manageable size, as a popular <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> dissection specimen, in both high schools and universities. Reported catches in 2000â2009 varied between 13,800 (2008) and 31,700 (2000) tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">Bottom trawlers</a> and sink <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a> are the primary equipment used to harvest spiny dogfish. In Mid-Atlantic and Southern New England fisheries, they are often caught when harvesting larger <a href="/wiki/Groundfish" title="Groundfish">groundfish</a>, classified as <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a>, and discarded. Recreational fishing accounts for an insignificant portion of the spiny dogfish harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cape Cod Commercial Fishermenâs Alliance has sponsored an initiative which promotes local, sustainably caught use of the dogfish in restaurants and fish markets in the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. The effort is funded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Oceanographic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanographic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanographic and Atmospheric Administration</a> and attempts to get the public to consume under utilized fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status_and_management">Conservation status and management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiny_dogfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conservation status and management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg/220px-Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg/330px-Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg/440px-Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_of_dogfish.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A large catch of spiny dogfish</div>

<p>Once the most abundant shark species in the world, populations of <i>Squalus acanthias</i> have declined significantly. They are classified in the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> of threatened species as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> globally and <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">Critically endangered</a> in the Northeast Atlantic, meaning stocks around Europe have decreased by at least 95%. This is a direct result of overfishing to supply northern Europe's taste for rock salmon, saumonette or zeepaling. Despite these alarming figures, very few management or conservation measures are in place for <i>Squalus acanthias</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> In EU waters, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Allowable_Catch" title="Total Allowable Catch">Total Allowable Catch</a> (TAC) has been in place since 1999, but until 2007 it only applied to ICES Areas IIa and IV. It was also set well above the actual weight of fish being caught until 2005, rendering it meaningless. Since 2009 a maximum landing size of 1Â m (3.3Â ft) has been imposed in order to protect the most valuable mature females. The TAC for 2011 was set at 0 tons, ending targeted fishing for the species in EU waters. It remains to be seen if populations will be able to recover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the recent past the European market for spiny dogfish has increased dramatically, which led to the overfishing and decline of the species. This drastic increase led to the creation and implementation of many fishery management policies placing restrictions on the fishing of spiny dogfish. However, since the species is a late maturing fish, it takes a while to rebuild the population.</p>
<p>In 2010, Greenpeace International added the spiny dogfish to its seafood red list. "The Greenpeace International seafood red list is a list of fish that are commonly sold in supermarkets around the world, and which have a very high risk of being sourced from unsustainable fisheries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In the same year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" title="Bonn Convention">Convention on Migratory Species</a> (CMS; also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" title="Bonn Convention">Bonn Convention</a>) listed the species (Northern Hemisphere populations) under Annex I of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migratory_Sharks_MoU" title="Migratory Sharks MoU">Migratory Shark Memorandum of Understanding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years however, the US has implemented fishing controls and opened up the fishery. The proposed quota for 2011 was 35.5 million pounds (16.1 million kg) with a trip limit of 4,000 pounds (1,800Â kg), an increase over past years in which the quota has ranged from 5 million pounds (2.3 million kg) to 20 million pounds (9.1 million kg) with trip limits from 2,000 to 3,000 pounds (910 to 1,360Â kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2010, NOAA announced the Eastern US Atlantic spiny dogfish stocks to be rebuilt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in 2011 concerns about dogfish posing a serious predatory threat to other stocks resulted in an emergency amendment of the quota with nearly 15 million pounds (6.8 million kg) being added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
