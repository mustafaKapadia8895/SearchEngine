<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Johnstown Flood</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Johnstown Flood</b> (locally, the <b>Great Flood of 1889</b>) occurred on May 31, 1889, after the <a href="/wiki/Catastrophic_failure" title="Catastrophic failure">catastrophic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dam_failure" title="Dam failure">failure</a> of the <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_Dam" title="South Fork Dam">South Fork Dam</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Little_Conemaugh_River" title="Little Conemaugh River">Little Conemaugh River</a> 14 miles (23 km) upstream of the town of <a href="/wiki/Johnstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Johnstown, Pennsylvania">Johnstown, Pennsylvania</a>. The dam broke after several days of extremely heavy rainfall, releasing 14.55 million cubic meters of water <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup>. With a volumetric flow rate that temporarily equaled the average flow rate of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perkins_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perkins-5">[5]</a></sup> 2,209 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson-6">[6]</a></sup> according to one account, lost their lives, and the flood accounted for US$17 million of damage (about $453 million in 2016 dollars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-US_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-3">[3]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Clara_Barton" title="Clara Barton">Clara Barton</a> and with 50 volunteers, undertook a major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disaster_relief" title="Disaster relief">disaster relief</a> effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Support for victims came from all over the United States and 18 foreign countries. After the flood, survivors suffered a series of legal defeats in their attempts to recover damages from the dam's owners. Public indignation at that failure prompted the development in American law changing a <a href="/wiki/Fault_(legal)" title="Fault (legal)">fault</a>-based regime to <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The village of Johnstown was founded in 1800 by the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_people" title="Swiss people">Swiss</a> immigrant Joseph Johns (anglicized from "Schantz") where the <a href="/wiki/Stony_Creek_(Susquehanna_River)" title="Stony Creek (Susquehanna River)">Stony Creek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Conemaugh_River" title="Little Conemaugh River">Little Conemaugh</a> rivers joined to form the <a href="/wiki/Conemaugh_River" title="Conemaugh River">Conemaugh River</a>. It began to prosper with the building of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Main_Line_Canal" title="Pennsylvania Main Line Canal">Pennsylvania Main Line Canal</a> in 1836 and the construction in the 1850s of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> and the Cambria Iron Works. By 1889, Johnstown's industries had attracted numerous <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> immigrants. With a population of 30,000, it was a growing industrial community known for the quality of its <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nps-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The high, steep hills of the narrow Conemaugh Valley and the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Mountains" title="Allegheny Mountains">Allegheny Mountains</a> range to the east kept development close to the riverfront areas. The valley had large amounts of runoff from rain and snowfall. The area surrounding Johnstown is prone to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a> due to its location on the rivers, whose upstream watersheds include an extensive drainage basin of the Allegheny plateau. Adding to these factors, slag from the iron furnaces of the steel mills was dumped along the river to create more land for building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weather_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-weather-9">[9]</a></sup> Developers' artificial narrowing of the riverbed to maximize early industries left the city even more flood-prone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-nps-8">[8]</a></sup> The Conemaugh River immediately downstream of Johnstown is hemmed in by steep mountainsides for about 10 miles (16 km). A roadside plaque alongside Route 56, which follows this river, proclaims that this stretch of valley is the deepest river gorge in North America east of the Rocky Mountains.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Fork_Dam_and_Lake_Conemaugh">South Fork Dam and Lake Conemaugh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: South Fork Dam and Lake Conemaugh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG/220px-Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG/330px-Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG/440px-Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dam_Site_Abutments_P6220313.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The remaining abutment of the South Fork Dam.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>High above the city, the Commonwealth of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> built the <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_Dam" title="South Fork Dam">South Fork Dam</a> between 1838 and 1853, as part of a cross-state canal system, the <a href="/wiki/Main_Line_of_Public_Works" title="Main Line of Public Works">Main Line of Public Works</a>. Johnstown was the eastern terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Main_Line_of_Public_Works#Western_Division_Canal" title="Main Line of Public Works">Western Division Canal</a>, supplied with water by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Conemaugh" title="Lake Conemaugh">Lake Conemaugh</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoir_(water)" title="Reservoir (water)">reservoir</a> behind the dam. As railroads superseded canal barge transport, the Commonwealth abandoned the canal and sold it to the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>. The dam and lake were part of the purchase, and the railroad sold them to private interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frank_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-frank-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_Frick" title="Henry Clay Frick">Henry Clay Frick</a> led a group of speculators, including Benjamin Ruff, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh</a> to purchase the abandoned reservoir, modify it, and convert it into a private resort lake for their wealthy associates. Many were connected through business and social links to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnegie_Steel" title="Carnegie Steel">Carnegie Steel</a>. Development included lowering the dam to make its top wide enough to hold a road, and putting a fish screen in the <a href="/wiki/Spillway" title="Spillway">spillway</a> (the screen also trapped debris). These alterations are thought to have increased the vulnerability of the dam. Moreover, a system of relief pipes and valves, a feature of the original dam, previously sold off for scrap, was not replaced, so the club had no way of lowering the water level in the lake in case of an emergency. The members built cottages and a clubhouse to create the <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_Fishing_and_Hunting_Club" title="South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club">South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club</a>, an exclusive and private mountain retreat. Membership grew to include more than 50 wealthy Pittsburgh steel, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, and railroad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialist" title="Industrialist">industrialists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-museum-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG/220px-South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG/330px-South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG/440px-South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:South_Fork_Reservoir_area_P6220296.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Location of the South Fork Reservoir at Johnstown Flood National Memorial, Penn.</div>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Conemaugh" title="Lake Conemaugh">Lake Conemaugh</a> at the club's site was 450 feet (140 m) in elevation above Johnstown. The lake was about 2 miles (3.2 km) long, about 1 mile (1.6 km) wide, and 60 feet (18 m) deep near the dam.</p>
<p>The dam was 72 feet (22 m) high and 931 feet (284 m) long. After 1881, when the club opened, the dam frequently sprang leaks. It was patched, mostly with mud and straw.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There had been some speculation as to the dam's integrity, and concerns had been raised by the head of the Cambria Iron Works downstream in Johnstown.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_of_the_flood"><span id="The_Great_Flood_of_1889"></span> Events of the flood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Events of the flood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>On May 28, 1889, a low-pressure area formed over <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>. By the time this weather pattern reached western Pennsylvania two days later, it had developed into what would be termed the heaviest rainfall event that had ever been recorded in that part of the United States. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Signal_Corps" title="U.S. Army Signal Corps">U.S. Army Signal Corps</a> estimated that 6 to 10 inches (150 to 250 mm) of rain fell in 24 hours over the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frank_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-frank-10">[10]</a></sup> During the night, small creeks became roaring torrents, ripping out trees and debris. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">Telegraph</a> lines were downed and rail lines were washed away. Before daybreak, the <a href="/wiki/Conemaugh_River" title="Conemaugh River">Conemaugh River</a> that ran through Johnstown was about to overwhelm its banks.</p>

<p>On the morning of May 31, in a farmhouse on a hill just above the South Fork Dam, Elias Unger, president of the South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club, awoke to the sight of Lake Conemaugh swollen after a night-long heavy rainfall. Unger ran outside in the still-pouring rain to assess the situation and saw that the water was nearly cresting the dam. He quickly assembled a group of men to save the face of the dam by trying to unclog the spillway; it was blocked by the broken fish trap and debris caused by the swollen waterline. Other men tried digging a ditch at the other end of the dam, on the western abutment which was lower than the dam crest. The idea was to let more water out of the lake to try to prevent overtopping of the crest, but without success. Most remained on top of the dam, some plowing earth to raise it, while others tried to pile mud and rock on the face to save the eroding wall.</p>
<p>John Parke, an engineer for the South Fork Club, briefly considered cutting through the dam's end, where the pressure would be less, but decided against it as that would have ensured failure of the dam. Twice, under orders from Unger, Parke rode on horseback to the nearby town of South Fork to the telegraph office to send warnings to Johnstown explaining the critical nature of the eroding dam. But the warnings were not passed to the authorities in town, as there had been many false alarms in the past of the South Fork Dam not holding against flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frank_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-frank-10">[10]</a></sup> Unger, Parke, and the rest of the men continued working until exhausted to save the face of the dam; they abandoned their efforts at around 1:30 p.m., fearing that their efforts were futile and the dam was at risk of imminent collapse. Unger ordered all of his men to fall back to high ground on both sides of the dam where they could do nothing but wait. During the day in Johnstown, the situation worsened as water rose to as high as 10 feet (3.0 m)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane.2C_F.W._1966_p.129_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lane.2C_F.W._1966_p.129-12">[12]</a></sup> in the streets, trapping some people in their houses.</p>
<p>Between 2:50 and 2:55 p.m. the South Fork Dam breached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A LiDAR analysis of the Conemaugh Lake basin reveals that it contained 14.55 million cubic meters (3.843 billion gallons) of water at the moment the dam collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup> Modern dam-breach computer modeling reveals that it took approximately 65 minutes for most of the lake to empty after the dam began to fail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup> The first town to be hit by the flood was <a href="/wiki/South_Fork,_Pennsylvania" title="South Fork, Pennsylvania">South Fork</a>. The town was on high ground, and most of the people escaped by running up the nearby hills when they saw the dam spill over. Some 20 to 30 houses were destroyed or washed away, and four people were killed.</p>
<p>Continuing on its way downstream to Johnstown, 14 miles (23 km) west, the water picked up debris, such as trees, houses, and animals. At the Conemaugh Viaduct, a 78-foot (24 m) high railroad bridge, the flood was momentarily stemmed when this debris jammed against the stone bridge's arch. But within seven minutes, the <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaduct</a> collapsed, allowing the flood to resume its course. However, owing to the delay at the stone arch, the flood waters gained renewed hydraulic head, resulting in a stronger, more abrupt wave of water hitting places downstream than otherwise would have been expected. The small town of <a href="/wiki/Mineral_Point,_Pennsylvania" title="Mineral Point, Pennsylvania">Mineral Point</a>, one mile (1.6 km) below the Conemaugh Viaduct, was the first populated place to be hit with this renewed force. About 30 families lived on the village's single street. After the flood, there were no structures, no topsoil, no sub-soil – only the bedrock was left. The death toll here was approximately 16 people. In 2009, studies showed that the flood's flow rate through the narrow valley exceeded 420,000 cubic feet per second (12,000 m<sup>3</sup>/s), comparable to the flow rate of the Mississippi River at its delta, which varies between 250,000 and 710,000 cu ft/s (7,000 and 20,000 m<sup>3</sup>/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perkins_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Perkins-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The village of East Conemaugh was next. One witness on high ground near the town described the water as almost obscured by debris, resembling "a huge hill rolling over and over".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> From his idle locomotive in the town's railyard, the engineer John Hess heard and felt the rumbling of the approaching flood. Throwing his locomotive into reverse, Hess raced backward toward East Conemaugh, the whistle blowing constantly. His warning saved many people who reached high ground. When the flood hit, it picked up the locomotive and floated it aside; Hess himself survived, but at least 50 people died, including about 25 passengers stranded on trains in the town.</p>
<p>Before hitting the main part of Johnstown, the flood surge hit the Cambria Iron Works at the town of <a href="/wiki/Woodvale_Historic_District" title="Woodvale Historic District">Woodvale</a>, sweeping up railroad cars and <a href="/wiki/Barbed_wire" title="Barbed wire">barbed wire</a> in its moil. Of Woodvale's 1,100 residents, 314 died in the flood. Boilers exploded when the flood hit the Gautier Wire Works, causing black smoke seen by the Johnstown residents. Miles of its barbed wire became entangled in the debris in the flood waters.</p>
<p>Some 57 minutes after the South Fork Dam collapsed, the flood hit Johnstown. The residents were caught by surprise as the wall of water and debris bore down, traveling at 40 miles per hour (64 km/h) and reaching a height of 60 feet (18 m) in places. Some people, realizing the danger, tried to escape by running towards high ground but most people were hit by the surging floodwater. Many people were crushed by pieces of debris, and others became caught in barbed wire from the wire factory upstream and/or drowned. Those who reached attics, or managed to stay afloat on pieces of floating debris, waited hours for help to arrive.</p>

<p>At Johnstown, the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Bridge_(Johnstown,_Pennsylvania)" title="Stone Bridge (Johnstown, Pennsylvania)">Stone Bridge</a>, which was a substantial arched structure, carried the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> across the Conemaugh River. The debris carried by the flood formed a temporary dam at the bridge, resulting in the flood surge rolling upstream along the Stoney Creek River. Eventually, gravity caused the surge to return to the dam, causing a second wave to hit the city, but from a different direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some people who had been washed downstream became trapped in an inferno as the debris piled up against the Stone Bridge caught fire; at least 80 people died there. The fire at the Stone Bridge burned for three days. After floodwaters receded, the pile of debris at the bridge was seen to cover 30 acres (12 ha), and reached 70 feet (21 m) in height. It took workers three months to remove the mass of debris, the delay owing in part to the huge quantity of steel barbed wire from the ironworks. <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">Dynamite</a> was eventually used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Still standing and in use as a railroad bridge, the Stone Bridge is a landmark associated with survival and recovery from the flood. In 2008, it was restored in a project including new lighting as part of commemorative activities related to the flood.</p>

<p>The total death toll was calculated originally as 2,209 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WDL1-1">[1]</a></sup> making the disaster the largest loss of civilian life in the United States at the time. This number of deaths was later surpassed by fatalities in the <a href="/wiki/1900_Galveston_hurricane" title="1900 Galveston hurricane">1900 Galveston hurricane</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_terrorist_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks">September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks</a>. However, as pointed out by David McCullough in 1968 (pages 266 and 278),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-2">[2]</a></sup> a man reported as presumed dead (not known to have been found) had survived. In 1900, Leroy Temple showed up in Johnstown to reveal he had not died but had extricated himself from the flood debris at the stone bridge below Johnstown and walked out of the valley. Until 1900 Temple had been living in Beverly, Massachusetts. Therefore, the official death toll should be 2,208.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ninety-nine entire families died in the flood,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-WDL1-1">[1]</a></sup> including 396 children. 124 women and 198 men were widowed, 98 children were orphaned. One third of the dead, 777 people, were never identified; their remains were buried in the "Plot of the Unknown" in Grandview Cemetery in Westmont.</p>
<p>It was the worst flood to hit the U.S. in the 19th century. Sixteen hundred homes were destroyed, $17 million in property damage levied (approx. $497 million in 2016), and 4 square miles (10 km<sup>2</sup>) of downtown Johnstown were completely destroyed. Clean-up operations continued for years. Although Cambria Iron and Steel's facilities were heavily damaged, they returned to full production within a year and a half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-WDL1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Working seven days and nights, workmen built a wooden trestle bridge to temporarily replace the huge stone railroad <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaduct</a>, which had been destroyed by the flood. The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> restored service to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh</a>, 55 miles (89 km) away, by June 2. Food, clothing, medicine, and other provisions began arriving by rail. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortician" title="Mortician">Morticians</a> traveled by railroad. Johnstown's first call for help requested <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffins" title="Coffins">coffins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undertaker" title="Undertaker">undertakers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolition</a> expert "Dynamite Bill" Flinn and his 900-man crew cleared the wreckage at the Stone Bridge. They carted off debris, distributed food, and erected temporary housing. At its peak, the army of relief workers totaled about 7,000.</p>
<p>One of the first outsiders to arrive was <a href="/wiki/Clara_Barton" title="Clara Barton">Clara Barton</a>, nurse, founder and president of the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-WDL1-1">[1]</a></sup> Barton arrived on June 5, 1889, to lead the group's first major disaster relief effort; she did not leave for more than 5 months. Donations for the relief effort came from all over the United States and overseas. $3,742,818.78 was collected for the Johnstown relief effort from within the U.S. and 18 foreign countries, including Russia, <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turkey</a>, France, Great Britain, Australia, and Germany.</p>
<p>Frank Shomo, the last known survivor of the 1889 flood, died March 20, 1997, at the age of 108.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shomo_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shomo-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_floods">Subsequent floods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subsequent floods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Floods have continued to be a concern for Johnstown, which had major flooding in 1894, 1907, 1924, 1936, and 1977. The biggest flood of the first half of the 20th century was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Day" title="St. Patrick's Day">St. Patrick's Day</a> <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_flood_of_1936" title="Johnstown flood of 1936">Flood of March 1936</a>. It also reached Pittsburgh, where it was known as the Great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Flood_1936" title="Pittsburgh Flood 1936">Pittsburgh Flood of 1936</a>. Following the 1936 flood, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers dredged the river within the city and built concrete river walls, creating a channel nearly 20 feet deep. Upon completion, the Corps proclaimed Johnstown "flood free."</p>
<p>The new river walls withstood <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a> in 1972, but on the night of <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_flood_of_1977" title="Johnstown flood of 1977">July 19, 1977</a>, a severe thunderstorm dropped 11 inches of rain in eight hours on the watershed above the city and the rivers began to rise. By dawn, the city was under water that reached as high as 8 feet (2.4 m). Seven counties were declared a disaster area, suffering $200 million in property damage, and 78 people died. Forty were killed by the <a href="/wiki/Laurel_Run_Dam" title="Laurel Run Dam">Laurel Run Dam</a> failure. Another 50,000 were rendered homeless as a result of this "<a href="/wiki/100-year_flood" title="100-year flood">100-year flood</a>". Markers on a corner of City Hall at 401 Main Street show the height of the crests of the 1889, 1936, and 1977 floods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_case_and_recovery">Court case and recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Court case and recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the years following the disaster, some people blamed the members of the <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_Fishing_and_Hunting_Club" title="South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club">South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club</a> for their modifications to the dam and failure to maintain it properly. The club had bought and redesigned the dam to turn the area into a vacation retreat in the mountains. They were accused of failing to maintain the dam properly, so that it was unable to contain the additional water of the unusually heavy rainfall.</p>
<p>Popular feeling ran high and was best summed up in the concluding lines of Isaac Reed's poem:</p>
<div class="poem">
<p><span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">All the horrors that hell could wish,</span><br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">Such was the price that was paid for – fish!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></span></p>
</div>
<p>The club was successfully defended by the firm of Knox and Reed (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reed_Smith_LLP" title="Reed Smith LLP">Reed Smith LLP</a>), whose partners <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philander_Knox" title="Philander Knox">Philander Knox</a> and James Hay Reed were both Club members. The Club was never held legally responsible for the disaster. The court held the dam break to have been an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_God" title="Act of God">Act of God</a>, and granted the survivors no legal compensation.</p>
<p>Individual members of the club, millionaires in their day, contributed to the recovery. Along with about half of the club members, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_Frick" title="Henry Clay Frick">Henry Clay Frick</a> donated thousands of dollars to the relief effort in Johnstown. After the flood, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>, already known as an industrialist and philanthropist, built the town a new library.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation_of_the_cause_of_the_1889_dam_breach_and_flood">Investigation of the cause of the 1889 dam breach and flood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Investigation of the cause of the 1889 dam breach and flood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 5, 1889, five days after the dam breach flood, the American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) appointed a committee of four prominent engineers to investigate the cause of the disaster. This committee was led by the esteemed James B. Francis, a hydraulic engineer best known for his work related to canals, flood control, turbine design, dam construction, and hydraulic calculations. Francis was a founding member of the ASCE and served as its president from November 1880 to January 1882. The ASCE committee visited the South Fork dam, reviewed the original engineering design of the dam and modifications made during repairs, interviewed eyewitnesses, commissioned a topographic survey of the dam remnants, and performed hydrologic calculations. In their final report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis1891_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francis1891-19">[19]</a></sup> they concluded the South Fork dam would have failed even if it had been maintained within the original design specifications, i.e., with a higher embankment crest and with five large discharge pipes at the dam’s base. This claim by the ASCE committee has now been challenged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ASCE committee completed their investigation report on January 15, 1890, but the report was sealed and not shared with other ASCE members or the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole2017_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole2017-20">[20]</a></sup> At ASCE’s annual convention in June 1890, committee member Max Becker was quoted as saying “We will hardly [publish our investigation] report this session, unless pressed to do so, as we do not want to become involved in any litigation”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole2017_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cole2017-20">[20]</a></sup> Although many ASCE members clamored for the report, it was not published in the society’s transactions until two years after the disaster, in June 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis1891_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Francis1891-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>A hydraulic analysis published in 2016 confirms what had long been suspected, that the changes made to the dam by the South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club severely reduced the ability of the dam to withstand major storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup> Lowering the dam by as much as 0.9 meter (3 ft) and failing to replace the discharge pipes at the base of the dam cut in half the safe discharge capacity of the dam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup> This fatal lowering of the dam greatly reduced the capacity of the main spillway and virtually eliminated the action of an emergency spillway on the western abutment. Walter Frank first documented the presence of that emergency spillway in a 1988 ASCE publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frank_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-frank-10">[10]</a></sup> The existence of the emergency spillway is supported by topographic data from 1889<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis1891_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Francis1891-19">[19]</a></sup> which shows the western abutment to be about one foot lower than the crest of the dam remnants, even after the dam had previously been lowered as much as 3 feet by the South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup> Adding the width of the emergency spillway to that of the main spillway yields the total width of spillway (wasteway) capacity that had been specified in the 1847 design of William Morris, a State Engineer. Lowering the top of the dam by the Club eliminated nearly all of the capacity of the emergency spillway and greatly reduced the discharge capacity of the main spillway on the eastern side. The dam was now vulnerable to large storm events and was unable to survive the May 1889 storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemanetal2016_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Colemanetal2016-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_the_development_of_American_law">Effect on the development of American law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect on the development of American law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Survivors were unable to recover damages in court because of the club's ample resources. First, the wealthy club owners had designed the club's financial structure to keep their personal assets separated from it and, secondly, it was difficult for any suit to prove that any particular owner had behaved negligently. Though the former reason was probably more central to the failure of survivors' suits against the club, the latter received coverage and extensive criticism in the national press.</p>
<p>As a result of this criticism, in the 1890s, state courts around the country adopted <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rylands_v._Fletcher" title="Rylands v. Fletcher">Rylands v. Fletcher</a></i>, a British common-law precedent which had formerly been largely ignored in the United States. State courts' adoption of <i>Rylands</i>, which held that a non-negligent defendant could be held liable for damage caused by the unnatural use of land, foreshadowed the legal system's 20th-century acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_popular_culture"><span id="Legacy"></span> Legacy and popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy and popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At Point Park in Johnstown, at the confluence of the Stonycreek and Little Conemaugh rivers, an <a href="/wiki/Eternal_flame" title="Eternal flame">eternal flame</a> burns in memory of the flood victims.</p>
<p>The Carnegie Library is now owned by the Johnstown Historical Society, which has adapted it for use as The Flood Museum.</p>
<p>Portions of the Stone Bridge have been made part of the <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Flood_National_Memorial" title="Johnstown Flood National Memorial">Johnstown Flood National Memorial</a>, established in 1969 and managed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>.</p>
<p><span id="Popular_culture"></span><span id="In_popular_culture"></span>The flood has been the subject or setting for numerous histories, novels, and other works as well.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theater">Theater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Theater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>

<li>By the early twentieth century, entertainers developed an exhibition portraying the flood, using moving scenery, light effects, and a live narrator. </ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>"<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mother_Country_(song)" title="Mother Country (song)">Mother Country</a>", written by singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_(musician)" title="John Stewart (musician)">John Stewart</a> in 1969, contains the lyrics "What ever happened to those faces in the old photographs / I mean, the little boys....... /  Boys? . . . . . Hell they were men /  Who stood knee deep in the Johnstown mud /  In the time of that terrible flood /  And they listened to the water, that awful noise /  And then they put away the dreams that belonged to little boys."</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Highway_Patrolman" title="Highway Patrolman">Highway Patrolman</a>", a track from <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a>'s 1982 album <a href="/wiki/Nebraska_(album)" title="Nebraska (album)"><i>Nebraska</i></a>, mentions a song titled "Night of the Johnstown Flood."</li>
<li>"Wall of Water" (2015), written and recorded by amateur singer-songwriter/musician David Zuder of Wadsworth, Ohio (whose maternal family came from Johnstown), includes music and lyrics describing the 1889 flood.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnstown_Flood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is literature about the flood. Poems include:</p>
<ul>
<li>"The Pennsylvania Disaster", a poem by <a href="/wiki/William_McGonagall" title="William McGonagall">William McGonagall</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>"<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/By_the_Conemaugh" title="s:By the Conemaugh">By the Conemaugh</a>", a poem by <a href="/wiki/Florence_Earle_Coates" title="Florence Earle Coates">Florence Earle Coates</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Short stories include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Brian Booker's "A Drowning Accident", in <i><a href="/wiki/One_Story" title="One Story">One Story</a></i> (Issue #57, May 30, 2005), was largely based on the Johnstown Flood of 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caitl%C3%ADn_R._Kiernan" title="Caitlín R. Kiernan">Caitlín R. Kiernan</a> featured the flood in her "To This Water (Johnstown, Pennsylvania, 1889)", in her collected <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Pain_and_Wonder" title="Tales of Pain and Wonder">Tales of Pain and Wonder</a></i> (1994).</li>
</ul>
<p>Books about the flood in a historical context include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Willis Fletcher Johnson wrote in 1889 a book called <i>History of the Johnstown Flood</i> (published by Edgewood Publishing Co.), likely the first book account of the flood.</li>
<li>Gertrude Quinn Slattery, who survived the flood as a six-year-old girl, published a memoir entitled <i>Johnstown and Its Flood</i> (1936).</li>
<li>Historian and author <a href="/wiki/David_McCullough" title="David McCullough">David McCullough</a>'s first book was <i>The Johnstown Flood</i> (1968), published by <a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">Simon &amp; Schuster</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Fictional novels include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a> noted the flood in his novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Captains_Courageous" title="Captains Courageous">Captains Courageous</a></i> (1897), as the disaster that destroyed the family of the minor character "Pennsylvania Pratt."</li>
<li>Marden A. Dahlstedt wrote the young adult novel, <i>The Terrible Wave</i> (1972), featuring a young girl as the main character, the book is inspired by the memoir of Gertrude Quinn (Slattery) who was six years old at the time of the flood.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Jakes" title="John Jakes">John Jakes</a> featured the flood in his novel, <i>The Americans</i> (1979), set in 1890 and the final book in the series of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kent_Family_Chronicles" title="The Kent Family Chronicles">The Kent Family Chronicles</a></i>.</li>
<li>Rosalyn Alsobrook wrote <i>Emerald Storm</i> (1985), a mass market historical romance set in Johnstown. The characters Patricia and Cole try to reunite with each other and loved ones after the flood.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Cambor" title="Kathleen Cambor">Kathleen Cambor</a> wrote the historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/In_Sunlight,_In_a_Beautiful_Garden" title="In Sunlight, In a Beautiful Garden">In Sunlight, In a Beautiful Garden</a></i> (2001), based on events of the flood. The book was a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> Notable Book of the Year.</li>
<li>Richard A. Gregory wrote <i>The Bosses Club, The conspiracy that caused the Johnstown Flood, destroying the iron and steel capital of America</i> (2011), a historical novel that proposes a theory of the involvement of Andrew Carnegie and other wealthy American industrialists in the Johnstown Flood, told through the lives of two survivors.</li>
<li>Judith Redline Coopey wrote <i>Waterproof: A Novel of the Johnstown Flood</i> (2012), a story of Pamela Gwynedd McCrae from 1889–1939 through flashbacks.</li>
<li>Kathleen Danielczyk wrote "Summer of Gold and Water" (2013) which tells story of life at the lake, the flood and a coming together of the classes.</li>
<li>Colleen Coble wrote "The Wedding Quilt Bride" (2001) which tells the story of a romance between a member of the club's granddaughter and a man brought in to see if the dam was really in trouble. It follows him trying to convince the people of the danger and then the flood.</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://authormichaeloates.com/#" rel="nofollow">Michael Stephan Oates</a> wrote the historical fiction novel "Wade in the Water" (2014), a coming of age tale set against the backdrop of the Johnstown flood.</li>
<li>Jeanette Watts's "Wealth and Privilege" (2014) portrays the Fishing and Hunting Club at its heyday, and then the main characters scramble for their lives in the Flood at the novel's climax.</li>
<li>Mary Hogan's "The Woman In the Photo" (2016) writes about two young women in present-day and Johnston, Pennsylvania in 1889.</li>
<li>Jane Claypool Miner wrote "Jennie" (1989). An historical fiction romance written about a young girl who rides the flood from South Fork to Johnstown and survives. She then works as a telegraph operator for the reporters flooding the town while advocating for the people living there.</li>
</ul>
