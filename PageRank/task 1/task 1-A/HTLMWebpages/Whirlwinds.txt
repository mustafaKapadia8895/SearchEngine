<p>A <b>whirlwind</b> is a weather phenomenon in which a <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortex</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> (a vertically oriented rotating column of air) forms due to instabilities and <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> created by heating and <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flow</a> (<a href="/wiki/Air_current" title="Air current">current</a>) gradients. Whirlwinds occur all over the world and in any season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whirlwind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whirlwinds are subdivided into two main types, the great (or major) whirlwinds and the lesser (or minor) whirlwinds. The first category includes <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Landspout" title="Landspout">landspouts</a>. The range of atmospheric vortices constitute a continuum and are difficult to categorize definitively. Some lesser whirlwinds may sometimes form in a similar manner to greater whirlwinds with related increase in intensity. These intermediate types include the <a href="/wiki/Gustnado" title="Gustnado">gustnado</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fire_whirl" title="Fire whirl">fire whirl</a>. Other lesser whirlwinds include <a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">dust devils</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Steam_devil" title="Steam devil">steam devils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterspout#Snowspout" title="Waterspout">snow devils</a>, debris devils, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_devils" title="Leaf devils">leaf devils</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shear_eddies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shear eddies (page does not exist)">shear eddies</a> such as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mountainado&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mountainado (page does not exist)">mountainado</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_whirlwinds&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eddy whirlwinds (page does not exist)">eddy whirlwinds</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whirlwind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Major whirlwind</b></p>
<p>A major whirlwind (such as a tornado) is formed from <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> (the most powerful type of thunderstorm) or other powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storms" title="Storms">storms</a>. When the storms start to spin, they react with other high altitude winds, causing a <a href="/wiki/Funnel" title="Funnel">funnel</a> to spin. A <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> forms over the funnel, making it visible.</p>
<p><b>Minor whirlwind</b> <span id="Minor_whirlwind"></span></p>
<p>A <b>minor</b> whirlwind is created when local winds start to spin on the ground. This causes a funnel to form. The funnel moves over the ground, pushed by the winds that first formed it. The funnel picks up materials such as <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> as it moves over the ground, thus becoming visible.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duration">Duration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whirlwind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Duration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Major whirlwinds last longer because they are formed from very powerful winds, and it is hard, though not impossible, to interrupt them. Minor whirlwinds are not as long-lived; the winds that form them do not last long, and when a minor whirlwind encounters an obstruction (a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a>, a <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a>, etc.), its rotation is interrupted, as is the windflow into it, causing it to dissipate.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_weather">Associated weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whirlwind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Associated weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supercell thunderstorms, other powerful storms, and strong winds are seen with major whirlwinds. Wind storms are commonly seen with minor whirlwinds. Also, small, semi-powerful "wind blasts" may be seen before some minor whirlwinds, which can come from a wind storm. These wind blasts can start to rotate and form minor whirlwinds. Winds from other small storms (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_storm" title="Rain storm">rain storms</a> and local <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>) can cause minor whirlwinds to form. Like major whirlwinds, these minor whirlwinds can also be dangerous at times.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_phenomena">Similar phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whirlwind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Similar phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">Eddies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a> may form in any <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a>. In water, a <a href="/wiki/Whirlpool" title="Whirlpool">whirlpool</a> is a similar phenomenon.</p>
