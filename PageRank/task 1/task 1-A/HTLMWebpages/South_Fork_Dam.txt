<p>The <b>South Fork Dam</b> was located on Lake Conemaugh, an artificial body of water located near <a href="/wiki/South_Fork,_Pennsylvania" title="South Fork, Pennsylvania">South Fork, Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. On May 31, 1889, the South Fork Dam <a href="/wiki/Catastrophic_failure" title="Catastrophic failure">failed catastrophically</a> and 20 million tons of water from Lake Conemaugh burst through and raced 14 miles (23 km) downstream, causing the <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Flood" title="Johnstown Flood">Johnstown Flood</a>.</p>
<p>The South Fork Dam was originally built between 1838–1853 by the Commonwealth of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a> system to be used as a reservoir for the state's <a href="/wiki/Main_Line_of_Public_Works" title="Main Line of Public Works">Main Line of Public Works</a> canal basin in Johnstown. It was abandoned by the commonwealth, sold to the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>, and sold again to private interests.</p>
<p>The dam was 72 feet (22 m) high and 931 feet (284 m) long. Between 1881 when the <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_Fishing_and_Hunting_Club" title="South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club">South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club</a> was opened, and 1889, the dam frequently sprang leaks. It was patched, mostly with mud and straw. Additionally, a previous owner had removed and sold for scrap the three cast iron discharge pipes that previously allowed a controlled release of water. There had been some speculation as to the dam's integrity, and concerns had been raised by the head of the Cambria Iron Works downstream in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johnstown,_New_York" title="Johnstown, New York">Johnstown</a>.</p>
<p>Prior to the flood, speculators had purchased the abandoned reservoir, made less than well-engineered repairs to the old dam, raised the lake level, built cottages and a clubhouse, and created the <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_Fishing_and_Hunting_Club" title="South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club">South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club</a>. Members of the exclusive and secretive retreat in the mountains were 61 wealthy <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> steel and coal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financier" title="Financier">financiers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialist" title="Industrialist">industrialists</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Mellon" title="Andrew Mellon">Andrew Mellon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philander_Knox" title="Philander Knox">Philander Knox</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_George_Alexander_Leishman" title="John George Alexander Leishman">John George Alexander Leishman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_Frick" title="Henry Clay Frick">Henry Clay Frick</a>. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Johnson_Morrell" title="Daniel Johnson Morrell">Daniel Johnson Morrell</a>, of the Cambria Iron Works of Johnstown, also became a member, ostensibly to watch the Pittsburgh members' activity.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake_Conemaugh">Lake Conemaugh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Fork_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lake Conemaugh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dam_Failure">Dam Failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Fork_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dam Failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Flood" title="Johnstown Flood">Johnstown Flood</a></div>
<p>On May 31, 1889, after several days of unprecedented rainfall, the dam gave way. A torrent of water raced downstream, destroying several towns. When it reached <a href="/wiki/Johnstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Johnstown, Pennsylvania">Johnstown</a>, 2,209 people were killed, and there was $17 million in damage.</p>
<p>This was the first major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disaster_relief" title="Disaster relief">disaster relief</a> effort handled by the new <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Clara_Barton" title="Clara Barton">Clara Barton</a>. After the flood, victims suffered a series of legal defeats in their attempt to recover damages from the dam's owners. Public indignation at that failure prompted a major development in American law: state courts' move from a fault-based regime to <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a>. The Johnstown Flood became a symbol of the havoc, 20 million tons of water unleashed from the reservoir.</p>
<p>Part of the St. Michael neighborhood, and most of Creslo, of the town <a href="/wiki/St._Michael-Sidman,_Pennsylvania" title="St. Michael-Sidman, Pennsylvania">St. Michael-Sidman, Pennsylvania</a> now sits on the bed of the former Lake Conemaugh.</p>
