<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Walvis Ridge</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WalvisRidge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="541" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/WalvisRidge.jpg/250px-WalvisRidge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/WalvisRidge.jpg/375px-WalvisRidge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/WalvisRidge.jpg/500px-WalvisRidge.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

The Walvis Ridge stretches some 3,000 km (1,900 mi) from the African continental shelf to the Tristan da Cunha hotspot, separating the Angola and Cape Basins.</div>

<p>The <b>Walvis Ridge</b> (<i>walvis</i> means <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whale</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aseismic" title="Aseismic">aseismic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_ridge" title="Ocean ridge">ocean ridge</a> in the southern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. More than 3,000 km (1,900 mi) in length, it extends from the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gough_Island" title="Gough Island">Gough Islands</a>, to the African coast (at 18°S).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goslin-etal-Intro_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goslin-etal-Intro-1">[1]</a></sup> The Walvis Ridge is one of few examples of a hotspot seamount chain that links a <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">flood basalt province</a> to an active hotspot. It is also considered one of the most important hotspot tracks because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tristan_Hotspot" title="Tristan Hotspot">Tristan Hotspot</a> is one of few primary or deep mantle hotspots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sager_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sager-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walvis_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the Walvis Ridge and the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_Rise" title="Rio Grande Rise">Rio Grande Rise</a> are the most distinctive feature of the South Atlantic sea floor. They originated from <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a> and together they form a mirrored symmetry across the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tristan_Hotspot" title="Tristan Hotspot">Tristan Hotspot</a> at its centre. Two of the distinct sections in the Walvis Ridge have similar mirrored regions in the Rio Grande Rise; for example, the eastern section of the Walvis Ridge evolved in conjunction with the Torres Arch (the western end of the Rio Grande Rise, off the Brazilian coast) and, as the South Atlantic gradually opened, these structures became separated. The complex of seamounts in the western end of the Walvis Ridge, however, does not have a similar structure on the American side, but there is a Zapiola Seamount Complex south of the eastern end of the Rio Grande Rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OConnor-Duncan-Intro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OConnor-Duncan-Intro-3">[3]</a></sup> The formation of this mirrored structure is the result of the opening of the South Atlantic some 120 Mya and the <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_and_Etendeka_traps" title="Paraná and Etendeka traps">Paraná and Etendeka</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_flood_basalt" title="Continental flood basalt">continental flood basalts</a>, the lateral-most parts of the structure, formed at the beginning of this process in areas that are now located in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sager_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sager-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Walvis Ridge is divided into three main sections:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goslin-etal-Intro_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goslin-etal-Intro-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>A first 600 km (370 mi) long segment stretching from Africa to approximately longitude 6°E and varying in width between 90–200 km (56–124 mi).</li>
<li>A second section, 500 km (310 mi) long, stretching north-south, and narrower than the first section.</li>
<li>A third more discontinuous section, which is marked by <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a> and connects the Walvis Ridge to the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.</li>
</ol>
<p>Cretaceous <a href="/wiki/Kimberlite" title="Kimberlite">kimberlites</a> in the central Democratic Republic of Congo and Angola align with the Walvis Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Tristan-Gough hotspot track first formed over the mantle plume that formed the Etendeka-Paraná continental flood basalts some <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=135–132">135 to 132</a> Ma</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rohde-etal-2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rohde-etal-2013-5">[5]</a></sup> The eastern section of the ridge is thought to have been created in the Middle <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period, between <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=120–80">120 to 80</a> Ma</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> While the mantle plume remained large and stable, the eastern Walvis Ridge formed along with the Rio Grande Rise over the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rohde-etal-2013_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rohde-etal-2013-5">[5]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=60">60</a> million years ago</span>, the orientation of spreading changed, which is still visible in the orientation of the various sections of the Walvis Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sager_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sager-2">[2]</a></sup> The mantle plume then gradually became unstable and bifurcated <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=60–70">60 to 70</a> Ma</span> to produce the two separate Tristan and Gough hotspot tracks. It finally disintegrated <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=35–45">35 to 45</a> Ma</span> and formed the guyot province in the western end of the ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rohde-etal-2013_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rohde-etal-2013-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hundreds of volcanic explosions were recorded on the Walvis Ridge in 2001 and 2002. These explosions seemed to come from an unnamed seamount on the northern side of the ridge and are thought to be unrelated to the Tristan hotspot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ewing_Seamount" title="Ewing Seamount">Ewing Seamount</a> is part of the ridge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeoclimatic_role">Palaeoclimatic role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walvis_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Palaeoclimatic role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eocene_Thermal_Maximum_2" title="Eocene Thermal Maximum 2">Eocene Layer of Mysterious Origin</a> (Elmo) is a period of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> that occurred <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=53.7">53.7</a> Ma</span>, about two million years after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETM" title="PETM">Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>. This period manifests as a carbonate-poor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_clay" title="Red clay">red clay</a> layer unique to the Walvis Ridge and is similar to the PETM, but of smaller magnitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanography">Oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walvis_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Walvis Ridge is a natural obstacle for the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Current#Agulhas_leakage_and_rings" title="Agulhas Current">Agulhas rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> <a href="/wiki/Warm_core_ring" title="Warm core ring">warm core rings</a> that are shed from the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Current" title="Agulhas Current">Agulhas Current</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Bank" title="Agulhas Bank">Agulhas Bank</a>. In average, five such rings are shed each year, a number that varies considerably between years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schouten-etal-Disc_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schouten-etal-Disc-11">[11]</a></sup> The rings tend cross the Walvis Ridge at its deepest part, but they still lose transitional speed and many rings decay rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Their transitional speed drop from 5.2±3.6 km/day to 4.6±3.1 km/day, but it is not clear how much the Walvis Ridge is responsible for this drop, since the rings' speed drop to 4.3±2.2 km/day between the Walvis Ridge and the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The rings can cross the South Atlantic in 2.5–3 years but only two thirds make it farther than the Walvis Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schouten-etal-Disc_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schouten-etal-Disc-11">[11]</a></sup> When the rings pass over the Cap Basin south of the Walvis Ridge they are frequently disturbed by the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a>, interaction between rings, and bottom topography such as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vema_Seamount&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vema Seamount (page does not exist)">Vema Seamount</a>, but there are fewer obstacles and disturbances west of the Walvis Ridge were the rings tend stabilise. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Agulhas rings transport an estimated 1-5 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sv</a> (millions m²/s) of water from the Indian Ocean to the South Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Originating around Antarctica, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_Bottom_Water" title="Antarctic Bottom Water">Antarctic Bottom Water</a> (AABW) enters the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cape Basin (page does not exist)">Cape Basin</a> between the Agulhas Bank and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agulhas_Ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agulhas Ridge (page does not exist)">Agulhas Ridge</a> after which it flows west north of the Agulhas Ridge. AABW then retroflects at the south-western end of the Walvis Ridge, flows north-east along the ridge before being retroflected south by <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>, with which it exits the Cape Basin and flows into the Indian Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
