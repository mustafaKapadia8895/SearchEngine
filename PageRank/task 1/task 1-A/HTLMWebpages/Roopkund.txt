<p><b>Roopkund</b> (locally known as <b>Mystery and Skeletons Lake</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mystery_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-1">[1]</a></sup> is a high altitude <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">glacial lake</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">Uttarakhand</a> state of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It lies in the lap of <a href="/wiki/Trisul" title="Trisul">Trishul</a> massif and is famous for the hundreds of human <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeletons</a> found at the edge of the lake. The area is uninhabited, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayas</a> at an altitude of 5,029 metres (16,499 feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mystery_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-1">[1]</a></sup> Surrounded by rock-strewn glaciers and snow-clad mountains, the lake is a popular trekking destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-play_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-play-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roopkund_Lake.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Roopkund_Lake.jpg/220px-Roopkund_Lake.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Roopkund_Lake.jpg/330px-Roopkund_Lake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Roopkund_Lake.jpg/440px-Roopkund_Lake.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The Roopkund Lake in August 2014</div>

<p>A shallow lake, having a depth of about two metres, Roopkund has attracted attention because of the human skeletal remains that are visible at its bottom when the snow melts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sati_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-sati-3">[3]</a></sup> Researchers have concluded that the skeletons are the remains of people killed in a sudden, violent hailstorm in the 9th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obscura_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obscura-4">[4]</a></sup> Because of the human remains, the lake has been called <b>Skeleton Lake</b> in recent times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-today_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-today-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_skeletons">Human skeletons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roopkund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Human skeletons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2044" data-file-width="2920" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg/220px-Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg/330px-Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg/440px-Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_Skeletons_in_Roopkund_Lake.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Human skeletons in Roopkund Lake</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Skeletons were rediscovered in 1942 by a <a href="/wiki/Nanda_Devi_National_Park" title="Nanda Devi National Park">Nanda Devi game reserve</a> ranger H K Madhwal, although there are reports about these bones from the late-19th century. At first, British authorities feared that the skeletons represented casualties of a hidden Japanese invasion force, but it was found that the skeletons were far too old to be Japanese soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obscura_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Obscura-4">[4]</a></sup> The skeletons are visible in the clear water of the shallow lake during a one-month period when the ice melts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mystery_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-1">[1]</a></sup> Along with the skeletons, wooden artifacts, iron spearheads, leather slippers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riddle_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-riddle-6">[6]</a></sup> and rings were also found. When a team from <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i> magazine retrieved about 30 skeletons, flesh was still attached to some of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mystery_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-1">[1]</a></sup> Geneticists, Niraj Rai along with Manvendra Singh at the Center for Cellular and Molecular Biology at Hyderabad, conducted DNA tests on a hundred samples from the lake and compared them to the current Indian population. Results indicated that 70 percent of them had an affinity with Iran, while the remaining belonged to the local population. It is hypothesized that the Iran group took the help of local porters to seek new land for settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mystery_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Later studies placed the time of mass death around the 9th century CE (1,200 years old).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deccan_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-deccan-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Local legend says that the King of Kanauj, Raja Jasdhaval, with his pregnant wife, Rani Balampa, their servants, a dance troupe and others went on a pilgrimage to Nanda Devi shrine, and the group faced a storm with large hailstones, from which the entire party perished near Roopkund Lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missing1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-missing1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legend_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-legend-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Remnants belonging to more than 300 people have been found. <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> of the bones at Oxford University's Radiocarbon Accelerator Unit determined the time period to be 850 CE ±30 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Anthropological Survey of India conducted a study of the skeletons during the 1950s and some samples are displayed at the Anthropological Survey of India Museum, <a href="/wiki/Dehradun" title="Dehradun">Dehradun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-protect_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-protect-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roopkund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scientific tests from a 2004 expedition to the lake revealed that the skeletons belonged to several groups of people, including a group of short people (probably local porters) and a taller group, who were closely related—with DNA mutations characteristic for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konkanastha_Brahmin" title="Konkanastha Brahmin">Konkanastha Brahmins</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chitpavan" title="Chitpavan">Chitpavans</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riddle_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-riddle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title='"One'>not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_test" title="DNA test">DNA test</a> conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Cellular_and_Molecular_Biology" title="Centre for Cellular and Molecular Biology">Centre for Cellular and Molecular Biology</a> (CCMB), Hyderabad, indicated that three samples with unique mutation in mitochondrial DNA, matched with those of the Chitpavans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riddle_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-riddle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twist_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-twist-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="A claim by one person in a news story. Not a peer-reviewed study. (March 2017)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The studies of the skeletons revealed a common cause of death: blows to the back of the head, caused by round objects falling from above. The researchers concluded that the victims had been caught in a sudden hailstorm, just as described in the local legends and songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obscura_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Obscura-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>What has not been determined is where the group was headed. There is no historical evidence of any trade routes to <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> in the area, but Roopkund is on an important pilgrimage route of the Nanda Devi cult with <a href="/wiki/Nanda_Devi_Raj_Jat" title="Nanda Devi Raj Jat">Nanda Devi Raj Jat</a> festivities taking place approximately once every twelve years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_concerns">Conservation concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roopkund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conservation concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a growing concern about the regular loss of skeletons and it is feared that, if steps are not taken to conserve them, the skeletons may gradually vanish in the years to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanish_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanish-14">[14]</a></sup> It is reported that tourists visiting the area are in the habit of taking back the skeletons in large numbers and the district administration has expressed the need to protect the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-protect_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-protect-11">[11]</a></sup> The district magistrate of Chamoli District has reported that tourists, trekkers, and curious researchers are transporting the skeletons on <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mules</a> and recommended that the area should be protected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missing1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-missing1-9">[9]</a></sup> Governmental agencies have made efforts to develop the area as an eco-tourism destination in an effort to protect the skeletons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eco_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-eco-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roopkund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Roopkund is a picturesque tourist destination and one of the important places for trekking in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamoli_District" title="Chamoli District">Chamoli District</a>, Himalayas, near the base of two Himalayan peaks: <a href="/wiki/Trisul" title="Trisul">Trisul</a> (7,120 m) and <a href="/wiki/Nanda_Ghunti" title="Nanda Ghunti">Nanda Ghunti</a> (6,310 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-important_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-important-16">[16]</a></sup> The Lake is flanked by a rock face named Junargali to the North and a peak named Chandania Kot to the East. A religious festival is held at the alpine meadow of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedni_Bugyal" title="Bedni Bugyal">Bedni Bugyal</a> every autumn with nearby villages participating. A larger celebration, the Nanda Devi Raj Jat, takes place once every twelve years at Roopkund, during which Goddess Nanda is worshipped.:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-important_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-important-16">[16]</a></sup> Roopkund lake is covered with ice for most of the year.</p>
