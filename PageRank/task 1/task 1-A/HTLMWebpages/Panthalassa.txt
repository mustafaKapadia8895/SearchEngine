<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Panthalassa</h1>

When Panthalassa surrounded <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a> was located in the northern Hemisphere and <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> in the southern. Outline of present-day landmasses added for orientation.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Panthalassa</b>, also known as the <b>Panthalassic</b> or <b>Panthalassan Ocean</b>, (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%E1%BE%B6%CE%BD" title="wikt:πᾶν">πᾶν</a></span> "all" and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B8%CE%AC%CE%BB%CE%B1%CF%83%CF%83%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:θάλασσα">θάλασσα</a></span> "sea"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineEtDict_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineEtDict-1">[1]</a></sup> was the <a href="/wiki/Superocean" title="Superocean">superocean</a> that surrounded the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a>—<a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> transition <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 250 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr> it occupied almost 70% of Earth's surface. Its ocean-floor has completely disappeared because of the continuous subduction along the continental margins on its circumference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Panthalassa is also referred to as the <b>Paleo-Pacific</b> ("old Pacific") or <b>Proto-Pacific</b> because the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> developed from its centre in the Mesozoic to the present.</p>

<p>The supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a> began to break-up 870–845 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr> probably as a consequence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superplume" title="Superplume">superplume</a> caused by mantle slab avalanches along the margins of the supercontinent. In a second episode <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 750 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr> the western half of Rodinia started to rift part: western <a href="/wiki/Kalahari_Craton" title="Kalahari Craton">Kalahari</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_China_(continent)" title="South China (continent)">South China</a>) broke away from the western margins of <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a>; and by 720 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr> Australia and East Antarctica had also separated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the Late Jurassic the Pacific Plate opened originating from a <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">triple junction</a> between the Panthalassic <a href="/wiki/Farallon_Plate" title="Farallon Plate">Farallon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Plate" title="Phoenix Plate">Phoenix</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Izanagi_Plate" title="Izanagi Plate">Izanagi</a> plates. Panthalassa can be reconstructed based on magnetic lineations and fracture zones preserved in the western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seton-M.C3.BCller-intro_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seton-M.C3.BCller-intro-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In western Laurentia (North America), a tectonic episode that preceded this rifting produced <a href="/wiki/Aulacogen" title="Aulacogen">failed rifts</a> that harboured large depositional basins in Western Laurentia. The global ocean of <a href="/wiki/Mirovia" title="Mirovia">Mirovia</a>, an ocean that surrounded Rodinia, started to shrink as the Pan-African ocean and Panthalassa expanded.</p>
<p>Between 650 million and 550 million years ago, another supercontinent started to form: <a href="/wiki/Pannotia" title="Pannotia">Pannotia</a>, which was shaped like a "V". Inside the "V" was Panthalassa, outside of the "V" were the <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_Ocean" title="Pan-African Ocean">Pan-African Ocean</a> and remnants of the Mirovia Ocean.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_of_ocean_basin">Reconstruction of ocean basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panthalassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reconstruction of ocean basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the oceanic plates that formed the ocean floor of Panthalassa have been subducted and traditional plate tectonic reconstructions based on <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anomaly" title="Magnetic anomaly">magnetic anomalies</a> can therefore only be used for remains from the Cretaceous and later. The former margins of the ocean, however, contain <a href="/wiki/Allochthon" title="Allochthon">allochthonous</a> <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a> with preserved Triassic–Jurassic intra-Panthalassic volcanic arcs, including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kolyma%E2%80%93Omolon_superterrane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kolyma–Omolon superterrane (page does not exist)">Kolyma–Omolon</a> (northeast Asia), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anadyr%E2%80%93Koryak_terrane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anadyr–Koryak terrane (page does not exist)">Anadyr–Koryak</a> (east Asia), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oku%E2%80%93Niikappu_terrane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oku–Niikappu terrane (page does not exist)">Oku–Niikappu</a> (Japan), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrangellia" title="Wrangellia">Wrangellia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stikinia" title="Stikinia">Stikinia</a> (western North America). Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Seismic_tomography" title="Seismic tomography">seismic tomography</a> is being used to identify subducted slabs in the mantle, from which the location of former Panthalassic subduction zones can be derived. A series of such subduction zones, called Telkhinia, defines two separate oceans or systems of oceanic plates — the Pontus and Thalassa oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Named marginal oceans or oceanic plates include (clockwise) <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mongol-Okhotsk_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mongol-Okhotsk Ocean (page does not exist)">Mongol-Okhotsk</a> (now a <a href="/wiki/Suture_(geology)" title="Suture (geology)">suture</a> between Mongolia and Sea of Okhotsk), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oimyakon_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oimyakon Ocean (page does not exist)">Oimyakon</a> (between Asian craton and Kolyma-Omolon), <a href="/wiki/Slide_Mountain_Ocean" title="Slide Mountain Ocean">Slide Mountain Ocean</a> (British Columbia),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-N2000-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mezcalera_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mezcalera Ocean (page does not exist)">Mezcalera</a> (western Mexico).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_margin">Eastern margin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panthalassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eastern margin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The western margin (modern coordinates) of Laurentia originated during the Neoproterozoic break-up of Rodinia. The <a href="/wiki/North_American_Cordillera" title="North American Cordillera">North American Cordillera</a> is an accretionary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orogen" title="Orogen">orogen</a> which grew by the progressive addition of <a href="/wiki/Allochthon" title="Allochthon">allochthonous</a> <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terranes</a> along this margin from the Late Palaeozoic. Devonian back-arc volcanism reveals how this eastern Panthalassic margin developed into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_margin" title="Active margin">active margin</a> it still is in the mid-Palaeozoic. Most of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_fragment" title="Continental fragment">continental fragments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_arc" title="Volcanic arc">volcanic arcs</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">ocean basins</a> added to Laurentia this way contained faunas of Tethyan or Asian affinity. Similar terranes added to the northern Laurentia, in contrast, have affinities with Baltica, Siberia, and the northern <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Caledonies</a>. These latter terranes were probably accreted along the eastern Panthalassa margin by a <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Plate" title="Caribbean Plate">Caribbean</a>–<a href="/wiki/Scotia_Plate" title="Scotia Plate">Scotia</a>-style subduction system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_margin">Western margin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panthalassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Western margin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The evolution of the Panthalassa–Tethys boundary is poorly known because little oceanic crust is preserved — both the Izanagi and the conjugate Pacific Ocean floor is subducted and the ocean ridge that separated them probably subducted <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 60–55 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>. Today the region is dominated by the collision of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Plate" title="Australian Plate">Australian Plate</a> with a complex network of plate boundaries in south-east Asia, including the <a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a> block. Spreading along the Pacific-Phoenix ridge ended 83 Ma at the Osbourn Trough at the <a href="/wiki/Tonga_Trench" title="Tonga Trench">Tonga</a>-<a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Trench" title="Kermadec Trench">Kermadec Trench</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seton-M.C3.BCller-intro_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seton-M.C3.BCller-intro-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Permian <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a> developed near the Equator on the mid-Panthalassic seamounts. As Panthalassa subducted along its western margin during the Triassic and Early Jurassic, these seamounts and palaeo-atolls were <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(geology)" title="Accretion (geology)">accreted</a> as <a href="/wiki/Allochthon" title="Allochthon">allochthonous</a> limestone blocks and fragments along the Asian margin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> One such migrating atoll complex now form a 2-kilometre-long (1.2 mi) and 100-to-150-metre-wide (330–490 ft) body of limestone in central <a href="/wiki/Kyushu" title="Kyushu">Kyushu</a>, south-west Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fusulinida" title="Fusulinida">Fusuline</a> <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>, a now extinct order of single-celled organisms, developed <a href="/wiki/Gigantism" title="Gigantism">gigantism</a> — the genus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eopolydiexodina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eopolydiexodina (page does not exist)">Eopolydiexodina</a></i>, for example, reached up to 16 cm (6.3 in) in size — and structural sophistication, including symbiont relationships with photosynthesising algae, during the Late Carboniferous and Permian. The <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 260 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>, however, put an end to this development with only dwarf taxa persisting throughout the Permian until the final fusuline extinction <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 252 <abbr title="million years ago">Ma</abbr>. Permian fusulines also developed a remarkable provincialism by which fusulines can be grouped into six domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Because of the large size of Panthalassa a hundred million years could separate the accretion of different groups of fusulines. Assuming a minimum accretion rate of 3 centimetres per year (1.2 in/year), the seamount chains on which these groups evolved would be separated by at least 3,000 km (1,900 mi) — these groups apparently evolved in completely different environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A significant sea-level drop at the end of the Permian led to the end-<a href="/wiki/Capitanian" title="Capitanian">Capitanian</a> extinction event. The cause for this extinction is disputed, but a likely candidate is an episode of global cooling which transformed large amount of sea-water into continental ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Seamounts accreted in eastern Australia as parts of the <a href="/wiki/Hunter-Bowen_orogeny" title="Hunter-Bowen orogeny">New England orogen</a> reveal the <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> history of Panthalassa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> From the Late Devonian to the Carboniferous Gondwana and Panthalassa converged along the eastern margin of Australia along a west-dipping subduction system which produced (west to east) a magmatic arc, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fore-arc_basin" title="Fore-arc basin">fore-arc basin</a>, and an accretionary wedge. Subduction ceased along this margin in the Late Carboniferous and jumped eastward. From the Late Carboniferous to the Early Permian the New England orogen was dominated by an extensional setting related to a subduction to strike-slip transition. Subduction was re-initiated in the Permian and the granitic rocks of the New England <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">Batholith</a> were produced by a magmatic arc, indicating the presence of an active plate margin along most of the orogen. Permian to Cretaceous remains of this convergent margin, preserved as fragments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zealandia_(continent)" title="Zealandia (continent)">Zealandia</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Rise" title="Lord Howe Rise">Lord Howe Rise</a>), were rifted off Australia during the Late Cretaceous to Early Tertiary break-up of eastern Gondwana and the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Sea" title="Tasman Sea">Tasman Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Junction_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Junction Plate (page does not exist)">Junction Plate</a>, located north of Australia, separated the eastern Tethys from Panthalassa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeo-oceanography">Palaeo-oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panthalassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Palaeo-oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Panthalassa was a hemisphere-sized ocean, much larger than the modern Pacific. It could be expected that the large size would result in relatively simple ocean current circulation patterns, such as a single gyre in each hemisphere, and a mostly stagnant and stratified ocean. Modelling studies, however, suggest that an east-west sea surface temperature (SST) gradient was present in which the coldest water was brought to the surface by <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> in the east while the warmest water extended west into the Tethys Ocean. Subtropical gyres dominated the circulation pattern. The two hemispherical belts were separated by the undulating <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arias-p3_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arias-p3-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In northern Panthalassa there was mid-latitude westerlies north of 60°N with easterlies between 60°N and the Equator. Atmospheric circulation north of 30°N is associated with the North Panthalassa High which created <a href="/wiki/Ekman_transport" title="Ekman transport">Ekman convergence</a> between 15°N and 50°N and Ekman divergence between 5°N and 10°N. A pattern which resulted in northward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sverdrup_transport" title="Sverdrup transport">Sverdrup transport</a> in divergence regions and southward in convergence regions. Western boundary currents resulted in an anti-cyclonic subtropical North Panthalassa gyre at mid-latitudes and a meridional anti-cyclonic circulation centred on 20°N.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arias-p3_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arias-p3-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In tropical northern Panthalassa trade winds created westward flows while equatorward flows were created by westerlies at higher latitudes. Consequently, trade winds moved water away from Gondwana towards Laurasia in the northern Panthalassa Equatorial Current. When the western margins of Panthalassa were reached intense western boundary currents would form the Eastern Laurasia Current. At mid-latitudes the North Panthalassa Current would bring the water back east where a weak Northwestern Gondwana Current would finally close the gyre. The accumulation of water along the western margin coupled with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> would have created a Panthalassa Equatorial Counter Current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arias-p3_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Arias-p3-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the southern Panthalassa the four currents of the subtropical gyre, the South Panthalassa Gyre, rotated counterclockwise. The South Equatorial Panthalassa Current flowed westward between the Equator and 10°S into the western, intense South Panthalasssa Current. The South Polar Current then completes the gyre as the Southwestern Gondwana Current. Near the poles easterlies created a subpolar gyre that rotated clockwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arias-p3_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Arias-p3-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
