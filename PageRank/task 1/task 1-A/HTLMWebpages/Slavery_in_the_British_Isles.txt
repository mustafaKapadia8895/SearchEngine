<p><b>Slavery in Great Britain</b> existed and was recognized from before the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman occupation</a> until the 12th century, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chattel_slavery" title="Chattel slavery">chattel slavery</a> disappeared after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a>. Former slaves merged into the larger body of <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> and no longer were recognized separately in law or custom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 17th century until well into the 19th century, transportation to the <a href="/wiki/Crown_colony" title="Crown colony">colonies</a> as a criminal or an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_servant" title="Indentured servant">indentured servant</a> served as punishment for both major and petty crimes in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a>. During the same period, <a href="/wiki/Workhouse" title="Workhouse">workhouses</a> employed people whose poverty left them no other alternative than to work under <a href="/wiki/Unfree_labour" title="Unfree labour">forced labour</a> conditions.</p>
<p>British merchants were among the largest participants in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>. Ship owners transported enslaved West Africans to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> to be sold into <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slave</a> labour. The ships brought commodities back to Britain then exported goods to Africa. After a long campaign for <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism" title="Abolitionism">abolition</a> led by <a href="/wiki/William_Wilberforce" title="William Wilberforce">William Wilberforce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> prohibited the practice by passing the <a href="/wiki/Slave_Trade_Act_1807" title="Slave Trade Act 1807">Slave Trade Act 1807</a> which was enforced by the Royal Navy's <a href="/wiki/West_Africa_Squadron" title="West Africa Squadron">West Africa Squadron</a>. Britain used its influence to persuade other countries around the world to abolish the slave trade and sign treaties to allow the Royal Navy to interdict their ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somersett%27s_case" title="Somersett's case">Somersett's case</a> in 1772 held that no slave could be forcibly removed from Britain. This case was generally taken at the time to have decided that the condition of slavery did not exist under <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emancipation" title="Emancipation">emancipated</a> the remaining ten to fourteen thousand slaves or possible slaves in England and Wales, who were mostly domestic servants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However slavery elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> was not affected. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Knight_(slave)" title="Joseph Knight (slave)">Joseph Knight</a>'s case in 1778 established a similar position in <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a>. Slavery was abolished throughout the British Empire by the <a href="/wiki/Slavery_Abolition_Act_1833" title="Slavery Abolition Act 1833">Slavery Abolition Act 1833</a>, with exceptions provided for the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Ceylon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a>. These exceptions were eliminated in 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SectionLXIV_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SectionLXIV-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prohibition on slavery and servitude is now codified under Article 4 of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, incorporated into <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> law by the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> law by the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights_Act_2003" title="European Convention on Human Rights Act 2003">European Convention on Human Rights Act 2003</a>. Article 4 of the Convention also bans forced or compulsory labour, with some exceptions such as a criminal penalty or military service.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1066">Before 1066</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Before 1066">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From before Roman times, slavery was normal in <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>, with slaves being routinely exported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Slavery continued as an accepted part of society under the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> and after; <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> continued the slave system, sometimes in league with <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> traders often selling slaves to the Irish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the early 5th century the Romano-Briton <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a> was captured by Irish raiders and taken as a slave to Ireland. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigit_of_Kildare" title="Brigit of Kildare">St. Brigit</a>, a patron saint of Ireland, was herself the daughter of Brocca, a Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" title="Britons (Celtic people)">Brythonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pict</a> and slave in Ireland who had been baptised by Saint Patrick. <a href="/wiki/Early_Irish_law" title="Early Irish law">Early Irish law</a> makes numerous reference to slaves and semi-free <i>sencléithe</i>. A female slave (<i>cumal</i>) was often used as a unit of value, e.g. in expressing the honour price of people of certain classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> From the 9th to the 12th century <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in particular was a major slave trading center which led to an increase in slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup> In 870, <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> besieged and captured the stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Strathclyde" title="Kingdom of Strathclyde">Alt Clut</a> (the capital of the Kingdom of Strathclyde) and in 871 took most of the site's inhabitants, most likely by <a href="/wiki/Olaf_the_White" title="Olaf the White">Olaf the White</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ivar_the_Boneless" title="Ivar the Boneless">Ivar the Boneless</a>, to the Dublin slave markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Maredudd_ab_Owain" title="Maredudd ab Owain">Maredudd ab Owain</a> (d. 999) paid a large ransom for 2,000 Welsh slaves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup> which demonstrates the large-scale slave raiding upon the British Isles. Vikings traded with the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a>, Pictish, Brythonic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxon" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> kingdoms in between raiding them for slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the earliest accounts of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> English comes from the account of the fair-haired boys from <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> seen in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_the_Great" title="Pope Gregory the Great">Pope Gregory the Great</a>. In the 7th century the English slave <a href="/wiki/Balthild" title="Balthild">Balthild</a> rose to be queen of the <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Frankish</a> <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">king</a> <a href="/wiki/Clovis_II" title="Clovis II">Clovis II</a>. Anglo-Saxon opinion turned against the sale of English abroad: a <a href="/wiki/Ine_of_Wessex#Laws" title="Ine of Wessex">law of Ine of Wessex</a> stated that anyone selling his own countryman, whether bond or free, across the sea, was to pay his own <a href="/wiki/Weregild" title="Weregild">weregild</a> in penalty, even when the man so sold was guilty of crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nevertheless, legal penalties and economic pressures that led to default in payments maintained the supply of slaves, and in the 11th century there was still a slave trade operating out of <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, as a passage in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vita_Wulfstani" title="Vita Wulfstani">Vita Wulfstani</a></i> makes clear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_England">Norman England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Norman England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> census, over 10% of England's population in 1086 were slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1102, the Church <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_London_(1102)" title="Council of London (1102)">Council of London</a> convened by <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm</a> issued a decree: "Let no one dare hereafter to engage in the infamous business, prevalent in England, of selling men like animals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, the Council had no legislative powers, and no act of law was valid unless signed by the monarch.</p>
<p>The influence of the new Norman <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a> led to the decline of slavery in England. Contemporary writers noted that the Scottish and Welsh took captives as slaves during raids, a practice which was no longer common in England by the 12th century. However, by the start of the 13th century references to people being taken as slave stopped. While there was no legislation against slavery in Ireland and Wales,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gillingham8-9_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gillingham8-9-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> introduced a law preventing the sale of slaves overseas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> According to historian <a href="/wiki/John_Gillingham" title="John Gillingham">John Gillingham</a>, by about 1200 slavery in the British Isles was non-existent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gillingham8-9_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gillingham8-9-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">Penal transportation</a></div>
<p>Transportation to the colonies as a criminal or an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_servant" title="Indentured servant">indentured servant</a> served as punishment for both major and petty crimes in England and Ireland from the 17th century until well into the 19th century. A sentence could be for life or a specific period. The penal system required convicts to work on government projects such as road construction, building works and mining, or be assigned to free individuals as unpaid labour. Women were expected to work as domestic servants and farm labourers. Similar to slaves, indentured servants could be bought and sold, could not marry without the permission of their owner, were subject to physical punishment, and saw their obligation to labour enforced by the courts. However, they did retain certain heavily restricted rights (this contrasts with slaves who had none).</p>
<p>A convict who had served part of his time might apply for a "ticket of leave" permitting some prescribed freedoms. This enabled some convicts to resume a more normal life, to marry and raise a family, and a few to develop the colonies while removing them from the society. Exile was an essential component and thought to be a major deterrent to crime. Transportation was also seen as a humane and productive alternative to <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">execution</a>, which would most likely have been the sentence for many if transportation had not been introduced.</p>
<p>The transportation of British subjects overseas can be traced back to the English <a href="/wiki/Vagabonds_Act_1597" title="Vagabonds Act 1597">Vagabonds Act 1597</a>. During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>, it has been estimated that approximately 72,000 people were put to death for a variety of crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1911EB_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-1911EB-18">[18]</a></sup> An alternate practice, borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a>, was to <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">commute</a> the death sentence and allow the use of convicts as a labour force for the colonies. One of the first references to a person being transported comes in 1607 when ‘'an apprentice dyer was sent to Virginia' from Bridewell for running away with his master's goods.’’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Act was little used despite attempts by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I</a> who, with limited success, tried to encourage its adoption by passing a series of Privy Council Orders in 1615, 1619 and 1620.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Transportation was seldom used as a criminal sentence until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piracy_Act_1717" title="Piracy Act 1717">Piracy Act 1717</a>, <i>(An Act for the further preventing Robbery, Burglary, and other Felonies, and for the more effectual Transportation of Felons, and unlawful Exporters of Wool; and for declaring the Law upon some Points relating to Pirates.)</i> established a seven-year penal transportation as a possible punishment for those convicted of lesser felonies, or as a possible sentence that capital punishment might be commuted to by royal pardon. Transportation of criminals to North America was undertaken from 1718 to 1776. When the American revolution made it unfeasible to carry out transportation to the thirteen colonies, those sentenced to it were typically punished with imprisonment or hard labour instead. From 1787 to 1868, criminals convicted and sentenced under the Act were transported to the colonies in Australia.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Irish uprising in 1641</a> and subsequent Cromwellian invasion, the English Parliament passed the <a href="/wiki/Act_for_the_Settlement_of_Ireland_1652" title="Act for the Settlement of Ireland 1652">Act for the Settlement of Ireland 1652</a> which classified the Irish population into one of several categories according to their degree of involvement in the uprising and subsequent war. Those who had participated in the uprising or assisted the rebels in any way were sentenced to be hanged and to have their property confiscated. Other categories were sentenced to banishment with whole or partial confiscation of their estates. While the majority of the resettlement took place within Ireland to the province of <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connaught</a>, perhaps as many as 50,000 were transported to the colonies in the West Indies and in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> During the early colonial period, the Scots and the English, along with other western European nations, dealt with their "Gypsy problem" by transporting them as slaves in large numbers to North America and the Caribbean. Cromwell shipped Romanichal Gypsies as slaves to the southern plantations and there is documentation of Gypsies being owned by former black slaves in Jamaica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Long before the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a>, some chiefs, such as <a href="/wiki/Ewen_Cameron_of_Lochiel" title="Ewen Cameron of Lochiel">Ewen Cameron of Lochiel</a>, sold some of their clans into indenture in North America. His goal was to alleviate over-population and lack of food resources in his glens.</p>
<p>Numerous Highland <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> supporters, captured in the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Culloden" title="Battle of Culloden">Culloden</a> and rigorous Government sweeps of the Highlands, were imprisoned on ships on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>. Some were sentenced to transportation to the Carolinas as indentured servants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery_and_bondage_in_Scottish_collieries">Slavery and bondage in Scottish collieries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Slavery and bondage in Scottish collieries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For a period in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_coal_mining" title="History of coal mining">history of coal mining</a> in Scotland, miners were bonded to their "maisters" by a 1606 Act "Anent Coalyers and Salters". A <a href="/wiki/Colliers_and_Salters_(Scotland)_Act_1775" title="Colliers and Salters (Scotland) Act 1775">Colliers and Salters (Scotland) Act 1775</a>, stated that "many colliers and salters are in a state of slavery and bondage" and announced emancipation; those starting work after 1 July 1775 would not become slaves, while those already in a state of slavery could, after 7 or 10 years depending on their age, apply for a decree of the Sheriff's Court granting their freedom. Few could afford this, until a further law in 1799 established their freedom and made this slavery and bondage illegal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Workhouse_slavery">Workhouse slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Workhouse slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Workhouse" title="Workhouse">Workhouse</a></div>
<p>From the 17th century to the 19th century, workhouses took in people whose poverty left them no other alternative. They were employed under forced labour conditions. Workhouses took in abandoned babies, usually presumed to be illegitimate. When they grew old enough, they were used as <a href="/wiki/Child_labour" title="Child labour">child labour</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> represented such issues in his fiction. A life example was <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley" title="Henry Morton Stanley">Henry Morton Stanley</a>. This was a time when many children worked; if families were poor, everyone worked. Only in 1833 and 1844 were the first general protective laws against child labour, the <a href="/wiki/Factory_Acts" title="Factory Acts">Factory Acts</a>, passed in Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbary_pirates">Barbary pirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Barbary pirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">Arab slave trade</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbary_corsairs" title="Barbary corsairs">Barbary corsairs</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="985" data-file-width="767" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg/210px-Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg/315px-Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg/420px-Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evasion_de_cinbq_esclaves_anglais_a_bord_d_un_radeau.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Five Englishmen escaping slavery from <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, Barbary Coast, 1684</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From the 16th to the 19th centuries it is estimated that between 1 million and 1.25 million Europeans were captured by <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbary_slave_trade" title="Barbary slave trade">Barbary slave traders</a> and sold as slaves. Barbary pirates were based on that coast of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> – what is now <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>. Reports of Barbary raids and kidnappings of those in France, Ireland, Italy, Portugal, Spain, and the United Kingdom and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and the fate of those abducted into slavery in North Africa and the Ottoman Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this time the corsairs plundered British shipping pretty much at will, taking no fewer than 466 vessels between 1609 and 1616, and 27 more vessels from near Plymouth in 1625.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and printed lists from London in 1682' state of 160 British ships captured by Algerians between 1677 and 1680. Considering what the number of sailors who were taken with each ship was likely to have been, these examples translate into a probable 7,000 to 9,000 able-bodied British men and women taken into slavery in those years.</p>
<p>On 20 June 1631, in an event known as the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Baltimore" title="Sack of Baltimore">Sack of Baltimore</a>, the village of Baltimore in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>, Ireland was attacked by Algerian pirates from the North African Barbary Coast. The pirates killed two villagers and captured almost the whole population of over 100 people, who were put in irons and taken to a life of slavery in North Africa.</p>
<p>Villagers along the south coast of England petitioned the king to protect them from abduction by Barbary pirates. Item 20 of The Grand Remonstrance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> a list of grievances against <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> and presented to him in 1641, contains the following complaint about Barbary pirates of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> abducting English people into slavery:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"<i>20. And although all this was taken upon pretense of guarding the seas, yet a new unheard-of tax of ship-money was devised, and upon the same pretense, by both which there was charged upon the subject near £700,000 some years, and yet the merchants have been left so naked to the violence of the Turkish pirates, that many great ships of value and thousands of His Majesty's subjects have been taken by them, and do still remain in miserable slavery.</i>"</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enslaved_Africans">Enslaved Africans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enslaved Africans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Slave_relief,_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1119" data-file-width="865" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Slave_relief%2C_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg/210px-Slave_relief%2C_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Slave_relief%2C_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg/315px-Slave_relief%2C_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Slave_relief%2C_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg/420px-Slave_relief%2C_Martins_Bank_Liverpool.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a>

Martins Bank building in <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, showing two African boys manacled, carrying money bags.</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_Africa" title="Slavery in Africa">Slavery in Africa</a></div>
<p>Admiral Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Hawkins_(naval_commander)" title="John Hawkins (naval commander)">John Hawkins</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, a notable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabethan" title="Elizabethan">Elizabethan</a> seafarer, is widely acknowledged to be "the Pioneer of the English Slave Trade". In 1554–1555, Hawkins formed a slave trading syndicate of wealthy merchants. He sailed with three ships for the Caribbean via Sierra Leone, hijacked a Portuguese slave ship and sold the 300 slaves from it in <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>. During a second voyage in 1564, his crew captured 400 Africans and sold them at <a href="/wiki/Riohacha" title="Riohacha">Rio de la Hacha</a> in present-day Colombia, making a 60% profit for his financiers. A third voyage involved both buying slaves directly in Africa and capturing a Portuguese ship with its cargo; upon reaching the Caribbean, Hawkins sold all the slaves. On his return, he published a book entitled <i>An Alliance to Raid for Slaves</i>. Britain soon became the leader in the Atlantic slave trade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the mid 18th century, London had the largest Black population in Britain, made up of free and enslaved people, as well as many runaways. The total number may have been about 10,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_people_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-black_people-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3323" data-file-width="4933" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/210px-Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/315px-Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/420px-Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Johan_Zoffany_-_The_Family_of_Sir_William_Young_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Johan Zoffany 'The Family of Sir William Young'</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was regarded as fashionable amongst the upper classes to have a Black servant and they sometimes feature in paintings, such as 'The Family of Sir William Young', circa 1768 by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Zoffany" title="Johan Zoffany">Zoffany</a>. There were Black people in many towns, such as <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Lancashire" title="Lancaster, Lancashire">Lancaster</a>. Smaller number of Black people were also found in rural areas throughout the country. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of Black people achieved prominence. <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_Sancho" title="Ignatius Sancho">Ignatius Sancho</a> (1729–1780) opened his own grocer's shop in Westminster. He wrote poetry and music and his friends included the novelist <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Sterne" title="Laurence Sterne">Laurence Sterne</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Garrick" title="David Garrick">David Garrick</a> the actor and the Duke and Duchess of Montague. He is best known for his letters which were published after his death. Others such as <a href="/wiki/Olaudah_Equiano" title="Olaudah Equiano">Olaudah Equiano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottobah_Cugoano" title="Ottobah Cugoano">Ottobah Cugoano</a> were active in the abolition campaigns.</p>
<p>The legal status of enslaved people in Europe was often unclear. In Britain it was not finally resolved until abolition in 1838, though after the famous <a href="/wiki/Somerset_v_Stewart" title="Somerset v Stewart">Somerset Case</a> in 1772 enslaved people could not be sent back to the colonies against their will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_people_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-black_people-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Triangular_trade">Triangular trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Triangular trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Triangle_trade2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="327" data-file-width="448" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Triangle_trade2.png/220px-Triangle_trade2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Triangle_trade2.png/330px-Triangle_trade2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Triangle_trade2.png/440px-Triangle_trade2.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Triangle_trade2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The three-way trade in the North Atlantic</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Triangular_trade" title="Triangular trade">Triangular trade</a></div>
<p>By the 18th century, the slave trade became a major economic mainstay for such cities as <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, engaged in the so-called "Triangular trade". The ships set out from Britain, loaded with trade goods which were exchanged on the West African shores for slaves captured by local rulers from deeper inland; the slaves were transported through the infamous "<a href="/wiki/Middle_Passage" title="Middle Passage">Middle Passage</a>" across the Atlantic, and were sold at considerable profit for labour in plantations. The ships were loaded with export crops and commodities, the products of slave labour, such as <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, and returned to Britain to sell the items.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Isle_of_Man_and_the_transatlantic_slave_trade">The Isle of Man and the transatlantic slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Isle of Man and the transatlantic slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> was involved in the transatlantic African slave trade. Goods from the slave trade were bought and sold on the Isle of Man, and Manx merchants, seamen, and ships were involved in the trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judicial_decisions">Judicial decisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Judicial decisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Slavery_at_common_law" title="Slavery at common law">Slavery at common law</a>.</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>, the philosophical champion of the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> argued against slavery (Ch.IV) and asserted that "every man has property in his own person" (§27, Ch.V). By the 18th century African slaves began to be brought into London and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> as personal servants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In a number of judicial decisions between slave merchants, it was tacitly accepted that slavery of Africans was legal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Butts_v._Penny&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Butts v. Penny (page does not exist)">Butts v. Penny</a></i> (1677) 2 Lev 201, 3 Keb 785, an action was brought to recover the value of 10 slaves who had been held by the plaintiff in India. The court held that an action for <a href="/wiki/Trover" title="Trover">trover</a> would lie in English law, because the sale of non-Christians as slaves was common in India. However, no judgment was delivered in the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>An English court case of 1569 involving Cartwright who had bought a slave from Russia ruled that English law could not recognise slavery. This ruling was overshadowed by later developments particularly in the navigation acts, but was upheld by the Lord Chief Justice in 1701 when he ruled that a slave became free as soon as he arrived in England. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>But agitation saw a series of judgments repulse the tide of slavery. In <i>Smith v. Gould</i> (1705–07) 2 Salk 666, Holt CJ stated that by</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the common law no man can have a property in another.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>But in 1729 the then-Attorney General and Solicitor General of England signed the <a href="/wiki/Yorke%E2%80%93Talbot_slavery_opinion" title="Yorke–Talbot slavery opinion">Yorke–Talbot slavery opinion</a> expressing their view (and, by implication, that of the Government) that slavery of Africans was lawful in England. At this time slaves were openly bought and sold on commodities markets at London and Liverpool. Slavery was also accepted in Britain's many colonies.</p>
<p>Lord Henley LC said in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shanley_v._Harvey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shanley v. Harvey (page does not exist)">Shanley v. Harvey</a></i> (1763) 2 Eden 126, 127 that as</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>soon as a man sets foot on English ground he is free.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>After <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R_v._Knowles,_ex_parte_Somersett" title="R v. Knowles, ex parte Somersett">R v. Knowles, ex parte Somersett</a></i> (1772) 20 State Tr 1 the law remained unsettled, although the decision was a significant advance for, at the least, preventing the forceable removal of anyone from England, whether or not a slave, against his will. A man called James Somersett was the slave of a Boston customs officer. They came to England, and Somersett escaped. Captain Knowles captured him and took him on his boat, Jamaica bound. Three abolitionists, saying they were his "godparents", applied for a writ of <i><a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">habeas corpus</a></i>. One of Somersett's lawyers, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Hargrave" title="Francis Hargrave">Francis Hargrave</a>, stated "In 1569, during the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a>, a lawsuit was brought against a man for beating another man he had bought as a slave overseas. The record states, 'That in the 11th [year] of Elizabeth [1569], one Cartwright brought a slave from Russia and would scourge him; for which he was questioned; and it was resolved, that England was too pure an air for a slave to breathe in'." He argued that the court had ruled in Cartwright's case that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Common_Law" title="English Common Law">English Common Law</a> made no provision for slavery, and without a basis for its legality, slavery would otherwise be unlawful as false imprisonment and/or assault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In his judgment of 22 June 1772, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Murray,_1st_Earl_of_Mansfield" title="William Murray, 1st Earl of Mansfield">William Murray, Lord Mansfield</a>, of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King%27s_Bench" title="King's Bench">Court of King's Bench</a>, started by talking about the capture and forcible detention of Somersett. He finished with:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>So high an act of dominion must be recognized by the law of the country where it is used. The power of a master over his slave has been exceedingly different, in different countries.</p>
<p>The state of slavery is of such a nature, that it is incapable of being introduced on any reasons, moral or political, but only by positive law, which preserves its force long after the reasons, occasion, and time itself from whence it was created, is erased from memory.</p>
<p>It is so odious, that nothing can be suffered to support it, but positive law. Whatever inconveniences, therefore, may follow from the decision, I cannot say this case is allowed or approved by the law of England; and therefore the black must be discharged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Several different reports of Mansfield's decision appeared. Most disagree as to what was said. The decision was only given orally; no formal written record of it was issued by the court. Abolitionists widely circulated the view that it was declared that the condition of slavery did not exist under <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a>, although Mansfield later said that all that he decided was that a slave could not be forcibly removed from England against his will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>After reading about Somersett's Case, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Knight_(slave)" title="Joseph Knight (slave)">Joseph Knight</a>, an enslaved African who had been purchased by his master John Wedderburn in Jamaica and brought to Scotland, left him. Married and with a child, he filed a <a href="/wiki/Freedom_suit" title="Freedom suit">freedom suit</a>, on the grounds that he could not be held as a slave in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. In the case of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_v._Wedderburn" title="Knight v. Wedderburn">Knight v. Wedderburn</a></i> (1778), Wedderburn said that Knight owed him "perpetual servitude". The <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Sessions</a> of Scotland ruled against him, saying that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chattel_slavery" title="Chattel slavery">chattel</a> slavery was not recognised under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_Scotland" title="Law of Scotland">law of Scotland</a>, and slaves could seek court protection to leave a master or avoid being forcibly removed from Scotland to be returned to slavery in the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_National_Archives_of_Scotland_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_National_Archives_of_Scotland-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/William_Wilberforce" title="William Wilberforce">William Wilberforce</a>, a member of the House of Commons as an independent, became intricately involved in the abolition of the slave trade in Britain. His conversion to Evangelical Christianity in 1784 played a key role in interesting him in this social reform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> William Wilberforce's <a href="/wiki/Slave_Trade_Act_1807" title="Slave Trade Act 1807">Slave Trade Act 1807</a> abolished the slave trade in the British Empire. It was not until the <a href="/wiki/Slavery_Abolition_Act_1833" title="Slavery Abolition Act 1833">Slavery Abolition Act 1833</a> that the institution finally was abolished, but on a gradual basis. Since land owners in the British West Indies were losing their unpaid labourers, they received compensation totaling £20 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> established the <a href="/wiki/West_Africa_Squadron" title="West Africa Squadron">West Africa Squadron</a> (or Preventative Squadron) at substantial expense in 1808 after Parliament passed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_Trade_Act_of_1807" title="Slave Trade Act of 1807">Slave Trade Act of 1807</a>. The squadron's task was to suppress the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a> by patrolling the coast of West Africa, preventing the slave trade by force of arms, including the interception of slave ships from Europe, the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a>, West Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naval_Museum_.E2.80.93_Chasing_Freedom_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naval_Museum_.E2.80.93_Chasing_Freedom-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> was implicated in slavery. Slaves were owned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican_Church" title="Anglican Church">Anglican Church</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Propagation_of_the_Gospel_in_Foreign_Parts" title="Society for the Propagation of the Gospel in Foreign Parts">Society for the Propagation of the Gospel in Foreign Parts</a> (SPGFP), which had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations" title="Sugar plantations">sugar plantations</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>. When slaves were <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Abolitionism in the United Kingdom">emancipated</a> by <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Parliament" title="British Parliament">British Parliament</a> in 1834, the British government paid compensation to slave owners. Among those they paid were the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Exeter" title="Bishop of Exeter">Bishop of Exeter</a> and three business colleagues, who received compensation for 665 slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_evaluations_of_economic_impact">Modern evaluations of economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavery_in_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern evaluations of economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade#Effects_on_the_British_economy" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade § Effects on the British economy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="400" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg/170px-FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg/255px-FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg/340px-FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FriendsOfNegroEmancipation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>"To the friends of Negro Emancipation"</i>, celebrating the abolition of slavery in the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>.</div>

<p>Historians and economists have debated the economic effects of slavery for Great Britain and the North American colonies. Many analysts suggest that it allowed the formation of capital that financed the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, although the evidence is inconclusive. Slave labour was integral to early settlement of the colonies, which needed more people for labour and other work. Also, slave labour produced the major consumer goods that were the basis of world trade during the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries: <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>. Slavery was far more important to the profitability of <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">plantations</a> and the economy in the American South; and the slave trade and associated businesses were important to both New York and New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, the then British Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, expressed his deep sorrow over the slave trade, which he described as <i>"profoundly shameful"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Some campaigners had demanded reparations from the former slave trading nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
