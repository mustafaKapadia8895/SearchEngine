<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mesoscale meteorology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Mesoscale meteorology</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> systems smaller than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic scale</a> systems but larger than <a href="/wiki/Microscale_meteorology" title="Microscale meteorology">microscale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Storm-scale" title="Storm-scale">storm-scale</a> cumulus systems. Horizontal dimensions generally range from around 5 kilometers to several hundred kilometers. Examples of mesoscale weather systems are <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breezes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_complex" title="Mesoscale convective complex">mesoscale convective complexes</a>.</p>
<p>Vertical velocity often equals or exceeds horizontal velocities in mesoscale meteorological systems due to nonhydrostatic processes such as buoyant acceleration of a rising thermal or acceleration through a narrow mountain pass.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subclasses">Subclasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subclasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mesoscale Meteorology is divided into these subclasses:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Meso-alpha</b> 200–2000 km scale of phenomena like fronts, squall lines, mesoscale convective systems (MCS), tropical cyclones at the edge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic scale</a></li>
<li><b>Meso-beta</b> 20–200 km scale of phenomena like sea breezes, lake effect snow storms</li>
<li><b>Meso-gamma</b> 2–20 km scale of phenomena like thunderstorm convection, complex terrain flows (at the edge to <a href="/wiki/Microscale_meteorology" title="Microscale meteorology">microscale</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Storm-scale" title="Storm-scale">storm-scale</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>As a note, tropical and subtropical cyclones are classified by National Hurricane Center as synoptic scale rather than mesoscale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscale_boundaries">Mesoscale boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesoscale_meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mesoscale boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As in <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">synoptic frontal analysis</a>, mesoscale analysis uses cold, warm, and occluded fronts on the mesoscale to help describe phenomena. On weather maps mesoscale fronts are depicted as smaller and with twice as many bumps or spikes as the synoptic variety. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, opposition to the use of the mesoscale versions of fronts on weather analyses, has led to the use of an overarching symbol (a trough symbol) with a label of outflow boundary as the frontal notation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
