<p><b>Hurricane Ivan</b> was a large, long-lived, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> that caused widespread damage in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a> and United States. The <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> was the ninth named storm, the sixth hurricane and the fourth major hurricane of the active <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Ivan formed in early September, and reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 5</a> strength on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.</p>
<p>Ivan caused catastrophic damage to <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> as a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> storm, heavy damage to <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> as a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4</a> storm and then <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> and the western tip of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 5</a> storm. After peaking in strength, the hurricane moved north-northwest across the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> to strike <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola</a>/<a href="/wiki/Milton,_Florida" title="Milton, Florida">Milton, Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> as a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> storm, causing significant damage. Ivan dropped heavy rains on the Southeastern United States as it progressed northeast and east through the eastern United States, becoming an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>. The remnant low from the storm moved into the western subtropical Atlantic and regenerated into a tropical cyclone, which then moved across Florida and the Gulf of Mexico into <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and Texas, causing minimal damage. Ivan caused an estimated US$18 billion (2004 USD, $22.8 billion 2017 USD) in damages to the United States, making it the seventh costliest hurricane ever to strike the country.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_history_of_Hurricane_Ivan" title="Meteorological history of Hurricane Ivan">Meteorological history of Hurricane Ivan</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>On September 2, 2004, Tropical Depression Nine formed from a large <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> southwest of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. As the system moved to the west, it strengthened gradually, becoming Tropical Storm Ivan on September 3 and reaching hurricane strength on September 5, 1,150 miles (1,850 km) to the east of <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>. Later that day, the storm intensified rapidly, and by 5 pm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Eastern_Time_Zone" title="North American Eastern Time Zone">EDT</a> (2100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>), Ivan became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> hurricane with winds of 125 miles per hour (200 km/h). The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> said that the rapid strengthening of Ivan on September 5 was unprecedented at such a low <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCdisc14_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCdisc14-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>As it moved east, Ivan weakened slightly because of <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-advis18_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-advis18-3">[3]</a></sup> The storm passed over <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> on September 7, battering several of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>. As it entered the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, Ivan reintensified rapidly and became a Category 5 hurricane just north of the Windward <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a> on September 9 with winds reaching 160 mph (260 km/h). Ivan weakened slightly as it moved west-northwest towards <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. As Ivan approached the island late on September 10, it began a westward jog that kept the eye and the strongest winds to the south and west. However, because of its proximity to the Jamaican coast, the island was battered with hurricane-force winds for hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After passing Cuba, Ivan resumed a more northerly track and regained Category 5 strength. Ivan's strength continued to fluctuate as it moved west on September 11, and the storm attained its highest winds of 163 mph (262 km/h) as it passed within 30 miles (50 km) of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a>. Ivan reached its peak strength with a minimum central pressure of 910 millibars (27 inHg) on September 12. Ivan passed through the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Channel" title="Yucatán Channel">Yucatán Channel</a> late on September 13 while its eyewall affected the westernmost tip of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. Once over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, it weakened slightly to Category 4 strength, which it maintained while approaching the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Just before it made landfall in the United States, Ivan's eyewall weakened considerably, and its southwestern portion almost disappeared.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Around 2 am CDT September 16 (0700 UTC), Ivan made landfall on the U.S. mainland in <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Shores,_Alabama" title="Gulf Shores, Alabama">Gulf Shores, Alabama</a> as a Category 3 hurricane with 120 mph (190 km/h) winds; some hurricane information sources put the winds from Hurricane Ivan near 130 mph (210 km/h) upon landfall in Alabama and northwestern Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Ivan then continued inland, maintaining hurricane strength until it was over central <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>. Ivan weakened rapidly that evening and became a tropical depression the same day, still over Alabama. Ivan lost tropical characteristics on September 18 while crossing <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. Later that day, the remnant low drifted off the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">mid-Atlantic</a> coast into the Atlantic Ocean, and the low pressure disturbance continued to dump rain on the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 20, Ivan's remnant surface low completed an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a> loop and moved across the Florida peninsula. As it continued west across the northern Gulf of Mexico, the system reorganized and again took on tropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> On September 22 the National Weather Service, "after considerable and sometimes animated in-house discussion [regarding] the demise of Ivan,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spdisc67_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spdisc67-6">[6]</a></sup> determined that the low was in fact a result of the remnants of Ivan and thus named it accordingly. On the evening of September 23, the revived Ivan made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Cameron,_Louisiana" title="Cameron, Louisiana">Cameron, Louisiana</a> as a tropical depression. Ivan finally dissipated on September 24 as it moved overland into Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6800" data-file-width="5200" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg/200px-Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg/300px-Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg/400px-Hurricane_Ivan_05_sept_2004_1330Z.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Ivan set 18 new records for intensity at low latitudes. When Ivan first became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> hurricane on September 3 (1800 UTC), it was centered near 10.2 degrees north from the equator. This is the most southerly location on record for a major hurricane in the Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Just six hours later, Ivan also became the most southerly Category 4 hurricane on record in the Atlantic basin when it reached that intensity while located at 10.6 degrees north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-7">[7]</a></sup> Finally, at midnight (UTC) on September 9 while centered at 13.7 degrees north, Ivan became the most southerly Category 5 hurricane on record in the Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-7">[7]</a></sup> The latter record would not be surpassed until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Matthew" title="Hurricane Matthew">Hurricane Matthew</a> in <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016</a>, which reached Category 5 intensity at 13.4 degrees north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matthew_16_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matthew_16-8">[8]</a></sup> Ivan was also tied with the <a href="/wiki/1924_Cuba_hurricane" title="1924 Cuba hurricane">"Cuba hurricane"</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_most_intense_tropical_cyclones#North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="List of most intense tropical cyclones">tenth-most intense Atlantic hurricane on record</a> attaining a minimum atmospheric pressure of 910 millibars (26.87 inHg). This record was later surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a> of <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a> which attained a minimum atmospheric pressure of 908 millibars (26.81 inHg).</p>
<p>Ivan had held the world record of 33 (with 32 consecutive) six-hour periods of intensity at or above Category 4 strength. This record was broken two years later by Pacific <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">Hurricane/Typhoon Ioke</a>, which had 36 (33 consecutive) six-hour periods at Category 4 strength. This contributed to Ivan's total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accumulated_Cyclone_Energy" title="Accumulated Cyclone Energy">Accumulated Cyclone Energy</a> (ACE) of 70.38.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The tornado outbreak associated with Ivan spawned 120 tornadoes, more than any other tropical cyclone worldwide.</p>
<p>Scientists from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="Naval Research Laboratory">Naval Research Laboratory</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stennis_Space_Center" title="Stennis Space Center">Stennis Space Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> have used a computer model to predict that, at the height of the storm, the maximum wave height within Ivan's eyewall reached 131 feet (40 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean">Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By September 5, a hurricane watch was posted for <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>. Early on the following day, a tropical storm watch was issued for Grenada. Later that day, hurricane watches were also put into effect for <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>. A tropical storm warning was issued for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and Grenadines">Saint Vincent and Grenadines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a> and Grenada. By 1500 UTC on September 6, the hurricane watches and tropical storm watches and warnings were upgraded to a hurricane warning and expanded to: Barbados, Saint Vincent and Grenadines, Saint Lucia, Tobago, Grenada. Simultaneously, a tropical storm warning was issued for <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>. On September 7, the hurricane warning in effect for several countries was downgraded to a tropical storm warning. By September, all tropical storm and hurricane watches and warnings were discontinued in the eastern portions of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Ivan continued westward, a hurricane watch was issued for the <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a> on September 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Many schools and businesses were closed in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp99_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp99-11">[11]</a></sup> and about 300 people evacuated their homes on <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Caribbean, 500,000 <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaicans</a> were told to evacuate from coastal areas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> but only 5,000 were reported to have moved to shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> 12,000 residents and tourists were evacuated from <a href="/wiki/Isla_Mujeres" title="Isla Mujeres">Isla Mujeres</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, mandatory evacuations of vulnerable areas in <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Jefferson Parish, Louisiana">Jefferson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lafourche_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Lafourche Parish, Louisiana">Lafourche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plaquemines_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Plaquemines Parish, Louisiana">Plaquemines</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Charles_Parish,_Louisiana" title="St. Charles Parish, Louisiana">St. Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._James_Parish,_Louisiana" title="St. James Parish, Louisiana">St. James</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._John_the_Baptist_Parish,_Louisiana" title="St. John the Baptist Parish, Louisiana">St. John the Baptist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tangipahoa_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Tangipahoa Parish, Louisiana">Tangipahoa</a> parishes took place, with voluntary evacuations ordered in six other parishes. More than one-third of the population of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_New_Orleans" title="Greater New Orleans">Greater New Orleans</a> evacuated voluntarily, including more than half of the residents of <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> itself. At the height of the evacuation, intense traffic congestion on local highways caused delays of up to 12 hours. About a thousand special-needs patients were housed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Superdome" title="Louisiana Superdome">Louisiana Superdome</a> during the storm. Ivan was considered a particular threat to the New Orleans area because dangers of catastrophic flooding. However, Plaquemines and St. Bernard Parishes suffered a moderate amount of wind damage. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_preparedness_for_New_Orleans" title="Hurricane preparedness for New Orleans">Hurricane preparedness for New Orleans</a> was judged poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> At one point, the media sparked fears of an "<a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantean</a>" catastrophe if the hurricane were to make a direct strike on the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These fears were not realized, as the storm's path turned further east.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, evacuation of <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile homes</a> and vulnerable areas took place in <a href="/wiki/Hancock_County,_Mississippi" title="Hancock County, Mississippi">Hancock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackson_County,_Mississippi" title="Jackson County, Mississippi">Jackson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrison_County,_Mississippi" title="Harrison County, Mississippi">Harrison</a> counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, evacuation in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Mobile_County,_Alabama" title="Mobile County, Alabama">Mobile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_County,_Alabama" title="Baldwin County, Alabama">Baldwin</a> counties south of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10" title="Interstate 10">Interstate 10</a> was ordered, including a third of the incorporated territory of the City of Mobile, as well as several of its suburbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In Florida, a full evacuation of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> began at 7:00 am EDT September 10 but was lifted at 5:00 am EDT September 13 as Ivan tracked further west than originally predicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Voluntary evacuations were declared in ten counties along the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, with strong emphasis in the immediate western counties of <a href="/wiki/Escambia_County,_Florida" title="Escambia County, Florida">Escambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_County,_Florida" title="Santa Rosa County, Florida">Santa Rosa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Okaloosa_County,_Florida" title="Okaloosa County, Florida">Okaloosa</a>. Ivan prompted the evacuation of 270 animals at "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Little_Zoo_That_Could" title="The Little Zoo That Could">The Little Zoo That Could</a>" in Alabama. The evacuation had to be completed within a couple of hours, with only 28 volunteers available to move the animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ivan killed 64 people in the Caribbean—mainly in <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>—three in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, and 25 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, including fourteen in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Thirty-two more deaths in the United States were indirectly attributed to Ivan. While traversing the eastern United States, Ivan spawned 120 tornadoes, striking communities along concentric arcs on the leading edge of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In Florida, <a href="/wiki/Blountstown,_Florida" title="Blountstown, Florida">Blountstown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marianna,_Florida" title="Marianna, Florida">Marianna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panama_City_Beach,_Florida" title="Panama City Beach, Florida">Panama City Beach</a> suffered three of the most devastating tornadoes. A Panama City Beach news station was nearly hit by an <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">F2</a> tornado during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Ivan also caused over US$13 billion (2004 USD, $16.5 billion 2017 USD) in damages in the United States and US$3 billion in the Caribbean (2004 USD, $3.8 billion 2017 USD).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeastern_Caribbean_and_Venezuela">Southeastern Caribbean and Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Southeastern Caribbean and Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grenada_ivan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="176" data-file-width="300" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Grenada_ivan.jpg/275px-Grenada_ivan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/Grenada_ivan.jpg 1.5x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grenada_ivan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Aftermath of Hurricane Ivan in <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Ivan_in_the_Lesser_Antilles_and_South_America" title="Effects of Hurricane Ivan in the Lesser Antilles and South America">Effects of Hurricane Ivan in the Lesser Antilles and South America</a></div>
<p>Ivan passed directly over <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> on September 7, 2004, killing 39 people. The capital, <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s,_Grenada" title="St. George's, Grenada">St. George's</a>, was severely damaged and several notable buildings were destroyed, including the residence of the prime minister. Ivan also caused extensive damage to a local prison, allowing most of the inmates to escape. The island, in the words of a Caribbean disaster official, suffered "total devastation." According to a member of the Grenadian parliament, at least 85% of the small island was devastated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Extensive looting was reported. In all, damage on the island totalled US$815 million (2004 USD, $1.03 billion 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-27"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elsewhere in the Caribbean, a pregnant woman was killed in <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a> when a tree fell on top of her home,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp99_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-afp99-11">[11]</a></sup> and a 75-year-old Canadian woman drowned in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-28"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Three deaths were reported in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-29">[29]</a></sup> Over five hundred homes on Barbados<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gob113_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-gob113-30">[30]</a></sup> and around 60 homes in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">Saint Vincent and the Grenadines</a> were either damaged or destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-29"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifrc98_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-ifrc98-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Ivan_in_the_Greater_Antilles" title="Effects of Hurricane Ivan in the Greater Antilles">Effects of Hurricane Ivan in the Greater Antilles</a></div>
<p>On September 11 – 12, the center of Ivan passed near <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, causing significant wind and flood damage. Overall, 17 people were killed in Jamaica and 18,000 people were left homeless as a result of the flood waters and high winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Most of the major resorts and hotels fared well, though, and were reopened only a few days after Ivan had passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Damage on Jamaica totaled US$360 million (2004 USD, $456 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-30"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cayman_Islands">Cayman Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cayman Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cayman_-_Ivan_damage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="300" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Cayman_-_Ivan_damage.jpg/250px-Cayman_-_Ivan_damage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/98/Cayman_-_Ivan_damage.jpg 1.5x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cayman_-_Ivan_damage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Damage from Ivan in the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, Governor <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Dinwiddy" title="Bruce Dinwiddy">Bruce Dinwiddy</a> described damage as "very, very severe and widespread." Despite strict building codes which made the islands' buildings well able to withstand even major hurricanes, Ivan's winds and storm surge were so strong that a quarter or more of the buildings on the islands were reported to be uninhabitable, with 85% damaged to some extent. Much of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a> still remained without power, water, or sewer services for several months later. After five months, barely half the pre-Ivan hotel rooms were usable. Only one person was killed on the islands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_2004_summary_22-12"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_2004_summary-22">[22]</a></sup> though at first many deaths were suspected because of the many graves that were washed up during the storm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Damage across the territory was catastrophic, with losses amounting to US$2.86 billion or 183 percent of its <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CaymanDamage_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-CaymanDamage-24">[24]</a></sup> The Letter from the Cayman Islands Government Office in the United Kingdom, 8 October 2004 by <a href="/wiki/McKeeva_Bush" title="McKeeva Bush">McKeeva Bush</a>, Leader of Government Business details the intensity, extent of damage, and recovery process during the months that followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere_in_the_Caribbean">Elsewhere in the Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Elsewhere in the Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were four deaths in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>. The region's <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Development_Bank" title="Caribbean Development Bank">Caribbean Development Bank</a> estimates Ivan caused over US$3 billion (2004 USD, $3.8 billion 2017 USD) damage on island nations, mostly in the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, Grenada, and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-31"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Minor damage, including some beach erosion, was reported in the <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even though Ivan did not make landfall on <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> soil, its storm surge caused localized flooding on <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Cuba" title="Santiago de Cuba">Santiago de Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Granma_Province" title="Granma Province">Granma</a>, on the southern part of the island. At <a href="/wiki/Cienfuegos" title="Cienfuegos">Cienfuegos</a>, the storm produced waves of 15 feet (4.6 m), and <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Pinar del Río">Pinar del Río</a> recorded 13.3 inches (340 mm) of rainfall. While there were no casualties on the island, the Cuban government estimates that about US$1.2 billion (2004 USD, $1.52 billion 2017 USD) of property damage were directly due to Ivan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_2004_summary_22-13"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_2004_summary-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Along with the 14 deaths in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, Ivan is blamed for eight deaths in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, two in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and one in <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. An additional 32 deaths were reported as indirectly caused by the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-32"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As it passed over the Gulf of Mexico off the coast of Louisiana, Ivan caused the destruction of <a class="external text" href="http://www.nola.com/environment/index.ssf/2013/07/taylor_energy_oil_platform_des_1.html" rel="nofollow">Taylor Energy's Mississippi Canyon 20-A production platform</a>, 550 feet above 28 producing oil and gas wells drilled in water 479 feet deep. Waves estimated to be 71 feet caused tremendous pressures below the surface, causing a landslide that obliterated the platform. <a class="external text" href="http://oil.skytruth.org/site-23051/site-23051-chronology" rel="nofollow">Hundreds of gallons of oil per day</a> were still leaking onto the surface of the Gulf ten years later in 2014, and continue to appear to the present date <a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150527031409/http://www.sunherald.com/2015/05/17/6232475/us-says-gulf-oil-spill-could-last.html" rel="nofollow">[2]</a>.</p>
<p>Ivan caused an estimated US$13 billion (2004 USD, $16.5 billion 2017 USD) in damage in the United States alone, making it the third costliest hurricane on record at the time, just behind <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charley" title="Hurricane Charley">Hurricane Charley's</a> US$14 billion (2004 USD, $17.8 billion 2017 USD), and above <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hurricane Hugo</a>. It has since fallen to seventh place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As Ivan made landfall on the U.S. coastline in Florida, there was heavy damage as observed in <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Breeze" title="Gulf Breeze">Gulf Breeze</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navarre_Beach" title="Navarre Beach">Navarre Beach</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pensacola_Beach" title="Pensacola Beach">Pensacola Beach</a>, dwellings situated far inland, as much as 20 miles (32 km) from the Gulf coast, along the shorelines of <a href="/wiki/Escambia_Bay" title="Escambia Bay">Escambia Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Bay_(Santa_Rosa_County,_Florida)" title="East Bay (Santa Rosa County, Florida)">East Bay</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blackwater_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blackwater Bay (page does not exist)">Blackwater Bay</a>, and Ward Basin in <a href="/wiki/Escambia_County,_Florida" title="Escambia County, Florida">Escambia County</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_County" title="Santa Rosa County">Santa Rosa County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Walton_Beach,_Florida" title="Fort Walton Beach, Florida">Fort Walton Beach, Florida</a> on the eastern side of the storm. The area just west of Pensacola, including the community of <a href="/wiki/Warrington,_Florida" title="Warrington, Florida">Warrington</a> (which includes <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Pensacola" title="Naval Air Station Pensacola">Pensacola NAS</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perdido_Key" title="Perdido Key">Perdido Key</a>, and Innerarity Point, took the brunt of the storm. Some of the subdivisions in this part of the county were completely destroyed, with a few key roads in the Perdido area only opened in late 2005, over a year after the storm hit. Shattered windows from gusts and flying projectiles experienced throughout the night of the storm were common. As of December 2007, roads remained closed on Pensacola Beach because of damage from Ivan's storm surge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulf_Breeze_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gulf_Breeze-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Pensacola, the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10_in_Florida" title="Interstate 10 in Florida">Interstate 10</a> <a href="/wiki/Escambia_Bay_Bridge" title="Escambia Bay Bridge">Escambia Bay Bridge</a> was heavily damaged, with as much as a quarter-mile (400 m) of the bridge collapsing into the bay. The causeway that carries <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_90_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 90 in Florida">U.S. Highway 90</a> across the northern part of the same bay was also heavily damaged. Virtually all of Perdido Key, an area on the outskirts of Pensacola that bore the brunt of Ivan's winds and rain, was essentially leveled. High surf and wind brought extensive damage to Innerarity Point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulf_Breeze_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gulf_Breeze-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 26, 2006, over two years after Ivan struck the region, funding for the last 501 <a href="/wiki/FEMA_trailer" title="FEMA trailer">FEMA-provided trailers</a> ran out for those living in Santa Rosa and Escambia counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulf_Breeze_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gulf_Breeze-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alabama">Alabama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Alabama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demopolis" title="Demopolis">Demopolis</a>, over 100 miles (160 km) inland in west-central <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, endured wind gusts estimated at 90 mph (140 km/h), while <a href="/wiki/Montgomery,_Alabama" title="Montgomery, Alabama">Montgomery</a> saw wind gusts in the 60 to 70 mph (97 to 113 km/h) range at the height of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The heaviest damage as Ivan made landfall on the U.S. coastline was observed in <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_County,_Alabama" title="Baldwin County, Alabama">Baldwin County</a> in Alabama, where the storm's eye (and eyewall) made landfall. High surf and wind brought extensive damage to <a href="/wiki/Orange_Beach,_Alabama" title="Orange Beach, Alabama">Orange Beach</a> near the border with Florida. There, two five-story condominium buildings were undermined to the point of collapse by Ivan's storm surge of 14 feet (4.3 m). Both were made of steel-reinforced concrete. Debris gathered in piles along the storm tide, exacerbating the damage when the floodwaters crashed into homes sitting on pilings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brewton,_Alabama" title="Brewton, Alabama">Brewton</a>, a community about 50 miles (80 km) inland, also suffered severe damage.</p>
<p>In addition to the damage to the southern portions of the state, there was extensive damage to the state's electrical grid. At the height of the outages, <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Power" title="Alabama Power">Alabama Power</a> reported 489,000 subscribers had lost electrical power—roughly half of its subscriber base.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere_in_the_United_States">Elsewhere in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Elsewhere in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Further inland, Ivan caused major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a>, bringing the <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> and many other rivers and streams to levels at or near 100-year records. The <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> and its tributaries crested just below their all-time records set by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Diane" title="Hurricane Diane">Hurricane Diane</a> in 1955. Locations in southern <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> received over 7 inches of rainfall from the remnants of Ivan, causing flooding and mudslides. In <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, high winds moved in quickly and unexpectedly, and a boater was killed when his <a href="/wiki/Trimaran" title="Trimaran">trimaran</a> capsized in 50-knot winds on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Sound" title="Long Island Sound">Long Island Sound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In western <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, many streams and rivers reached well above flood stage in an area that was heavily flood damaged just a week and a half prior from the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Frances" title="Hurricane Frances">Hurricane Frances</a>, causing many roads to be closed. High winds contributed to widespread power outages throughout the mountainous region. The <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_Parkway" title="Blue Ridge Parkway">Blue Ridge Parkway</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Interstate_40" title="Interstate 40">Interstate 40</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_River_(Tennessee_%E2%80%93_North_Carolina)" title="Pigeon River (Tennessee – North Carolina)">Pigeon River</a> gorge in <a href="/wiki/Haywood_County,_North_Carolina" title="Haywood County, North Carolina">Haywood County, North Carolina</a> sustained major damage, and landslides were common across the mountains. There was major flooding along the <a href="/wiki/French_Broad_River" title="French Broad River">French Broad River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swannanoa_River" title="Swannanoa River">Swannanoa River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Asheville,_North_Carolina" title="Asheville, North Carolina">Asheville, North Carolina</a> and along the Pigeon River near <a href="/wiki/Canton,_North_Carolina" title="Canton, North Carolina">Canton, North Carolina</a>. As a result of the rain, a major debris flow of mud, rocks, trees, and water surged down <a href="/wiki/Peek%27s_Creek" title="Peek's Creek">Peek's Creek</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Franklin,_North_Carolina" title="Franklin, North Carolina">Franklin, North Carolina</a>, sweeping away 15 houses and killing five people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The system also <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan_tornado_outbreak" title="Hurricane Ivan tornado outbreak">spawned</a> deadly <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> as far north as <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> and destroyed seven oil platforms in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> while at sea. While crossing over the Mid-Atlantic states, Ivan's remnants spawned 117 tornadoes across the eastern United States, with the 40 tornadoes spawned in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> on September 17 setting a daily record for the commonwealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> Ivan then moved into the <a href="/wiki/Wheeling,_West_Virginia" title="Wheeling, West Virginia">Wheeling, West Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> area, causing major flooding and gusty winds. <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_International_Airport" title="Pittsburgh International Airport">Pittsburgh International Airport</a> recorded the highest 24-hour rainfall for Pittsburgh, recording 5.95 inches (151 mm) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Ivan's rain caused widespread flooding. The <a href="/wiki/Juniata_River" title="Juniata River">Juniata River</a> basin was flooded, and the Frankstown Branch crested at its highest level ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> After Ivan regenerated in the Gulf of Mexico, it caused further heavy rainfall up to 8 inches (200 mm) in areas of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and Texas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the morning of September 21, the remnant mid-level circulation of Ivan combined with a frontal system. This produced a plume of moisture over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Maritimes" title="Canadian Maritimes">Canadian Maritimes</a> for four days, producing heavy rainfall totaling 6.2 inches (160 mm) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gander,_Newfoundland" title="Gander, Newfoundland">Gander, Newfoundland</a>. High winds of up to 89 mph (143 km/h) downed trees and caused power outages in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>, Prince Edward Island, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. The system produced intense waves of up to 50 feet (15 m) near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bonavista" title="Cape Bonavista">Cape Bonavista</a>. The system killed two when it grounded a fishing vessel and was indirectly responsible for four traffic fatalities in Newfoundland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chc_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-chc-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grenada">Grenada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Grenada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Ivan_in_the_Lesser_Antilles_and_South_America#Grenada" title="Effects of Hurricane Ivan in the Lesser Antilles and South America">Effects of Hurricane Ivan in the Lesser Antilles and South America § Grenada</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> suffered serious economic repercussions following the destruction caused by Ivan. Before Ivan, the economy of Grenada was projected to grow by 4.7%, but the island's economy instead contracted by nearly 3% in 2004. The economy was also projected to grow by at least 5% through 2007, but, as of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, that estimate had been lowered to less than 1%. The government of Grenada also admitted that <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">government debt</a>, 130% of the island's GDP, was "unsustainable" in October 2004 and appointed a group of professional debt advisors in January 2005 to help seek a cooperative restructuring agreement with creditors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than US$150 million was sent to Grenada in 2004 to aid reconstruction following Ivan, but the economic situation remains fragile. The <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> reports that as "difficult enough as the present fiscal situation is, it is unfortunately quite easy to envisage circumstances that would make it even more so." Furthermore, "shortfalls in donor financing and tax revenues, or events such as a further rise in global oil prices, pose a grave risk."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica_2">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By two days after Ivan's passage, USAID’s hurricane recovery program distributed emergency relief supplies for families who were displaced by the storm. During phase one of the recovery program, communities restored three tourist sites, cleared agricultural lands, and completed disaster mitigation. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">U.S. Peace Corps</a> completed thirty small projects in rural communities and low income neighborhoods. 66 health clinics, 25 schools, and 62 water and sanitation systems were repaired during the first phase of recovery. About 1,379 farmers, herders and micro businesses became eligible for grants. By 2005, 55 schools and colleges were repaired, while restoration of 1,560 houses had occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_3">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 27, 2004, President of the United States <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> submitted a budget to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> which requested over $7 billion (2004 USD) in aid to victims of Hurricane Ivan and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Jeanne" title="Hurricane Jeanne">Jeanne</a> in the following states: Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, North Carolina, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and West Virginia. Over half of the $7 billion (2004 USD) was to cover uninsured damage to property and public infrastructure. $889 million was spent to repair Department of Defense facilities. About $600 million was earmarked for emergency repairs to highways and road damaged by Hurricanes Charley, Frances, Ivan, and Jeanne. The <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Administration" title="Small Business Administration">Small Business Administration</a> (SBA) used $472 million to provide loans for small businesses and homeowners affected by the storm. Approximately $400 million was given by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> to provide financial assistance agricultural producers suffering crop and other losses. Around $132 million (2004 USD) was used to repair Federal facilities by several government agencies, including: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Prisons" title="Federal Bureau of Prisons">Federal Bureau of Prisons</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service" title="United States Forest Service">United States Forest Service</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">United States Army Corps of Engineers</a> used $81 million (2004 USD) for restoration of coastal areas affected by Ivan. In addition, $50 million (2004 USD) of which was for disaster and famine assistance funds Grenada, Jamaica, and Haiti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the storm in Alabama, more than 167,700 people applied for assistance in <a href="/wiki/List_of_counties_in_Alabama" title="List of counties in Alabama">65 counties</a> in the state. over 51 counties in the state became eligible for public assistance. As a result, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">U.S. Department of Homeland Security's</a> <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> (FEMA) and the Alabama Emergency Management Agency (AEMA) received $735 million (2004 USD), which was spent in disaster assistance, and included: low-interest loans for homeowners and businesses, disaster food stamps, Disaster Unemployment Assistance to those left unemployed as a result of Ivan, "Project Rebound", and to fill the 5,856 National Flood Insurance Program claims. In addition, there were repairs to public infrastructure such as roads, bridges, buildings, utilities, facilities, and parks. 20 Disaster Recovery Centers were opened in 13 counties, which also included the <a href="/wiki/Poarch_Creek_Indian_Reservation" title="Poarch Creek Indian Reservation">Poarch Creek Indian Reservation</a>. Overall, FEMA paid 90% of the $735 million (2004 USD), while the AEMA paid for the other 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ivan is suspected of bringing spores of <a href="/wiki/Soybean_rust" title="Soybean rust">soybean rust</a> from <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> into the United States, the first ever occurrences of soybean rust found in North America. Since the Florida soybean crop had already been mostly harvested, economic damage was limited. Some of the most severe outbreaks in South America have been known to reduce <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> crop yields by half or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> Following the storm, more than 138,500 residents in 15 counties of the Florida Panhandle applied for federal and state aid. In those counties, a total of $162.6 million was approved by FEMA’s Individuals and Households Program. In addition, residents of 24 other counties in Florida were eligible for grants and loans. By September 2005, more than $1.4 billion (2004 USD) in federal and state assistance was approved for residents and communities in the Florida Panhandle. In addition, the National Flood Insurance Program paid nearly $869 million (2004 USD) for more than 9,800 insurance claims after Ivan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than $4 million (2004 USD) in disaster assistance was approved for Mississippi by FEMA and the Mississippi Emergency Management Agency (MEMA). In addition, the SBA issued nearly 3,000 applications for low-interest loans to homeowners, renters, landlords, businesses, and non-profit organizations. The loans covered up to $200,000 in real estate repairs/replacements and up to $40,000 in repairs/replacements of personal property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Residents and business owners in eight parishes of Louisiana became eligible for disaster assistance. By one week before the deadline to apply on November 15, 2004, about 9,527 residents applied for disaster assistance. Overall, FEMA and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Louisiana" title="Government of Louisiana">Government of Louisiana</a> provided more than $3.8 million (2004 USD) to those that requested assistance. In addition, the SBA also allowed applications for loans to repair personal property until that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>This storm marked the third occasion the name "Ivan" had been used to name a tropical cyclone in the Atlantic, as well as the fifth of six occurrences worldwide. Because of the severe damage and number of deaths in the Caribbean and United States, the name Ivan was retired in the spring of 2005 by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> and will never again be used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retirednames_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-retirednames-60">[59]</a></sup> It was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Igor" title="Hurricane Igor">Igor</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2010_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Igor" title="2010 Atlantic hurricane season">2010 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCnames_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCnames-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrological_records">Hydrological records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Hydrological records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ivan broke several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological" title="Hydrological">hydrological</a> records; it is credited with possibly causing the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_wave" title="Ocean wave">ocean wave</a> ever recorded, a 91-foot (28-meter) wave that may have been as high as 131 ft (40 m), and the fastest seafloor current, at 2.25 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a> (5 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
