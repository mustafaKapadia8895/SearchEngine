<p><b>HMS <i>Beagle</i></b> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherokee_class_brig-sloop" title="Cherokee class brig-sloop"><i>Cherokee</i>-class</a> 10-gun <a href="/wiki/Sloop-of-war#Rigging" title="Sloop-of-war">brig-sloop</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, one of more than 100 ships of this class. The vessel, constructed at a cost of £7,803 (£563 thousand in today's currency), was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">launched</a> on 11 May 1820 from the <a href="/wiki/Woolwich_Dockyard" title="Woolwich Dockyard">Woolwich Dockyard</a> on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>. In July of that year she took part in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleet_Review,_Royal_Navy" title="Fleet Review, Royal Navy">fleet review</a> celebrating the coronation of King <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV of the United Kingdom</a>, and for that occasion is said to have been the first ship to sail completely under the old <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_22.E2.80.9324_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_22.E2.80.9324-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There was no immediate need for <i>Beagle</i> so she "<a href="/wiki/Reserve_Fleet_(United_Kingdom)" title="Reserve Fleet (United Kingdom)">lay in ordinary</a>", moored afloat but without masts or rigging. She was then adapted as a survey <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">barque</a> and took part in three survey expeditions. On the second voyage a young naturalist named <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> was on board; the pivotal role this round the world voyage played in forming his scientific theories made <i>Beagle</i> one of the most famous ships in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherokee_class_brig-sloop" title="Cherokee class brig-sloop"><i>Cherokee</i>-class</a> of 10-gun <a href="/wiki/Sloop-of-war#Rigging" title="Sloop-of-war">brig-sloops</a> was designed by Sir Henry Peake in 1807, and eventually over 100 were constructed. The working drawings for HMS <i>Beagle</i> and HMS <i>Barracouta</i> were issued to the <a href="/wiki/Woolwich_Dockyard" title="Woolwich Dockyard">Woolwich Dockyard</a> on 16 February 1817, and amended in coloured ink on 16 July 1817 with modifications to increase the height of the bulwarks (the sides of the ship extended above the upper deck) by an amount varying from 6 inches (150 mm) at the stem to 4 inches (100 mm) at the stern. The <i>Beagle'</i>s keel was laid in June 1818, construction cost £7,803, and the ship was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">launched</a> on 11 May 1820. In July of that year she took part in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleet_Review,_Royal_Navy" title="Fleet Review, Royal Navy">fleet review</a> on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, celebrating the coronation of King <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_22.E2.80.9324_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_22.E2.80.9324-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="First_voyage_(1826–1830)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage_.281826.E2.80.931830.29">First voyage (1826–1830)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First voyage (1826–1830)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Captain <a href="/wiki/Pringle_Stokes" title="Pringle Stokes">Pringle Stokes</a> was appointed captain of <i>Beagle</i> on 7 September 1825, and the ship was allocated to the surveying section of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Hydrographic_Office" title="United Kingdom Hydrographic Office">Hydrographic Office</a>. On 27 September 1825 <i>Beagle</i> docked at Woolwich to be repaired and fitted out for her new duties. Her guns were reduced from ten cannon to six and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mizzen" title="Mizzen">mizzen</a> mast was added to improve her handling, thereby changing her from a <a href="/wiki/Brig" title="Brig">brig</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">bark</a> (or barque).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_33.2C36.E2.80.9337_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_33.2C36.E2.80.9337-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Beagle</i> set sail from <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> on 22 May 1826 on her first voyage, under the command of Captain Stokes. The mission was to accompany the larger ship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Aid_(1809)" title="HMS Aid (1809)">HMS <i>Adventure</i></a> (380 tons) on a <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">hydrographic</a> survey of <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, under the overall command of the Australian Captain <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Parker_King" title="Phillip Parker King">Phillip Parker King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commander" title="Commander">Commander</a> and Surveyor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Faced with the more difficult part of the survey in the desolate waters of <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, Captain Stokes fell into a deep depression. At <a href="/wiki/Puerto_del_Hambre" title="Puerto del Hambre">Port Famine</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a> he locked himself in his cabin for 14 days, then after getting over-excited and talking of preparing for the next cruise, shot himself on 2 August 1828. Following four days of delirium Stokes recovered slightly, but then his condition deteriorated and he died on 12 August 1828.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Captain Parker King then replaced Stokes with the First Lieutenant of the <i>Beagle</i>, Lieutenant <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_George_Skyring&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William George Skyring (page does not exist)">William George Skyring</a> as commander, and both ships sailed to <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>. On 13 October King sailed the <i>Adventure</i> to <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> for refitting and provisions. During this work Rear Admiral Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Otway" title="Robert Otway">Robert Otway</a>, commander in chief of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Station" title="Pacific Station">South American station</a>, arrived aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ganges_(1821)" title="HMS Ganges (1821)">HMS <i>Ganges</i></a> and announced his decision that the <i>Beagle</i> was also to be brought to Montevideo for repairs, and that he intended to supersede Skyring. When the <i>Beagle</i> arrived, Otway put the ship under the command of his aide, Flag Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 23-year-old aristocrat FitzRoy proved an able commander and meticulous surveyor. In one incident a group of <a href="/wiki/Fuegians" title="Fuegians">Fuegians</a> stole a ship's boat, and FitzRoy took their families on board as hostages. Eventually he held two men, a girl and a boy, who was given the name of <a href="/wiki/Jemmy_Button" title="Jemmy Button">Jemmy Button</a>, and these four native Fuegians were taken back with them when the <i>Beagle</i> returned to England on 14 October 1830.</p>
<p>During this survey, the <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel" title="Beagle Channel">Beagle Channel</a> was identified and named after the ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glaciers_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-glaciers-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The log book from the first voyage, in Captain FitzRoy's handwriting, was acquired at auction at <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">Sotheby's</a> by the Museo Naval de la Nación (under the administration of the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Navy" title="Argentine Navy">Argentine Navy</a>) located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigre,_Buenos_Aires_Province" title="Tigre, Buenos Aires Province">Tigre, Buenos Aires Province</a>, Argentina, where it is now preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Second_voyage_(1831–1836)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_voyage_.281831.E2.80.931836.29">Second voyage (1831–1836)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Second voyage (1831–1836)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle" title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle">Second voyage of HMS Beagle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="821" data-file-width="1235" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg/220px-HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg/330px-HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg/440px-HMS_Beagle_by_Conrad_Martens.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>

<p>FitzRoy had been given reason to hope that the South American Survey would be continued under his command, but when the Lords of the Admiralty appeared to abandon the plan, he made alternative arrangements to return the Fuegians. A kind uncle heard of this and contacted the Admiralty. Soon afterwards FitzRoy heard that he was to be appointed commander of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Chanticleer_(1808)" title="HMS Chanticleer (1808)">HMS <i>Chanticleer</i></a> to go to <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, but due to her poor condition <i>Beagle</i> was substituted for the voyage. FitzRoy was re-appointed as commander on 27 June 1831 and the <i>Beagle</i> was <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> on 4 July 1831 under his command, with Lieutenants <a href="/wiki/John_Clements_Wickham" title="John Clements Wickham">John Clements Wickham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Sulivan" title="Bartholomew Sulivan">Bartholomew James Sulivan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitz13_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-fitz13-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="955" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg/220px-HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg/330px-HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg/440px-HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Beagle_1832_longitudinal_section_larger.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Longitudinal section of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Ship" title="Her Majesty's Ship">HMS</a> <i>Beagle</i> as of 1832</div>

<p><i>Beagle</i> was immediately taken into dock at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Devonport" title="HMNB Devonport">Devonport</a> for extensive rebuilding and refitting. As she required a new deck, FitzRoy had the upper-deck raised considerably, by 8 inches (200 mm) aft and 12 inches (300 mm) forward. The <i>Cherokee</i>-class ships had the reputation of being "<a href="/wiki/Coffin" title="Coffin">coffin</a>" brigs, which handled badly and were prone to sinking. Apart from increasing headroom below, the raised deck made <i>Beagle</i> less liable to top-heaviness and possible capsize in heavy weather by reducing the volume of water that could collect on top of the upper deck, trapped aboard by the <a href="/wiki/Gunwale" title="Gunwale">gunwales</a>. Additional sheathing added to the hull added about seven tons to her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burthen" title="Burthen">burthen</a> and perhaps fifteen to her displacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitz17.E2.80.9318_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fitz17.E2.80.9318-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ship was one of the first to be fitted with the <a href="/wiki/Lightning_rod" title="Lightning rod">lightning conductor</a> invented by <a href="/wiki/William_Snow_Harris" title="William Snow Harris">William Snow Harris</a>. FitzRoy spared no expense in her fitting out, which included <a href="/wiki/List_of_chronometers_on_HMS_Beagle" title="List of chronometers on HMS Beagle">22 chronometers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitz17.E2.80.9318_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fitz17.E2.80.9318-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PortCities_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-PortCities-14">[14]</a></sup> and five examples of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sympiesometer" title="Sympiesometer">Sympiesometer</a></i>, a kind of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>-free <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a> patented by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Adie" title="Alexander Adie">Alexander Adie</a> which was favoured by FitzRoy as giving the accurate readings required by the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> To reduce magnetic interference with the navigational instruments, FitzRoy proposed replacing the iron guns with brass guns, but the Admiralty turned this request down. (When the ship reached <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> in April 1832, he used his own funds for replacements: the ship now had a "six-pound boat-carronade" on a turntable on the forecastle, two brass six-pound guns before the main-mast, and aft of it another four brass guns; two of these were nine-pound, and the other two six-pound.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>FitzRoy had found a need for expert advice on geology during the first voyage, and had resolved that if on a similar expedition, he would "endeavour to carry out a person qualified to examine the land; while the officers, and myself, would attend to hydrography."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-king-18">[18]</a></sup> Command in that era could involve stress and loneliness, as shown by the suicide of Captain Stokes, and FitzRoy's own uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscount_Castlereagh" title="Viscount Castlereagh">Viscount Castlereagh</a> had committed suicide under stress of overwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> His attempts to get a friend to accompany him fell through, and he asked his friend and superior, Captain <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a>, to seek a gentleman <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> as a self-financing passenger who would give him company during the voyage. A sequence of inquiries led to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, a young gentleman on his way to becoming a rural clergyman, joining the voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Fitzroy was influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Physiognomy" title="Physiognomy">physiognomy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Kaspar_Lavater" title="Johann Kaspar Lavater">Lavater</a>, and Darwin recounted in his autobiography that he was nearly "rejected, on account of the shape of my nose! He was an ardent disciple of Lavater, &amp; was convinced that he could judge a man's character by the outline of his features; &amp; he doubted whether anyone with my nose could possess sufficient energy &amp; determination for the voyage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheBeagleLaidAshore.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2738" data-file-width="4200" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/TheBeagleLaidAshore.png/220px-TheBeagleLaidAshore.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/TheBeagleLaidAshore.png/330px-TheBeagleLaidAshore.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/TheBeagleLaidAshore.png/440px-TheBeagleLaidAshore.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheBeagleLaidAshore.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Beagle Laid Ashore</i> drawn by <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Martens" title="Conrad Martens">Conrad Martens</a> (1834) and engraved by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Landseer" title="Thomas Landseer">Thomas Landseer</a> (1838)</div>

<p><i>Beagle</i> was originally scheduled to leave on 24 October 1831 but because of delays in her preparations the departure was delayed until December. Setting forth on what was to become a ground-breaking scientific expedition she departed from Devonport on 10 December. Due to bad weather her first stop was just a few miles ahead, at Barn Pool, on the west side of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Sound" title="Plymouth Sound">Plymouth Sound</a>,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The <i>Beagle</i> left anchorage from Barn Pool on 27 December, passing the nearby town of Plymouth. After completing extensive surveys in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> she returned via <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, Sydney, Hobart Town (6 February 1836), to <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth, Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, on 2 October 1836.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darwin had kept a diary of his experiences, and rewrote this as the book titled <i>Journal and Remarks</i>, published in 1839 as the third volume of the official account of the expedition. This travelogue and scientific journal was widely popular, and was reprinted many times with various titles, becoming known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle" title="The Voyage of the Beagle">The Voyage of the Beagle</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This diary is where Darwin drew most of the ideas for his publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Darwin attributes his first real training in natural history to his voyage on the <i>Beagle</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Third_voyage_(1837–1843)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_voyage_.281837.E2.80.931843.29">Third voyage (1837–1843)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Third voyage (1837–1843)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In the six months after returning from the second voyage, some light repairs were made and <i>Beagle</i> was commissioned to survey large parts of the coast of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> under the command of Commander <a href="/wiki/John_Clements_Wickham" title="John Clements Wickham">John Clements Wickham</a>, who had been a lieutenant on the second voyage, with assistant surveyor Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/John_Lort_Stokes" title="John Lort Stokes">John Lort Stokes</a> who had been a Midshipman on the first voyage of <i>Beagle</i>, then mate and assistant surveyor on the second voyage (no relation to Pringle Stokes). They left Woolwich on 9 June 1837, towed by H.M. Steamer <i>Boxer</i>, and after reaching Plymouth spent the remainder of the month adjusting their instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stokes_1.1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stokes_1.1-27">[27]</a></sup> They set off from Plymouth Sound on the morning of 5 July 1837, and sailed south with stops for observations at <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvador,_Bahia" title="Salvador, Bahia">Bahia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stokes_1.2_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stokes_1.2-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>They reached the <a href="/wiki/Swan_River_(Western_Australia)" title="Swan River (Western Australia)">Swan River</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>) on 15 November 1837.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stokes_1.3_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stokes_1.3-29">[29]</a></sup> Their survey started with the western coast between there and the <a href="/wiki/Fitzroy_River_(Western_Australia)" title="Fitzroy River (Western Australia)">Fitzroy River, Western Australia</a>, then surveyed both shores of the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Strait" title="Bass Strait">Bass Strait</a> at the southeast corner of the continent. To aid <i>Beagle</i> in her surveying operations in Bass Strait, the Colonial cutter <i>Vansittart</i>, of Van Diemen’s Land, was most liberally lent by His Excellency Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Franklin" title="John Franklin">John Franklin</a>, and placed under the command of Mr Charles Codrington Forsyth, the senior mate, assisted by Mr Pasco, another of her mates. In May 1839, they sailed north to survey the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Timor_Sea" title="Timor Sea">Arafura Sea</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a>. When Wickham fell ill and resigned, the command was taken over in March 1841 by Lieutenant John Lort Stokes who continued the survey. The third voyage was completed in 1843.</p>
<p>Numerous places around the coast were named by Wickham, and subsequently by Stokes when he became captain, often honouring eminent people or the members of the crew. On 9 October 1839 Wickham named <a href="/wiki/Port_Darwin" title="Port Darwin">Port Darwin</a>, which was first sighted by Stokes, in honour of their former shipmate Charles Darwin. They were reminded of him (and his "geologising") by the discovery there of a new fine-grained sandstone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_and_Exploration_of_Australia_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery_and_Exploration_of_Australia-30">[30]</a></sup> A settlement there became the town of Palmerston in 1869, and was renamed <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a> in 1911 (not to be confused with the present day city of <a href="/wiki/Palmerston,_Northern_Territory" title="Palmerston, Northern Territory">Palmerston</a> near Darwin).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlDarwin_-_Northern_Territory_-_Australia_-_Travel_-_smh.com.au_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlDarwin_-_Northern_Territory_-_Australia_-_Travel_-_smh.com.au-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this survey, the <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Gulf" title="Beagle Gulf">Beagle Gulf</a> was named after the ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana_benthamiana" title="Nicotiana benthamiana">Nicotiana benthamiana</a></i>, a species of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> being used as a platform for the production of <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant pharmaceutical proteins</a>, was first collected for scientific study on the north coast of Australia by Benjamin Bynoe during this voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patton_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patton-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1845, <i>Beagle</i> was refitted as a static <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Coastguard" title="Her Majesty's Coastguard">coastguard</a> watch vessel like many similar watch ships stationed in rivers and harbours throughout the nation. She was transferred to <a href="/wiki/HM_Customs_and_Excise" title="HM Customs and Excise">HM Customs and Excise</a> to control smuggling on the <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> coast in the navigable waterways beyond the north bank of the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Estuary" title="Thames Estuary">Thames Estuary</a>. She was moored mid-river in the <a href="/wiki/River_Roach" title="River Roach">River Roach</a> which forms part of an extensive maze of waterways and marshes known as The River Crouch and River Roach Tidal River System, located around and to the south and west of <a href="/wiki/Burnham-on-Crouch" title="Burnham-on-Crouch">Burnham-on-Crouch</a>. This large maritime area has a tidal coastline of 243 km (151 mi), part of Essex's 565 km (351 mi) of coastline – the largest coastline in the United Kingdom. In 1851, <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> companies and traders who cultivated and harvested the "Walflete" or "Walfleet" oyster <i><a href="/wiki/Ostrea_edulis" title="Ostrea edulis">Ostrea edulis</a></i>, petitioned for the Customs and Excise watch vessel <i>WV-7</i> (ex HMS <i>Beagle</i>) to be removed as she was obstructing the river and its oyster-beds. In the 1851 <a href="/wiki/Navy_List" title="Navy List">Navy List</a> dated 25 May, it showed her renamed <i>Southend "W.V. No. 7" at Paglesham</i>. In 1870, she was sold to "Messrs Murray and Trainer" to be broken up.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_resting_place">Possible resting place</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Possible resting place">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Investigations started in 2000 by a team led by Dr Robert Prescott of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">University of St Andrews</a> found documents confirming that <i>"W.V. 7"</i> was <i>Beagle</i>, and noted a vessel matching her size shown midstream on the <a href="/wiki/River_Roach" title="River Roach">River Roach</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Paglesham" title="Paglesham">Paglesham</a> Reach) on the 1847 hydrographic survey chart. A later chart showed a nearby indentation to the north bank of Paglesham Reach near the Eastend Wharf and near Waterside Farm. This could have been a dock for <i>W.V. 7</i> – <i>Beagle</i>. Site investigations found an area of <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshy</a> ground some 15 ft (4.6 m) deep on the tidal river-bank, about 150 metres west of the boat-house. This discovery matched the chart position and many fragments of <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> of the correct period were found in the same area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>An atomic dielectric resonance survey carried out in November 2003 found traces of timbers forming the size and shape of the lower hull, indicating a substantial amount of timbers from below the waterline still in place.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An old <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchor</a> of 1841 pattern was excavated. It was also found that the 1871 <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> recorded a new <a href="/wiki/Farmhouse" title="Farmhouse">farmhouse</a> in the name of William Murray and Thomas Rainer, leading to speculation that the merchant's name was a misprint for T. Rainer. The farmhouse was demolished in the 1940s, but a nearby boathouse incorporated timbers matching <a href="/wiki/Knee_(construction)" title="Knee (construction)">knee timbers</a> used in <i>Beagle</i>. Two more large anchors similar to the one excavated from the ship's present location are known to have been found in neighbouring villages. It is believed that there were four anchors in the ship.</p>
<p>Their investigations featured in a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> programme which showed how each watch ship would have accommodated seven coastguard officers, drawn from other areas to minimise collusion with the locals. Each officer had about three rooms to house his family, forming a small community. They would use small boats to intercept smugglers, and the investigators found a <a href="/wiki/Causeway" title="Causeway">causeway</a> giving access at low tide across the soft mud of the river bank. Apparently the next coastguard station along was <i>Kangaroo</i>, a sister ship of <i>Beagle</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replica">Replica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Beagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Replica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 31 December 2011, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nao_Victoria_Museum" title="Nao Victoria Museum">Nao Victoria Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Punta_Arenas" title="Punta Arenas">Punta Arenas</a> announced the building of the first full-scale replica of HMS <i>Beagle</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Construction began on 1 November 2012, using <i><a href="/wiki/Nothofagus_dombeyi" title="Nothofagus dombeyi">Nothofagus dombeyi</a></i> timber from the local rainforest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 2013, the Chilean national press started to take an interest in the work in progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
