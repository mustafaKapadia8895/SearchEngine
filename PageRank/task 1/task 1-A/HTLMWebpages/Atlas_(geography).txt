<p>An <b>atlas</b> is a collection of <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a>; it is typically a bundle of maps of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> or a region of Earth, but there are atlases of the other planets (and their satellites) in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. Furthermore, atlases of <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a> exist, mapping out organs of the human body or other organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Atlases have traditionally been bound into book form, but today many atlases are in <a href="/wiki/Multimedia" title="Multimedia">multimedia</a> formats. In addition to presenting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic" title="Geographic">geographic</a> features and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political" title="Political">political</a> boundaries, many atlases often feature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geopolitical" title="Geopolitical">geopolitical</a>, social, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious" title="Religious">religious</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a> <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>. They also have information about the map and places in it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="469" height="426" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png/250px-Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png/375px-Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercator_-_Atlas_-_1595.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Frontispiece of the 1595 atlas of Mercator</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The use of the word atlas in a geographical context dates from 1595 when the geographer <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">Gerardus Mercator</a> published <i>Atlas Sive Cosmographicae Meditationes de Fabrica Mundi et Fabricati Figura.</i> (Atlas or cosmographical meditations upon the creation of the universe, and the universe as created.) This title provides Mercator's definition of the word as a description of the creation and form of the whole universe, not simply as a collection of maps. The volume that was published posthumously one year after his death is a wide-ranging text but, as the editions evolved, it became simply a collection of maps and it is in that sense that the word was used from the middle of the seventeenth century. The neologism coined by Mercator was a mark of his respect for <a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_Mauretania" title="Atlas of Mauretania">King Atlas of Mauretania</a> whom he considered to be the first great geographer and it is that King who is portrayed on the frontispiece of the 1595 edition, however, by the time of the 1636 edition, the frontispiece image had become the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">Titan Atlas</a> supporting the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first work that contained systematically arranged woodcut maps of uniform size, intended to be published in a book, thus representing the first modern atlas, was <i>De Summa totius Orbis</i> (1524–26) by the 16th-century Italian cartographer <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Coppo" title="Pietro Coppo">Pietro Coppo</a>. Nonetheless, this distinction is conventionally awarded to the Flemish cartographer <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Ortelius" title="Abraham Ortelius">Abraham Ortelius</a> who in 1570 published the collection of maps <i><a href="/wiki/Theatrum_Orbis_Terrarum" title="Theatrum Orbis Terrarum">Theatrum Orbis Terrarum</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>travel atlas</i> is made for easy use during travel, and often has spiral bindings so it may be folded flat (for example <a href="/wiki/Geographers%27_A-Z_Map_Company" title="Geographers' A-Z Map Company">Geographers' A-Z Map Company</a> famous A-Z Atlases). It has maps at a large zoom so the maps can be reviewed easily. A travel atlas may also be referred to as a <i>road map</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <i>desk atlas</i> is made similar to a reference book. It may be in hardback or paperback form.</p>

<p>With the coming of the global market, publishers in different countries can reprint maps from places made elsewhere. This means that the place names on the maps often use the designations or abbreviations of the language of the country in which the feature is located, to serve the widest market. For example, islands near Russia have the abbreviation "O." for "ostrov", not "I." for "island". This practice differs from what is standard for any given language, and it reaches its extremity concerning <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterations</a> from other languages. In particular, German mapmakers use the transliterations from <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> developed by the <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czechs</a>, which are hardly used in English-speaking countries.</p>
