<p><b>Sebastian Junger</b> (born January 17, 1962) is an American journalist, author and filmmaker famous for the best-selling book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(book)" title="The Perfect Storm (book)">The Perfect Storm: A True Story of Men Against the Sea</a></i> (1997), his award-winning chronicle of the war in Afghanistan in the documentary films <i><a href="/wiki/Restrepo_(film)" title="Restrepo (film)">Restrepo</a></i> (2010) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korengal_(film)" title="Korengal (film)">Korengal</a></i> (2014), and his book <i>War</i> (2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-politicalbest_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-politicalbest-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Junger was born in <a href="/wiki/Belmont,_Massachusetts" title="Belmont, Massachusetts">Belmont, Massachusetts</a>, the son of Ellen Sinclair, a painter, and Miguel Chapero Junger, a physicist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nose_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nose-5">[5]</a></sup> His father was born in Dresden, Germany, of Russian, Austrian, Spanish, and Italian descent; he came to the United States during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> because his own father had been Jewish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Junger grew up in the neighborhood of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Strangler" title="Boston Strangler">Boston Strangler</a>, a circumstance that later inspired his 2006 book <i>A Death in Belmont</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nose_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nose-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Concord_Academy" title="Concord Academy">Concord Academy</a> in 1980<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and received a bachelor of arts degree from <a href="/wiki/Wesleyan_University" title="Wesleyan University">Wesleyan University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">cultural anthropology</a> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, with the publication of his book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(book)" title="The Perfect Storm (book)">The Perfect Storm</a></i>, he was touted as a new <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Hemingway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gotowar_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-gotowar-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His work stimulated renewed interest in adventure non-fiction. He received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Magazine_Award" title="National Magazine Award">National Magazine Award</a> in 2000 for "The Forensics of War," published in <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, where he works as a contributing editor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanityFairBio_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-VanityFairBio-12">[12]</a></sup> In early 2007 he reported from Nigeria on the subject of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_oil" title="Blood oil">blood oil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloodoil_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloodoil-13">[13]</a></sup> With the photographer <a href="/wiki/Tim_Hetherington" title="Tim Hetherington">Tim Hetherington</a>, Junger received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DuPont-Columbia_Award" title="DuPont-Columbia Award">DuPont-Columbia Award</a> for broadcast journalism for his work on <i>The Other War: Afghanistan,</i> produced with ABC News and Vanity Fair, which appeared on <a href="/wiki/Nightline" title="Nightline">Nightline</a> in September 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>His book <i>War</i> revolves around the time Junger spent with a United States Army platoon of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/173rd_Airborne" title="173rd Airborne">173rd Airborne</a> in <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">Afghanistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-combathigh_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-combathigh-16">[16]</a></sup> Junger, along with Hetherington, used material gathered in the <a href="/wiki/Korangal_Valley" title="Korangal Valley">Korengal Valley</a> of Afghanistan for the book and to create a documentary feature <i><a href="/wiki/Restrepo_(film)" title="Restrepo (film)">Restrepo</a>.</i> The film was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Documentary_Feature" title="Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature">Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature</a> and won the Grand Jury Prize for a domestic documentary at the <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a> in 2010. On April 27, 2011, Junger was presented with the "Leadership in Entertainment Award" by <a href="/wiki/Iraq_and_Afghanistan_Veterans_of_America" title="Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America">Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America</a> (IAVA) for his work on <i><a href="/wiki/Restrepo_(film)" title="Restrepo (film)">Restrepo</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>His most recent book <i>Tribe: On Homecoming and Belonging</i> was published in May 2016.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Junger married Daniela Petrova in 2002. They divorced in 2014. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He lives in New York City and Massachusetts, and co-owns a bar in New York called The Half-King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_work">Notable work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Perfect_Storm"><i>The Perfect Storm</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Perfect Storm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(book)" title="The Perfect Storm (book)">The Perfect Storm (book)</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(film)" title="The Perfect Storm (film)">The Perfect Storm (film)</a></div>
<p>He found fame after writing the international bestseller <i>The Perfect Storm: A True Story of Men Against the Sea</i>. Published in 1997, it recounts the tale of the October 1991 "<a href="/wiki/Perfect_storm" title="Perfect storm">perfect storm</a>" (in fact, the general use of the term originates from this book), focusing on the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Gloucester,_Massachusetts" title="Gloucester, Massachusetts">Gloucester</a> fishing boat <i><a href="/wiki/Andrea_Gail" title="Andrea Gail">Andrea Gail</a></i> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> and its six crew members, Billy Tyne, Bobby Shatford, Alfred Pierre, David Sullivan, Michael Moran and Dale Murphy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andreagailcrew_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-andreagailcrew-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Brothers" title="Warner Brothers">Warner Brothers</a> made the book into a <a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(film)" title="The Perfect Storm (film)">film of the same name</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/George_Clooney" title="George Clooney">George Clooney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wahlberg" title="Mark Wahlberg">Mark Wahlberg</a>.</p>
<p>At the time of the storm, Junger was recovering from a wound to the left leg that he suffered while working as an <a href="/wiki/Arborist" title="Arborist">arborist</a> in the Boston area (his chainsaw had torn into his leg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bn.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-bn.com-22">[22]</a></sup> He claims that while recovering from that injury, he was inspired to write about dangerous jobs. He planned to start with commercial fishing in Gloucester Massachusetts, a project that evolved into "The Perfect Storm".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHYY.org_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHYY.org-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Junger established The Perfect Storm Foundation to provide cultural and educational grants to children across the country whose parents make their living in the commercial fishing industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aboutpsf_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-aboutpsf-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Death_in_Belmont"><i>A Death in Belmont</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: A Death in Belmont">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>A Death in Belmont</i> centers on the rape and murder of Bessie Goldberg in Junger's hometown in the spring of 1963. From 1962 to 1964, the <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> area was gripped with fear as a result of the infamous <a href="/wiki/Boston_Strangler" title="Boston Strangler">Boston Strangler</a> crimes. Junger received the 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L._L._Winship/PEN_New_England_Award" title="L. L. Winship/PEN New England Award">PEN/Winship</a> award for the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Although a different man was convicted, Junger raises the possibility that the real killer was <a href="/wiki/Albert_DeSalvo" title="Albert DeSalvo">Albert DeSalvo</a>, who eventually confessed to committing several Strangler murders, but not Goldberg's. Goldberg's house was a mile and a quarter from the Junger family home, where Albert DeSalvo was doing construction work on the day Goldberg was killed. In fact, Junger stated in an interview that he grew up with a studio portrait of DeSalvo on his family's wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utubebelmont_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-utubebelmont-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book includes a photograph that was taken one day after Goldberg's murder. It shows Junger as a one-year-old baby, sitting on his mother's lap; They are in the new room that had just been added onto their house, and standing behind them are the two contractors who had just completed its construction. Junger describes it thus:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Al and I are the only people looking directly at the camera, and whereas I have an infant's expression of puzzled amazement, Al wears an odd smirk. His dark hair is greased up in a pampadour, and he is clean-shaven but unmistakably rough looking, and he has placed across his stomach one enormous, outstretched hand. … The hand is at the exact center of the photograph, as if it were the true subject around which the rest of have been arranged."</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Sebastian Junger, <i>A Death in Belmont</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-junger2007_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-junger2007-27">[27]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Junger's book raises the possibility that Smith's conviction was founded on circumstantial evidence, and in part on racism, because the prosecution's narrative of Smith's day in Belmont was built on witnesses who remembered seeing Smith chiefly because he was a black man walking in a white neighborhood. Smith had cleaned the victim's house on the day in question and left a receipt (for his work) with his name on the victim's kitchen counter. There was no physical evidence, such as bruises or blood, linking Smith to the crime. In 1976, he was granted commutation of his life sentence; however, before his release, Smith died of lung cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burden_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-burden-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadcertain_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-deadcertain-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scenes_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-scenes-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gwinn_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-gwinn-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwnorton_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-wwnorton-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his final analysis in <i>A Death in Belmont</i>, Junger draws no conclusions about the guilt or innocence of Smith or DeSalvo. The victim's daughter has vigorously disputed Junger's suggestion that Smith might have been innocent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadcertain_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-deadcertain-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire"><i>Fire</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Fire</i> is a collection of articles dealing with dangerous regions of the world or dangerous occupations. It is most notable for its chapter "Lion in Winter" in which Junger interviews Afghan Northern Alliance leader <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shah_Massoud" title="Ahmad Shah Massoud">Ahmad Shah Massoud</a>, the Lion of the Panjshir, a famed resistance fighter against first the Soviets and then the Taliban. Junger was one of the last Western journalists to interview Massoud in depth. The bulk of this interview was first published in March 2001 for <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Adventure_(magazine)" title="National Geographic Adventure (magazine)">National Geographic Adventure</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lion_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-lion-33">[33]</a></sup> along with photographs by the renowned Iranian photographer <a href="/wiki/Reza_Deghati" title="Reza Deghati">Reza Deghati</a> and video by cinematographer Stephen Cocklin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Massoud was assassinated on September 9, 2001. Junger's portrait of Massoud gives one insight into how differently Afghanistan might have fared in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_operations_of_the_War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)#The_9-11_attacks" title="Military operations of the War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">post-9/11 invasion</a> had Massoud lived to help reclaim the country from the Taliban. <i>Fire</i> also details the <a href="/wiki/Blood_diamond" title="Blood diamond">conflict diamond trade</a> in Sierra Leone, genocide in <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> and the hazards of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire-fighting" title="Fire-fighting">fire-fighting</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> wild.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restrepo"><i>Restrepo</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Restrepo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="915" data-file-width="1008" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg/220px-TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg/330px-TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg/440px-TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TimHetheringtonSebastianJungerFeb2011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Junger with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Hetherington" title="Tim Hetherington">Tim Hetherington</a> in 2011</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2009, Junger made his first film, the documentary feature <i><a href="/wiki/Restrepo_(film)" title="Restrepo (film)">Restrepo</a></i>, as director with photographer <a href="/wiki/Tim_Hetherington" title="Tim Hetherington">Tim Hetherington</a>. The two worked together in Afghanistan on assignment for <i>Vanity Fair</i>. Junger and Hetherington spent a year with one platoon in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korengal_Valley" title="Korengal Valley">Korengal Valley</a>, which is billed as the deadliest valley in Afghanistan. They recorded video to document their experience, and this footage went on to form the basis for <i>Restrepo</i>. The title refers to the outpost where Junger was embedded, which was named after a combat medic, Pfc. Juan Restrepo, killed in action. As Junger explained, "It's a completely apolitical film. We wanted to give viewers the experience of being in combat with soldiers, and so our cameras never leave their side. There are no interviews with generals; there is no moral or political analysis. It is a purely experiential film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-headtosundance_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-headtosundance-38">[38]</a></sup> <i>Restrepo,</i> which premiered on the opening night of the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indieback_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-indieback-39">[39]</a></sup> won the grand jury prize for a domestic documentary. The actor <a href="/wiki/David_Hyde_Pierce" title="David Hyde Pierce">David Hyde Pierce</a> presented the award in Park City, Utah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wintersbone_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-wintersbone-40">[40]</a></sup> Junger self-financed the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-docdays_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-docdays-41">[41]</a></sup> <i>Restrepo</i> was nominated for the 2011 Academy Award for Best Documentary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War"><i>War</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1788" data-file-width="2504" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg/220px-Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg/330px-Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg/440px-Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Medal_of_honor_recipient_Sgt._Salvatore_Giunta_beside_President_Barack_Obama.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Medal of honor recipient Sgt. Salvatore Giunta beside President Barack Obama</div>

<p>The visits from June 2007 to June 2008 to eastern Afghanistan to the Korengal Valley with Tim Hetherington resulted not only in their reports and pictures published in <i>Vanity Fair</i> in 2008 and the film <i>Restrepo</i> (2010), but also in Junger's best-selling book <i>War</i> (2010),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-politicalbest_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-politicalbest-3">[3]</a></sup> which rewrites and expands upon his <i>Vanity Fair</i> dispatches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caputoreview_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-caputoreview-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jungerswar_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-jungerswar-44">[44]</a></sup> Junger in <i>War</i>, tells the story of Staff Sergent <a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Giunta" title="Salvatore Giunta">Sal Giunta</a>. His actions during the fighting in the <a href="/wiki/Korangal_Valley" title="Korangal Valley">Korengal Valley</a> made him the first soldier to still be alive when receiving the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time magazine</a> named <i>War</i> a "Top Ten Non-fiction Book" of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Which_Way_is_the_Front_Line_From_Here?"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Which_Way_is_the_Front_Line_From_Here.3F"><i>Which Way is the Front Line From Here?</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Which Way is the Front Line From Here?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In April 2013, Junger's film <i>Which Way is the Front Line From Here? The Life and Time of Tim Hetherington</i>, debuted at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LBJ_Presidential_Library" title="LBJ Presidential Library">LBJ Presidential Library</a>. Produced in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> Documentary Films, it documents the life of Tim Hetherington, focusing upon his documenting the humanity of people caught up in war.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korengal"><i>Korengal</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Korengal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2014 film <i>Korengal</i> continues to follow the soldiers in Battle Company 2/503 during and after their service in the Korengal Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The film takes a deeper look into the psychology of the men, who are deployed in the rugged mountains of the Korengal Valley. Junger sought to find out what combat did to, and for them, and seek a deeper understanding of why war is meaningful to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr.org_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr.org-48">[48]</a></sup> The film opened in June 2013 in theaters. It also played at the Pritzker Military Library and Museum, The Pentagon, Army Heritage and Education Foundation Center, Capitol Hill, United States Military Academy, The National Infantry Museum, Little Rock Film Festival, Key West Film Festival, and the DocuWest Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Last_Patrol"><i>The Last Patrol</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Last Patrol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The last of the trilogy about war and its effects on soldiers, this documentary explores "what it means for combat soldiers to reintegrate into daily American life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Junger recruited former US Army Sgt. Brendan O'Byrne, who appeared in the film "Restrepo," US Army soldier Dave Roels, and Spanish photo-journalist Guillermo Cervera to walk the rail corridor between Washington, D.C., Philadelphia, and Pittsburgh. The journey was planned as a tribute to deceased photographer Tim Hetherington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr.org_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-npr.org-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The film premiered at the Margaret Mead Film Festival <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and aired on HBO in November. The film played in theaters in NY, and Los Angeles, as well as at the Savannah Film Festival, and at SIFF in Seattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tribe"><i>Tribe</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Junger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tribe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i>Tribe</i> (2016) Junger studies war veterans from an anthropological perspective and asks how “do you make veterans feel that they are returning to a cohesive society that was worth fighting for in the first place?”. Junger's premise is that "soldiers all but ignore differences of race, religion and politics within their platoon," and upon return to America, find a fractious society splintered into various competing factions, often hostile to one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
