<p>The <b>2008 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was a very active <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane season</a> with sixteen named storms formed, including eight that became hurricanes and five that became major hurricanes. The season officially started on June 1 and ended on November 30. These dates conventionally delimit the period of each year when most tropical cyclones form in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a>. However, the formation of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Arthur_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Arthur (2008)">Tropical Storm Arthur</a> caused the season to start two days early. This season is the fifth most costly on record, behind only the <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a> seasons, with over $47.5 billion in damage (2008 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>). It was the fourth busiest year since 1944 and the only year on record in which a major hurricane existed in every month from July through November in the North Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Bertha became the longest lived July tropical cyclone on record for the basin, the first of several long-lived systems during 2008. The season was devastating for <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, where over 800 people were killed by four consecutive tropical cyclones (Fay, Gustav, Hanna, and Ike), especially <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hanna" title="Hurricane Hanna">Hanna</a>, in August and September. Hurricane Paloma's outer rain bands also made landfall over Haiti. Ike was also the most destructive storm of the season, as well as the strongest, devastating <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> as a major hurricane and later making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a> at Category 2 (nearly Category 3) intensity.</p>
<p>One very unusual feat was a streak of tropical cyclones affecting land. All but one system impacted land in 2008. The unprecedented number of storms with impact led to one of the deadliest and destructive seasons in the history of the Atlantic basin, especially with Hurricane <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Ike</a>, as its overall damages made it the third costliest hurricane in the Atlantic.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_seasonal_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone seasonal forecasting">Forecasts of hurricane activity</a> are issued before each hurricane season by noted hurricane experts Dr. Philip J. Klotzbach, Dr. <a href="/wiki/William_M._Gray" title="William M. Gray">William M. Gray</a>, and their associates at <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University</a>; and separately by <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> forecasters.</p>
<p>Dr. Klotzbach's team (formerly led by Dr. Gray) defined the average number of storms per season (1950 to 2000) as 9.6 tropical storms, 5.9 hurricanes, and 2.3 major hurricanes (storms reaching at least Category 3 strength in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>). A normal season, as defined by NOAA, has 9 to 12 named storms, with 5 to 7 of those reaching hurricane strength, and 1 to 3 major hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_Dec_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gray_Dec-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPCAHOBI_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPCAHOBI-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-season_forecasts">Pre-season forecasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-season forecasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 7, 2007, Klotzbach's team issued its first extended-range forecast for the 2008 season, predicting above-average activity (13 named storms, 7 hurricanes, 3 of Category 3 or higher).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_Dec_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gray_Dec-2">[2]</a></sup> On April 9, 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">CSU</a> issued a new forecast, anticipating a well above average hurricane season of 15 named storms, 8 hurricanes, and 4 intense hurricanes. The <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) predicted an above average season with 12 to 16 storms, 6 to 9 hurricanes, and 2 to 5 major hurricanes on May 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apr08_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Apr08-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KloApr08_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-KloApr08-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midseason_outlooks">Midseason outlooks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Midseason outlooks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CSU kept their April 9 forecast on June 3 with 15 storms, 8 hurricanes, and 4 major hurricanes. On June 18 the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UKMO" title="UKMO">UKMO</a>) issued a forecast of 15 tropical storms in the July to November period with a 70% chance that the number would be in the range 10 to 20. However, actual predictions of the number of tropical storms, hurricanes, and major hurricanes were not given.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UKMO_June_forecast_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-UKMO_June_forecast-6">[6]</a></sup> On August 5, following the intense start, the CSU called for 17 storms, 9 hurricanes, and 5 major hurricanes, slightly higher than their prior forecast of 15 storms. On August 7, in response to the season's strong start, NOAA reaffirmed its pre-season outlook of above-average activity. The organization increased its expected probability of such a season to 85% from 65% in its May forecast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Aug7_forecast_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Aug7_forecast-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 2008 hurricane season saw the first occurrence of major hurricanes in the months of July through November. Four storms formed before the start of August, and the season also had the earliest known date for three storms to be active on the same day: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bertha_(2008)" title="Hurricane Bertha (2008)">Hurricane Bertha</a>, and Tropical Storms <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Cristobal_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Cristobal (2008)">Cristobal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dolly_(2008)" title="Hurricane Dolly (2008)">Dolly</a> were all active on July 20. This season was also one of only nine Atlantic seasons on record to have a major hurricane form before August. This is also the first year four or more Category 4 storms have formed in a single year since 2005, which had 5, and was one of only 7 Atlantic seasons to feature a major hurricane in November.</p>
<p>The season was devastating for <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, where over 800 people were killed by four consecutive tropical cyclones (Fay, Gustav, Hanna, and Ike) in August and September. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> was the most destructive storm of the season, as well as the strongest, devastating <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> as a major hurricane and later making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a> at Category 2 (nearly Category 3) intensity. It caused a particularly devastating storm surge along the western Gulf Coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> due to in part to its large size. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hanna_(2008)" title="Hurricane Hanna (2008)">Hurricane Hanna</a> was the deadliest storm of the season, killing 537 people, mostly in Haiti. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gustav" title="Hurricane Gustav">Hurricane Gustav</a> was another very destructive storm, causing up to $6.61 billion in damage to <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dolly_(2008)" title="Hurricane Dolly (2008)">Hurricane Dolly</a> caused up to $1.35 billion in damage to south Texas and northeastern Mexico. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bertha_(2008)" title="Hurricane Bertha (2008)">Hurricane Bertha</a> was an early season <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> that became the longest lived pre-August <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">North Atlantic tropical cyclone</a> on record, though it caused few deaths and only minor damage.</p>
<p>Other notable storms in the year included <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Arthur_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Arthur (2008)">Tropical Storm Arthur</a>, which marked the first recorded time the Atlantic saw a named storm form in May in consecutive years, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Fay_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Fay (2008)">Tropical Storm Fay</a>, which became the first Atlantic tropical cyclone to make landfall in the same U.S. state on 4 separate occasions; <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Marco_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Marco (2008)">Tropical Storm Marco</a>, the smallest Atlantic tropical cyclone on record, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Omar_(2008)" title="Hurricane Omar (2008)">Hurricane Omar</a>, a powerful late-season major hurricane which caused moderate damage to the <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> in mid-October; and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Paloma" title="Hurricane Paloma">Hurricane Paloma</a>, which became the third-strongest November hurricane in recorded history and caused about $900 million in damage to the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. The only storm of the season to not reach tropical storm status, <a href="/wiki/October_2008_Central_America_floods" title="October 2008 Central America floods">Tropical Depression Sixteen</a>, caused significant flooding in Central America which killed more than 75 people and caused at least $150 million in damages.</p>
<p>Overall, the season's activity was reflected with a total cumulative <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 146.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. ACE is only calculated for full advisories on tropical systems at or exceeding 34 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (39 mph, 63 km/h) or tropical storm strength. Although officially, <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a> are excluded from the total,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the figure above includes periods when storms were in a subtropical phase.</p>

<p>By May 27, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> was crossing the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and reached the western Caribbean on May 30. That day, the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alma" title="Tropical Storm Alma">Tropical Storm Alma</a> entered the region from the north coast of <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. Interaction between the wave and the remnants of Alma resulted in the development of a surface low pressure area, which quickly organized into Tropical Storm Arthur about 50 mi (80 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> at 00:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on May 31. The storm intensified slightly further, attaining its peaking at 06:00 UTC with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 45 mph (75 km/h) and a minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 1,004 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.6 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). Around 09:00 UTC on May 31, Arthur made landfall in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>. The cyclone weakened to a tropical depression at 12:00 UTC on June 1 and degenerated into a remnant low by 00:00 UTC on June 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur_TCR_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur_TCR-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arthur dropped heavy rainfall in Belize, with precipitation estimates as high as 15 in (380 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur_TCR_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur_TCR-10">[10]</a></sup> Combined with recent heavy rains from Tropical Storm Alma, Arthur triggered flash flooding and caused rivers throughout the country to overflow. High water left dozens of people were left stranded on their rooftops and forced the evacuation of one village. The flooding also damaged a bridge and a highway, while several other bridges were inundated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A total of 714 homes were flooded by Arthur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur_TCR_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur_TCR-10">[10]</a></sup> Overall, damage in Belize totaled approximately $78 million and there were nine fatalities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur_TCR_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur_TCR-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> five of them were direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur_TCR_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur_TCR-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bertha_(2008)" title="Hurricane Bertha (2008)">Hurricane Bertha (2008)</a></div>
<p>Early on July 1, a weak <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> emerged off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By early the next day, a surface low developed and the wave became better organized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> upgraded the system to Tropical Depression Two in the morning hours of July 3 after the system was able to maintain <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> over its center for at least 12 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-02L_Disco_1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-02L_Disco_1-15">[15]</a></sup> The depression organized further and developed two distinct <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Banding" title="Tropical cyclone">bands</a> of convection. Six hours after becoming a depression, it was upgraded to Tropical Storm Bertha, the second named storm of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The National Hurricane Center noted that this tropical cyclone was remarkably forecast up to a week in advance by many <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">global computer models</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-02L_Disco_1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-02L_Disco_1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a bout of strengthening on July 6, Bertha was upgraded to a hurricane early on July 7 as satellite and microwave imagery indicated an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> feature had formed. It continued to strengthen that morning. Rapid intensification continued that afternoon and Bertha strengthened into a major hurricane with 125 mph (205 km/h) winds and a well-defined eye. The strengthening trend abated early on July 8, due to wind shear, and Bertha rapidly weakened back to a Category 1 hurricane that afternoon.</p>
<p>Bertha again began to rapidly intensify on July 9 as a new eye had formed and the system became more symmetrical. The NHC upgraded Bertha to a Category 2 with winds of 105 mph (170 km/h) and stated that Bertha could intensify further to major hurricane status again, but instead weakened into an 85 mph (135 km/h) Category 1 hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bertha_Public_Advisory_27_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bertha_Public_Advisory_27-17">[17]</a></sup> On July 12, Bertha slowed in movement, becoming almost stationary and by July 13 this slow movement weakened the storm to tropical storm strength. The storm brought rain and tropical storm-force winds to <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on July 14, but no damage was reported. After slowly meandering to the east and then the southeast, Bertha regained hurricane strength on the 18th as it began accelerating towards the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As it moved over cooler waters, it weakened slightly to a tropical storm late on July 19. It finally became extratropical on July 20 southwest of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical disturbance located off the east coast central <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> formed on July 15. The system slowly developed into a Tropical Depression on July 18 while located 65 mi (105 km) to the south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>. The depression gradually became better organized and was upgraded to Tropical Storm Cristobal the next day while located 225 mi (362 km) to the southwest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Cape Hatteras, North Carolina">Cape Hatteras, North Carolina</a>. Cristobal paralleled the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> coastline for the next two days with minimal to no impact as most of the convection and wind was located on the eastern half of the storm. However, there was some heavy rainfall amounting up to 5 in (130 mm) in localized areas of southern North Carolina. On July 20, Cristobal began to move away from the coastline and began to intensify as it passed over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>. Cristobal peaked the next day with winds of 65 mph (105 km/h). As Cristobal moved closer to <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, it began to lose its tropical characteristics. By July 23, Cristobal had transitioned into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dolly_(2008)" title="Hurricane Dolly (2008)">Hurricane Dolly (2008)</a></div>
<p>A strong tropical wave tracked across the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> in the third week of July. Despite producing strong convection and tropical storm-force sustained winds, it failed to develop a low-level circulation until July 20. That morning, reconnaissance aircraft found a low-level circulation and the system was declared Tropical Storm Dolly. This marked the fastest start of a hurricane season since <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>It made landfall early on July 21 as a weak and disorganized tropical storm near Cancun, and emerged over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> later that morning. 17 deaths were reported in <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> from landslides caused by heavy rain on the fringes of Dolly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radio_Netherlands_deaths_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radio_Netherlands_deaths-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 22 at 4 p.m. CDT, it strengthened into the second hurricane of the season. It steadily strengthened that night into the morning of July 23 and reached Category 2 intensity. Dolly then weakened some before it made landfall at 1 p.m. CDT (1800 UTC) on <a href="/wiki/South_Padre_Island,_Texas" title="South Padre Island, Texas">South Padre Island</a> as a Category 1 hurricane. Dolly caused no deaths in Texas; it did, however, become the most damaging hurricane to affect <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">2005's Hurricane Rita</a>, with US$1.05 billion in damage. It was also the most destructive hurricane to make landfall in Texas since 1983's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alicia" title="Hurricane Alicia">Alicia</a>, and was the fourth costliest Texas hurricane in history, behind <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alicia" title="Hurricane Alicia">Hurricane Alicia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> later in the season. The remnant low caused flash flooding and two deaths in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> before dissipating late on July 27.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Edouard_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Edouard (2008)">Tropical Storm Edouard (2008)</a></div>
<p>A trough developed northern Gulf of Mexico on August 2 and quickly developed into a tropical depression about 85 mi (140 km) southeast of the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> on August 3. The depression moved westward and intensified into Tropical Storm Edouard on August 4. However, northerly wind shear initially halted any further significant intensification and also caused the storm to struggle to maintain deep convection over the center. Edouard eventually intensified further and peaked with winds of 65 mph (100 km/h) on August 5. Shortly thereafter, the storm made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Gilchrist,_Texas" title="Gilchrist, Texas">Gilchrist, Texas</a>. Edouard quickly weakened and was downgraded to tropical depression by early on August 6, six hours before degenerated into a remnant low pressure area. The remnant low continued to move northwestward across Texas and dissipated later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-etcr-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the relatively weak nature of the storm, damage was generally minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-etcr-23">[23]</a></sup> Rip currents in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and Florida caused five fatalities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd8_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-sd8-24">[24]</a></sup> while a sixth death occurred after a man fell overboard a shrimp boat in rough seas near the mouth of the Mississippi River in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. Storm surge and high tides also caused coastal flooding in the state, especially in <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Cameron Parish, Louisiana">Cameron Parish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-etcr-23">[23]</a></sup> Relatively strong winds left more than 2,000 without electricity and damaged trees and the roofs of mobile homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd8_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-sd8-24">[24]</a></sup> Storm surge caused coastal flooding in eastern Texas, particularly in the Gilchrist area. At least 25 homes sustained damage, while portions of a few major roads, such as <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10" title="Interstate 10">Interstate 10</a>, were closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etcr_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-etcr-23">[23]</a></sup> Minor inland flooding occurred due to heavy rainfall, with a portion of <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Highway_36" title="Texas State Highway 36">State Highway 36</a> being temporarily shutdown. Strong winds in the region damaged hundreds of homes, downed trees, and left about 300,000 people without electricity. Overall, damage from Edouard totaled slightly more than $550,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd8_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-sd8-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Fay_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Fay (2008)">Tropical Storm Fay (2008)</a></div>
<p>A tropical wave developed into a tropical depression just west of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> on August 15. It soon intensified into Tropical Storm Fay, before striking <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> later that day. Fay re-emerged into the Caribbean early on August 16 after crossing <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. The cyclone clipped Cuba's <a href="/wiki/Zapata_Peninsula" title="Zapata Peninsula">Zapata Peninsula</a> on August 17, before striking near <a href="/wiki/Cienfuegos" title="Cienfuegos">Cienfuegos</a> on August 18. Fay strengthened further after reaching the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Florida" title="Straits of Florida">Straits of Florida</a> on the following day. The system then struck Florida a record four times while zigzagging from water to land, beginning with a landfall near <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West</a> on August 18. Fay peaked with winds of 70 mph (110 km/h) near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Okeechobee" title="Lake Okeechobee">Lake Okeechobee</a> on the next day. After Fay's final Florida landfall near <a href="/wiki/Carrabelle,_Florida" title="Carrabelle, Florida">Carrabelle</a> on August 23, the storm continued inland and weakened to a tropical depression early on August 24. Fay curved northeastward over <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> on August 25, before becoming extratropical over <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> on August 27. The remnants dissipated over <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> by the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Dominican Republic, by wind and flood waters damaged or destroyed more than 2,400 homes. At least five people were killed, all of them from vehicles were swept off roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr-25">[25]</a></sup> Fay's winds and rainfall in Haiti damaged the nation's agricultural sector, especially rice and banana crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Extensive flooding was reported on <a href="/wiki/Gon%C3%A2ve_Island" title="Gonâve Island">Gonâve Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr-25">[25]</a></sup> The storm left at least 10 fatalities in Haiti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hs_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-hs-27">[27]</a></sup> One death occurred in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> after floodwaters overturned a car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hs_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-hs-27">[27]</a></sup> With several weather stations across Cuba observing over 10 in (250 mm) of rain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftcr_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-ftcr-25">[25]</a></sup> flooding with reported across the island. The storm damaged crops, flooded hundreds of homes, isolated several communities, and disrupted electricity and telephone communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall was reported across Florida, with a peak total of 27.65 in (702 mm) near <a href="/wiki/Melbourne,_Florida" title="Melbourne, Florida">Melbourne</a>.</p>
<p>Significant flooding resulted in much of eastern Florida, along with some wind damage. Fay was responsible for 36 deaths and at least $560 million in damage.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gustav" title="Hurricane Gustav">Hurricane Gustav</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> formed off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> on August 18. It intensified into a disturbance which developed in the deep tropical Atlantic in the fourth week of August. It tracked westward into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> where it encountered more favorable conditions, and became a tropical depression on the morning of August 25, west of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>. It rapidly intensified into Tropical Storm Gustav early that afternoon and into Hurricane Gustav early on August 26. Striking southwest <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, it weakened into a tropical storm on the evening of August 27 due to land interaction and slowed down considerably. It re-organized further south into a strong tropical storm once again on August 28 before speeding up and hitting <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. Gustav killed 92  people in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. It then was upgraded to a hurricane again during the late afternoon of August 29. On the morning of August 30, Gustav was upgraded to a major Category 3 hurricane. After intensification slowed for a few hours, another round of rapid intensification occurred and Gustav was upgraded to a Category 4 hurricane during a hurricane hunter flight around 1 p.m. EDT (1700 UTC), with 145 mph (215 km/h) winds. Continuing to intensify, it became a 155 mph storm that afternoon before landfall. Soon after Gustav made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, firstly on the island of <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isla de la Juventud</a> and later on the mainland near Los Palacios in <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo_Province" title="Pinar del Río Province">Pinar del Río Province</a>, causing catastrophic damage, although it was difficult to estimate it; damage was later <i>estimated</i> at 3 billion dollars (2008 USD). It then emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> on August 30, weakening into a Category 3 hurricane with 115 mph (185 km/h) winds. However, the hurricane was still large, and early the next day, it made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. At 8 a.m. CDT September 1 the storm weakened to Category 2 just before landfall. On September 4, Gustav was absorbed by a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> while over the <a href="/wiki/Ozarks" title="Ozarks">Ozarks</a>. Gustav is responsible for 153 deaths and at least $6.6 billion in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical Depression Eight formed on August 28 from a low pressure area east-northeast of the northern <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>. It was upgraded to a tropical storm later that day and named Hanna. On September 1, while Hanna was moving very near to the island of <a href="/wiki/Mayaguana" title="Mayaguana">Mayaguana</a> in the <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, it was upgraded to Category 1 hurricane status. Hanna meandered around the southeastern Bahamas, weakening to a tropical storm while also dumping heavy rain on already-devastated <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>. Hanna made landfall around Myrtle Beach, SC late on September 5, and quickly northeastward near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_coast_of_the_United_States" title="East coast of the United States">east coast of the United States</a> as a tropical storm. Hanna transitioned into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical" title="Extratropical">extratropical cyclone</a> as it moved offshore from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> early on September 7. At least 537 deaths were blamed on Hanna, primarily in Haiti.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a></div>
<p>A tropical disturbance developed off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> near the end of August. It tracked south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> and slowly developed. On September 1 it became Tropical Depression Nine while west of the Cape Verde islands and intensified into a tropical storm later that day, when it was given the name Ike. Ike developed an eye late on September 3 as it underwent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening#Explosive_intensification" title="Rapid deepening">explosive intensification</a>, as it strengthened from a tropical storm to a Category 4 hurricane in twelve hours with an estimated pressure drop of 43 millibars (1.3 inHg), from 991 to 948 millibars (29.3 to 28.0 inHg); and a 24‑hour pressure drop of 61 millibars (1.8 inHg), from 996 to 935 millibars (29.4 to 27.6 inHg). Fearing that the new forecast tracks would come to fruition, residents in South Florida prepared for a direct strike from a strong hurricane, with forecasts measuring the storm to be stronger than <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a>, which had struck the region three years earlier. Fortunately for Floridians, whose state had endured eight hurricane strikes in the period 2004 - 2005, a strong Bermuda high pressure system pushed Ike toward a more southerly track. Ike weakened to a Category 2 hurricane before re-intensifying back to Category 4. It ripped across <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Inagua_Island" title="Great Inagua Island">Great Inagua Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk Island</a>, where 80% of the buildings on Grand Turk were severely damaged or completely destroyed. It weakened to a strong Category 3 and then re-strengthened to Category 4 late in the afternoon of September 7 as it headed for the northeastern coast of Cuba that evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In addition, hurricane Ike killed at least 74 people in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> and 2 people in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Ike crossed Cuba on September 8 it weakened to Category 1 and emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, where it moved along the southern coast. Ike killed 7 people in Cuba, having traversed nearly the entirety of the island nation. It crossed into the Gulf of Mexico on September 9 and ballooned in size. Ike maintained a double <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a> structure across most of the Gulf of Mexico and continued to expand in size. It made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Island" title="Galveston Island">Galveston Island</a> on September 13 as a strong Category 2 hurricane, but its large size brought a storm surge of over 15 feet (4.6 m) from Galveston Island eastward into southern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolivar_Peninsula" title="Bolivar Peninsula">Bolivar Peninsula</a> was worst affected by the surge, while Galveston Island (where waves topped the <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Seawall" title="Galveston Seawall">seawall</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Port_Arthur,_Texas" title="Port Arthur, Texas">Port Arthur</a> areas also saw extensive damage. Power was knocked out to most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a> area and windows were blown out of skyscrapers in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Houston" title="Downtown Houston">downtown Houston</a>. As Ike moved inland, it brought extensive flooding and wind damage throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a> and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>. Ike was categorized extratropical on September 14.</p>
<p>Damage from Ike is estimated at $37.5 billion (2008 USD) of which $29.5 billion was in the US, the third most destructive U.S. hurricane on record, behind <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Sandy</a> of 2012 and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> At least 195 fatalities have been blamed on Ike, of which 112 were in the United States. It was the most destructive hurricane in Texas history. Ike was an extremely large and powerful storm. At one point, the diameter of Ike's tropical storm and hurricane-force winds were 600 and 240 miles (965 and 390 km), respectively. Ike also had the highest Integrated Kinetic Energy (IKE) of any Atlantic storm. IKE is a measure of storm surge destructive potential, similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>, though it is more complex and in many ways more accurate. On a scale that ranges from 1 to 6, with 6 being highest destructive potential, Ike registered 5.6.</p>
<p>Ike crossed the U.S. northward into southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario,_Canada" title="Ontario, Canada">Ontario, Canada</a>, releasing copious rainfall which broke records dating back many years, in several cities. It was noted as the second most active tropical storm to have reached the Canadian mainland since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hazel</a> in 1954. Like Ike, Hazel had also affected southern Ontario. The storm dissipated as it reached southern Québec on September 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Josephine_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Josephine (2008)">Tropical Storm Josephine (2008)</a></div>
<p>A tropical disturbance developed off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> near the end of August. It tracked south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> and slowly developed. On September 2 it became Tropical Depression Ten while south-southeast of the southernmost Cape Verde islands. The depression was upgraded to a tropical storm, "Josephine" later that same day as it passed to the south of the Cape Verde islands. Strong wind shear weakened the system over the next few days, and it dissipated on September 6 without coming near any land. On September 7 the ex-Josephine disturbance regained some organization and regeneration seemed possible, but the low became exposed again and the NHC discontinued their statement about regeneration possibilities. The remnant lingered on for a few days, with a report on September 11 that it might regenerate, but dissipated without further action.</p>
<p>As Josephine passed to the south of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands on September 2, outer rain bands produced minor rainfall, totaling around 0.55 inches (14 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There were no reports of damage or flooding from the rain and overall effects were minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Several days after the low dissipated, the remnant moisture from Josephine brought showers and thunderstorms to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix" title="St. Croix">St. Croix</a> where up to 1 in (25.4 mm) of rain fell. The heavy rains led to minor street flooding and some urban flooding. No known damage was caused by the flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kyle_(2008)" title="Hurricane Kyle (2008)">Hurricane Kyle (2008)</a></div>
<p>A strong tropical disturbance tracked across the northeastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> in the evening of September 20. It meandered around <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, dumping torrential rains across the islands causing a significant amount of damage, despite never developing a closed circulation. By September 24, it began to track northward away from the islands and into the open Atlantic water, and it became a tropical storm on September 25, thus being named <i>Kyle</i>. Kyle was upgraded to a hurricane during the afternoon of September 27. It continued northward and maintained hurricane strength until landfall near <a href="/wiki/Yarmouth,_Nova_Scotia" title="Yarmouth, Nova Scotia">Yarmouth, Nova Scotia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> late on September 28. A few hours later, Kyle became extratropical as the cold waters of the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">Bay of Fundy</a> took effect. In general, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritimes" title="Maritimes">Maritimers</a> were spared the anticipated damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the last week of September, a very large non-tropical system over the north-central Atlantic slowly moved westward away from the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. <p>Tropical Depression Thirteen formed from a very small but well-organized low in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on October 6. It rapidly intensified into Tropical Storm Marco with 65 mph (100 km/h) winds that afternoon. Marco made landfall near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz, Mexico</a> the next morning at the same intensity. Marco dissipated that night as the small circulation moved inland. At one point, tropical storm-force winds were estimated to extend only 10 miles (16 km) from the center, which makes it the smallest tropical cyclone on record.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Nana">Tropical Storm Nana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Nana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<th scope="row">Peak intensity</th>
<td>40 mph (65 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  1004 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<p>A tropical wave exited the west coast of Africa on October 6, accompanied by a broad area of low pressure. Initially there was little convection, until thunderstorms increased on October 8. Three days later, <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak classifications began</a>, and following the development of <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">convective banding features</a> on October 12, the NHC reported the low developed into a tropical depression around 06:00 UTC; at the time, it was located about 795 mi (1,280 km) west of the Cape Verde Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTCR_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTCR-39">[39]</a></sup> Operationally, the NHC did not begin issuing advisories on the depression until it had become a tropical storm later on October 12, when satellite passes indicated sustained winds of 40 mph (65 km/h);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-D1-40">[40]</a></sup> as such, it was named <i>Nana</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTCR_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-NTCR-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The newly upgraded storm tracked west-northwestward in response to a ridge to its north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-D1-40">[40]</a></sup> Little intensification took place due to increasing wind shear on the western side of the cyclone. The storm attained its lowest pressure of 1004 mbar (hPa; 29.65 inHg) around 00:00 UTC on October 13. Within 12 hours of its peak intensity, Nana weakened to a tropical depression as shear continued to displace convection from the center. Upon weakening to a depression, it accelerated northwestward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTCR_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-NTCR-39">[39]</a></sup> After a brief burst of convection early on October 14,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D6_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-D6-41">[41]</a></sup> Nana degenerated into a non-convective remnant low pressure area. The remnants persisted until 12:00 UTC on October 15, when it dissipated 945 mi (1,520 km) east-northeast of the Leeward Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTCR_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-NTCR-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Omar" title="Hurricane Omar">Hurricane Omar</a></div>
<p>A tropical disturbance in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> was in an area unfavorable for development in the second week of October. While drifting across the region, upper-level winds diminished enough for the tropical disturbance to strengthen and to develop into Tropical Depression Fifteen on October 13. It strengthened to Tropical Storm Omar the next day. Omar quickly strengthened into a 70 mph (110 km/h) storm that afternoon, and became a hurricane that night. It remained a hurricane through that night and it changed little in intensity through the next day, but that evening Omar intensified quickly and became a 115 mph Category 3 storm, and became a 125 mph (205 km/h) storm the next morning. In a discussion supplied by the National Hurricane Center, it stated that Omar may have peaked as a minimal Category 4 early that morning. This was later confirmed in November in their Running Best Track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> However, wind shear and dry air quickly weakened Omar to a minimal hurricane that afternoon as it raced towards the northeast and soon it dropped to tropical storm strength. It then degenerated to a remnant low the next day. Omar caused at least $60 million in damage to the Lesser Antilles (2008 USD), and killed two people, both in Puerto Rico.</p>

<p>A vigorous disturbance in the western Caribbean slowly developed off the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. It organized enough to become Tropical Depression Sixteen on October 14. Because the depression hugged the <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> coast it prevented much strengthening. The disorganized center made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Patuca" title="Patuca">Punta Patuca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> on October 15. It dissipated inland that evening. Its remnants however became nearly stationary in the area between northern <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> and southeastern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and continued to produce locally heavy rains for several days. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Paloma">Hurricane Paloma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hurricane Paloma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Paloma" title="Hurricane Paloma">Hurricane Paloma</a></div>
<p>An area of low pressure became stationary in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> without showing tropical development for several days at the beginning of November. Finally, on November 5 the low pressure system organized and became Tropical Depression Seventeen just east of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. The next day it strengthened to become Tropical Storm Paloma then later, Hurricane Paloma. The following days proved to be a rarity in terms of tropical cyclone intensity in the month of November. This was seen when Paloma was upgraded to a Category 2, then later a Category 3 major hurricane, making it the first P named storm to reach major hurricane status in the Atlantic Basin. On November 8, the system attained was upgraded to a Category 4 and reached its peak intensity with sustained winds of 145 mph (245 km/h). Thereafter, Paloma began a period of rapid weakening before making landfall as a Category 2 hurricane near <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_del_Sur" title="Santa Cruz del Sur">Santa Cruz del Sur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. On November 9, Paloma was downgraded to a tropical storm and then a depression. Later that evening, the storm finally met its demise as it transformed into a remnant low. Paloma caused up to $300 million (2008 USD) in damage to Cuba, and $15 million in damage to the Cayman Islands. In the spring of 2009 the WMO retired the name Paloma, making it the first retired P named storm in the Atlantic Basin.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 22, 2009, at the 31st Session of the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>'s Regional Association IV Hurricane Committee, the WMO retired the names <i>Gustav</i>, <i>Ike</i>, and <i>Paloma</i> from its <a href="/wiki/2014_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2014 Atlantic hurricane season">rotating name lists</a>. The names were replaced with <i>Gonzalo</i>, <i>Isaias</i>, and <i>Paulette</i> for <a href="/wiki/2014_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2014 Atlantic hurricane season">2014</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_effects">Season effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Season effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a table of all of the storms that formed in the 2008 Atlantic hurricane season. It includes their duration, names, landfall(s) – denoted by bold location names – damages, and death totals. Deaths in parentheses are additional and indirect (an example of an indirect death would be a traffic accident), but were still related to that storm. Damage and deaths include totals while the storm was extratropical, a wave, or a low, and all of the damage figures are in 2008 USD.</p>
