<p>The fictional <b>Land of Oz</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_world" title="Fantasy world">magical country</a> first introduced in the classic children's novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> (1900).</p>
<p>Oz consists of four vast quadrants, the <a href="/wiki/Gillikin_Country" title="Gillikin Country">Gillikin Country</a> in the north, <a href="/wiki/Quadling_Country" title="Quadling Country">Quadling Country</a> in the south, <a href="/wiki/Munchkin_Country" title="Munchkin Country">Munchkin Country</a> in the east and <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkie Country</a> in the west. Each province has its own ruler, but the realm itself has always been ruled by a single monarch. After <a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a>, this monarch is <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Princess Ozma</a>.</p>
<p>Originally, Baum did not intend for <a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a> to have any sequels, but it achieved a greater popularity than any of the other fairylands he created, including the land of <a href="/wiki/Merryland_(Oz)" title="Merryland (Oz)">Merryland</a> in Baum's children's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dot_and_Tot_of_Merryland" title="Dot and Tot of Merryland">Dot and Tot in Merryland</a></i>, written a year later. Due to Oz's worldwide success, Baum decided to return to it four years after The Wonderful Wizard of Oz was published. For the next two decades, he described and expanded upon the land in <a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_books" title="List of Oz books">the Oz Books</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> a series which introduced many fictional characters and creatures. Baum intended to end the series with the sixth Oz book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i> (1910), in which Oz is forever sealed off and made invisible to the outside world, but this did not sit well with fans, and he quickly abandoned the idea, writing eight more successful Oz books, and even naming himself the "Royal Historian of Oz".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thurber_p._66_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thurber_p._66-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In all, Baum wrote fourteen best-selling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_books" title="Children's books">children's books</a> about Oz and its enchanted inhabitants, as well as a spin off-series of <a href="/wiki/Little_Wizard_Stories_of_Oz" title="Little Wizard Stories of Oz">six early readers</a>. After his death in 1919, author <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Plumly_Thompson" title="Ruth Plumly Thompson">Ruth Plumly Thompson</a>, illustrator <a href="/wiki/John_R._Neill" title="John R. Neill">John R. Neill</a> (who had previously collaborated with Baum on his Oz books) and several other writers and artists continued the series. There are now over 50 novels based upon Baum's original Oz saga.</p>
<p>Baum characterized Oz as a real place, unlike <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">MGM's 1939 musical movie adaptation</a>, which presents it as a dream of lead character <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a>. According to the Oz books, it is a hidden fairyland cut off from the rest of the world by the <a href="/wiki/Deadly_Desert" title="Deadly Desert">Deadly Desert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The canonical <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a> for Oz is "Ozite". The term appears in <i>Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz</i>, <i>The Road to Oz</i>, and <i>The Emerald City of Oz</i>. Elsewhere in the canon, "Ozmie" is also used. In the animated 1974 semi-sequel to the MGM film, <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_Back_to_Oz" title="Journey Back to Oz">Journey Back to Oz</a></i>, "Ozonian" is used. The term "Ozian" appears in the script for the Royal Shakespeare Company's stage adaptation of the MGM movie and in the non-canonical modern work <i><a href="/wiki/Wicked_(musical)" title="Wicked (musical)">Wicked</a></i>. "Ozmite" was used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reilly_%26_Lee" title="Reilly &amp; Lee">Reilly &amp; Lee</a> marketing in the 1920s, which has suggested to some critics that "Ozmie" may have been a <a href="/wiki/Typographical_error" title="Typographical error">typographical error</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oz is, in the first book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i>, distinguished from Dorothy's native Kansas by not being civilized; this explains why Kansas does not have witches and wizards, while Oz does.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-5">[5]</a></sup> In the third book, <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>, Oz is described as a "fairy country", new terminology that remained to explain its wonders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Land_of_Oz">The Land of Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Land of Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map-of-Oz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="717" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Map-of-Oz.jpg/400px-Map-of-Oz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Map-of-Oz.jpg/600px-Map-of-Oz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/Map-of-Oz.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map-of-Oz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Land of Oz; note that the map is a mirror image of "actual" locations, but that the compass rose shows east on the right-hand side.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Oz is roughly rectangular in shape, and divided along the diagonals into four countries: <a href="/wiki/Munchkin_Country" title="Munchkin Country">Munchkin Country</a> (but commonly referred to as 'Munchkinland' in adaptations) in the East, <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkie Country</a> in the West (sometimes West and East are reversed on maps of Oz, see <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Oz#West_and_East" title="Land of Oz">West and East</a> below), <a href="/wiki/Gillikin_Country" title="Gillikin Country">Gillikin Country</a> in the North, and <a href="/wiki/Quadling_Country" title="Quadling Country">Quadling Country</a> in the South. In the center of Oz, where the diagonals cross, is the fabled <a href="/wiki/Emerald_City" title="Emerald City">Emerald City</a>, capital of the land of Oz and seat to the monarch of Oz, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Princess Ozma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The regions have a color schema: blue for Munchkins, yellow for Winkies, red for Quadlings, green for the Emerald city, and (in works after the first) purple for the Gillikins, which region was also not named in the first book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-8">[8]</a></sup> (This contrasts with Kansas; Baum, describing it, used "gray" nine times in four paragraphs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>) In <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, this is merely the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Favorite_color" title="Favorite color">favorite color</a>, used for clothing and other man-made objects, and having some influence on their choice of crops, but the basic colors of the world are natural colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-8">[8]</a></sup> The effect is less consistent in later works. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i>, the book states that everything in the land of the Gillikins is purple, including the plants and mud, and a character can see that he is leaving when the grass turns from purple to green, but it also describes pumpkins as orange and corn as green in that land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Baum, indeed, never used the color schema consistently; in many books, he alluded to the colors to orient the characters and readers to their location, and then did not refer to it again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley.2C_p._106_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riley.2C_p._106-11">[11]</a></sup> His most common technique was to depict the man-made articles and flowers as the color of the country, leaving leaves, grass, and fruit their natural colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of these regions are settled with prosperous and contented people. However, this naturally is lacking in scope for plot. Numerous pockets throughout the land of Oz are cut off from the main culture, for geographic or cultural reasons. Many have never heard of Ozma, making it impossible for them to acknowledge her as their rightful queen. These regions are concentrated around the edges of the country, and constitute the main settings for books that are set entirely within Oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Princess_of_Oz" title="The Lost Princess of Oz">The Lost Princess of Oz</a></i>, for instance is set entirely in rough country in Winkie Country, between two settled areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Glinda_of_Oz" title="Glinda of Oz">Glinda of Oz</a></i>, Ozma speaks of her duty to discover all these stray corners of Oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Yellow_brick_road" title="Yellow brick road">yellow brick road</a> leads from the lands of the Munchkins to the Emerald City. Other such roads featured in other works: one from Gillikin Country in <i>The Marvelous Land of Oz</i>, and a second one from Munchkin Land in <i>The Patchwork Girl of Oz</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oz is completely surrounded on all four sides by a desert which insulates the citizens of the Land of Oz from discovery and invasion. In the first two books, this is merely a natural desert, with only its extent making it dangerous to the traveler, but in <i>The Road to Oz</i> it is said to turn anyone who touches it to sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Indeed, in <i>The Marvelous Land of Oz</i>, Mombi tries to escape through it and Glinda chases her over the sands. Still, it is the dividing land between the magic of Oz and the outside world, and the <a href="/wiki/Winged_monkeys" title="Winged monkeys">Winged Monkeys</a> can not obey Dorothy's command to carry her home because it would take them outside the lands of Oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>, it has become a magical desert called the <a href="/wiki/Deadly_Desert" title="Deadly Desert">Deadly Desert</a> with life-destroying sands and noxious fumes, a feature that remained constant through the rest of the series, although no actual destruction is depicted in the Oz books, unlike in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_Oz" title="Return to Oz">Return to Oz</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-19">[19]</a></sup> The desert has nonetheless been breached numerous times, both by children from our world (mostly harmless), by the Wizard of Oz himself, and by more sinister characters, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nome_King" title="Nome King">Nome King</a>, who attempted to conquer Oz. After such an attempt in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i>, the book ends with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glinda" title="Glinda">Glinda</a> creating a barrier of invisibility around the Land of Oz, for further protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This was, indeed, an earnest effort on Baum's part to end the series, but the insistence of readers meant the continuation of the series, and therefore the discovery of many ways for people to pass through this barrier as well as over the sands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thurber_p._66_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thurber_p._66-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite this continual evasion, the barrier itself remained; nowhere in any Oz book did Baum hint that the inhabitants were even considering removing the magical barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_and_East">West and East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: West and East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first known map of Oz was a glass slide used in Baum's <i>Fairylogue and Radio-Play</i> traveling show, showing the blue land of the Munchkins in the east and the yellow land of the Winkies in the west. These directions are confirmed by the text of all of Baum's Oz books, especially the first, in which the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_East" title="Wicked Witch of the East">Wicked Witch of the East</a> rules over the <a href="/wiki/Munchkin" title="Munchkin">Munchkins</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> rules over the <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkies</a>.</p>
<p>Like traditional western maps, the <i>Fairylogue and Radio-Play</i> map showed the west on the left, and the east on the right. However, the first map of Oz to appear in an Oz book had those directions reversed, and the <a href="/wiki/Compass_rose" title="Compass rose">compass rose</a> adjusted accordingly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It is believed that this is a result of Baum copying the map from the wrong side of the glass slide, effectively getting a mirror image of his intended map. When he realized he was copying the slide backward, he reversed the compass rose to make the directions correct. However, an editor at Reilly and Lee reversed the compass rose, thinking he was fixing an error, and resulting in further confusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Most notably, this confused Ruth Plumly Thompson, who frequently reversed directions in her own Oz books as a result.</p>
<p>Another speculation stems from the original conception of Oz, which at first appeared to be situated in an American desert. If Baum thought of the country of the Munchkins as the nearest region to him, it would have been in the east while he lived in Chicago, but when he moved to California, it would have been in the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern maps of Oz are almost universally drawn with the Winkies in the west and the Munchkins in the east, although west and east often appear reversed. Many Oz fans believe this is the correct orientation, perhaps as a result of Glinda's spell, which has the effect of confusing most standard compasses; perhaps resembling its similarity to the world Alice found <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Through_the_looking_glass" title="Through the looking glass">through the looking glass</a> in which everything was a mirror image; or perhaps just reflecting the alien nature of Oz. In <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Number_of_the_Beast_(novel)" title="The Number of the Beast (novel)">The Number of the Beast</a></i> he posits that Oz is on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_direct_motion" title="Retrograde and direct motion">retrograde</a> planet, meaning that it spins in the opposite direction of Earth so that the sun seems to rise on one's left as one faces north. <a href="/wiki/March_Laumer" title="March Laumer">March Laumer</a>'s <i>The Magic Mirror of Oz</i> attributes the changes to a character named Till Orangespiegel attempting to turn the Land of Oz orange.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oz, like all of Baum's fantasy countries, was presented as existing as part of the real world, albeit protected from civilization by natural barriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Indeed, in the first books, nothing indicated that it was not hidden in the deserts of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">[7]</a></sup> It gradually acquired neighboring magical countries, often from works of Baum's that had been independent, as <a href="/wiki/Ix_(Oz)" title="Ix (Oz)">Ix</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Zixi_of_Ix" title="Queen Zixi of Ix">Queen Zixi of Ix</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Mo_(Oz)" title="Mo (Oz)">Mo</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magical_Monarch_of_Mo" title="The Magical Monarch of Mo">The Magical Monarch of Mo</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The first of these is <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Ev" title="Land of Ev">Ev</a>, introduced in <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="1511" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg/400px-Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg/600px-Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg/800px-Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oz-and-surrounding-countrie.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Oz and surrounding countries, on the map from <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i> (The directional indicator in the map's corner shows North and South correctly, but it flips East to the left, and West to the right).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i>, Baum included maps in the endpapers which definitively situated Oz on a continent with its neighboring countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Oz is the largest country on the continent unofficially known as <a href="/wiki/Nonestica" title="Nonestica">Nonestica</a> (this name was proposed by Robert R. Pattrick for the whole of the countries surrounding Oz; Pattrick proposed "Ozeria" for the whole continent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> but that name is generally unused in fan discussions), which also includes the countries of Ev, Ix, and Mo, which has also been known as Phunniland, among others. Nonestica is, according to the map, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonestic_Ocean" title="Nonestic Ocean">Nonestic Ocean</a>. A fair amount of evidence in the books point to this continent as being envisioned as somewhere in the southern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> At the opening of <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a> is sailing to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> with her <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Henry_(Oz)" title="Uncle Henry (Oz)">Uncle Henry</a> when she is washed overboard (in a chicken coop, with <a href="/wiki/Billina" title="Billina">Billina</a> the yellow hen), and lands on the shore of Ev—a rare instance in which an outsider reaches the Oz landmass through non-magical (or <i>apparently</i> non-magical) means. Palm trees grow outside the Royal Palace in the Emerald City, and horses are not native to Oz, both points of consistency with a South-Pacific location; illustrations and descriptions of round-shaped and domed Ozite houses suggest a non-Western architecture. Conversely, Oz has technological, architectural, and urban elements typical of Europe and North America around the turn of the twentieth century; but this may involve cultural input from unusual external sources (see History below). Ruth Plumly Thompson asserts in her first Oz book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Book_of_Oz" title="The Royal Book of Oz">The Royal Book of Oz</a></i>, that the language of Oz is English, which also suggests European or American influence.</p>
<p>An argument against the South Pacific is that the seasons in Oz are shown as the same seasons in the United States at the same time. In addition, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wishing_Horse_of_Oz" title="The Wishing Horse of Oz">The Wishing Horse of Oz</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Pigasus_(literature)" title="Pigasus (literature)">Pigasus</a> follows the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Star" title="North Star">North Star</a> when he flies to Thunder Mountain, which could only be done in the Northern Hemisphere.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inspiration">Inspiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inspiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baum's creation of the Emerald City may have been inspired by the White City of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Columbian_Exposition" title="World Columbian Exposition">World Columbian Exposition</a>, which he visited frequently. Its quick building, in less than a year, may have been an element in the quick construction of the Emerald City in the first book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schematically, Oz is much like the United States, with the Emerald City taking the place of Chicago: to the East, mixed forest and farmland; to the West, treeless plains and fields of wheat; to the South, warmth and lush growth, and red earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ruth Plumly Thompson took a different direction with her Oz books, introducing European elements such as the title character of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Yellow_Knight_of_Oz" title="The Yellow Knight of Oz">The Yellow Knight of Oz</a></i>, a knight straight out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthurian_Legend" title="Arthurian Legend">Arthurian Legend</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Witches_and_wizards">Witches and wizards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Witches and wizards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wicked_Witch2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2153" data-file-width="3285" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Wicked_Witch2.jpg/250px-Wicked_Witch2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Wicked_Witch2.jpg/375px-Wicked_Witch2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Wicked_Witch2.jpg/500px-Wicked_Witch2.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wicked_Witch2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> melts, from the <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_Denslow" title="William Wallace Denslow">William Wallace Denslow</a> illustration in the first edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> (1900)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the time of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i>, the lands in the North, South, East and West of Oz are each ruled by a <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">Witch</a>. The Witches of the North and South are good, while the Witches of the East and West are wicked. <a href="/wiki/Glinda_the_Good_Witch" title="Glinda the Good Witch">Glinda</a> (the Good Witch of the South) is later revealed to be the most powerful of the four, although later Oz books reveal that the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> was so powerful, even Glinda feared her. After Dorothy's house crushes the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_East" title="Wicked Witch of the East">Wicked Witch of the East</a>, thereby liberating the Munchkins from bondage, the <a href="/wiki/Good_Witch_of_the_North" title="Good Witch of the North">Good Witch of the North</a> tells Dorothy that she (the Witch of the North) is not as powerful as the Wicked Witch of the East had been, or she would have freed the Munchkins herself.</p>
<p>During the first scene in Oz in <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, the Good Witch of the North (Locasta or Tattypoo) explains to Dorothy that Oz still has witches and wizards, not being civilized, and goes on to explain that witches and wizards can be both good and evil, unlike the evil witches that Dorothy had been told of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> That book contained only the four witches (besides the humbug wizard), but despite Ozma's prohibition on magic, many more magicians feature in later works.</p>
<p>Baum tended to capitalize the word "Witch" when referring to the Witches of the North, South, East or West, but did not do so when referring to witches in general. For example, in the aforementioned first scene of <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, Locasta (or Tattypoo) thanks Dorothy for killing the "Wicked Witch of the East", and introduces herself as "the Witch of the North", with the word "Witch" capitalized in both cases. However, when she goes on to tell Dorothy that "I [the Witch of the North] am a good witch, and the people love me", the word "witch" is not capitalized.</p>
<p>White is the traditional color of witches in Oz. The Good Witch of the North wears a pointed white hat and a white gown decorated with stars, while Glinda, the Good Witch of the South (called a "sorceress" in later books), wears a pure white dress. Dorothy is taken for a witch not only because she had killed the Wicked Witch of the East, but because her dress is blue and white checked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ozma, once on the throne, prohibits the use of magic by anyone other than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glinda" title="Glinda">Glinda</a> the Good, the <a href="/wiki/Wizard_of_Oz_(character)" title="Wizard of Oz (character)">Wizard of Oz</a>, and herself – as, earlier, the Good Witch of the North had prohibited magic by any other witch in her domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The illicit use of magic is a frequent feature of villains in later works in the series, appearing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scarecrow_of_Oz" title="The Scarecrow of Oz">The Scarecrow of Oz</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rinkitink_in_Oz" title="Rinkitink in Oz">Rinkitink in Oz</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Princess_of_Oz" title="The Lost Princess of Oz">The Lost Princess of Oz</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tin_Woodman_of_Oz" title="The Tin Woodman of Oz">The Tin Woodman of Oz</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_of_Oz" title="The Magic of Oz">The Magic of Oz</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are different kinds of animals living in Oz. According to Baum, all animals in Oz have the ability to speak because it is a "fairy" kingdom. When asked by his readers why Dorothy's dog <a href="/wiki/Toto_(Oz)" title="Toto (Oz)">Toto</a> did not speak, Baum insisted that he had the ability to, but did not choose to speak, but he finally does so in <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i>.</p>
<p>Among the many animals in Oz are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>A-B-Sea Serpent</b> – A 200 feet (61 m) long sea serpent that is composed of alphabet blocks.</li>
<li><b>Chiss</b> – An evil <a href="/wiki/Porcupine" title="Porcupine">porcupine</a> spirit that can launch his quills.</li>
<li><b>Choggenmugger</b> - Thick, hard scales cover its body. It has wide jaws, sharp teeth. A dragon-eater that ate all dragons, serpents and crocodiles on the Regos Island to extinction.</li>
<li><b>Comfortable <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">Camel</a></b> – A camel from outside of Oz, who found his way into Oz, along with the Doubtful Dromedary, and they joined Dorothy Gale's party to find the Scarecrow. He almost always feels comfortable during his eventful journey.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cowardly_Lion" title="Cowardly Lion">Cowardly Lion</a></b> – The Cowardly Lion is one of Dorothy Gale's friends.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">Crab</a></b> – A crab ended up in an argument with a Zebra to determine if the world had more water, or more land</li>
<li><b>Doubtful <a href="/wiki/Dromedary" title="Dromedary">Dromedary</a></b> – A dromedary from outside of Oz, who found his way into Oz, along with the Comfortable Camel, and they joined Dorothy Gale's party to find the Scarecrow. He doubts nearly everything that anyone says.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">Dragons</a></b> – Dragons are the toughest creatures in the Land of Oz and its neighboring countries. The ones in Gillikin Country live underground and are allowed to come out once every 100 years in search of food.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wood_mouse" title="Wood mouse">Field Mice</a></b> – The Tin Man once saved the Queen of the Field Mice from a wildcat. Her kind later helps Dorothy and her friends get out of a deadly poppy field. The mice also reappear in the Magical Land of Oz to help the Scarecrow on his quest to reclaim his throne from General Jinjur, by hiding inside his clothes and jumping out to scare Jinjur's guards.</li>
<li><b>Foolish <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">Owl</a></b> – The Foolish Owl lives in Munchkin Country. She and the Wise Donkey are public advisers.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Gargoyle" title="Gargoyle">Gargoyles</a></b> – A bunch of wooden gargoyles live in the Land of Naught (underground, outside of Oz). They once captured Dorothy Gale, Zeb Hugson, and their animal friends when they visited the Land of Naught.</li>
<li><b>Giant Purple <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">Spiders</a></b> – A race of spiders in Gillikin Country that catch travelers in their webs and make them their servants.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Glass_Cat" title="Glass Cat">Glass Cat</a></b> – A glass sculpture brought to life by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powder_of_Life" title="Powder of Life">Powder of Life</a>.</li>
<li><b>Gump</b> – Gumps are common creatures in the Land of Oz. They are <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a>-like creatures with wide-spreading antlers, caprine whiskers, and a turned-up nose.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hippocampus_(mythology)" title="Hippocampus (mythology)">Hippocampus</a></b> – A race of half-horse half-fish aquatic creatures that live in Lake Orizon within Munchkin Country. The lake monster Quiberon once ate them into extinction. After Quiberon was turned to stone by the Wizard of Oz, he used his magic to reconstitute the bones of the Hippocampus causing their species to live once more.</li>
<li><b>Hip-po-gy-raf</b> – A Hip-po-gy-raf lives in Munchkin Country west of Mount Munch. It appears to be a combination of a <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamus</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">giraffe</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hungry_Tiger" title="Hungry Tiger">Hungry Tiger</a></b> – The Hungry Tiger is the Cowardly Lion's best friend.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Western_jackdaw" title="Western jackdaw">Jackdaws</a></b> – A bunch of Jackdaws live in Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kabumpo" title="Kabumpo">Kabumpo</a></b> – The elegant <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a> of Pumperdink.</li>
<li><b>Kalidahs</b> – The Kalidahs have the head and back legs of a <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tiger</a> and the arms, torso, and feet of a <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bear</a>. Their claws are known to rip a lion in half.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kangaroo" title="Kangaroo">Kangaroo</a></b> – A mittens-wearing kangaroo lives near the village of Fuddlecumjig.</li>
<li><b>Lonesome <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">Duck</a></b> – The Lonesome Duck is the only duck in the Land of Oz.</li>
<li><b>Orks</b> – Orks are unusual flying animals that have the blended characteristics of a <a href="/wiki/Common_ostrich" title="Common ostrich">common ostrich</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Parrot" title="Parrot">parrot</a>.</li>
<li><b>Rak</b> – A terrible beast with a horrible appetite and a bad disposition. It is said to be bigger than 100 men and can eat any living thing.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rattlesnake" title="Rattlesnake">Rattlesnake</a></b> – A rattlesnake serves as a companion of the A-B-Sea Serpent.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">Squirrel</a> King</b> – The King of the Squirrels that live in Winkie Country.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">Stork</a></b> – A stork once helped Dorothy and her companions rescue Scarecrow.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Unicorn" title="Unicorn">Unicorns</a></b> – A group of unicorns live that at Unicorners within Munchkin Country.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Winged_monkeys" title="Winged monkeys">Winged Monkeys</a></b> – Monkeys with wings. They obey the owner of the Golden Hat that summons them for three times.</li>
<li><b>Wise <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">Donkey</a></b> – The Wise Donkey was a former citizen of the <a href="/wiki/Mo_(Oz)" title="Mo (Oz)">Land of Mo</a> who often advised the King of Mo. He now lives in Munchkin Country with the Foolish Owl.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Zebra" title="Zebra">Zebra</a></b> – A Zebra ended up in an argument with a Crab to determine if there was either more water in the world or more land.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_races">Other races</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other races">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a multitude of other races living in the land of Oz, many of which only appear once. Among the known races are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>The Bun People of Bunbury</b> – The Bun People are made of baked goods.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">Bunnies</a> of Bunnybury</b> – A race of civilized rabbits.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_ceramics" title="Chinese ceramics">China</a> People</b> – A race of beings that are made of china (porcelain) that live in Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b>The Cuttenclips</b> – A race of living paper dolls that live in Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b>The Dicks</b> – The topsy-turvy inhabitants of Dicksy Land.</li>
<li><b>The Equinots</b> – A race of <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaurs</a>.</li>
<li><b>The Flatheads</b> – A race of flat-headed humans who carry their brains in cans.</li>
<li><b>The Flutterbudgets</b> – A race of people who entertain foolish fears and spend time worrying over nothing.</li>
<li><b>The Fuddles</b> – A race of anthropomorphic <a href="/wiki/Jigsaw_puzzle" title="Jigsaw puzzle">jigsaw puzzles</a> that live in Fuddlecumjig.</li>
<li><b>The Hammerheads</b> – An armless race with extensible necks and hard heads.</li>
<li><b>The Hoopers</b> – A race of 10 ft. tall humanoids that live in the Purple Forest of Gillikin Country and can roll into hoops by grabbing their toes with their hands.</li>
<li><b>The Hoppers</b> – A race of one-legged people that live inside a mountain in Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b>The Horners</b> – A race of strange one-horned people that live inside a mountain in Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b>The Hyups</b> – A subspecies of Munchkins that live on Mount Munch.</li>
<li><b>The Loons</b> – A race of living balloon people that live in Loonville.</li>
<li><b>The Magical Mimics</b> – A race of evil shape-shifting creatures that are a type of Erb that live on Mount Illuso.</li>
<li><b>The Phanfasms</b> – A magically powerful race of Erb spirits that change their appearance into beasts.</li>
<li><b>The Rigmaroles</b> – A race of people that make long deliberate speeches which makes use of many words.</li>
<li><b>The Scares</b> – A race of grotesque beings that reside in Scare City within Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b>The Scooters</b> – A race of people that live on the waters of the Gillikin River. They have long boat-like feet and have sails growing from their wrists to their ribs.</li>
<li><b>The Skeezers</b> – A race of anatomically normal humans who are often in conflicts with the Flatheads.</li>
<li><b>The Sticks-in-the-Muds</b> – A tribe of mud-covered people on stilts that live in Marsh Land within Winkie Country.</li>
<li><b>The Thists</b> – A race of creatures that has diamond-shaped heads and heart-shaped bodies that live in Thi.</li>
<li><b>The Tottenhots</b> – A race of small mischievous people who live on the borders of Quadling Country and Winkie Country.</li>
<li><b>The Utensians</b> – A race of living <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_utensil" title="Kitchen utensil">utensils</a> that live in Utensia in Quadling Country.</li>
<li><b>The Yips</b> – A small community of people that resemble the Hyups.</li>
</ul>
<p>Outside of them are many other strange races who are often found living in the wilderness of Oz. Despite the overlordship of Ozma, many of the communities live autonomously. Oz has great tolerance for eccentricity and oddness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many characters in Oz are animated objects. Such figures as the Glass Cat, the <a href="/wiki/Scarecrow_(Oz)" title="Scarecrow (Oz)">Scarecrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Pumpkinhead" title="Jack Pumpkinhead">Jack Pumpkinhead</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sawhorse_(Oz)" title="Sawhorse (Oz)">Sawhorse</a>, and others are common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">[7]</a></sup> Entire regions are the homes of such animated beings. The Dainty China Country is entirely filled with creatures made of china, who would freeze into figurines if removed. The China princess lives in fear of breaking, because she would never be as pretty even if repaired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many other characters are highly individual, even unique members of a species. Many such people from the outer worlds find refuge in Oz, which is highly tolerant of eccentricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of Oz prior to <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> (often called the prehistory of Oz as it takes place before Baum's "histories") is often the subject of dispute, as Baum himself gave conflicting accounts. In <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, the title character recounts that he was a ventriloquist and a circus balloonist from <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha</a>, and during one flight the rope for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloons#Vents" title="Hot air balloons">parachute vent</a> became tangled, preventing him from descending until the next morning, and he awoke to find that he was floating over a strange land. When he landed, the people thought he was a great wizard because of his ability to fly. He did not disabuse them of this notion, and with his new power over them, he had them build a city with a palace in the center of Oz. He also ordered them to wear green glasses so it would appear to be made entirely of emeralds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-29">[29]</a></sup> However, in the later Oz books the city is depicted as actually being made of emerald or other green materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley.2C_p._106_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riley.2C_p._106-11">[11]</a></sup> The Wizard was a young man when he first arrived in Oz, and grew old while he was there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley.2C_p._146_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riley.2C_p._146-37">[37]</a></sup> Afraid of the Wicked Witches of the West and the East, who, unlike him, could do real magic, the Wizard hid away in a room of his palace and refused to see visitors. He lived in this way until the arrival of Dorothy in the first book.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i> the prehistory was changed slightly. Glinda, the Good Witch of the South, reveals that the Wizard usurped the previous king of Oz <a href="/wiki/Pastoria" title="Pastoria">Pastoria</a> and hid away his daughter Ozma. This was Baum's reaction to the popular 1903 Broadway extravaganza Baum adapted from his book, <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, in which the Wizard took the role of the main antagonist and the Wicked Witch of the West was left out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Wizard, however, had been more popular with his readers than he thought. In <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>, he omitted any mention of the Wizard's having usurped the throne of Ozma's father,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> but the largest changes occurred in the next book.</p>
<p>In the preface to <i><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_and_the_Wizard_in_Oz" title="Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz">Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz</a></i>, Baum remarks that the Wizard had turned out to be a popular character with the children who had read the first book and so he brought the Wizard back. During it, the Wizard relates yet another account of his history in Oz, telling Ozma that his birth name was Oscar Zoroaster Phadrig Isaac Norman Henkle Emmanuel Ambroise Diggs, which, being a very long and cumbersome name, and as his other initials spelled out "PINHEAD," he preferred to leave just as O.Z. The balloon part of his story was unchanged, except for the detail added by Ozma, that the people probably saw his initials on his balloon and took them as a message that he was to be their king. She relates that the country was already named Oz (a word which in their language means "great and good"), and that it was typical for the rulers to have names that are variations of Oz (King Pastoria being a notable exception to this rule).</p>
<p>Ozma elaborates further, saying that there were once four Wicked Witches in Oz, who leagued together to depose the King, but the Wicked Witches of the North and South were defeated by Good Witches before the Wizard arrived in Oz. According to this version, the King at the time was Ozma's grandfather. This version of prehistory restores the Wizard's reputation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley.2C_p._146_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riley.2C_p._146-37">[37]</a></sup> but adds the awkwardness of both Ozma and her father having been born in captivity.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tin_Woodman_of_Oz" title="The Tin Woodman of Oz">The Tin Woodman of Oz</a></i> Baum writes how Oz came to be a fairyland:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Oz was not always a fairyland, I am told. Once it was much like other lands, except it was shut in by a dreadful desert of sandy wastes that lay all around it, thus preventing its people from all contact with the rest of the world. Seeing this isolation, the fairy band of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Lurline" title="Queen Lurline">Queen Lurline</a>, passing over Oz while on a journey, enchanted the country and so made it a Fairyland. And Queen Lurline left one of her fairies to rule this enchanted Land of Oz, and then passed on and forgot all about it.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Thenceforward, no one in Oz would ever age, get sick, or die. After becoming a fairyland, Oz harbored many Witches, Magicians, and Sorcerers until the time when Ozma made magic illegal without a permit. In yet another inconsistency, it is implied that Ozma was the fairy left behind by Queen Lurline to rule the country, contradicting the story where she was Pastoria's daughter. This is later confirmed in <i><a href="/wiki/Glinda_of_Oz" title="Glinda of Oz">Glinda of Oz</a></i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>"If you are really Princess Ozma of Oz," the Flathead said, "you are one of that band of fairies who, under Queen Lurline, made all Oz a Fairyland. I have heard that Lurline left one of her own fairies to rule Oz, and gave the fairy the name of Ozma."</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>While this explains why no one dies or ages, and nevertheless there are people of differing ages in Oz, it is completely inconsistent with the earlier versions of the prehistory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maguire, author of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked:_The_Life_and_Times_of_the_Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked</a></i> addresses this inconsistency by saying that the people of Oz believe that Ozma is reincarnated—that her spirit was left behind by Lurline, but her body is reborn to different mortal queens.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jack_Snow_(writer)" title="Jack Snow (writer)">Jack Snow</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magical_Mimics_in_Oz" title="The Magical Mimics in Oz">The Magical Mimics in Oz</a></i>, the prehistory story is retold. This version relates that Ozma was given to the king of Oz as an adoptive daughter, for he was old and had no children.</p>
<p>In the Magic Land stories of Alexander Melentyevich Volkov, the prehistory is quite different. The land was created 6,000–7,000 years ago by a wizard named Hurricap, who was tired of people coming to him with requests, so he decided to find a place without them annoying him. He found a remote land and separated it from the rest of the world, along with putting the enchantments of eternal spring and talking animals (Volkov's version doesn't include any forms of immortality). However, he failed to notice that the land already contained people (since he was a giant, already suffering from nearsightedness in his advanced age, and the people in the Magic Land were much shorter than in other places), but, upon discovering the fact, decided that removing the enchantments would be unnecessary. Instead, he ordered the people to keep away from his castle. After that, the notable events included a conquest attempt by a sorceress named Arachna (Gurrikap was still alive, and put her in an enchanted sleep for 5,000 years. Her awakening formed the story for the fifth book in Volkov's series), an unsuccessful coup by a prince named Bofaro to overthrow his father about 1,000 years ago (He and his accomplices were banished to an underground cave and became the Magic Land's main source of metal and gems, an analogue to the Nomes), and the arrival of the Four Witches (which only occurred about 500 years ago in this continuity).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_through_the_first_six_books">History through the first six books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History through the first six books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eventually, Dorothy Gale and her whole house are blown into Oz from <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> by a tornado. When the house lands, it crushes the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_East" title="Wicked Witch of the East">Wicked Witch of the East</a> (in Gregory Maguire's book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked:_The_Life_and_Times_of_the_Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West</a></i>, she is given a name, Nessarose), ruler of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munchkins" title="Munchkins">Munchkins</a>. In an attempt to get back to her home, she journeys to the Emerald City. Along the way, she meets the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">Tin Woodman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cowardly_Lion" title="Cowardly Lion">Cowardly Lion</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Scarecrow_(Oz)" title="Scarecrow (Oz)">Scarecrow</a>, all of whom accompany her. Once there, they become the first people to gain an audience with the Wizard since he went into seclusion, although he disguises himself because Dorothy now has the Wicked Witch of the East's magic silver slippers, and he is afraid of her. The Wizard sends Dorothy and her party to destroy the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> and in exchange promises to grant her request to be sent home. Surprisingly, Dorothy "destroys" the Witch by throwing a pail of water on her, causing her to melt. Defeated, the Wizard reveals to the group that he is in fact not a real wizard and has no magical powers, but he promises to grant Dorothy's wish and take her home himself in his balloon. He leaves the Scarecrow in his place to rule Oz.</p>
<p>Finally, it is discovered that the wizard had given the daughter of the last king of Oz, Princess Ozma, to the old witch <a href="/wiki/Mombi" title="Mombi">Mombi</a> to have her hidden away. Mombi had turned Ozma into a boy named Tip, whom she raised. When all of this is revealed Tip is turned back into Ozma and takes her rightful place as the benevolent ruler of all of Oz. Ozma successfully wards off several attempts by various armies to overthrow her. To prevent any upheaval of her rule over Oz, she outlaws the practice of all magic in Oz except by herself, the returned and reformed wizard, and by Glinda, and she has Glinda make all of Oz invisible to outsiders. Ozma remains the ruler of Oz for the entire series.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_politics">Economy and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some political analysts have claimed that Oz is a thinly disguised <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a> <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopia</a>, though some Baum scholars disagree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Advocates of this theory support it using this quotation from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>There were no poor people in the land of Oz, because there was no such thing as money, and all property of every sort belonged to the Ruler. Each person was given freely by his neighbors whatever he required for his use, which is as much as anyone may reasonably desire. Every one worked half the time and played half the time, and the people enjoyed the work as much as they did the play, because it is good to be occupied and to have something to do. There were no cruel overseers set to watch them, and no one to rebuke them or to find fault with them. So each one was proud to do all he could for his friends and neighbors, and was glad when they would accept the things he produced.</dd>
</dl>
<p>This is a revision of the original society: in the first two books, the people of Oz lived in a money-based economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-5">[5]</a></sup> For instance, the people of the Emerald City use "green pennies" as coinage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-42">[42]</a></sup> Money was not abolished in the course of the series, but excised from the conception of Oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Indeed, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_of_Oz" title="The Magic of Oz">The Magic of Oz</a></i>, a character from Oz gets into trouble when he goes to Ev because he was unaware of the concept of money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> This decision to remove money from Oz may reflect Baum's own financial difficulties in the times when he was writing these books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since Oz is ruled by a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a>, benevolent though she may be, Oz is closer in nature to an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a> than a communist or Marxist state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> When she was first introduced, Ozma was the monarch specifically of the Emerald City, but in the description of <i>Ozma of Oz</i>, Oz is presented as a federal state, rather like the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, in monarchies rather than republics: having an <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">overall ruler</a> in Ozma, and individual kings and queens of smaller portions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The society grew steadily more utopian, in that its peace and prosperity were organized, but from the first book, it was a stupendously wealthy country, in contrast to Kansas's crop failures, droughts, and mortgages—just as it also is colorful to contrast with Kansas's gray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On the other hand, despite the presence of the Emerald City, Oz is an agrarian country, similar to Kansas; the story has been interpreted as a <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populist</a> parable, and certainly contains many populist themes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonder_City_of_Oz" title="The Wonder City of Oz">The Wonder City of Oz</a></i>, Princess Ozma (called "Queen Ozma" in this book) is seen running for election ("ozlection") to her office as ruler against <a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_characters#Jenny_Jump" title="List of Oz characters">Jenny Jump</a>, a half-fairy newcomer from <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. However, this book was not written by Baum, but by John R. Neill, Baum's second successor. Further, the concept of the "ozlection" was not in Neill's manuscript for the book, but was added by an editor at Reilly and Lee, the publisher.</p>
<p>At times the rulers of Oz's territories have grander titles than would normally be customary, but this is done mostly for the satisfaction of the incumbents. The ruler of the Winkie Country is the Emperor, the Tin Woodman. The ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Quadling_Country" title="Quadling Country">Quadling Country</a> is Glinda the Good. The Munchkin Country is ruled by a king, later identified as Cheeriobed, who is revealed to be married to the Good Witch of the North, who, a spell broken, abdicates leadership of the Gillikin Country to Joe King and Queen Hyacinth of Up Town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Royal Flag of Oz is based on the map of the Land of Oz; the four colors represent the four countries, and the green star represents the Emerald City.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense">Defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oz is mostly a peaceful land and the idea of subversion is largely unknown to its people. Most military positions are only formal. This has caused many problems, such as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i> when the Emerald City (which was only guarded by an elderly doorman and one soldier who was the entire Army of Oz at the time) was easily conquered by the Army of Revolt led by <a href="/wiki/Jinjur" title="Jinjur">General Jinjur</a>. This army was in turn overwhelmed by another army of girls led by Glinda.</p>
<p>Security of Oz is mostly maintained by magic such as Glinda's spell making Oz completely invisible. Oz also has a natural barrier in the form of a desert that surrounds the land: anyone who touches the desert turns to sand. The <a href="/wiki/Nome_King" title="Nome King">Nome King</a> has tried to conquer Oz on several occasions. A nominal army once existed, but it had an extremely large officers/privates ratio; other than its commander the Tin Woodman and one private, the portion of it seen in <i>Ozma of Oz</i> was composed entirely of cowardly officers. In the end of the book it was said that there are three privates all in all, and it is unknown how many—if any—officers were left at home during Ozma's travel to Ev. The private seen in the book, named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omby_Amby" title="Omby Amby">Omby Amby</a>, is later promoted to Captain General.</p>
<p>In the book <i>Emerald City of Oz</i>, there are 2 towns called Rigmarole Town and Flutterbudgets that are the defensive settlements of Oz.</p>
<p>In the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_Oz" title="Return to Oz">Return to Oz</a>,</i> the mechanical man <a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_(Oz)" title="Tik-Tok (Oz)">Tik-Tok</a> is the entire Royal Army of Oz.</p>
<p>Attempts by outsiders to conquer the Land of Oz are frequent, particularly in the Oz books by Ruth Plumly Thompson. But these attempts are always successfully thwarted in the end, usually by Ozma or by forces sympathetic to her.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_characters" title="List of Oz characters">List of Oz characters</a></div>
<p>Recurring characters in the classic Oz series include:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a></b> – A heroic little orphan girl from <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>. In later Oz books, she eventually moves to Oz permanently after visiting the land several times and having several adventures there. Dorothy ultimately becomes best friends with <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Princess Ozma</a> who proclaims her as an official princess of Oz.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Toto_(Oz)" title="Toto (Oz)">Toto</a></b> – Dorothy's little black dog whom she loves dearly. Toto is a sidekick companion, loyally following his mistress Dorothy on most of her adventures. It is revealed in "<a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a>" that even though Toto is not a fairy dog, he can still talk in Oz, he just chooses not to.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Princess Ozma</a></b> – The long-lost child Queen and rightful ruler of Oz. She is the only child of Oz's deceased mortal king, <a href="/wiki/Pastoria" title="Pastoria">Pastoria</a> who ruled before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscar_Diggs" title="Oscar Diggs">Wizard</a> arrived. She is established upon the throne as the true heir roughly five years after <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> takes place.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Henry_(Oz)" title="Uncle Henry (Oz)">Uncle Henry</a></b> – Dorothy's uncle who is a Kansas farmer and the husband of aunt Em. He ultimately moves to Oz with Dorothy and aunt Em when the bank forcloses on his farm.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aunt_Em" title="Aunt Em">Aunt Em</a></b> – Dorothy's aunt, and the wife of uncle Henry. She ultimately moves to Oz with Dorothy and uncle Henry.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Good_Witch_of_the_North" title="Good Witch of the North">Good Witch of the North</a></b> – The elderly ruler of the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillikins" title="Gillikins">Gillikins</a>. She is known by <b>Locasta Tattypoo</b> and became the ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Gillikin_Country" title="Gillikin Country">Gillikin Country</a> after she overthrew <a href="/wiki/Mombi" title="Mombi">Mombi, the Wicked Witch of the North</a>. She was also the first witch Dorothy encountered upon her first arrival in Oz in <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Scarecrow_(Oz)" title="Scarecrow (Oz)">Scarecrow</a></b> – A living stuffed man of straw who was made in Oz's eastern quadrant called <a href="/wiki/Munchkin_Country" title="Munchkin Country">Munchkin Country</a>. He is also a good friend of Dorothy. At the end of <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> he became the King of Oz and ruled its imperial capital called <a href="/wiki/Emerald_City" title="Emerald City">Emerald City</a> after the Wizard left. However, he gladly relinquished the title when Ozma was found.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Tin_Woodman" title="Tin Woodman">Tin Woodman</a></b> (aka Nick Chopper) – An enchanted woodsman made entirely out of tin and is a good friend of Dorothy. He was originally a <a href="/wiki/Munchkin" title="Munchkin">Munchkin</a> but is the current Emperor of the western <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkies</a> and lives in a tin castle. He became the official ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkie Country</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> was destroyed by Dorothy in <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Cowardly_Lion" title="Cowardly Lion">Cowardly Lion</a></b> – The talking lion who is a good friend of Dorothy. In the end of <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i>, he became the King of the Beasts and overcame his cowardly ways. In later Oz books he becomes best friends with the <a href="/wiki/Hungry_Tiger" title="Hungry Tiger">Hungry Tiger</a>, together they rule Oz's Animal Kingdom and are the royal chariot pullers for Princess Ozma.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Wizard_of_Oz_(character)" title="Wizard of Oz (character)">Wizard</a> (Oscar Diggs)</b> – The former ruler of Oz and the Emerald City before the Scarecrow and Princess Ozma. Originally a skilled circus entertainer and ventriloquist from <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a>, he used his magic tricks to create illusions that made it appear as if he had real powers. Once a humbug, he is now a real <a href="/wiki/Magician_(fantasy)" title="Magician (fantasy)">wizard</a> after being trained by both Ozma and Glinda who successfully taught him how to perform honest magic.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Glinda_the_Good_Witch" title="Glinda the Good Witch">Glinda</a></b> – The beautiful and wise Good Witch of the South who rules the southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadlings" title="Quadlings">Quadlings</a> in a ruby palace. She became the benevolent sorceress of the <a href="/wiki/Quadling_Country" title="Quadling Country">Quadling Country</a> after she vanquished the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_South" title="Wicked Witch of the South">Wicked Witch of the South</a> (although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939)">classic MGM musical movie of 1939</a> incorrectly portrays her as the Good Witch of the North).</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardian_of_the_Gates" title="Guardian of the Gates">Guardian of the Gates</a></b> – The friendly gate keeper responsible for adorning the visitors who wish to enter Oz's capital with the green tinted spectacles. He is described as being a very jolly and short man who wears fancy green clothing and has green tinted skin.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Soldier_with_the_Green_Whiskers" title="Soldier with the Green Whiskers">Soldier with the Green Whiskers</a></b> – The Captain-General of the Royal Army of Oz who guards the main entrance of the royal palace in the Emerald City. His real name is <b>Omby Amby Wantowin Battles</b>. He is a very tall man who has a very long green beard.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jellia_Jamb" title="Jellia Jamb">Jellia Jamb</a></b> – The young maid who works in the Emerald City's royal palace. She is also known as the "<i>pretty green girl</i>", with pretty green eyes and pretty green hair. Jellia becomes Ozma and Dorothy's favorite servant out of the city's staff administration.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jack_Pumpkinhead" title="Jack Pumpkinhead">Jack Pumpkinhead</a></b> – An enchanted man made out of wooden branches with a carved jack-o-lantern pumpkin for a head. Jack was brought to life with the magic "Powder of Life" potion.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Sawhorse_(Oz)" title="Sawhorse (Oz)">Sawhorse</a></b> – An animated sawhorse who becomes a steed of sorts for Princess Ozma and her friends. He was brought to life with the "Powder of Life".</li>
<li><b>H. M. <a href="/wiki/Woggle-Bug" title="Woggle-Bug">Woggle-Bug</a>, T.E.</b> – An intelligent insect who was magnified into a human-sized bug.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jinjur" title="Jinjur">Jinjur</a></b> – A former General of the all female Army of Revolt.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Billina" title="Billina">Billina</a></b> – A yellow hen and good friend of Dorothy. Upon arriving to Oz Billina becomes Queen of the chickens.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_(Oz)" title="Tik-Tok (Oz)">Tik-Tok</a></b> – A mechanical and super-intelligent, <a href="/wiki/Clockwork" title="Clockwork">clockwork</a> <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robot</a> (one of the first robots in literature).</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Hungry_Tiger" title="Hungry Tiger">Hungry Tiger</a></b> – The Cowardly Lion's closest companion besides the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Shaggy_Man" title="Shaggy Man">Shaggy Man</a></b> – A homeless, but friendly man from "the real world". He ends up living in Oz permanently.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Button-Bright" title="Button-Bright">Button-Bright</a></b> – A lost little boy of only four or five-years-old. He is from a wealthy family in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, but has many adventures in Oz and its neighboring kingdoms.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Eureka_(Oz)" title="Eureka (Oz)">Eureka</a></b> – Dorothy's pink and purple kitten.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Patchwork_Girl" title="Patchwork Girl">Patchwork Girl</a></b> (a.k.a. "Scraps") – A life-size living <a href="/wiki/Doll" title="Doll">doll</a> made entirely of a colorful patchwork quilt. She was brought to life with the magic "Powder of Life" potion by a young Munchkin boy named Ojo. Scraps also becomes the love interest of the Scarecrow.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ojo_the_Lucky" title="Ojo the Lucky">Ojo</a></b> – A young boy of Munchkin descent.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Betsy_Bobbin" title="Betsy Bobbin">Betsy Bobbin</a></b> – A girl a year older than Dorothy from <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> who comes to Oz with a talking mule named Hank after being shipwrecked.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polychrome_(fictional_character)" title="Polychrome (fictional character)">Polychrome</a></b> – A colorful and ethereal sky fairy and the youngest daughter of the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">Rainbow</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Trot_(Oz)" title="Trot (Oz)">Trot</a></b> – A girl who comes to Oz by accident and who's a year younger than Dorothy.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cap%27n_Bill" title="Cap'n Bill">Cap'n Bill</a></b> – An ex-sailor with a wooden leg who visits Oz and is friends with Trot.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kabumpo" title="Kabumpo">Kabumpo</a></b> – The Elegant Elephant of Pumperdink.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_characters#Jenny_Jump" title="List of Oz characters">Jenny Jump</a></b> – A 15-year-old girl from <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> who became a half-fairy.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Pastoria" title="Pastoria">Pastoria</a></b> – The former mortal King of Oz who ruled long before the Wizard came and is the deceased father of Princess Ozma.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mombi" title="Mombi">Mombi</a></b> – The former Wicked Witch of the North. Mombi once ruled the northern quadrant of Oz until the Good Witch Locasta Tattypoo overthrew her. She also was partially responsible for the mysterious disappearance of Princess Ozma.</li>
<li><b>The <a href="/wiki/Nome_King" title="Nome King">Nome King</a></b> – the main villain and antagonist in the Oz books. He is Oz's most threatening enemy who resides in a neighbouring kingdom separated from Oz by the <a href="/wiki/Deadly_Desert" title="Deadly Desert">Deadly Desert</a>. The Nome King is always trying to conquer Oz or thinking of ways to overthrow it throughout most of the entire series.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="The_1939_MGM_film's_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1939_MGM_film.27s_Oz">The 1939 MGM film's Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The 1939 MGM film's Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Land of Oz as portrayed in the <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">classic MGM musical movie of 1939</a>, is quite different from that portrayed in Baum's books. The most notable difference is that in the film the entire land of Oz appears to be dreamed up by <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a> (thus making it a <a href="/wiki/Dream_world_(plot_device)" title="Dream world (plot device)">dream world</a>), although, Dorothy earnestly corrects the adults at the end that she was indeed there, and an image of Dorothy's falling farmhouse returning to earth is presented. The apparent message is that one should appreciate one's home, no matter how dull it may look or uninteresting its surroundings may be, for having a home and a family is not something that should be taken for granted. This contrasts sharply with the books, in which Dorothy and her family are eventually invited to move to Oz due to a bank foreclosure on the farm, showing both that Oz is a real place, and that it is a utopia compared to the prairies of <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>.</p>
<p>There are many other small differences between the books and the movie. For example, when Dorothy arrives in <a href="/wiki/Munchkin_Country" title="Munchkin Country">Munchkinland</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munchkins" title="Munchkins">Munchkins</a> are seen wearing colorful costumes, but in the book Munchkins are said to only wear blue as blue is the official dominant color of the east. The first witch Dorothy meets in Oz in the book is the <a href="/wiki/Good_Witch_of_the_North" title="Good Witch of the North">Good Witch of the North</a>, a minor character that only had one other appearance in Baum's books but is an important figure of Oz nonetheless. In the movie this character is conflated with that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glinda" title="Glinda">Glinda</a>, who is the Good Witch of the South and does not make an appearance until the very end of Baum's story. The character of Glinda in the books dresses in all white silk, as white is the traditional color for good witches, whereas in the film she is seen in pink.</p>
<p>It is also worthy of note that the Dorothy of the books is only a little girl who is no older than twelve-years-old. However, she is mature and very resourceful, only crying when faced with ultimate despair, whereas the older Dorothy of the movie (portrayed as a twelve-year-old by sixteen-year-old <a href="/wiki/Judy_Garland" title="Judy Garland">Judy Garland</a>) spends several portions of the film crying and being told by others what to do, however her fear was overshadowed by the Cowardly Lion's. This is more consistent with Thompson's portrayal of Dorothy—Baum is known for his strong and independent female characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="The Wicked Witch of the West">The Wicked Witch of the West</a> also changes significantly between books and movie. In the books no mention is ever made of her skin color, whereas in the movie she is green without explanation, although the <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkies</a> she has enslaved and turned into soldiers are also green. In the book it is implied but not stated that she dresses in yellow, as yellow is the official dominant color of the <a href="/wiki/Winkie_Country" title="Winkie Country">Winkie Country</a> where she rules, whereas in the movie she dresses entirely in black. In the book she is portrayed as having only one eye, which is so powerful it could see distant objects like a telescope, but in the movie she uses a crystal ball to watch Dorothy and her friends from afar. The 1939 MGM film makes the first reference to The Witches of the East and West being sisters, which was not the case in the book; none of the Witches in Baum's Oz are related.</p>
<p>The Wizard of Oz does not resort to anywhere near as much trickery in the movie as the book. In the book he entertains each member of Dorothy's party on a different day, and takes a different form for each; appearing as a giant green head, a beautiful fairy, a great beast, and a levitating ball of fire. In the movie he takes only one of these forms—that of the giant green head.</p>
<p>The nature of the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_City" title="Emerald City">Emerald City</a> is changed in the film. In the book, the city is not actually all green, but everyone is forced to wear green tinted spectacles (ostensibly to protect their eyes from the glory and splendor of the luxurious city), thus making everything appear green. In the film, the city is actually all green. The architecture of the Emerald City in the movie uses a much more contemporary Art Deco style than Baum could have imagined. In the book, a giant green wall studded in glittering emeralds surrounds the entire city, whereas in the movie there is only a gate opening.</p>
<p>The movie replaces the charmed <a href="/wiki/Silver_Shoes" title="Silver Shoes">Silver Shoes with pointed toes</a> of the book with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruby_Slippers" title="Ruby Slippers">Ruby-Red Slippers</a>. This was because full color motion pictures were still a relatively new technology in 1939, and MGM wanted to show off the visually dazzling process. Shiny red shoes were more impressive in a color motion picture compared to silver ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Due to the popularity of the movie, the green witch and the Ruby Slippers are more well known to the general public and pop culture than their book counterparts, and are even considered iconic.</p>
<h3><span id="Gregory_Maguire's_revisionist_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gregory_Maguire.27s_revisionist_Oz">Gregory Maguire's revisionist Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Gregory Maguire's revisionist Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his <a href="/wiki/Revisionism_(fictional)" title="Revisionism (fictional)">revisionist</a> Oz novels <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked:_The_Life_and_Times_of_the_Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Son_of_a_Witch" title="Son of a Witch">Son of a Witch</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Lion_Among_Men" title="A Lion Among Men">A Lion Among Men</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_Oz" title="Out of Oz">Out of Oz</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Maguire" title="Gregory Maguire">Gregory Maguire</a> portrays a very different version of the Land of Oz. Maguire's Oz is not Baum's utopia, but a land troubled by political unrest and economic hardship. One political issue in Maguire's novels is the oppression of the Animals (Maguire distinguishes speaking Animals from non-speaking animals by the use of initial capital letters). There are many religious traditions in Maguire's Oz, including Lurlinism (which regards the Fairy Lurline as Oz's creator), Unionism, which worships the Unnamed God, and the pleasure faiths which had swept Oz during the time that the witches were at Shiz. An example of the pleasure faiths were tic-toc (where creatures were enchanted to tell secrets or the future and run by clockwork), and sorcery.</p>
<p>Maguire's presentation of Oz's geography is also tinged with politics. A large political prison, Southstairs, exists in caverns below the Emerald City. Gillikin, home of Shiz University, has more industrial development than other parts of Oz. Munchkinland is Oz's breadbasket and at one point declares its independence from the rule of the Emerald City. Quadling Country is largely marshland, inhabited by the artistic and sexually free Quadlings. The Vinkus (Maguire's name for Winkie Country) is largely open grassland, populated by semi-nomadic tribes with brown skin.</p>
<p>The musical <i><a href="/wiki/Wicked_(musical)" title="Wicked (musical)">Wicked</a></i>, based on Maguire's first Oz novel, portrays an Oz slightly closer to the version seen in Baum's novels and the 1939 <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">film</a>. The oppression of the Animals is still a theme, but the geographical and religious divisions portrayed in Maguire's novel are barely present.</p>
<p>In both the book and musical, several characters from the traditional Oz stories are present with different names. Glinda is originally called Galinda, but changes her name. The Wicked Witch of the West is called <a href="/wiki/Elphaba" title="Elphaba">Elphaba</a>, the Wicked Witch of the East is called <a href="/wiki/Nessarose" title="Nessarose">Nessarose</a>. In the musical, but not in the book, <a href="/wiki/Boq" title="Boq">Boq</a> becomes the Tin Man, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiyero" title="Fiyero">Fiyero</a> becomes the Scarecrow.</p>
<h3><span id="Alexander_Melentyevich_Volkov's_Magic_Land"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alexander_Melentyevich_Volkov.27s_Magic_Land">Alexander Melentyevich Volkov's Magic Land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Alexander Melentyevich Volkov's Magic Land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Volkov_(writer)" title="Alexander Volkov (writer)">Alexander Melentyevich Volkov</a> was a Russian author best known for his translation of <i>The Wizard of Oz</i> into Russian, and for writing his own original sequels, which were based only loosely on Baum's. Volkov's books have been translated into many other languages, and are better known than Baum's in some countries. The books, while still aimed at children, feature many mature political and ethical elements. They have been retranslated into English by Peter L. Blystone and partially by March Laumer, who used elements of them in his own books.</p>
<h3><span id="March_Laumer's_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="March_Laumer.27s_Oz">March Laumer's Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: March Laumer's Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/March_Laumer" title="March Laumer">March Laumer</a> was one of the first authors to continue the Oz series after the Famous Forty. His books were written with the permission of Contemporary Books, who owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reilly_%26_Lee" title="Reilly &amp; Lee">Reilly &amp; Lee</a>, the original publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> His canon includes everything he knew of that was set in the land of Oz, including Volkov's Russian Oz, the MGM movie, the Disney sequel, and many of Baum's own books that most fans do not consider canonical.</p>
<p>Laumer also made several controversial changes to Oz. He married off several of the major characters, often to unlikely prospects. For example, the intelligent and mature sorceress Glinda was married to Button Bright, who had been a small and dim-witted child throughout Baum's books. He also aged Dorothy to a teenager to make her a romantic prospect for several characters, made Ozma a <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> based on her upbringing as a boy, and made the Shaggy Man an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephebophile" title="Ephebophile">ephebophile</a> based on his frequent travels with young girls.</p>
<p>Laumer's books do not portray one consistent version of Oz. Because most of his books were collaborations, he often included elements of other author's visions of Oz which may have been inconsistent with his own. For example, while he explicitly made Dorothy sixteen in <i>A Fairy Queen in Oz</i>, he had her physically eight in <i>Aunt Em and Uncle Henry in Oz</i>; and while he portrayed Volkov's Oz as a parallel universe in <i>Farewell to Oz</i> he also showed Volkov's characters living in Baum's Oz in many of his other books, such as <i>Aunt Em and Uncle Henry in Oz</i>. Despite these discrepancies, many of his books are consistent with each other, and characters introduced in some often appear in others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Philip_José_Farmer's_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Philip_Jos.C3.A9_Farmer.27s_Oz">Philip José Farmer's Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Philip José Farmer's Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philip_Jos%C3%A9_Farmer" title="Philip José Farmer">Philip José Farmer</a> portrays a very different Oz in his book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Barnstormer_in_Oz" title="A Barnstormer in Oz">A Barnstormer in Oz</a></i>. The premise is that nothing after the first book occurred—Dorothy never returned to Oz, and instead grew up, got married, and had a son. Her son, Hank Stover, is the main character, a <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> veteran flier and the titular barnstormer. While flying in his <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_JN-4" title="Curtiss JN-4">Curtiss JN-4</a> <a href="/wiki/Biplane" title="Biplane">biplane</a> he enters a green haze and emerges in the civil war-stricken land of Oz.</p>
<p>Farmer portrays the land of Oz as a <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> author, attempting to explain scientifically many of the "magical" elements of Baum's story.</p>
<h3><span id="Robert_A._Heinlein's_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_A._Heinlein.27s_Oz">Robert A. Heinlein's Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Robert A. Heinlein's Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>'s book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Number_of_the_Beast_(novel)" title="The Number of the Beast (novel)">The Number of the Beast</a></i> passes through many famous fictional worlds including those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland" title="Alice in Wonderland">Alice in Wonderland</a>; <a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver's Travels">Gulliver's Travels</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Lilliput_and_Blefuscu" title="Lilliput and Blefuscu">Lilliput</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.E._%22Doc%22_Smith" title='E.E. "Doc" Smith'>E.E. "Doc" Smith</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lensman" title="Lensman">Lensman</a> universe; <a href="/wiki/Asgard" title="Asgard">Asgard</a>, connected to Heinlein's own Future History universe by the Rainbow Bridge for one specific reason in one specific location; and <a href="/wiki/Ringworld" title="Ringworld">Ringworld</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>; as well as some of Heinlein's own works, specifically those set in his Future History through which Lazarus Long moves; and of course the Land of Oz itself.</p>
<p>The Oz portrayed in the book is very close to Baum's Oz, although Heinlein does make an attempt to explain some things from the standpoint of a science fiction author. He explains that Oz is on a retrograde planet, where the direction of rotation relative to the poles is reversed, resulting in the sun seeming to rise in what would normally be the west.</p>
<p>Heinlein also explains that the population remains steady in Oz despite the lack of death because it is impossible for children to be born in Oz. When the population does increase through immigration, Glinda just extends the borders an inch or two in each direction, which makes more than enough space for all additional people.</p>
<h3><span id="L._Sprague_de_Camp's_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="L._Sprague_de_Camp.27s_Oz">L. Sprague de Camp's Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: L. Sprague de Camp's Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/L._Sprague_de_Camp" title="L. Sprague de Camp">L. Sprague de Camp</a>, like Heinlein, brings his own characters to Oz in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Sir_Harold_and_the_Gnome_King" title="Sir Harold and the Gnome King">Sir Harold and the Gnome King</a></i>, part of the originally collaborative <a href="/wiki/Harold_Shea" title="Harold Shea">Harold Shea</a> series. Unlike Heinlein, he does not attempt to explain Oz as science fiction, though he does deviate from the original corpus. He follows Thompson's Oz books, thus using her spelling of "Gnome" and her final fate of the character, but he postulates an incident that has removed the Ozites' immortality, with the result that both Ozma and Dorothy have aged and married by the time his story takes place.</p>
<h3><span id="Tad_Williams'_Otherland_Oz"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tad_Williams.27_Otherland_Oz">Tad Williams' Otherland Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Tad Williams' Otherland Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Otherland" title="Otherland">Otherland</a> series, by <a href="/wiki/Tad_Williams" title="Tad Williams">Tad Williams</a>, a virtual reality version of Oz exists, wherein real-world antagonists play sadistic versions of the roles of the Tin Man, The Scarecrow, and the Cowardly Lion, in a twisted, martial, and post-apocalyptic version of Oz, populated both by characters from the novels, and a large quantity of male and female humans who go by the names "Henry" and "Em" respectively. The humans, <a href="/wiki/Computer_animation" title="Computer animation">computer-generated</a> characters based on the lost minds of children drawn into the Otherland program, look forward to a messianic prophecy foretelling the coming of "The Dorothy," where a child would be born among them.</p>
<h3><span id="The_Outer_Zone_(Tin_Man)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Outer_Zone_.28Tin_Man.29">The Outer Zone (<i>Tin Man</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: The Outer Zone (Tin Man)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sci_Fi_Pictures_original_films" title="Sci Fi Pictures original films">Sci Fi</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_miniseries" title="Television miniseries">television miniseries</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tin_Man_(TV_miniseries)" title="Tin Man (TV miniseries)">Tin Man</a></i> reinvents Oz as the Outer Zone (O.Z.), a parallel universe that was first visited by <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a> during the latter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Era" title="Victorian Era">Victorian Era</a> and is ruled over by her descendants. It is implied, by reference to centuries having elapsed since Dorothy came to the O.Z., that time has progressed at different rates in the O.Z. and "the other side". The re-imagined Oz is described as a place where "the paint has peeled, and what was once the goodness of Oz has become the horrible bleakness of the O.Z."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scenic_design" title="Scenic design">scenic design</a> of the O.Z. features elements of <a href="/wiki/Steampunk" title="Steampunk">steampunk</a>, particularly the "1930's fascist realist" decor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azkadellia" title="Azkadellia">evil sorceress's</a> palace and the computer-generated Central City, analogue of the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_City" title="Emerald City">Emerald City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Once_Upon_a_Time">Once Upon a Time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Once Upon a Time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic">Magic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Magic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Being a fantasy series Oz is rich in magic. In particular, there are many magic items which play an important role in the series.</p>
<h3><span id="Silver_Shoes/Ruby_Slippers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Silver_Shoes.2FRuby_Slippers">Silver Shoes/Ruby Slippers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Silver Shoes/Ruby Slippers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Dorothy leaves Oz after having several adventures there and befriending many of Oz's natives, she is magically carried over the <a href="/wiki/Deadly_Desert" title="Deadly Desert">Deadly Desert</a> by means of the charmed <a href="/wiki/Silver_Shoes" title="Silver Shoes">Silver Shoes</a> she had been given shortly after her unexpected arrival when her farmhouse landed on and killed the previous pair's owner, the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_East" title="Wicked Witch of the East">Wicked Witch of the East</a>. After knocking her heels together three times and wishing to return home, Dorothy is lifted into the air and transported to Kansas. The shoes however, slip off of Dorothy's feet and are lost forever in the desert. Baum states the silver shoes are never recovered. In the 1939 film the shoes are changed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruby_Slippers" title="Ruby Slippers">Ruby Slippers</a>. When Dorothy clicks her heels together she closes her eyes and says: <i>"Theres no place like home</i>". She then wakes up in her bedroom in Kansas believing her experience in Oz to be an elaborate dream.</p>
<p>The silver shoes and ruby slippers are also used in several other versions including <i>Wicked</i> by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Maguire" title="Gregory Maguire">Gregory Maguire</a>. Here the shoes are constructed as a gift and have a chameleon effect. They are decorated with thousands of glittering glass beads that change colors according to the lighting. They can also appear to be several different colors all at once. The shoes are also lost when Dorothy is teleported back home just like in Baum's novel. The Broadway musical based on Maguire's book further shows that they were all silver, but were changed to ruby red by a spell put upon them by the Witch of the West <a href="/wiki/Elphaba" title="Elphaba">Elphaba</a>, enabling her sister the Witch of the East <a href="/wiki/Nessarose" title="Nessarose">Nessarose</a>, who prior had been confined to a wheelchair, to magically walk.</p>
<p>A little-known adaptation of the original story made for British television in the mid-90's starring <a href="/wiki/Denise_van_Outen" title="Denise van Outen">Denise van Outen</a> explained that they had belonged to a visitor from over the rainbow who came to Oz before Dorothy and they were obtained by the Witch when the visitor wished herself home and they fell off her feet on the return trip.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powder_of_Life">Powder of Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Powder of Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Powder of Life</b> is a magic substance from the book series, which first appears in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i>.</p>
<p>It is a magical powder that brings inanimate objects to life. The <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witch</a> <a href="/wiki/Mombi" title="Mombi">Mombi</a> first obtained it from a "crooked magician." Later in the series it is revealed that the substance is made by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._Pipt_(Oz)" title="Dr. Pipt (Oz)">Dr. Pipt</a>. In order to make the substance, Dr. Pipt had to stir four large <a href="/wiki/Cauldron" title="Cauldron">cauldrons</a> for six years. Only a few grains of the powder could be made at a time. It is always described as being carried in a <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">pepper</a> box.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marvelous_Land_of_Oz" title="The Marvelous Land of Oz">The Marvelous Land of Oz</a></i>, the Powder of Life was used to bring Jack Pumpkinhead, the Sawhorse, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gump_(Oz)" title="The Gump (Oz)">the Gump</a> to life. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Patchwork_Girl_of_Oz" title="The Patchwork Girl of Oz">The Patchwork Girl of Oz</a></i> it brings the title character to life, also the glass cat and a phonograph.</p>
<p>Mombi's shaker also contained three "wishing pills" fabricated by Dr. Nikidik.</p>
<p>The Powder has been used by Volkov in his series. There, it is produced from a certain plant of such viability that the smallest piece can grow into a plant within a day, on any surface except for solid metal. However, if it is sun dried on such a surface, it turns into the Powder of Life. No incantation is required to make the powder work. The second book of the series is centered around a man who animates an army of wooden soldiers with the Powder and uses them for conquering the Magic Land.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_Oz" title="Return to Oz">Return to Oz</a></i>, the magic words to bring the inanimate object to life were "WEAUGH, TEAUGH, PEAUGH".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic_Belt">Magic Belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Magic Belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Magic Belt</b> is first introduced in <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>. Originally the belt belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Nome_King" title="Nome King">Nome King</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a> stole it and defeated him. When she leaves Oz, she gives it to <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Ozma</a> for safekeeping.</p>
<p>In most Oz books, the Magic Belt grants its wearer the ability to transform anyone into any form, and the ability to transport anyone anywhere, and also makes its wearer impervious to harm. In some books, it also grants limited wishes.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>, its power is limited: its magic cannot affect objects which are made of wood.</p>
<p>In non-canonical <i><a href="/wiki/The_Oz_Kids" title="The Oz Kids">The Oz Kids</a></i> animated series, the Magic Belt belonged to Dot.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic_Picture">Magic Picture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Magic Picture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Ozma's boudoir hangs a picture in a radium frame. This picture usually appears to be of a pleasant countryside, but when anyone wishes for the picture to show a particular person or place, the scene will display what is wished for. Sometimes the onlooker is able to hear sounds from the scene within the Magic Picture and sometimes an additional device is necessary to transmit sound.</p>
<p>A similar device is present in Volkov's series. There, it is given as a present to the Scarecrow by the <a href="/wiki/Glinda_the_Good_Witch" title="Glinda the Good Witch">Good Witch of the South</a>. It is a box of pink wood with a thick frosted glass screen. The device is password activated, and limited in range to the Magic Land (with the exception of deep underground caverns and certain types of magical interference). The box is shown to be virtually indestructible; it withstood repeated abuse from a villain attempting to use it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Book_of_Records">Great Book of Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Great Book of Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Great Book of Records" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a>.</div>
<p>Glinda's <b>Great Book of Records</b> is introduced in Chapter 29 of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i>: " 'It is a record of everything that happens,' replied the Sorceress. 'As soon as an event takes place, anywhere in the world, it is immediately found printed in my Magic Book. So when I read its pages I am well informed.' " The Book proves useful in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scarecrow_of_Oz" title="The Scarecrow of Oz">The Scarecrow of Oz</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Glinda_of_Oz" title="Glinda of Oz">Glinda of Oz</a></i>; and it recurs in many of the stories of Baum's successors and imitators. It is one of the prime magic devices of Oz; villains steal it when they can (as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Princess_of_Oz" title="The Lost Princess of Oz">The Lost Princess of Oz</a></i> or in <i><a href="/wiki/Handy_Mandy_in_Oz" title="Handy Mandy in Oz">Handy Mandy in Oz</a></i>). Since it covers the planet and not merely Oz, the Book's entries are compressed and sometimes cryptic, and difficult to decipher (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Paradox_in_Oz" title="Paradox in Oz">Paradox in Oz</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Ann_in_Oz" title="Queen Ann in Oz">Queen Ann in Oz</a></i>).</p>
<p>The book is also featured in fantasy series Once Upon a Time. Zelena reads the book ignoring Glinda's warning and turns green again because she felt betrayed as it was mentioned in the book that Dorothy would save Oz from a great evil (Zelena thought that evil was her but this was never revealed).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Love_Magnet">The Love Magnet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: The Love Magnet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A rusty-looking horseshoe magnet which causes everyone to love its owner. It is closely associated with the <a href="/wiki/Shaggy_Man" title="Shaggy Man">Shaggy Man</a>. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Road_to_Oz" title="The Road to Oz">The Road to Oz</a></i>, he finds that being loved by everyone can be inconvenient. In <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i> he reveals that Ozma has modified its powers so that it only works when it is displayed and affects only the feelings of those who see it. It is an essential plot-element in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shaggy_Man_of_Oz" title="The Shaggy Man of Oz">The Shaggy Man of Oz</a></i>. Ozma keeps it hanging over the gate into the Emerald City, so that all who enter will come with love, although this does not always seem to happen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic_Fan">Magic Fan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Magic Fan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Magic Fan is brought to Oz by Dorothy in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Book_of_Oz" title="The Royal Book of Oz">The Royal Book of Oz</a></i>. Several subsequent books mention it. It creates a powerful wind, capable of blowing away an invading army.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fountain_of_Oblivion">Fountain of Oblivion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Fountain of Oblivion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A fountain in the Emerald City, anyone who drinks from it forgets everything he knows, including his own name. It is used against the Nome King in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i> and again in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_of_Oz" title="The Magic of Oz">The Magic of Oz</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic_Dinner_Bell">Magic Dinner Bell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Magic Dinner Bell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Created by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jinnicky" title="Jinnicky">Red Jinn</a>, it summons a slave named Ginger, who appears bearing a tray full of delicious food when the bell is rung. Besides providing food, the bell also provides a means of escape from danger: anyone who holds onto the slave when he disappears after bringing the food is transported with him to the Red Jinn's castle. There are actually two magic dinner bells, one in the Emerald City and another which the Red Jinn keeps for himself and uses while traveling. The bell is first introduced in <i><a href="/wiki/Jack_Pumpkinhead_of_Oz" title="Jack Pumpkinhead of Oz">Jack Pumpkinhead of Oz</a></i> and also appears in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Purple_Prince_of_Oz" title="The Purple Prince of Oz">The Purple Prince of Oz</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silver_Princess_in_Oz" title="The Silver Princess in Oz">The Silver Princess in Oz</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous">Miscellaneous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_in_Oz">Death in Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Death in Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the later Oz books, no one can die. One of the books assures us that while you are in the Land of Oz, you cannot die. Unfortunately, this information comes <i>after</i> characters in the books have been chopped into pieces, beheaded, melted, and so forth and it is mentioned that you could be transformed into an inanimate object, turned into sand, and buried. Even so, you would still be alive and presumably conscious.</p>
<p>Baum puts it this way in the third chapter of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emerald_City_of_Oz" title="The Emerald City of Oz">The Emerald City of Oz</a></i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"No disease of any sort was ever known among the Ozites, and so no one ever died unless he met with an accident that prevented him from living."</dd>
</dl>
<p>Note also the spell which caused this also prevented aging, and took effect on everyone in Oz at the same time; this means that any babies in Oz are eternally babies, and that anyone who was at the moment of death is permanently caught there, and so on.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Death is treated inconsistently; in some books it is said that it is impossible to die, in others, people die. Problematically, the plot often depends on something either dying, or not being killable.</p>
<p><i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> shows an early example of the problem: although the Tin Woodman does not die when his limbs and head are severed, the two wicked witches are killed (The Wicked Witch of the East could be explained that she was simply crushed and not really dead, but couldn't do anything anyway, and in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Oz_Odyssey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Oz Odyssey (page does not exist)">The Oz Odyssey</a></i>, the Wicked Witch of the West comes back to life.) One theory brought forth as to why the Tin Woodman does not die is discussed in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Einhorn" title="Edward Einhorn">Edward Einhorn</a>'s book "<a href="/wiki/Paradox_in_Oz" title="Paradox in Oz">Paradox in Oz</a>", where King Oz says that the Tin Woodman (then Nick Chopper) would have died were it not for his time magic. When the Tin Woodman rescues the Queen of the Field Mice by chopping off the head of a pursuing wildcat, it seems unlikely the cat's unjoined head and body continue to live independently of each other, although this goes unmentioned. Again, although the Tin Woodman survived losing all his body, prior to that, he had grown up and lost his parents in a manner inconsistent with later descriptions of Oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Again, in <i><a href="/wiki/Ozma_of_Oz" title="Ozma of Oz">Ozma of Oz</a></i>, Jack Pumpkinhead was described as "a little overripe", and in <i><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_and_the_Wizard_in_Oz" title="Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz">Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz</a></i>, he does not appear at all, although all the other characters do reappear; the implication is that he spoiled, as he feared from his creation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> It is unlikely, however, since, according to the <a href="/wiki/The_Road_to_Oz" title="The Road to Oz">previous book</a>, while a pumpkin which serves as Jack's head can spoil, it can be replaced, which was done several times without a problem.</p>
<p>Both <i>Ozma of Oz</i> and <i>Tik-Tok of Oz</i> describe trees with meat growing on them, so it is possible that no animal was killed for most of the meat eaten in Oz. However, in <i>Tin Woodman of Oz</i> a hungry Jaguar tries to eat a live monkey, suggesting that occasionally (among non-human animals, at least) animal flesh is preferred to that of plants.</p>
<p>Another of Baum's attempts to explain death in Oz is the following passage from <i>The Emerald City of Oz</i>.</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>No disease of any sort was ever known among the Ozites, and no one ever died unless he met with an accident that prevented him from living.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>This passage has been translated by some fans to mean that one ceases to live if one's body is damaged to the extent that it cannot be repaired. However, in <i>Tik-Tok of Oz</i> Baum suggested that Oz people could go on living after being eaten and digested, and also that Nomes would continue to live after being cut into tiny pieces, which disproves the destruction theory.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Talking_animals">Talking animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Talking animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Oz, animals such as the Cowardly Lion and the Hungry Tiger can talk, and all native animals appear to be capable of speech. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked:_The_Life_and_Times_of_the_Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West</a></i> emphasized the difference between Animals and animals. Animals (capitalized) are sentient beings that can talk. Several theories exist as to how animals gained the gift of speech.</p>
<p>The treatment of non-native animals was inconsistent. In the first book, the <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toto_(dog)" title="Toto (dog)">Toto</a> never speaks, although brought to Oz; in <i>The Patchwork Girl of Oz</i>, Dorothy specifies that he cannot speak because he is not a fairy dog. However, in <i>Ozma of Oz</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a> <a href="/wiki/Billina" title="Billina">Billina</a> acquires speech merely by being swept to the lands near Oz, and in <i><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_and_the_Wizard_in_Oz" title="Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz">Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz</a></i>, the same is true of the kitten Eureka and the cab <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a> Jim when reaching the land of Mangaboos, a similarly magical land. In <i><a href="/wiki/Tik-Tok_of_Oz" title="Tik-Tok of Oz">Tik-Tok of Oz</a></i>, Baum restored the <a href="/wiki/Continuity_(fiction)" title="Continuity (fiction)">continuity</a>: Toto can speak, and always could, but never bothered to, because it was unnecessary.</p>
<p>An additional inconsistency is introduced with <i>Tik-Tok of Oz</i>: Hank the Mule cannot speak until reaching the Land of Oz, although he lands on the shore of Ev first, where Billina the chicken gained the ability to speak. This might be because <i>Tik-Tok of Oz</i> was originally a stageplay version of <i>Ozma of Oz</i>; Dorothy was replaced by Betsy because he had sold the stage rights for Dorothy, and Billina was replaced by Hank because a mule could more convincingly be played by two people in a costume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Hank probably could not talk because Baum already had two speaking comedy characters, the Shaggy Man and Tik-Tok. Thus Hank would fill a better niche as a visual comedy character, in the tradition of British pantomime. The part of Hank was also an analog to the part of Dorothy's cow Imogene, Toto's replacement on stage in the immensely successful 1903 Broadway version of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_stage_play)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1902 stage play)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i>, a success that Baum sought to duplicate for the rest of his life.</p>
<p>There is one small kingdom in Oz where animals are unable to talk: Corumbia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_name_Oz">Origin of the name Oz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_of_Oz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Origin of the name Oz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A legend of uncertain validity is that when relating bedtime stories (the earliest form of the Oz books) Baum was asked by his niece, Ramona Baxter Bowden, the name of the magical land. He glanced at a nearby filing cabinet, which had three drawers, labeled A–G, H–N, and O–Z. Thus he named the land Oz. This story was first told in 1903, but his wife always insisted that the part about the filing cabinet was not true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated8_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated8-61">[61]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_and_the_Wizard_in_Oz" title="Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz">Dorothy and the Wizard in Oz</a></i>, the name is translated as "great and good". It has also been speculated that Oz was named after the abbreviation for <a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">ounce</a>, in the theory that Oz is an allegory for the populist struggle against the illusion (the wizard) of the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Others have said that Oz stands for <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, since the letters of the alphabet before O and Z are N and Y respectively. However, this works just as well with Oz standing for <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, because the letters following O and Z are P and A (starting again at the beginning of the alphabet).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (July 2011)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Several of Baum's fairy stories that take place in the United States were situated on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozark_Plateau" title="Ozark Plateau">Ozark Plateau</a>, and the similarity of name may not be a coincidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Oz</i> is a common vernacular contraction of <i><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a></i> (Australia—Aussie—Aus—Oz). Australia is a large continent predominated by desert regions, with pockets of intense green tropical, sub-tropical and sub-alpine greenlands and rainforests. It is quite possible that Baum took the popular nickname of Australia as the national name for his fictional world. Also note that many fans place Oz in the South Pacific, see <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Oz#Location" title="Land of Oz">Location</a> above. However, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_English_dictionary" title="Oxford English dictionary">Oxford English dictionary</a>, the first references to Australia by this name were made in 1902—after the first book had been published. Nevertheless, spoken references would certainly have been in use before first appearing in print (as noted by Oxford), hence possibly before the writing of the book.</p>
<p>It is also possible that it is based on the "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93g&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Óg (page does not exist)">Óg</a>" in <a href="/wiki/T%C3%ADr_na_n%C3%93g" title="Tír na nÓg">Tír na nÓg</a>. While no direct link between the mythology of Oz and the realm of Irish folklore has been conclusively proven, certain similarities, for instance <a href="/wiki/Ogma" title="Ogma">Ogma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Princess Ozma</a>, are compelling.</p>
