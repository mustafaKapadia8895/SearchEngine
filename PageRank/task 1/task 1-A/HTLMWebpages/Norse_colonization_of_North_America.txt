<p>The <b>Norse colonization of North America</b> began in the late 10th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">AD</a> when <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> explored and settled areas of the North <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> including the northeastern fringes of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Viking houses were found at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%E2%80%99Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L’Anse aux Meadows">L’Anse aux Meadows</a> near the northern tip of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> in 1960. This discovery aided the reignition of archaeological exploration for the Vikings in the north Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norse colony in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> lasted for almost 500 years. Continental North American settlements were small and did not develop into permanent colonies. While voyages, for example to collect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>, are likely to have occurred for some time, there is no evidence of any lasting Norse settlements on mainland North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse_Greenland">Norse Greenland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Norse Greenland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Greenland" title="History of Greenland">History of Greenland</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eastern-settlement-eng.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="557" data-file-width="638" height="393" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Eastern-settlement-eng.png/450px-Eastern-settlement-eng.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Eastern-settlement-eng.png 1.5x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eastern-settlement-eng.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map of the Eastern Settlement on Greenland, covering approximately the modern municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Qaqortoq" title="Qaqortoq">Qaqortoq</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narssaq" title="Narssaq">Narssaq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanortalik" title="Nanortalik">Nanortalik</a>. Eiriksfjord (Erik's fjord) and his farm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brattahlid" title="Brattahlid">Brattahlid</a> are shown, as is the location of the bishopric at Gardar.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Sagas_of_Icelanders" title="Sagas of Icelanders">Sagas of Icelanders</a>, Norsemen from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> first settled Greenland in the 980s. There is no special reason to doubt the authority of the information that the sagas supply regarding the very beginning of the settlement, but they cannot be treated as primary evidence for the history of Norse Greenland because they embody the literary preoccupations of writers and audiences in medieval Iceland that are not always reliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Erik_the_Red" title="Erik the Red">Erik the Red</a> (Old Norse: Eiríkr rauði), having been banished from Iceland for <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">manslaughter</a>, explored the uninhabited southwestern coast of Greenland during the three years of his banishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He made plans to entice settlers to the area, naming it Greenland on the assumption that "people would be more eager to go there because the land had a good name".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The inner reaches of one long <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a>, named <i>Eiriksfjord</i> after him, was where he eventually established his estate <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brattahlid" title="Brattahlid">Brattahlid</a></i>. He issued tracts of land to his followers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NorseExplorations-v2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="839" data-file-width="786" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/NorseExplorations-v2.png/220px-NorseExplorations-v2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/NorseExplorations-v2.png/330px-NorseExplorations-v2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/NorseExplorations-v2.png/440px-NorseExplorations-v2.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NorseExplorations-v2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing the extent of the Norse world</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At its peak the colony consisted of two settlements. The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Settlement" title="Eastern Settlement">Eastern</a> was at the southwestern tip of Greenland, while the <a href="/wiki/Western_Settlement" title="Western Settlement">Western Settlement</a> was about 500 km up the west coast, inland from present-day <a href="/wiki/Nuuk" title="Nuuk">Nuuk</a>. A smaller settlement near the Eastern Settlement is sometimes considered the <a href="/wiki/Ivittuut" title="Ivittuut">Middle Settlement</a>. The combined population was around 2,000–3,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At least 400 farms have been identified by archaeologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup> Norse Greenland had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric" title="Bishopric">bishopric</a> (at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gar%C3%B0ar_(Greenland)" title="Garðar (Greenland)">Garðar</a>) and exported walrus <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>, furs, rope, sheep, whale or seal <a href="/wiki/Blubber" title="Blubber">blubber</a>, live animals such as <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a>, supposed "unicorn horns" (in reality <a href="/wiki/Narwhal#In_legend" title="Narwhal">narwhal tusks</a>), and cattle hides. In 1126, the population requested a Bishop (headquartered at Garðar), and in 1261, they accepted the overlordship of the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> King. They continued to have their own law and became almost completely independent after 1349, the time of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>. In 1380, the Norwegian Kingdom entered into <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">a personal union</a> with the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TVA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_trade_and_decline">Western trade and decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Western trade and decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is evidence of Norse trade with the natives (called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skraelings" title="Skraelings">Skraelings</a> by the Vikings). The Norse would have encountered both <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a>, related to the Algonquin) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thule_People" title="Thule People">Thule</a>, the ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dorset_culture" title="Dorset culture">Dorset</a> had withdrawn from Greenland before the Norse settlement of the island. Items such as <a href="/wiki/Comb" title="Comb">comb</a> fragments, pieces of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> cooking utensils and chisels, <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> pieces, ship <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivets" title="Rivets">rivets</a>, carpenter's planes, and oaken ship fragments used in Inuit boats have been found far beyond the traditional range of Norse colonization. A small <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> statue that appears to represent a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">European</a> has also been found among the ruins of an Inuit community house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVA_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TVA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colony began to decline in the 14th century. The Western Settlement was abandoned around 1350, and the last bishop at Garðar died in 1377.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVA_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-TVA-10">[10]</a></sup> After a <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> was recorded in 1408, no written records mention the settlers. It is probable that the Eastern Settlement was defunct by the late 15th century. The most recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiocarbon" title="Radiocarbon">radiocarbon</a> date found in Norse settlements as of 2002 was 1430 (±15 years). Several theories have been advanced to explain the decline.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> of this period would have made travel between Greenland and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, as well as farming, more difficult; although fishing and seal hunting provided a healthy diet, there was more prestige in cattle farming, and there was increased availability of farms in Scandinavian countries depopulated by famine and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Plague" title="Black Plague">plague</a> epidemics. In addition, Greenlandic ivory may have been supplanted in European markets by cheaper ivory from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.spiegel.de_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.spiegel.de-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite the loss of contact with the Greenlanders, the Norwegian-Danish <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">crown</a> continued to consider Greenland a possession.</p>
<p>Not knowing whether the old Norse civilization remained in Greenland or not—and worried that if it did, it would still be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> 200 years after the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> homelands had experienced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>—a joint merchant-clerical expedition led by the <a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">Dano-Norwegian</a> missionary <a href="/wiki/Hans_Egede" title="Hans Egede">Hans Egede</a> was sent to Greenland in 1721. Though this expedition found no surviving Europeans, it marked the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Danish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Danish colonization of the Americas">Denmark's re-assertion of sovereignty</a> over the island.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vinland">Vinland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vinland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Point_Rosee" title="Point Rosee">Point Rosee</a></div>

<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_sagas" title="Icelandic sagas">Icelandic sagas</a>—<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eirik_the_Red%27s_Saga" title="Eirik the Red's Saga">Eirik the Red's Saga</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saga_of_the_Greenlanders" title="Saga of the Greenlanders">Saga of the Greenlanders</a></i>, plus chapters of the <i><a href="/wiki/Hauksb%C3%B3k" title="Hauksbók">Hauksbók</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatey_Book" title="Flatey Book">Flatey Book</a></i>—the Norse started to explore lands to the west of Greenland only a few years after the Greenland settlements were established. In 985 while sailing from Iceland to Greenland with a migration fleet consisting of 400–700 settlers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NM2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NM2-13">[13]</a></sup> and 25 other ships (14 of which completed the journey), a merchant named <a href="/wiki/Bjarni_Herj%C3%B3lfsson" title="Bjarni Herjólfsson">Bjarni Herjólfsson</a> was blown off course, and after three days' sailing he sighted land west of the fleet. Bjarni was only interested in finding his father's farm, but he described his discovery to <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">Leif Erikson</a> who explored the area in more detail and planted a small settlement fifteen years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sagas describe three separate areas discovered during this exploration: <a href="/wiki/Helluland" title="Helluland">Helluland</a>, which means "land of the flat stones"; <a href="/wiki/Markland" title="Markland">Markland</a>, "the land of forests", definitely of interest to settlers in Greenland where there were few trees; and <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, "the land of wine", found somewhere south of Markland. It was in Vinland that the settlement described in the sagas was founded.</p>
<p>Three of <a href="/wiki/Erik_the_Red" title="Erik the Red">Erik the Red</a>'s children visited the North American continent: his sons Leif and Thorvald, and their sister (or half-sister) Freydis. Thorvald died there.</p>

<h3><span id="Leif's_winter_camp"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Leif.27s_winter_camp">Leif's winter camp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leif's winter camp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:343px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="398" data-file-width="341" height="398" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png" width="341"/></a>

The <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1lholt#Sk.C3.A1lholt_Map" title="Skálholt">Skálholt Map</a></div>

<p>Using the routes, landmarks, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents</a>, rocks, and winds that Bjarni had described to him, Leif sailed some 1,800 miles to the west of the land, with a crew of 35—sailing the same <a href="/wiki/Knarr" title="Knarr">knarr</a> Bjarni had used to make the voyage. He described Helluland as "level and wooded, with broad white beaches wherever they went and a gently sloping shoreline."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup> Leif and others had wanted his father, Erik the Red, to lead this expedition and talked him into it. However, as Erik attempted to join his son Leif on the voyage towards these new lands, he fell off his horse as it slipped on the wet rocks near the shore; thus he was injured and stayed behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leif wintered in 1001, probably near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bauld" title="Cape Bauld">Cape Bauld</a> on the northern tip of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, where one day his foster father <a href="/wiki/Tyrker" title="Tyrker">Tyrker</a> was found drunk, on what the saga describes as "wine-berries." <a href="/wiki/Viburnum_edule" title="Viburnum edule">Squashberries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gooseberries" title="Gooseberries">gooseberries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberries</a> all grew wild in the area. There are varying explanations for Leif apparently describing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermented berries</a> as "wine."</p>
<p>Leif spent another winter at "Leifsbúðir" without conflict, and sailed back to <a href="/wiki/Brattahl%C3%AD%C3%B0" title="Brattahlíð">Brattahlíð</a> in Greenland to assume filial duties to his father.</p>
<h3><span id="Thorvald's_voyage_(1004_AD)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thorvald.27s_voyage_.281004_AD.29">Thorvald's voyage (1004 AD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thorvald's voyage (1004 AD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1004, Leif's brother <a href="/wiki/Thorvald_Eiriksson" title="Thorvald Eiriksson">Thorvald Eiriksson</a> sailed with a crew of 30 men to Vinland and spent the following winter at Leif's camp. In the spring, Thorvald attacked nine of the local people who were sleeping under three skin-covered <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoes</a>. The ninth victim escaped and soon came back to the Norse camp with a force. Thorvald was killed by an arrow that succeeded in passing through the <a href="/wiki/Barricade" title="Barricade">barricade</a>. Although brief hostilities ensued, the Norse explorers stayed another winter and left the following spring. Subsequently, another of Leif's brothers, Thorstein, sailed to the New World to retrieve his dead brother's body, but he died before leaving Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROBW_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-ROBW-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span id="Karlsefni's_expedition_(1009_AD)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Karlsefni.27s_expedition_.281009_AD.29">Karlsefni's expedition (1009 AD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Karlsefni's expedition (1009 AD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1009 <a href="/wiki/Thorfinn_Karlsefni" title="Thorfinn Karlsefni">Thorfinn Karlsefni</a>, also known as "Thorfinn the Valiant", supplied three <a href="/wiki/Knarr" title="Knarr">ships</a> with livestock and 160 men and women<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NM2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NM2-13">[13]</a></sup> (although another source sets the number of settlers at 250). After a cruel winter, he headed south and landed at <a href="/wiki/Straumfj%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Straumfjörð"><i>Straumfjord</i></a>. He later moved to <i>Straumsöy</i>, possibly because the current was stronger there. A sign of peaceful relations between the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples</a> and the Norsemen is noted here. The two sides <a href="/wiki/Barter" title="Barter">bartered</a> with furs and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_gray_squirrel" title="Eastern gray squirrel">gray squirrel</a> skins for <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> and red cloth, which the natives tied around their heads as a sort of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headdress" title="Headdress">headdress</a>.</p>
<p>There are conflicting stories but one account states that a bull belonging to Karlsefni came storming out of the wood, so frightening the natives that they ran to their skin-boats and rowed away. They returned three days later, in force. The natives used catapults, hoisting "a large sphere on a pole; it was dark blue in color" and about the size of a sheep's belly,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VS_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-VS-14">[14]</a></sup> which flew over the heads of the men and made an ugly din.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VS_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-VS-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norsemen retreated. Leif Erikson's half-sister <a href="/wiki/Freyd%C3%ADs_Eir%C3%ADksd%C3%B3ttir" title="Freydís Eiríksdóttir">Freydís Eiríksdóttir</a> was pregnant and unable to keep up with the retreating Norsemen. She called out to them to stop fleeing from "such pitiful wretches", adding that if she had weapons, she could do better than that. Freydís seized the sword belonging to a man who had been killed by the natives. She pulled one of her breasts out of her bodice and struck it with the sword, frightening the natives, who fled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VS_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-VS-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudohistory">Pseudohistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pseudohistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Purported <a href="/wiki/Runestone" title="Runestone">runestones</a> have been found in North America, most famously the <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Runestone" title="Kensington Runestone">Kensington Runestone</a>. These are generally considered to be hoaxes or misinterpretations of <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph#North_America" title="Petroglyph">Native American petroglyphs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many claims of Norse colonization in New England, none well-founded.</p>
<p>Monuments claimed to be Norse include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Newport_Tower_(Rhode_Island)" title="Newport Tower (Rhode Island)">Stone Tower</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroglyphs" title="Petroglyphs">petroglyphs</a> on <a href="/wiki/Dighton_Rock" title="Dighton Rock">Dighton Rock</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Taunton_River" title="Taunton River">Taunton River</a> in Massachusetts</li>
<li>The runes on <a href="/wiki/Narragansett_Runestone" title="Narragansett Runestone">Narragansett Runestone</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="Horsford's_Norumbega"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Horsford.27s_Norumbega">Horsford's Norumbega</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Horsford's Norumbega">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eben_Norton_Horsford#Viking" title="Eben Norton Horsford">Eben Norton Horsford § Viking</a></div>
<p>The nineteenth-century Harvard chemist <a href="/wiki/Eben_Norton_Horsford" title="Eben Norton Horsford">Eben Norton Horsford</a> connected the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River Basin</a> to places described in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_saga" title="Norse saga">Norse sagas</a> and elsewhere, notably <a href="/wiki/Norumbega" title="Norumbega">Norumbega</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fleming_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fleming-17">[17]</a></sup> He published several books on the topic and had plaques, monuments, and statues erected in honor of the Norse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His work received little support from mainstream historians and archeologists at the time, and even less today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope.com_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-straightdope.com-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duration_of_Norse_contact">Duration of Norse contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Duration of Norse contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Settlements in continental North America aimed to exploit natural resources such as furs and in particular lumber, which was in short supply in Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It is unclear why the short-term settlements did not become permanent, though it was likely in part because of hostile relations with the indigenous peoples, referred to as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skr%C3%A6lings" title="Skrælings">Skrælings</a></i> by the Norse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Nevertheless, it appears that sporadic voyages to Markland for forages, timber, and trade with the locals could have lasted as long as 400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence of continuing trips includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maine_Penny" title="Maine Penny">Maine Penny</a>, a Norwegian coin from King <a href="/wiki/Olaf_III_of_Norway" title="Olaf III of Norway">Olaf Kyrre</a>'s reign (1067–1093) allegedly found in a Native American archaeological site in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, suggesting an exchange between the Norse and the Native Americans late in or after the 11th century; and an entry in the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Annals" title="Icelandic Annals">Icelandic Annals</a> from 1347 which refers to a small Greenlandic vessel with a crew of eighteen that arrived in Iceland while attempting to return to Greenland from Markland with a load of timber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norse_colonization_of_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For centuries it remained unclear whether the Icelandic stories represented real voyages by the Norse to North America. The sagas first gained serious historic respectability when, in 1837, the Danish antiquarian <a href="/wiki/Carl_Christian_Rafn" title="Carl Christian Rafn">Carl Christian Rafn</a> pointed out the possibility for a Norse settlement in, or voyages to, North America. North America, by the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winland" title="Winland">Winland</a>, first appeared in written sources in a work by <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a> from approximately 1075. The most important works about North America and the early Norse activities there, namely the <a href="/wiki/Sagas_of_Icelanders" title="Sagas of Icelanders">Sagas of Icelanders</a>, first reached written form only in the 13th and 14th centuries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Evidence of Norse west of Greenland came in the 1960s when archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Anne_Stine_Ingstad" title="Anne Stine Ingstad">Anne Stine Ingstad</a> and her husband, outdoorsman and author <a href="/wiki/Helge_Ingstad" title="Helge Ingstad">Helge Ingstad</a>, excavated a Norse settlement at <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L'Anse aux Meadows">L'Anse aux Meadows</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>. The location of the various lands described in the sagas remains unclear, however. Many historians identify Helluland with <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a> and Markland with <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a>. The location of Vinland poses a thornier question. Most believe that the L'Anse aux Meadows settlement represents the Vinland settlement described in the sagas; others argue that the sagas depict Vinland as warmer than Newfoundland and therefore lying farther south.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2012 Canadian researchers identified possible signs of Norse outposts in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nanook,_Baffin_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nanook, Baffin Island (page does not exist)">Nanook</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tanfield_Valley" title="Tanfield Valley">Tanfield Valley</a> on <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>, as well as on Nunguvik, Willows Island and <a href="/wiki/Avayalik" title="Avayalik">Avayalik</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Unusual fabric cordage found on Baffin Island in the 1980s and stored at the Canadian Museum of Civilization was identified in 1999 as possibly of Norse manufacture; that discovery led to more in-depth exploration of the Tanfield Valley archaeological site.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Archeological findings in 2015 at <a href="/wiki/Point_Rosee" title="Point Rosee">Point Rosee</a>, on the southwest coast of Newfoundland, reveal evidence of the location being a <a href="/wiki/Bog_iron" title="Bog iron">bog iron</a>-smelting site and therefore a possible second 10th century Viking settlement in Canada. The possible settlement was initially discovered through <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> readings and archaeologists have begun excavating the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1lholt#Sk.C3.A1lholt_Map" title="Skálholt">Skálholt Map</a>, made by an Icelandic teacher in 1570, depicts part of north-eastern North America mentioning Helluland, Markland and Vinland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
