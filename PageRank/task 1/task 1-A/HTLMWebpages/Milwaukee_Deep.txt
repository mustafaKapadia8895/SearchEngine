<p><b>Milwaukee Deep</b>, also known as The Milwaukee Depth, (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Milwaukee_Deep&amp;params=19_35_N_66_30_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">19°35′N</span> <span class="longitude">66°30′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">19.583°N 66.500°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">19.583; -66.500</span></span></span></a></span>) is the deepest part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucsd1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucsd1-1">[1]</a></sup> It has a maximum depth of at least 27,493 feet (8,380 m). It is just 76.0 miles (122.3 km) north of the coast of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> at <i>Punto Palmas Altas</i> in <a href="/wiki/Manat%C3%AD,_Puerto_Rico" title="Manatí, Puerto Rico">Manatí</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mit1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>This ocean floor feature is named for the <a href="/wiki/USS_Milwaukee_(CL-5)" title="USS Milwaukee (CL-5)">USS <i>Milwaukee</i> (CL-5)</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omaha_class_cruiser" title="Omaha class cruiser">Omaha class cruiser</a>, which discovered the Milwaukee Deep on February 14, 1939 with a reading of 28,680 feet (8,740 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucsd2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucsd2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWII_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWII-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 19, 1952, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" title="United States Fish and Wildlife Service">U.S. Fish and Wildlife</a> vessel <i>Theodore N. Gill</i> obtained a reading of 28,560 feet (8,710 m) at (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Milwaukee_Deep&amp;params=19_36_N_68_19_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">19°36′N</span> <span class="longitude">68°19′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">19.600°N 68.317°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">19.600; -68.317</span></span></span></a></span>), virtually identical with the <i>Milwaukee</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s reading.</p>
<p>The existence of deep water to the Atlantic Ocean side of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> has been known for more than a century. One of the area's earliest soundings was obtained June 12, 1852 by Lt. S. P. Lee, U.S. Navy <a href="/wiki/Brig" title="Brig">brig</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Dolphin_(1836)" title="USS Dolphin (1836)"><i>Dolphin</i></a>, with a reading of 22,950 feet (7,000 m) at (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Milwaukee_Deep&amp;params=26_32_N_60_06_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">26°32′N</span> <span class="longitude">60°06′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">26.533°N 60.100°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">26.533; -60.100</span></span></span></a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
