<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Manganese nodule</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Polymetallic_nodules&amp;redirect=no" title="Polymetallic nodules">Polymetallic nodules</a>)</span></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Polymetallic nodules</b>, also called <b>manganese nodules</b>, are rock <a href="/wiki/Concretion" title="Concretion">concretions</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> bottom formed of concentric layers of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxides</a> around a core. The core may be microscopically small and is sometimes completely transformed into manganese minerals by <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallization</a>. When visible to the naked eye, it can be a small test (shell) of a microfossil (<a href="/wiki/Radiolaria" title="Radiolaria">radiolarian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifer</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Phosphatic_fossilization" title="Phosphatic fossilization">phosphatized</a> <a href="/wiki/Shark_tooth" title="Shark tooth">shark tooth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> debris or even fragments of earlier nodules. As nodules can be found in vast quantities, and contain valuable metals, deposits were identified as having economic interest in the 1960s by John Mero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Nodules vary in size from tiny particles visible only under a <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a> to large pellets more than 20 centimetres (8 in) across. However, most nodules are between 3 and 10 cm (1 and 4 in) in diameter, about the size of hens <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>. Their surface textures vary from smooth to rough. They frequently have <a href="/wiki/Botryoidal" title="Botryoidal">botryoidal</a> (mammilated or knobby) texture and vary from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical" title="Spherical">spherical</a> in shape to typically oblate, sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate" title="Prolate">prolate</a>, or are otherwise irregular. The bottom surface, buried in sediment, is generally rougher than the top due to a different type of growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isa2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-isa2010-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_nodule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nodules lie on the <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a> sediment, often partly or completely buried. They vary greatly in abundance, in some cases touching one another and covering more than 70% of the sea floor. The total amount of polymetallic nodules on the sea floor was estimated at 500 billion tons by Alan A. Archer of the London <a href="/wiki/Geological_Museum" title="Geological Museum">Geological Museum</a> in 1981.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Polymetallic nodules are found in both shallow (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) and deeper waters (e.g. the central Pacific), even in lakes,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and are thought to have been a feature of the seas and oceans at least since the deep oceans oxidised in the <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a> period over 540 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Polymetallic nodules were discovered in 1868 in the <a href="/wiki/Kara_Sea" title="Kara Sea">Kara Sea</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. During the scientific expeditions of the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Challenger_(1858)" title="HMS Challenger (1858)">HMS Challenger</a> (1872–1876), they were found to occur in most oceans of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their composition varies by location, and sizeable deposits been found in four areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>Penrhyn Basin near within the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>North central <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> in a region called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarion_Clipperton_Zone" title="Clarion Clipperton Zone">Clarion Clipperton Zone</a> (CCZ) roughly midway between <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clipperton_Island" title="Clipperton Island">Clipperton</a> Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isa2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-isa2010-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peru_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peru Basin (page does not exist)">Peru Basin</a> in the southeast Pacific,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and</li>
<li>Southern tropical <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> in a region termed the Indian Ocean Nodule Field (IONF) roughly 500 km SE of <a href="/wiki/Diego_Garcia" title="Diego Garcia">Diego Garcia</a> Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The largest of these deposits in terms of nodule abundance and metal concentration occur in the Clarion Clipperton Zone on vast <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plains</a> in the deep ocean between 4,000 and 6,000 m (13,000 and 20,000 ft). The <a href="/wiki/International_Seabed_Authority" title="International Seabed Authority">International Seabed Authority</a> estimates that the total amount of nodules in the <a href="/wiki/Clipperton_Fracture_Zone" title="Clipperton Fracture Zone">Clarion Clipperton Zone</a> exceeds 21 Bt of nodules containing more than 270 Mt of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, 5.95 Bt of <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, 234 Mt of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and 46.6 Mt of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isa2010_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-isa2010-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>All of these deposits are in international waters apart from the Penrhyn Basin, which lies within the <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> of the Cook Islands.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_composition">Growth and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_nodule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Growth and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the seabed the abundance of nodules varies and is likely controlled by the thickness and stability of a geochemically active layer that forms at the seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipton2016_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-lipton2016-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_sediment" title="Pelagic sediment">Pelagic sediment</a> type and seabed <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetry</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>) likely influence the characteristics of the geochemically active layer.</p>
<p>Nodule growth is one of the slowest of all known <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> phenomena, on the order of a centimeter over several million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Several processes are hypothesized to be involved in the formation of nodules, including the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of metals from seawater (hydrogenous), the remobilization of manganese in the water column (diagenetic), the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">derivation</a> of metals from <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal" title="Hydrothermal">hydrothermal</a>), the decomposition of basaltic debris by seawater (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Halmyrolitic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Halmyrolitic (page does not exist)">halmyrolitic</a>) and the precipitation of metal hydroxides through the activity of <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> (biogenic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>). Several of these processes may operate concurrently or they may follow one another during the formation of a nodule.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="638" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg/220px-Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg/330px-Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg/440px-Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Konkrecje_manganowe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Polymetallic nodules</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The mineral composition of manganese-bearing minerals is dependent on how the nodules are formed; sedimentary nodules, which have a lower Mn<sup>2+</sup> content than diagenetic, are dominated by Fe-vernadite, Mn-<a href="/wiki/Feroxyhyte" title="Feroxyhyte">feroxyhyte</a>, and asbolane-buserite while diagenetic nodules are dominated by buserite I, <a href="/wiki/Birnessite" title="Birnessite">birnessite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Todorokite" title="Todorokite">todorokite</a>, and asbolane-buserite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The growth types termed diagenetic and hydrogenetic reflect suboxic and oxic growth, which in turn could relate to periods of interglacial and glacial climate. It has been estimated that suboxic-diagenentic type 2 layers make up about 50–60% of the chemical inventory of the CCZ nodules whereas oxic-hydrogenetic type 1 layers comprise about 35–40%.The remaining part (5–10%) of the nodules consists of incorporated sediment particles occurring along cracks and pores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The chemical composition of nodules varies according to the kind of manganese minerals and the size and characteristics of the core. Those of greatest economic interest contain <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> (27-30%), <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> (1.25-1.5 %), <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> (1-1.4 %) and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> (0.2-0.25 %). Other constituents include <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> (6%), <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> (5%) and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> (3%), with lesser amounts of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Water_of_crystallization" title="Water of crystallization">water of crystallization</a> and free water.</p>
<p>A wide range of trace elements and trace minerals are found in nodules with many of these incorporated from the seabed sediment, which itself includes particles carried as dust from all over the planet before settling to the seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isa2010_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-isa2010-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_Mining">Proposed Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_nodule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proposed Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Interest in the potential exploitation of polymetallic nodules generated a great deal of activity among prospective mining consortia in the 1960s and 1970s. Almost half a billion dollars was invested in identifying potential deposits and in research and development of technology for mining and processing nodules. These initial undertakings were carried out primarily by four multinational consortia composed of companies from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the Federal Republic of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and two groups of private companies and agencies from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and Japan. There were also three publicly sponsored entities from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.</p>
<p>In the late-seventies, two of the international joint ventures succeeded in collecting several hundred ton quantities of <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> nodules from the abyssal plains (18,000 feet, 5.5 km + depth) of the eastern equatorial <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipton2016_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-lipton2016-9">[9]</a></sup> Significant quantities of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> (the primary target) as well as <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> were subsequently extracted from this "ore" using both <a href="/wiki/Pyrometallurgy" title="Pyrometallurgy">pyrometallurgical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrometallurgy" title="Hydrometallurgy">hydrometallurgical</a> methods. In the course of these projects, a number of ancillary developments evolved, including the use of near-bottom towed <a href="/wiki/Side-scan_sonar" title="Side-scan sonar">side-scan sonar</a> array to assay the nodule population density on the abyssal <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> whilst simultaneously performing a sub-bottom profile with a derived, vertically oriented, low-frequency acoustic beam.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The technology and experience developed during the course of this project were never commercialized because the last two decades of the 20th century saw an excess of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> production. The estimated $3.5-billion (1978 US dollars) investment to implement commercialization was an additional factor. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Metal_Mining" title="Sumitomo Metal Mining">Sumitomo Metal Mining</a> continues to maintain a small (place-keeping) organization in this field<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kennecott_Copper" title="Kennecott Copper">Kennecott Copper</a> had explored the potential profits in manganese nodule mining and found that it was not worth the cost. On top of the environmental issues and the fact that the profits had to be shared, there was no cheap way to get the manganese nodules off the sea floor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Since the late 1970s, deep sea technology has improved significantly: including widespread and low cost use of navigation technology such as <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> (GPS) and <a href="/wiki/Ultra-short_baseline" title="Ultra-short baseline">Ultra-short baseline</a> (USBL); survey technology such as <a href="/wiki/Multibeam_echosounder" title="Multibeam echosounder">Multibeam echosounder</a> (MBES) and <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_underwater_vehicle" title="Autonomous underwater vehicle">Autonomous underwater vehicle</a> (AUV); and intervention technology including <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_underwater_vehicle" title="Remotely operated underwater vehicle">Remotely operated underwater vehicle</a> (ROV) and high power <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">Umbilical cables</a>. There is also improved technology that could be used in mining including <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">Pumps</a>, tracked and screw drive rovers, rigid and flexible <a href="/wiki/Drilling_riser" title="Drilling riser">Drilling risers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene</a> rope. Mining is considered to be similar to the potato harvest on land, which involves mining a field partitioned into long, narrow strips. The mining support vessel follows the mining route of the seafloor mining tools, picking up the about potato-sized nodules from the seafloor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time the International Seabed Authority was in place in 1994, interest in the extraction of nodules waned. Three factors were largely responsible:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Difficulty and expense of developing and operating mining technology that could economically remove the nodules from depths of five or six kilometers and transport them to the ocean surface</li>
<li>High taxes the international community would charge for the mining, and</li>
<li>Continuing availability of the key minerals from land-based sources at market prices.</li>
</ul>
<p>At this time, the commercial extraction of polymetallic nodules was not considered likely to occur during the next two decades.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In recent times, nickel and other metal supply has needed to turn to higher cost deposits in order to meet increased demand, and commercial interest in nodules has revived. The International Seabed Authority has granted new exploration contracts and is progressing development of a Mining Code for The Area, with most interest being in the Clarion Clipperton Zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2011, a number of commercial companies have received exploration contracts. These include subsidiaries of larger companies like <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/DEME" title="DEME">DEME</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keppel_Corporation" title="Keppel Corporation">Keppel Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/China_Minmetals" title="China Minmetals">China Minmetals</a> and smaller companies like Nauru Ocean Resources Inc and Tonga Offshore Mining Limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipton2016_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-lipton2016-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The renewed interest in mining nodules has led to increased concern and scrutiny regarding possible environmental impacts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manganese_nodules_mining_value_chain">Manganese nodules mining value chain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_nodule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Manganese nodules mining value chain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the value chain concept of manganese nodules mining, seven main stages from prospecting to sales can be identified:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td><b>1.</b> Prospecting and application
<p><b>2.</b> Exploration</p>
<p><b>3.</b> Resource assessment, evaluation and mine planning</p>
</td>
<td>Value is added in relation to resource classification</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="2">
<p><b>(3-4)</b> – Pilot mining test – Intermediate phase – a phase where the <b>value</b> of the project actually starts. For mature terrestrial mining the value can start as early as prospecting and application.</p>
</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><b>4.</b> Extraction, lifting and surface operations
<p><b>5.</b> Offshore and onshore logistics, transport operations</p>
<p><b>6.</b> Metallurgical processing stage</p>
<p><b>7.</b> Distribution and sales</p>
</td>
<td>Value is added basing on product processing</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The exact components and stages can be arranged individually for the particular deep-sea mining projects of various contractors. The current focus of deep sea mining projects is aimed at exploration where phases of mining, extraction, lifting and surface operation techniques are now in planning or are tested on a smaller scale.</p>
<p>As presented in the list the main steps of manganese nodules project value chain can be differentiated using the criteria of the type of activities where the value is actually added. Whereas within prospecting, exploration and resource assessment phases the value is added to intangible assets, for the extraction, processing and distribution phases the value increases with relation to product processing. There is an intermediate phase – the pilot mining test which could be considered to be an inevitable step in the shift from “resources” to “reserves” classification, where the actual value starts.</p>
<p>Exploration phase involves such operations as locating, sea bottom scanning and sampling using technologies such as echo-sounders, side scan sonars, deep-towed photography, <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_underwater_vehicle" title="Remotely operated underwater vehicle">ROVs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_underwater_vehicle" title="Autonomous underwater vehicle">AUVs</a>. The resource valuation incorporates the examination of data in the context of potential mining feasibility. A reliable mineral resources classification is a necessary condition for economic feasibility assessment. At first a sample of nodules is taken and it is processed in ship laboratories according to the specified technology in order to determine such quantities as nodules abundance and chemical content of the deposit. The spatial distribution of nodule ore abundance and metal content is processed in <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">GIS</a> computer systems. Eventually statistical analysis provides for the estimation of nodule tonnage and metals in the deposit, which are the subject of the report on mineral resources classification.</p>
<p>Value chain based on product processing involves such operations as actual mining (or extraction), vertical transport, storing, offloading, transport, metallurgical processing for final products. Unlike the exploration phase, the value increases after each operation on processed material eventually delivered to the metal market. This phase is also the subject of a taxation procedure. Logistics involves technologies analogous to those applied in land mines. This is also the case for the metallurgical processing, although rich and polymetallic mineral composition which distinguishes marine minerals from its land analogs requires special treatment of the deposit. Environmental monitoring and impact assessment analysis relate to the temporal and spatial discharges of the mining system if they occur, sediment plumes, disturbance to the benthic environment and the analysis of the regions affected by seafloor machines. This involves an examination of disturbances near the seafloor, as well as disturbances near the surface. Observations include baseline comparisons for the sake of quantitative impact assessments. After a certain reporting period feedback information is provided to improve the sustainability of the mining process.</p>
<h2><span id="Legal_developments_in_'The_Area'"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_developments_in_.27The_Area.27">Legal developments in 'The Area'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_nodule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legal developments in 'The Area'">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the Second World War the United Nations started a lengthy process of developing international treaties that moved away from the then held concept of <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_seas" title="Freedom of the seas">Freedom of the seas</a>.</p>
<p>By 1972, the promise of nodule exploitation was one of the main factors that led developing nations to propose that the deep seabed beyond the limits of national jurisdiction should be treated as a “common heritage of mankind”, with proceeds to be shared between those who developed this resource and the rest of the international community. This initiative eventually resulted in the adoption (1982) of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a> (UNCLOS) and after negotiation of Part XI by 1994, the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/International_Seabed_Authority" title="International Seabed Authority">International Seabed Authority</a>, with responsibility for controlling all deep-sea mining in international areas. The first legislative achievement of this intergovernmental organization was the adoption (2000) of regulations for prospecting and exploration for polymetallic nodules, with special provisions to protect the marine environment from any adverse effects. The Authority followed this up (2001-2002) by signing 15-year contracts with seven private and public entities, giving them exclusive rights to explore for nodules in specified tracts of the seabed, each 75,000 square kilometers in size. The United States, whose companies were among the key actors in the earlier period of exploration, remains outside this compact as a non-party to the Law of the Sea Convention.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Per UNCLOS the Authority has four main functions. Essentially these are:</p>
<ul>
<li>To administer the mineral resources of the seabed in the Area;</li>
<li>To enact rules, regulations and procedures relating to these resources;</li>
<li>To promote and encourage marine scientific research and development in the Area;</li>
<li>To protect and conserve the natural resources of the Area and prevent significant damage to the environment.</li>
</ul>
<p>Currently the International Seabed Authority is defining and debating aspects of its Mining Code which encompasses Polymetallic Sulphides (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seafloor_massive_sulphide_deposits" title="Seafloor massive sulphide deposits">Seafloor massive sulphide deposits</a>) and Cobalt-Rich Crusts as well as Polymetallic Nodules. The Mining Code includes exploration and draft exploitation regulations, an Environmental Management Plan for the Clarion Clipperton Zone, and recommendations for the guidance of Contractors in terms of reporting, environmental impact assessment, expenditure reporting and training for scientists and engineers from developing nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a>; on 19 June 2015 the General Assembly of the UN adapted resolution A/RES/69/292 - Development of an international legally-binding instrument under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea on the conservation and sustainable use of marine biological diversity of areas beyond national jurisdiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This resolution calls for a PrepCom to be established to examine what this instrument could look like and what it would address specifically in addition to the existing environmental parts of UNCLOS. It would take into account the various reports of the Co-Chairs on the work of the relevant Ad Hoc Open-ended Informal Working Group. In due course an intergovernmental conference would review and debate the recommendations of the PrepCom.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues_and_sensitivities">Environmental issues and sensitivities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_nodule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental issues and sensitivities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any future mining of nodules in The Area needs to be authorised by the International Seabed Authority and would need to quantify impact in advance via an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_statement" title="Environmental impact statement">Environmental impact statement</a> and associated Environmental Management Plan. These assessments, monitoring plans and guidance controls would likely work at the scale of proposed operations.</p>
<p>The International Seabed Authority already has an Environmental Management Plan that considers the entire Clarion Clipperton Zone and that includes reference areas that are not available for mining (termed Areas of Particular Environmental Interest).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Environmental assessments would need to have an unbiased scientific basis, and to account for:</p>
<ul>
<li>the remote nature of the nodules making detailed data collection challenging;</li>
<li>the large variety in scale (e.g. sub decimeter nodule communities spread over thousands of kilometers) in terms of ecosystem function and biodiversity;</li>
<li>the severity and scale of local impacts (such as habitat removal, resedimentation).</li>
</ul>
<p>Past environmental studies such as the Deep Ocean Mining Environmental Study (DOMES) and resultant Benthic Impact Experiments (BIE) concluded in part that trial mining at a reasonable scale would likely help best constrain real impacts from any commercial mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research shows that polymetallic nodule fields are hotspots of abundance and diversity for a highly vulnerable abyssal fauna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea_mining" title="Deep sea mining">Nodule mining</a> could affect tens of thousands of square kilometers of these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_sea_ecosystem" title="Deep sea ecosystem">deep sea ecosystems</a>. Nodule regrowth takes decades to millions of years and that would make such mining an unsustainable and nonrenewable practice. Any prediction about the effects of mining is extremely uncertain. Thus, nodule mining could cause habitat alteration, direct mortality of <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a> creatures, or suspension of sediment, which can smother <a href="/wiki/Filter_feeder" title="Filter feeder">filter feeders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Future environmental impact studies should address the impact on disruption and release of <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a> deposits in the deep oceans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
