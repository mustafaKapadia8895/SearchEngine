<p>The <b>Sea of Galilee</b>, also <b>Kinneret</b>, <b>Lake of Gennesaret</b>, or <b>Lake Tiberias</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Shofar, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', Cardo, 'Noto Sans Hebrew', David, FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;">יָם כִּנֶּרֶת</span>‎, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judeo-Aramaic_language" title="Judeo-Aramaic language">Judeo-Aramaic</a>: יַמּא דטבריא; גִּנֵּיסַר <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">بحيرة طبريا</span>‎‎), is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. It is approximately 53 km (33 mi) in circumference, about 21 km (13 mi) long, and 13 km (8.1 mi) wide. Its area is 166.7 km<sup>2</sup> (64.4 sq mi) at its fullest, and its maximum depth is approximately 43 m (141 feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilec_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ilec-3">[3]</a></sup> At levels between 215 metres (705 ft) and 209 metres (686 ft) below sea level,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> it is the lowest freshwater lake on Earth and the second-lowest lake in the world (after the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Salt_lake" title="Salt lake">saltwater lake</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The lake is fed partly by underground springs although its main source is the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a> which flows through it from north to south.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JordanRiver_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1285" data-file-width="1016" height="316" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/JordanRiver_en.svg/250px-JordanRiver_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/JordanRiver_en.svg/375px-JordanRiver_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/JordanRiver_en.svg/500px-JordanRiver_en.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JordanRiver_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sea of Galilee in relation to the Dead Sea</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Sea of Galilee is situated in northeast Israel, between the <a href="/wiki/Golan_Heights" title="Golan Heights">Golan Heights</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a> region, in the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Rift_Valley" title="Jordan Rift Valley">Jordan Rift Valley</a>, the valley caused by the separation of the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Plate" title="Arabian Plate">Arabian Plates</a>. Consequently, the area is subject to <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, and in the past, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> activity. This is evident by the abundant <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">igneous</a> rocks that define the geology of the Galilee.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_and_their_etymology">Names and their etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names and their etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lake has been called by different names throughout its history, usually depending on the dominant settlement on its shores. With changing fate of the towns, the lake's name also changed.</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Lake or Sea of Kinneret</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>The modern Hebrew name, <i>Kinneret</i>, comes from the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, the main source of the Christian <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>, where it appears as the "sea of Kinneret" in <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0434.htm#11" rel="nofollow">Numbers 34:11</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0613.htm#27" rel="nofollow">Joshua 13:27</a>, spelled כנרות "Kinnerot" in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> in <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0611.htm#2" rel="nofollow">Joshua 11:2</a>. This name was also found in the scripts of <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Danel" title="Danel">Aqhat Epic</a>. <i>Kinneret</i> was listed among the "fenced cities" in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Joshua+19%3A35&amp;version=9" rel="nofollow">Joshua 19:35</a></span>. A persistent, though likely erroneous popular etymology of the name presumes that the name <i>Kinneret</i> may originate from the Hebrew word <i><a href="/wiki/Kinnor" title="Kinnor">kinnor</a></i> ("harp" or "lyre"), in view of the shape of the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The scholarly consensus though is that the origin of the name lies with the important <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gennesaret" title="Gennesaret">Kinneret</a>, excavated at Tell el-'Oreimeh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Negev_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Negev-7">[7]</a></sup> However, there is no evidence that the city of Kinneret itself was not named after the body of water rather than vice versa, or for the origin of the town's name.</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Lake of Gennesaret</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>All Old and <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> writers use the term "sea" (Hebrew יָם <i>yam</i>, Greek θάλασσα), with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a> who calls it "the Lake of Gennesaret" (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+5%3A1&amp;version=9%3BTR1550" rel="nofollow">Luke 5:1</a></span>), from the Greek λίμνη Γεννησαρέτ (<i>limnē Gennēsaret</i>), the "Grecized form of Chinnereth" according to Easton (1897).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Sea of Ginosar</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_Talmud" title="Babylonian Talmud">Babylonian Talmud</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Flavius Josephus</a> mention the sea by the name "Sea of Ginosar" after the small fertile plain of <a href="/wiki/Ginosar" title="Ginosar">Ginosar</a> that lies on its western side.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ginosar is yet another name derived from "Kinneret".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Negev_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Negev-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Sea of Galilee</b>, <b>Sea of Tiberias</b>, <b>Lake Tiberias</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>In the New Testament the term "sea of Galilee" (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">θάλασσαν τῆς Γαλιλαίας</span>, <i>thalassan tēs Galilaias</i>) is used in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">gospel of Matthew</a> <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+4%3A18%3B15%3A29&amp;version=9" rel="nofollow">4:18; 15:29</a></span>, the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">gospel of Mark</a> <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+1%3A16%3B7%3A31&amp;version=9" rel="nofollow">1:16; 7:31</a></span>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">gospel of John</a> <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+6%3A1&amp;version=9" rel="nofollow">6:1</a></span> as "the sea of Galilee, which is <i>the sea</i> of Tiberias" (θαλάσσης τῆς Γαλιλαίας τῆς Τιβεριάδος, <i>thalassēs tēs Galilaias tēs Tiberiados</i>), the late 1st century CE name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Sea of <a href="/wiki/Tiberias" title="Tiberias">Tiberias</a> is also the name mentioned in Roman texts and in the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Talmud" title="Jerusalem Talmud">Jerusalem Talmud</a>, and was adopted into Arabic as <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:ArTiberias.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3c/ArTiberias.ogg" title="ArTiberias.ogg"><i>Buhairet Tabariyya</i></a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:ArTiberias.ogg" title="File:ArTiberias.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> (بحيرة طبريا), "Lake Tiberias".</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Sea of Minya</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>From the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk" title="Mamluk">Mamluk</a> period the lake was known in Arabic as "Bahr al-Minya", the "Sea of Minya", after the Umayyad <i><a href="/wiki/Ksar" title="Ksar">qasr</a></i> complex whose ruins are still visible at <a href="/wiki/Khirbat_al-Minya" title="Khirbat al-Minya">Khirbat al-Minya</a>. This is the name employed by the medieval <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arab</a> scholars <a href="/wiki/Al-Baladhuri" title="Al-Baladhuri">Al-Baladhuri</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Jarir_al-Tabari" title="Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari">Al-Tabari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Kathir" title="Ibn Kathir">Ibn Kathir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, remains of a <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> site were found under the water at the southern end. Remains of mud huts were found which are the oldest known buildings in the world. See <a href="/wiki/Ohalo" title="Ohalo">Ohalo</a>. Nahal Ein Gev, located about 3 km east of the lake, contains a village from the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natufian" title="Natufian">Natufian</a> period. The site is considered one of the first permanent human settlements in the world from a time predating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_revolution" title="Neolithic revolution">Neolithic revolution</a>.</p>

<p>The Sea of Galilee lies on the ancient <a href="/wiki/Via_Maris" title="Via Maris">Via Maris</a>, which linked <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> with the northern empires. The <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hasmonean" title="Hasmonean">Hasmoneans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> founded flourishing towns and settlements on the land-locked lake including <a href="/wiki/Hippos" title="Hippos">Hippos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiberias" title="Tiberias">Tiberias</a>. The first-century historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavius_Josephus" title="Flavius Josephus">Flavius Josephus</a> was so impressed by the area that he wrote, "One may call this place the ambition of Nature"; he also reported a thriving fishing industry at this time, with 230 boats regularly working in the lake. Archaeologists discovered one such boat, nicknamed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Boat" title="Jesus Boat">Jesus Boat</a>, in 1986.</p>
<p>Much of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">ministry of Jesus</a> occurred on the shores of Lake Galilee. In those days, there was a continuous ribbon development of settlements and villages around the lake and plenty of trade and ferrying by boat. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_gospels" title="Synoptic gospels">Synoptic gospels</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Mark</a> (1:14–20), <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a> (4:18–22), and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a> (5:1–11) describe how Jesus recruited four of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_apostles" title="Twelve apostles">apostles</a> from the shores of Lake Galilee: the fishermen <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Simon</a> and his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew" title="Saint Andrew">Andrew</a> and the brothers <a href="/wiki/John_the_Apostle" title="John the Apostle">John</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_James_the_Great" title="Saint James the Great">James</a>. One of Jesus' famous teaching episodes, the <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">Sermon on the Mount</a>, is supposed to have been given on a hill overlooking the lake. Many of his miracles are also said to have occurred here including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_walks_on_water" title="Jesus walks on water">his walking on water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calming_the_storm" title="Calming the storm">calming the storm</a>, the disciples and the <a href="/wiki/Miraculous_catch_of_fish" title="Miraculous catch of fish">miraculous catch of fish</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Feeding_the_multitude" title="Feeding the multitude">feeding five thousand people</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Tabgha" title="Tabgha">Tabgha</a>). In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John%27s_Gospel" title="John's Gospel">John's Gospel</a> the sea provides the setting for Jesus' third <a href="/wiki/Post-Resurrection_appearances_of_Jesus" title="Post-Resurrection appearances of Jesus">post-resurrection appearance</a> to his disciples (John 21).</p>
<p>In 135 CE, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_Kokhba%27s_revolt" title="Bar Kokhba's revolt">Bar Kokhba's revolt</a> was put down. The Romans responded by banning all Jews from <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The center of Jewish culture and learning shifted to the region of the Galilee and the Kinneret, particularly the city of <a href="/wiki/Tiberias" title="Tiberias">Tiberias</a>. It was in this region that the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Talmud" title="Jerusalem Talmud">Jerusalem Talmud</a> was compiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The lake's importance declined when the Byzantines lost control and the area was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a> and subsequent Islamic empires. Apart from Tiberias, the major towns and cities in the area were gradually abandoned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The palace <a href="/wiki/Khirbat_al-Minya" title="Khirbat al-Minya">Khirbat al-Minya</a> was built by the lake during the reign of the Umayyad caliph <a href="/wiki/Al-Walid_I" title="Al-Walid I">al-Walid I</a> (705–715 CE). In 1187, <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a> defeated the armies of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hattin" title="Battle of Hattin">Battle of Hattin</a>, largely because he was able to cut the Crusaders off from the valuable fresh water of the Sea of Galilee.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Throughout the early Ottoman era, the lake had little importance within the Ottoman Empire. Tiberias did see a significant revival of its Jewish community in the 16th century, but had gradually declined, until in 1660 the city was completely <a href="/wiki/1660_destruction_of_Tiberias" title="1660 destruction of Tiberias">destroyed</a>. In the early 18th century, Tiberias was rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Zahir_al-Umar" title="Zahir al-Umar">Zahir al-Umar</a>, becoming the center of his rule over Galilee, and seeing also a revival of its Jewish community.</p>
<p>In 1909, Jewish pioneers established the first cooperative farming village (<a href="/wiki/Kibbutz" title="Kibbutz">kibbutz</a>), <a href="/wiki/Kvutzat_Kinneret" title="Kvutzat Kinneret">Kvutzat Kinneret</a> in the immediate vicinity of the lake. The settlement trained Jewish immigrants in farming and agriculture. Later, Kvutzat Kinneret pioneers established Kibbutz <a href="/wiki/Degania_Alef" title="Degania Alef">Degania Alef</a>. The Kvutzat Kinneret is considered the cradle of the kibbutz culture of early <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a> and is the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Shemer" title="Naomi Shemer">Naomi Shemer</a> and the burial site of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rachel_(poet)" title="Rachel (poet)">Rachel</a>—two of the most prominent Israeli poets.</p>
<p>In 1917, the British defeated Ottoman Turkish forces and took control of Palestine, while France took control of Syria. In the carve-up of the Ottoman territories between Britain and France, it was agreed that Britain would retain control of <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a>, while France would control Syria. However, the allies had to fix the border between the <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Mandate_of_Syria" title="French Mandate of Syria">French Mandate of Syria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The boundary was defined in broad terms by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-British_Boundary_Agreement_(1920)" title="Franco-British Boundary Agreement (1920)">Franco-British Boundary Agreement</a> of December 1920, which drew it across the middle of the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treaty1920_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-treaty1920-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the commission established by the 1920 treaty redrew the boundary. The Zionist movement pressured the French and British to assign as many water sources as possible to <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a> during the demarcating negotiations. The High Commissioner of Palestine, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel,_1st_Viscount_Samuel" title="Herbert Samuel, 1st Viscount Samuel">Herbert Samuel</a>, had sought full control of the Sea of Galilee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The negotiations led to the inclusion into the Palestine territory of the whole Sea of Galilee, both sides of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Jordan" title="River Jordan">River Jordan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Hula" title="Lake Hula">Lake Hula</a>, Dan spring, and part of the <a href="/wiki/Yarmouk_River" title="Yarmouk River">Yarmouk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The final border approved in 1923 followed a 10-meter wide strip along the lake's northeastern shore,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> cutting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandatory_Syria" title="Mandatory Syria">Mandatory Syria</a> (<a href="/wiki/State_of_Damascus" title="State of Damascus">State of Damascus</a>) off from the lake.</p>
<p>The British and French Agreement provided that existing rights over the use of the waters of the Jordan by the inhabitants of Syria would be maintained; the Government of Syria would have the right to erect a new pier at Semakh on Lake Tiberias or jointly use the existing pier; persons or goods passing between the landing-stage on the Lake of Tiberias and Semakh would not be subject to customs regulations, and the Syrian government would have access to the said landing-stage; the inhabitants of Syria and Lebanon would have the same fishing and navigation rights on Lakes Huleh, Tiberias and River Jordan, while the Government of Palestine would be responsible for policing of lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 15, 1948, Syria invaded the newborn State of Israel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> capturing territory along the Sea of Galilee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Under the 1949 <a href="/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Syria_Mixed_Armistice_Commission" title="Israel–Syria Mixed Armistice Commission">armistice agreement</a> between Israel and Syria, Syria occupied the northeast shoreline of the Sea of Galilee. The agreement, though, stated that the armistice line was "not to be interpreted as having any relation whatsoever to ultimate territorial arrangements." Syria remained in possession of the lake's northeast shoreline until the 1967 <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">Arab-Israeli war</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1950s, Israel formulated a plan to link the Kinneret with the rest of the country's water infrastructure via the <a href="/wiki/National_Water_Carrier_of_Israel" title="National Water Carrier of Israel">National Water Carrier</a>, in order to supply the water demand of the growing country. The carrier was completed in 1964. The Israeli plan, in line with the <a href="/wiki/Headwater_Diversion_Plan_(Jordan_River)" title="Headwater Diversion Plan (Jordan River)">Headwater Diversion Plan (Jordan River)</a> of the Arab League, sparked political and sometimes even armed <a href="/wiki/War_over_Water_(Jordan_river)" title="War over Water (Jordan river)">confrontations</a> over the Jordan River basin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During a routine sonar scan in 2003 (finding published in 2013),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> archaeologists discovered an enormous conical stone structure. The structure, which has a diameter of around 230 feet (70 m), is made of boulders and stones. The ruins are estimated to be between 2,000 and 12,000 years old, and are about 10 metres (33 ft) underwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The estimated weight of the monument is over 60,000 tons. Researchers explain that the site resembles early burial sites in Europe and was likely built in the early Bronze Age.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_use">Water use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Water use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1700" data-file-width="2200" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg/220px-KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg/330px-KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg/440px-KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KinneretLevels-Apr21.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sea of Galilee water levels January 2004 – February 2012</div>

<p>Israel's <a href="/wiki/National_Water_Carrier_of_Israel" title="National Water Carrier of Israel">National Water Carrier</a>, completed in 1964, transports water from the lake to the population centers of Israel, and in the past supplied most of the country's drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Nowadays the lake supplies approximately 10% of Israel's drinking water needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-themaker1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-themaker1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1964, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> attempted construction of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headwater_Diversion_Plan" title="Headwater Diversion Plan">Headwater Diversion Plan</a> that would have blocked the flow of water into the Sea of Galilee, sharply reducing the water flow into the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This project and Israel's attempt to block these efforts in 1965 were factors which played into regional tensions culminating in the 1967 <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">Six-Day War</a>. During the war, Israel captured the <a href="/wiki/Golan_Heights" title="Golan Heights">Golan Heights</a>, which contain some of the sources of water for the Sea of Galilee.</p>
<p>The Israeli government monitors water levels and publishes the results daily at <a href="#WaterLevel">this web page</a>. The level over the past eight years can be retrieved from that site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Increasing water demand in Israel, Lebanon and Jordan, as well as dry winters, have resulted in stress on the lake and a decreasing water line to dangerously low levels at times. The Sea of Galilee is at risk of becoming irreversibly salinized by the salt water springs under the lake, which are held in check by the weight of the freshwater on top of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Up until the mid-2010s, about 400,000,000 cubic metres (1.4<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> cu ft) of water was pumped through the National Water Carrier each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Jordan_peace_treaty" title="Israel–Jordan peace treaty">Israel–Jordan peace treaty</a>, Israel also supplies 50,000,000 cubic metres (1.8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> cu ft) of water annually from the lake to <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In recent years the Israeli government has made extensive investments in water conservation, <a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_water" title="Reclaimed water">reclamation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desalination#Israel" title="Desalination">desalination</a> infrastructure in the country. This has allowed it to significantly reduce the amount of water pumped from the lake annually in an effort to restore and improve its ecological environment, as well as respond to some of the most extreme drought conditions in hundreds of years affecting the lake's <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">intake basin</a> since 1998. Therefore, it is expected that in 2016 only about 25,000,000 cubic metres (880,000,000 cu ft) of water will be drawn from the lake for Israeli domestic consumption, a small fraction of the amount typically drawn from the lake over the previous decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-themaker1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-themaker1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Israel" title="Tourism in Israel">Tourism</a> around the Sea of Galilee is an important economic branch. Historical and religious sites in the region draw both local and foreign tourists. The Sea of Galilee is an attraction for <a href="/wiki/Christian_pilgrimage" title="Christian pilgrimage">Christian pilgrims</a> who visit Israel to see the places where Jesus performed miracles according the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_walks_on_water" title="Jesus walks on water">his walking on water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calming_the_storm" title="Calming the storm">calming the storm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feeding_the_multitude" title="Feeding the multitude">feeding the multitude</a>. Alonzo Ketcham Parker, a nineteenth-century American traveler, called visiting the Sea of Galilee "a 'fifth gospel' which one read devoutly, his heart overflowing with quiet joy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2011, Israel unveiled a 40-mile (64 km) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiking_trail" title="Hiking trail">hiking trail</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a> for Christian pilgrims, called the "<a href="/wiki/Jesus_Trail" title="Jesus Trail">Jesus Trail</a>". It includes a network of footpaths, roads and bicycle paths linking sites central to the lives of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> and his disciples. It ends at <a href="/wiki/Capernaum" title="Capernaum">Capernaum</a> on the shores of the Sea of Galilee, where Jesus espoused his teachings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another key attraction is the site where the Sea of Galilee's water flows into the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a>, to which thousands of pilgrims from all over the world come to be baptized every year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Israel's most well-known open water swim race, the Kinneret Crossing, is held every year in September, drawing thousands of open water swimmers to participate in competitive and noncompetitive events.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Tourists also partake in the building of rafts on <i>Lavnun Beach</i>, called Rafsodia. Here many different age groups work together to build a raft with their bare hands and then sail that raft across the sea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Other economic activities include fishing in the lake and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Israel" title="Agriculture in Israel">agriculture</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">bananas</a>, dates, mangoes, grapes and olives in the fertile belt of land surrounding it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_of_Galilee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The warm waters of the Sea of Galilee support various flora and fauna, which have supported a significant commercial fishery for more than two millennia. Local flora include various reeds along most of the shoreline as well as <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>. Fauna include <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthos</a> and a number of fish species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Acanthobrama_terraesanctae" title="Acanthobrama terraesanctae">Acanthobrama terraesanctae</a></i>. Fish caught commercially include <i><a href="/wiki/Tristramella_simonis" title="Tristramella simonis">Tristramella simonis</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarotherodon_galilaeus" title="Sarotherodon galilaeus">Sarotherodon galilaeus</a></i>, locally called "St. Peter’s Fish".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilec_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ilec-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2005, 300 short tons (270 t) of tilapia were caught by local fishermen. This dropped to 8 short tons (7.3 t) in 2009 due to overfishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, low water levels in drought years have stressed the lake's ecology. This may have been aggravated by over-extraction of water for either the National Water Carrier to supply other parts of Israel or, since 1994, for the supply of water to Jordan (see <i>"Water use"</i> section above). Droughts of the early and mid-1990s dried out the marshy northern margin of the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-32">[32]</a></sup> A fish species that is unique to the lake, <i><a href="/wiki/Tristramella_sacra" title="Tristramella sacra">Tristramella sacra</a></i>, used to <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawn</a> in the marsh and has not been seen since the 1990s droughts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-32">[32]</a></sup> Conservationists fear this species may have become <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-32">[32]</a></sup> It is hoped that drastic reductions in the amount of water pumped through the National Water Carrier will help restore the lake's ecology over the span of several years. As such, the amount planned to be drawn in 2016 for Israeli domestic water use is expected to be less than 10% of the amount commonly drawn on an annual basis in the decades before the mid-2010s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-themaker1_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-themaker1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
