<p>The <b>Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope</b> (<b>FGST</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), formerly called the <b>Gamma-ray Large Area Space Telescope</b> (<b>GLAST</b>), is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">space observatory</a> being used to perform <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_astronomy" title="Gamma-ray astronomy">gamma-ray astronomy</a> observations from <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>. Its main instrument is the Large Area Telescope (LAT), with which astronomers mostly intend to perform an all-sky survey studying <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> phenomena such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">active galactic nuclei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>, other high-energy sources and <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>. Another instrument aboard Fermi, the Gamma-ray Burst Monitor (GBM; formerly GLAST Burst Monitor), is being used to study <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray bursts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fermi was launched on 11 June 2008 at 16:05 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> aboard a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> 7920-H rocket. The mission is a joint venture of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a>, and government agencies in France, Germany, Italy, Japan, and Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is the most sensitive gamma-ray observatory in orbit, succeeding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/INTEGRAL" title="INTEGRAL">INTEGRAL</a> satellite.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Fermi includes two scientific instruments, the Large Area Telescope (LAT) and the Gamma-ray Burst Monitor (GBM).</p>
<ul>
<li>The LAT<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is an imaging gamma-ray detector (a <a href="/wiki/Pair-conversion" title="Pair-conversion">pair-conversion</a> instrument) which detects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a> with energy from about 20 million to about 300 billion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volts" title="Electron volts">electron volts</a> (20 MeV to 300 GeV),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20080826_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa20080826-7">[7]</a></sup> with a field of view of about 20% of the sky; it may be thought of as a sequel to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EGRET" title="EGRET">EGRET</a> instrument on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compton_gamma_ray_observatory" title="Compton gamma ray observatory">Compton gamma ray observatory</a>.</li>
<li>The GBM<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> consists of 14 scintillation detectors (twelve <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a> crystals for the 8 keV to 1 MeV range and two <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_germanate" title="Bismuth germanate">bismuth germanate</a> crystals with sensitivity from 150 keV to 30 MeV), and can detect gamma-ray bursts in that energy range across the whole of the sky not occluded by the Earth.</li>
</ul>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2014" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg/170px-GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg/255px-GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg/340px-GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GLAST_on_the_payload_attach_fitting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fermi on Earth, solar arrays folded</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a> Advanced Information Systems (formerly Spectrum Astro and now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences" title="Orbital Sciences">Orbital Sciences</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Gilbert,_Arizona" title="Gilbert, Arizona">Gilbert, Arizona</a> designed and built the <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> that carries the instruments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>. It travels in a low, circular orbit with a period of about 95 minutes. Its normal mode of operation maintains its orientation so that the instruments will look away from the Earth, with a "rocking" motion to equalize the coverage of the sky. The view of the instruments will sweep out across most of the sky about 16 times per day. The spacecraft can also maintain an orientation that points to a chosen target.</p>
<p>Both science instruments underwent environmental testing, including vibration, vacuum, and high and low temperatures to ensure that they can withstand the stresses of launch and continue to operate in space. They were integrated with the spacecraft at the General Dynamics ASCENT facility in Gilbert, Arizona<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>Data from the instruments are available to the public through the Fermi Science Support Center web site<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>. Software for analyzing the data is also available<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GLAST_renamed_Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope">GLAST renamed Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: GLAST renamed Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fermi gained its new name in 2008: On 26 August 2008, GLAST was renamed the "Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope" in honor of <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a>, a pioneer in high-energy physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstlight_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-firstlight-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>NASA's <a href="/wiki/Alan_Stern" title="Alan Stern">Alan Stern</a>, associate administrator for Science at NASA Headquarters, launched a public competition 7 February 2008, closing 31 March 2008, to rename GLAST in a way that would "capture the excitement of GLAST's mission and call attention to gamma-ray and high-energy astronomy ... something memorable to commemorate this spectacular new astronomy mission ... a name that is catchy, easy to say and will help make the satellite and its mission a topic of dinner table and classroom discussion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>NASA designed the mission with a five-year lifetime, with a goal of ten years of operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The key scientific objectives of the Fermi mission have been described as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>To understand the mechanisms of particle acceleration in <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nuclei</a> (AGN), pulsars, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernova_remnants" title="Supernova remnants">supernova remnants</a> (SNR).</li>
<li>Resolve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">gamma-ray</a> sky: unidentified sources and diffuse emission.</li>
<li>Determine the high-energy behavior of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_bursts" title="Gamma-ray bursts">gamma-ray bursts</a> and transients.</li>
<li>Probe dark matter (e.g. by looking for an excess of gamma rays from the center of the Milky Way) and early Universe.</li>
<li>Search for evaporating primordial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro_black_holes" title="Micro black holes">micro black holes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Micro_black_hole" title="Micro black hole">MBH</a>) from their presumed gamma burst signatures [Hawking Radiation component].</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Academies_of_Sciences" title="National Academies of Sciences">National Academies of Sciences</a> ranked this mission as a top priority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlastQ.26A_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlastQ.26A-18">[18]</a></sup> Many new possibilities and discoveries are anticipated to emerge from this single mission and greatly expand our view of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlastQ.26A_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-GlastQ.26A-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blazar" title="Blazar">Blazars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galaxies</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Study energy spectra and variability of wavelengths of light coming from blazars so as to determine the composition of the black hole jets aimed directly at Earth -- whether they are
<dl>
<dd>(a) a combination of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> or</dd>
<dd>(b) only <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">Gamma-ray bursts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Study gamma-ray bursts with an energy range several times more intense than ever before so that scientists may be able to understand them better.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">Neutron stars</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Study younger, more energetic <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> than ever before so as to broaden our understanding of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Study the pulsed emissions of <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetospheres</a> so as to possibly solve how they are produced. Study how pulsars generate winds of interstellar particles.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy" title="Milky Way galaxy">Milky Way galaxy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Provide new data to help improve upon existing theoretical models of our own galaxy.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst_progenitors" title="Gamma-ray burst progenitors">Gamma-ray background radiation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Study better than ever before whether ordinary <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> are responsible for gamma-ray background radiation. The potential for a tremendous discovery awaits if ordinary sources are determined to be irresponsible, in which case the cause may be anything from self-annihilating dark matter to entirely new chain reactions among interstellar particles that have yet to be conceived.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_universe" title="Early universe">early universe</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Study better than ever before how concentrations of visible and ultraviolet light change over time. The mission should easily detect regions of spacetime where gamma-rays interacted with visible or UV light to make matter. This can be seen as an example of E=mc<sup>2</sup> working in reverse, where energy is converted into mass, in the early universe.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Study better than ever before how our own <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> produces gamma rays in <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Dark matter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Search for evidence that dark matter is made up of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weakly_interacting_massive_particle" title="Weakly interacting massive particle">weakly interacting massive particles</a>, complementing similar experiments already planned for the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> as well as other underground detectors. The potential for a tremendous discovery in this area is possible over the next several years.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Fundamental <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Test better than ever before certain established theories of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, such as whether the speed of light in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> remains constant regardless of <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">general theory of relativity</a> contends that it does, yet some models in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">quantum gravity</a> predict that it may not. Search for gamma rays emanating from former black holes that once exploded, providing yet another potential step toward the unification of quantum mechanics and general relativity. Determine whether <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> naturally split into smaller photons, as predicted by quantum mechanics and already achieved under controlled, man-made experimental conditions.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Unknown discoveries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Scientists estimate a very high possibility for new scientific discoveries, even revolutionary discoveries, emerging from this single mission.</dd>
</dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelaunch">Prelaunch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prelaunch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 4 March 2008, the spacecraft arrived at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrotech" title="Astrotech">Astrotech</a> payload processing facility in <a href="/wiki/Titusville,_Florida" title="Titusville, Florida">Titusville, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On 4 June 2008, after several previous delays, launch status was retargeted for 11 June at the earliest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the last delays resulting from the need to replace the Flight Termination System batteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-06-06staus_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-06-06staus-33">[33]</a></sup> The launch window extended from 15:45 to 17:40 UTC daily, until 7 August 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-06-06staus_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-06-06staus-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Launch occurred successfully on 11 June 2008 at 16:05 UTC aboard a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta 7920H-10C</a> rocket from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station</a> <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Space_Launch_Complex_17" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 17">Space Launch Complex 17-B</a>. Spacecraft separation took place about 75 minutes after launch.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fermi resides in a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low-Earth circular orbit</a> at an altitude of 550 km (340 mi), and at an inclination of 28.5 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_modifications">Software modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Software modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GLAST received some minor modifications to its computer software on 23 June 2008.</p>
<h3><span id="LAT/GBM_computers_operational"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="LAT.2FGBM_computers_operational">LAT/GBM computers operational</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: LAT/GBM computers operational">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Computers operating both the LAT and GBM and most of the LAT's components were turned on 24 June 2008. The LAT high voltage was turned on 25 June, and it began detecting high-energy particles from space, but minor adjustments were still needed to calibrate the instrument. The GBM high voltage was also turned on 25 June, but the GBM still required one more week of testing/calibrations before searching for gamma-ray bursts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sky_survey_mode">Sky survey mode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sky survey mode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After presenting an overview of the Fermi instrumentation and goals, Jennifer Carson of <a href="/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory" title="SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory">SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory</a> had concluded that the primary goals were "all achievable with the all-sky scanning mode of observing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carson_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-carson-35">[35]</a></sup> Fermi switched to "sky survey mode" on 26 June 2008 so as to begin sweeping its field of view over the entire sky every three hours (every two orbits).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collision_avoided">Collision avoided</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Collision avoided">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 30 April 2013, NASA revealed that the telescope had narrowly avoided a collision a year earlier with a defunct Cold War-era Soviet spy satellite, <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_1805" title="Kosmos 1805">Kosmos 1805</a>, in April 2012. Orbital predictions several days earlier indicated that the two satellites were expected to occupy the same point in space within 30 milliseconds of each other. On 3 April, telescope operators decided to stow the satellite's high-gain parabolic antenna, rotate the solar panels out of the way and to fire Fermi's rocket thrusters for one second to move it out of the way. Even though the thrusters had been idle since the telescope had been placed in orbit nearly five years earlier, they worked correctly and potential disaster was thus avoided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extended_mission_2013-2018">Extended mission 2013-2018</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extended mission 2013-2018">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2013 Fermi started its 5-year mission extension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fme2013_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-fme2013-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discoveries">Discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulsar_discovery">Pulsar discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pulsar discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first major discovery came when the space telescope detected a <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a> in the CTA 1 <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> remnant that appeared to emit radiation in the <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> bands only, a first for its kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This new pulsar sweeps the Earth every 316.86 milliseconds and is about 4,600 light years away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greatest_GRB_energy_release">Greatest GRB energy release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Greatest GRB energy release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2008, the gamma-ray burst <a href="/wiki/GRB_080916C" title="GRB 080916C">GRB 080916C</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Carina_(constellation)" title="Carina (constellation)">Carina</a> was recorded by the Fermi telescope. This burst is notable as having “the largest apparent energy release yet measured”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The explosion had the power of about 9,000 ordinary supernovae, and the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativistic</a> jet of material ejected in the blast must have moved at a minimum of 99.9999% the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>. Overall, GRB 080916C had "the greatest total energy, the fastest motions, and the highest initial-energy emissions" ever seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_rays_and_supernova_remnants">Cosmic rays and supernova remnants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cosmic rays and supernova remnants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> it was announced that Fermi-LAT had determined that <a href="/wiki/Supernova_remnant" title="Supernova remnant">supernova remnants</a> act as enormous accelerators for <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic particles</a>. This determination fulfills one of the stated missions for this project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_gamma_ray_sources">Background gamma ray sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Background gamma ray sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst_progenitors" title="Gamma-ray burst progenitors">Gamma-ray burst progenitors</a></div>
<p>In March 2010 it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nuclei</a> are not responsible for most gamma-ray background radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Though active galactic nuclei do produce some of the gamma-ray radiation detected here on Earth, less than 30% originates from these sources. The search now is to locate the sources for the remaining 70% or so of all gamma-rays detected. Possibilities include <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star forming galaxies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution#Galaxy_mergers_and_the_formation_of_elliptical_galaxies" title="Galaxy formation and evolution">galactic mergers</a>, and yet-to-be explained dark matter <a href="/wiki/Self-interacting_dark_matter" title="Self-interacting dark matter">interactions</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Milky_Way_Gamma/X-ray_bubbles"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Milky_Way_Gamma.2FX-ray_bubbles">Milky Way Gamma/X-ray bubbles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Milky Way Gamma/X-ray bubbles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2010, it was announced two gamma-ray and X-ray bubbles were detected around Earth's galaxy, the Milky Way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bubbles_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-bubbles-45">[45]</a></sup> The bubbles, named Fermi Bubbles, extend about 25 thousand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_years" title="Light years">light years</a> distant above and below the center of the galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bubbles_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-bubbles-45">[45]</a></sup> The galaxy's diffuse gamma-ray fog hampered prior observations, but the discovery team led by D. Finkbeiner, building on research by G. Dobler, worked around this problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bubbles_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-bubbles-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highest_energy_light_ever_seen_from_the_Sun">Highest energy light ever seen from the Sun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Highest energy light ever seen from the Sun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 2012, Fermi/GLAST observed the highest energy light ever seen in a solar eruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flare_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-flare-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>At the flare's peak, the LAT detected gamma rays with two billion times the energy of visible light, or about four billion electron volts (GeV), easily setting a record for the highest-energy light ever detected during or immediately after a solar flare</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial_gamma-ray_flash_observations">Terrestrial gamma-ray flash observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Terrestrial gamma-ray flash observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fermi telescope has observed and detected numerous <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_gamma-ray_flash" title="Terrestrial gamma-ray flash">terrestrial gamma-ray flashes</a> and discovered that such flashes can produce 100 trillion positrons, far more than scientists had previously expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GRB_130427A">GRB 130427A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: GRB 130427A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>On 27 April 2013, Fermi detected <a href="/wiki/GRB_130427A" title="GRB 130427A">GRB 130427A</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray burst</a> with one of the highest energy outputs yet recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa1-48">[48]</a></sup> This included detection of a gamma-ray over 94 billion electron volts (GeV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa1-48">[48]</a></sup> This broke Fermi's previous record detection, by over three times the amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasa1-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GRB_coincident_with_gravitational_wave_event">GRB coincident with gravitational wave event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: GRB coincident with gravitational wave event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fermi reported that its GBM instrument detected a weak gamma-ray burst above 50 keV, starting 0.4 seconds after the LIGO event and with a positional uncertainty region overlapping that of the LIGO observation. The Fermi team calculated the odds of such an event being the result of a coincidence or noise at 0.22%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connaughton2016_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Connaughton2016-49">[49]</a></sup> However, observations from the <a href="/wiki/INTEGRAL" title="INTEGRAL">INTEGRAL</a> telescope's all-sky SPI-ACS instrument indicated that any energy emission in gamma-rays and hard X-rays from the event was less than one millionth of the energy emitted as gravitational waves, concluding that "this limit excludes the possibility that the event is associated with substantial gamma-ray radiation, directed towards the observer." If the signal observed by the Fermi GBM was genuinely astrophysical, SPI-ACS would have detected it with a significance of 15 sigma above the background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savchenko2016_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Savchenko2016-50">[50]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/AGILE_(satellite)" title="AGILE (satellite)">AGILE</a> space telescope also did not detect a gamma-ray counterpart of the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGILE_4Apr16_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGILE_4Apr16-51">[51]</a></sup> A follow-up analysis of the Fermi report by an independent group, released in June 2016, identified statistical flaws in the initial analysis, and concluded that the observation was consistent with a non-event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greiner2016_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greiner2016-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel2016_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel2016-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Black hole mergers of the type thought to have produced the gravitational wave event are not expected to produce gamma-ray bursts, as stellar-mass black hole binaries are not expected to have large amounts of orbiting matter. <a href="/wiki/Avi_Loeb" title="Avi Loeb">Avi Loeb</a> has theorised that if a massive star is rapidly rotating, the centrifugal force produced during its collapse will lead to the formation of a rotating bar that breaks into two dense clumps of matter with a dumbbell configuration that becomes a black hole binary, and at the end of the star's collapse it triggers a gamma-ray burst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fermi_woo_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-fermi_woo-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loeb_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loeb-55">[55]</a></sup> Loeb suggests that the 0.4 second delay is the time it took the gamma-ray burst to cross the star, relative to the gravitational waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loeb_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loeb-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRB_on_BBH_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRB_on_BBH-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GRB_170817A_signals_a_multi-messenger_transient">GRB 170817A signals a multi-messenger transient</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: GRB 170817A signals a multi-messenger transient">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 August 2017, Fermi Gamma-Ray Burst Monitor software detected, classified, and localized a gamma-ray burst which was later designated as GRB 170817A. Six minutes later, a single detector at Hanford LIGO registered a gravitational-wave candidate which was consistent with a binary <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star_merger" title="Neutron star merger">neutron star merger</a>, occurring 2 seconds before the GRB 170817A event. This observation was "the first joint detection of <a href="/wiki/GW170817" title="GW170817">gravitational</a> and <a href="/wiki/AT_2017gfo" title="AT 2017gfo">electromagnetic radiation from a single source</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma-ray_Burst_Monitor">Gamma-ray Burst Monitor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Gamma-ray Burst Monitor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Gamma-ray Burst Monitor (GBM) (formerly GLAST Burst Monitor) detects sudden flares of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">gamma-rays</a> produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst" title="Gamma ray burst">gamma ray bursts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillators</a> are on the sides of the spacecraft to view all of the sky which is not blocked by the Earth. The design is optimized for good resolution in time and photon energy.</p>
<p>"Gamma-ray bursts are so bright we can see them from billions of light years away, which means they occurred billions of years ago, and we see them as they looked then," stated Charles Meegan of NASA's <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">Marshall Space Flight Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gamma-ray Burst Monitor has detected gamma rays from <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> generated in powerful thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Large_Area_Telescope">Large Area Telescope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Large Area Telescope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Large Area Telescope (LAT) detects individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a> using technology similar to that used in terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">Photons</a> hit thin metal sheets, converting to <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>-<a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> pairs, via a process known as <a href="/wiki/Pair_production" title="Pair production">pair production</a>. These charged particles pass through interleaved layers of silicon <a href="/wiki/Microstrip_detector" title="Microstrip detector">microstrip detectors</a>, causing <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> which produce detectable tiny pulses of electric charge. Researchers can combine information from several layers of this tracker to determine the path of the particles. After passing through the tracker, the particles enter the <a href="/wiki/Calorimeter" title="Calorimeter">calorimeter</a>, which consists of a stack of <a href="/wiki/Caesium_iodide" title="Caesium iodide">caesium iodide</a> <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillator</a> crystals to measure the total energy of the particles. The LAT's field of view is large, about 20% of the sky. The resolution of its images is modest by astronomical standards, a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_minute" title="Arc minute">arc minutes</a> for the highest-energy photons and about 3 degrees at 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MeV" title="MeV">MeV</a>. The LAT is a bigger and better successor to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EGRET_(telescope)" title="EGRET (telescope)">EGRET</a> instrument on <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory" title="Compton Gamma Ray Observatory">Compton Gamma Ray Observatory</a> satellite in the 1990s. Several countries produced the components of the LAT, who then sent the components for assembly at <a href="/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory" title="SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory">SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory</a>. SLAC also hosts the LAT Instrument Science Operations Center, which supports the operation of the LAT during the Fermi mission for the LAT scientific collaboration and for NASA.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_public_outreach">Education and public outreach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Education and public outreach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Education and public outreach are important components of the Fermi project. The main Fermi education and public outreach website at <a class="external free" href="http://glast.sonoma.edu" rel="nofollow">http://glast.sonoma.edu</a> offers gateways to resources for students, educators, scientists, and the public. NASA's <a href="/wiki/NASA_Education_and_Public_Outreach_Group" title="NASA Education and Public Outreach Group">Education and Public Outreach</a> (E/PO) group operates the Fermi education and outreach resources at <a href="/wiki/Sonoma_State_University" title="Sonoma State University">Sonoma State University</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rossi_Prize">Rossi Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Rossi Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2011 <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Rossi_Prize" title="Bruno Rossi Prize">Bruno Rossi Prize</a> was awarded to Bill Atwood, Peter Michelson and the Fermi LAT team "for enabling, through the development of the Large Area Telescope, new insights into neutron stars, supernova remnants, cosmic rays, binary systems, active galactic nuclei and gamma-ray bursts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the prize was awarded to Roger W. Romani of Leland Stanford Junior University and Alice Harding of Goddard Space Flight Center for their work in developing the theoretical framework underpinning the many exciting pulsar results from Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope.</p>
<p>The 2014 prize went to <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Slatyer" title="Tracy Slatyer">Tracy Slatyer</a>, Douglas Finkeiner and Meng Su "for their discovery, in gamma rays, of the large unanticipated Galactic structure called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermi_bubbles" title="Fermi bubbles">Fermi bubbles</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossi2014_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rossi2014-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
