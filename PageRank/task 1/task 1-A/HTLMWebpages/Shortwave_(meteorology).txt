<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Shortwave (meteorology)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

Shortwave trough with associated vorticity.</div>

<p>A <b>shortwave</b> or <b>shortwave trough</b> is an embedded kink in the <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> / <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> pattern. Its length scale is much smaller than that of <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">longwaves</a>, which are responsible for the largest scale (<a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a>) weather systems. Shortwaves may be contained within or found ahead of longwaves and range from the <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> to the synoptic scale. Shortwaves are most frequently caused by either a cold pool or an upper level <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">front</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corresponding_weather_and_effects">Corresponding weather and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shortwave_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corresponding weather and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shortwaves are often associated with warm air advection (WAA) or cold air <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a> (CAA), which influence temperature. Due to the way they move the air around them and the way air moves away from them, shortwaves produce positive curvature <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> and positive shear vorticity, respectively. Ahead of a shortwave there is large-scale lift due to divergence from <a href="/wiki/Positive_vorticity_advection" title="Positive vorticity advection">positive vorticity advection</a> (PVA). This lift often causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/Convective_inhibition" title="Convective inhibition">capped environment</a>, the lift generated by a shortwave may cool the inversion layer as a result of the rapid expansion of the air (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_cooling#Adiabatic_heating_and_cooling" title="Adiabatic cooling">adiabatic cooling</a>), allowing for deep, moist <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a>.</p>
