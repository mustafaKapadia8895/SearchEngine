<p>A <b>superstructure</b> is an upward extension of an existing structure above a baseline. This term is applied to various kinds of physical structures such as <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> having the <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics)" title="Degrees of freedom (mechanics)">degree of freedom</a> zero (in the terms of theory of machines). The word "superstructure" is a combination of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefix</a>, <i>super</i> (meaning <i>above, in addition</i>) with the Latin <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">stem word</a>, <i>structure</i> (meaning <i>to build</i> or <i>to heap up</i>).</p>
<p>In order to improve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic_performance" title="Seismic performance">response during earthquakes</a> of buildings and bridges, the superstructure might be separated from its <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(engineering)" title="Foundation (engineering)">foundation</a> by various <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering)" title="Mechanism (engineering)">mechanisms</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinery" title="Machinery">machinery</a>. All together, these implement the system of earthquake protection called <a href="/wiki/Base_isolation" title="Base isolation">base isolation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aboard_ships_and_large_boats"><span id="Ships"></span>Aboard ships and large boats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superstructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aboard ships and large boats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As stated above, superstructure consists of the parts of the ship or a <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">sailboats</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_boat" title="Fishing boat">fishing boats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Passenger_ship" title="Passenger ship">passenger ships</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>, that project above her main deck. This does not usually include its <a href="/wiki/Guyed_mast" title="Guyed mast">masts</a> or any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armament" title="Armament">armament</a> <a href="/wiki/Gun_turret" title="Gun turret">turrets</a>. Note that in modern times, turrets do not always carry <a href="/wiki/Naval_artillery" title="Naval artillery">naval artillery</a>, but they can also carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_launcher" title="Missile launcher">missile launchers</a> and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antisubmarine_warfare" title="Antisubmarine warfare">antisubmarine warfare</a> weapons.</p>
<p>The size of a watercraft's superstructure can have many implications in the performance of ships and boats, since these structures can alter their structural rigidity, their displacements, or both. These can be detrimental to any vessel's performance if they are taken into consideration incorrectly.</p>
<p>The height and the weight of superstructure on board a ship or a boat also affects the amount of <a href="/wiki/Freeboard_(nautical)" title="Freeboard (nautical)">freeboard</a> that such a vessel requires along its sides, down to her <a href="/wiki/Waterline" title="Waterline">waterline</a>. In broad terms, the more and heavier superstructure that a ship possesses (as a fraction of her length), the less the freeboard that is needed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges">Bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superstructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On a bridge, the portion of the structure that is the span and directly receives the live load is referred to as the superstructure. In contrast, the <a href="/wiki/Abutment" title="Abutment">abutment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pier" title="Pier">piers</a>, and other support structures are called the <a href="/wiki/Superstructure#Bridges" title="Superstructure">substructure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
