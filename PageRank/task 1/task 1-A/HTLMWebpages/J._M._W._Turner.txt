<p><b>Joseph Mallord William Turner</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academician" title="Royal Academician">RA</a></span> (23 April 1775 – 19 December 1851), known as <b>J. M. W. Turner</b>,<sup class="reference" id="ref_Anone"><a href="#endnote_Anone">[a]</a></sup> was an English <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> painter, <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">watercolourist</a>, known for his expressive colourisation, imaginative <a href="/wiki/Landscape_painting" title="Landscape painting">landscapes</a> and turbulent, often violent <a href="/wiki/Marine_art" title="Marine art">marine</a> paintings. Turner was born in <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Lane,_Covent_Garden" title="Maiden Lane, Covent Garden">Maiden Lane, Covent Garden</a>, London, to a modest lower middle-class family. He lived in London all his life, retaining his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockney_accent" title="Cockney accent">Cockney accent</a> and assiduously avoiding the trappings of success and fame. A child prodigy, Turner studied at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a> from 1789, enrolling when he was 14, and exhibited his first work there at 21. During this period, he also served as an architectural draftsman. He earned a steady income from commissions and sales, which due to his troubled, contrary nature, were often begrudgingly accepted. He opened his own gallery in 1804 and became professor of <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">perspective</a> at the Academy in 1807, where he lectured until 1828, although he was viewed as profoundly inarticulate. He traveled to Europe from 1802, typically returning with voluminous sketchbooks.</p>
<p>An intensely private, eccentric and reclusive man, he was a controversial figure throughout his career. He did not marry, but fathered two daughters, Eveline (1801–1874) and Georgiana (1811–1843), by his housekeeper Sarah Danby. He became more pessimistic and morose as he got older, especially after the death of his father, after which his life deteriorated, and his gallery fell into disrepair and neglect. He lived in poor health from 1845, dying in London in 1851 aged seventy-six. He is buried in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="Saint Paul's Cathedral">Saint Paul's Cathedral</a>, London.</p>
<p>He left behind over 2,000 paintings and 19,000 drawings and sketches. He had been championed by the leading English art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> from 1840, and is today regarded as having elevated landscape painting to an eminence rivaling <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history painting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Widely considered one of the greatest masters of British watercolour landscape and marine painting, he is sometimes referred to as "the painter of light".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-national-2">[2]</a></sup> A deeply experimental and progressive artist, his influence can be found in the works of painters as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a>.</p>

<p>Joseph Mallord William Turner was born on 23 April 1775 and baptised on 14 May.<sup class="reference" id="ref_Cnone"><a href="#endnote_Cnone">[c]</a></sup> He was born in <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Lane,_Covent_Garden" title="Maiden Lane, Covent Garden">Maiden Lane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a>, in London, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shanes2008_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-shanes2008-3">[3]</a></sup> His father, William Turner (1745–21 September 1829), was a barber and wig maker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-4">[4]</a></sup> His mother, Mary Marshall, came from a family of butchers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamilton2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton2007-5">[5]</a></sup> A younger sister, Mary Ann, was born in September 1778 but died in August 1783.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailey_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bailey-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1785, due to his mother showing signs of the mental disturbance for which she was admitted first to <a href="/wiki/St_Luke%27s_Hospital_for_Lunatics" title="St Luke's Hospital for Lunatics">St Luke's Hospital for Lunatics</a> in Old Street in 1799 and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bethlem_Hospital" title="Bethlem Hospital">Bethlem Hospital</a> in 1800,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> where she died in 1804, the young Turner was sent to stay with his maternal uncle, Joseph Mallord William Marshall, in <a href="/wiki/Brentford" title="Brentford">Brentford</a>, then a small town on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> west of London. The earliest known artistic exercise by Turner is from this period—a series of simple colourings of engraved plates from Henry Boswell's <i>Picturesque View of the Antiquities of England and Wales</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilton14_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilton14-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1786, Turner was sent to <a href="/wiki/Margate" title="Margate">Margate</a> on the north-east <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> coast. Here he produced a series of drawings of the town and surrounding area foreshadowing his later work. Turner returned to Margate many times in later life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By this time, Turner's drawings were being exhibited in his father's shop window and sold for a few shillings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamilton2007_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton2007-5">[5]</a></sup> His father boasted to the artist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stothard" title="Thomas Stothard">Thomas Stothard</a> that: "My son, sir, is going to be a painter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1789, Turner again stayed with his uncle who had retired to <a href="/wiki/Sunningwell" title="Sunningwell">Sunningwell</a> in Berkshire (now part of Oxfordshire). A whole <a href="/wiki/Sketchbook" title="Sketchbook">sketchbook</a> of work from this time in Berkshire survives as well as a watercolour of Oxford. The use of pencil sketches on location, as the foundation for later finished paintings, formed the basis of Turner's essential working style for his whole career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilton14_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilton14-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many early sketches by Turner were architectural studies or exercises in perspective, and it is known that, as a young man, he worked for several architects including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardwick" title="Thomas Hardwick">Thomas Hardwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Wyatt" title="James Wyatt">James Wyatt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bonomi_the_Elder" title="Joseph Bonomi the Elder">Joseph Bonomi the Elder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By the end of 1789, he had also begun to study under the topographical draughtsman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malton" title="Thomas Malton">Thomas Malton</a>, specialised in London views. Turner learned from him the basic tricks of the trade, copying and colouring outline prints of British castles and abbeys. He would later call Malton "My real master".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Topography was a thriving industry by which a young artist could pay for his studies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Academy">Royal Academy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Royal Academy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> of Art in 1789, aged of 14,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finberg_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-finberg-14">[14]</a></sup> and was accepted into the academy a year later. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds" title="Sir Joshua Reynolds">Sir Joshua Reynolds</a>, president of the Royal Academy, chaired the panel that admitted him. At first Turner showed a keen interest in architecture, but was advised by the architect Thomas Hardwick to continue painting. His first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercolour_painting" title="Watercolour painting">watercolour painting</a> <i>A View of the Archbishop's Palace, Lambeth</i> was accepted for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_summer_exhibition" title="Royal Academy summer exhibition">Royal Academy summer exhibition</a> of 1790 when Turner was 15.</p>
<p>As a probationer in the academy, he was taught drawing from plaster casts of antique sculptures. From July 1790 to October 1793, his name appears in the registry of the academy over a hundred times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamilton1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton1-15">[15]</a></sup> In June 1792, he was admitted to the life class to learn to draw the human body from nude models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Turner exhibited watercolours each year at the academy while painting in the winter and travelling in the summer widely throughout Britain, particularly to <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, where he produced a wide range of sketches for working up into studies and watercolours. These particularly focused on architectural work, which utilised his skills as a draughtsman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamilton1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton1-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1793, he showed the watercolour titled <i>The Rising Squall – Hot Wells from St Vincent's Rock Bristol</i> (now lost), which foreshadowed his later climatic effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilton20_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-wilton20-7">[7]</a></sup> Cunningham in his obituary of Turner wrote that it was: "recognised by the wiser few as a noble attempt at lifting landscape art out of the tame insipidities...[and] evinced for the first time that mastery of effect for which he is now justly celebrated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1796, Turner exhibited <i><a href="/wiki/Fishermen_at_Sea" title="Fishermen at Sea">Fishermen at Sea</a></i>, his first <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">oil painting</a> at the academy, of a nocturnal moonlit scene of <a href="/wiki/The_Needles" title="The Needles">the Needles</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>. The image of boats in peril contrasts the cold light of the moon with the firelight glow of the fishermen's lantern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Wilton said that the image: "Is a summary of all that had been said about the sea by the artists of the 18th century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and shows strong influence by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Claude_Joseph_Vernet" title="Claude Joseph Vernet">Claude Joseph Vernet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg" title="Philip James de Loutherbourg">Philip James de Loutherbourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Monamy" title="Peter Monamy">Peter Monamy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Swaine" title="Francis Swaine">Francis Swaine</a>, who was admired for his moonlight marine paintings. This particular painting cannot be said to show any influence of <a href="/wiki/Willem_van_de_Velde_the_Younger" title="Willem van de Velde the Younger">Willem van de Velde the Younger</a>, as not a single nocturnal scene is known by that painter. Some later work, however, was created to rival or complement the manner of the Dutch artist. The image was praised by contemporary critics and founded Turner's reputation, as both an oil painter and a painter of maritime scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Turner travelled widely in Europe, starting with France and Switzerland in 1802 and studying in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Louvre" title="The Louvre">the Louvre</a> in Paris in the same year. He made many visits to Venice.</p>
<p>Important support for his work came from <a href="/wiki/Walter_Fawkes" title="Walter Fawkes">Walter Ramsden Fawkes</a>, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farnley_Hall_(North_Yorkshire)" title="Farnley Hall (North Yorkshire)">Farnley Hall</a>, near Otley in Yorkshire, who became a close friend of the artist. Turner first visited Otley in 1797, aged 22, when commissioned to paint watercolours of the area. He was so attracted to Otley and the surrounding area that he returned to it throughout his career. The stormy backdrop of <i><a href="/wiki/Snow_Storm:_Hannibal_and_his_Army_Crossing_the_Alps" title="Snow Storm: Hannibal and his Army Crossing the Alps">Hannibal Crossing The Alps</a></i> is reputed to have been inspired by a storm over <a href="/wiki/The_Chevin" title="The Chevin">the Chevin</a> in Otley while he was staying at Farnley Hall.</p>
<p>Turner was a frequent guest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_O%27Brien_Wyndham,_3rd_Earl_of_Egremont" title="George O'Brien Wyndham, 3rd Earl of Egremont">George O'Brien Wyndham, 3rd Earl of Egremont</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Petworth_House" title="Petworth House">Petworth House</a> in West Sussex and painted scenes that Egremont funded taken from the grounds of the house and of the Sussex countryside, including a view of the Chichester Canal. Petworth House still displays a number of paintings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Turner grew older, he became more eccentric. He had few close friends except for his father, who lived with him for 30 years and worked as his studio assistant. His father's death in 1829 had a profound effect on him, and thereafter he was subject to bouts of depression. He never married but had a relationship with an older widow, Sarah Danby. He is believed to have been the father of her two daughters born in 1801 and 1811.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, he had a relationship with Sophia Caroline Booth after her second husband died, living for about 18 years as 'Mr Booth' in her house in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Turner was a habitual user of <a href="/wiki/Snuff_(tobacco)" title="Snuff (tobacco)">snuff</a>; in 1838 the King of France, Louis-Philippe, presented a gold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snuff_box" title="Snuff box">snuff box</a> to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Of two other snuffboxes, an <a href="/wiki/Agate" title="Agate">agate</a> and silver example bears Turner's name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and another, made of wood, was collected along with his spectacles, magnifying glass and card case by an associate housekeeper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Turner died of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> at the home of his lover Sophia Caroline Booth, in <a href="/wiki/Cheyne_Walk" title="Cheyne Walk">Cheyne Walk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>, on 19 December 1851. Apparently his last words were "The Sun is God",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> though this may be apocryphal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He is buried in <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a>, where he lies next to Sir <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a>.</p>
<p>His friend, the architect <a href="/wiki/Philip_Hardwick" title="Philip Hardwick">Philip Hardwick</a> (1792–1870), son of his tutor, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardwick" title="Thomas Hardwick">Thomas Hardwick</a>, was in charge of making the funeral arrangements and wrote to those who knew Turner to tell them at the time of his death that, "I must inform you, we have lost him." Other executors were his cousin and chief mourner at the funeral, Henry Harpur IV (benefactor of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Hospital" title="Westminster Hospital">Westminster – now Chelsea &amp; Westminster – Hospital</a>), Revd. Henry Scott Trimmer, <a href="/wiki/George_Jones_(painter)" title="George Jones (painter)">George Jones RA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Turner_(engraver)" title="Charles Turner (engraver)">Charles Turner ARA</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Turner's talent was recognised early in his life. Financial independence allowed Turner to innovate freely; his mature work is characterised by a chromatic palette and broadly applied atmospheric washes of paint. According to David Piper's <i>The Illustrated History of Art</i>, his later pictures were called "fantastic puzzles." Turner was recognised as an artistic genius: influential <a href="/wiki/English_art" title="English art">English art</a> critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> described him as the artist who could most "stirringly and truthfully measure the moods of Nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his own time his work also met with criticism from more traditional contemporaries for its unique style, in particular <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Beaumont,_7th_Baronet" title="Sir George Beaumont, 7th Baronet">Sir George Beaumont</a>, a landscape painter and fellow member of the Royal Academy, who described his paintings as 'blots'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suitable vehicles for Turner's imagination were found in shipwrecks, fires (such as the <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Parliament" title="Burning of Parliament">burning of Parliament</a> in 1834, an event which Turner rushed to witness first-hand, and which he transcribed in a series of watercolour sketches), natural catastrophes, and natural phenomena such as sunlight, storm, rain, and fog. He was fascinated by the violent power of the sea, as seen in <i>Dawn after the Wreck</i> (1840) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Slave_Ship" title="The Slave Ship">The Slave Ship</a></i> (1840).</p>

<p>Turner's major venture into printmaking was the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Studiorum" title="Liber Studiorum">Liber Studiorum</a></i> (Book of Studies), seventy prints that he worked on from 1806 to 1819. The <i>Liber Studiorum</i> was an expression of his intentions for landscape art. The idea was loosely based on <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lorrain" title="Claude Lorrain">Claude Lorrain</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Veritatis" title="Liber Veritatis">Liber Veritatis</a></i> (Book of Truth), where Claude had recorded his completed paintings; a series of print copies of these drawings, by then at <a href="/wiki/Devonshire_House" title="Devonshire House">Devonshire House</a>, had been a huge publishing success. Turner's plates were meant to be widely disseminated, and categorised the genre into six types: Marine, Mountainous, Pastoral, Historical, Architectural, and Elevated or Epic Pastoral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> His printmaking was a major part of his output, and a museum is devoted to it, the Turner Museum in <a href="/wiki/Sarasota,_Florida" title="Sarasota, Florida">Sarasota, Florida</a>, founded in 1974 by Douglass Montrose-Graem to house his collection of Turner prints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Turner placed human beings in many of his paintings to indicate his affection for humanity on the one hand (note the frequent scenes of people drinking and merry-making or working in the foreground), but its vulnerability and vulgarity amid the 'sublime' nature of the world on the other. 'Sublime' here means awe-inspiring, savage grandeur, a natural world unmastered by man, evidence of the power of God – a theme that romanticist artists and poets were exploring in this period. To Turner, light was the emanation of God's spirit and this was why he focused the subject matter of his later paintings by leaving out distractions such as solid objects and detail, concentrating on the play of light on water, the radiance of skies and fires. Although these late paintings appear to be 'impressionistic' and therefore a forerunner of the French school, Turner was striving for expression of spirituality in the world, rather than responding primarily to optical phenomena.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>His early works, such as <i><a href="/wiki/File:Turner_Tintern1.jpg" title="File:Turner Tintern1.jpg">Tintern Abbey</a></i> (1795), stayed true to the traditions of English landscape. However, in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_Storm:_Hannibal_and_His_Army_Crossing_the_Alps" title="Snow Storm: Hannibal and His Army Crossing the Alps">Hannibal Crossing the Alps</a></i> (1812), an emphasis on the destructive power of nature had already come into play. His distinctive style of painting, in which he used watercolour technique with oil paints, created lightness, fluency, and ephemeral atmospheric effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his later years he used oils ever more transparently, and turned to an evocation of almost pure light by use of shimmering colour. A prime example of his mature style can be seen in <i><a href="/wiki/Rain,_Steam_and_Speed_%E2%80%93_The_Great_Western_Railway" title="Rain, Steam and Speed – The Great Western Railway">Rain, Steam and Speed – The Great Western Railway</a></i>, where the objects are barely recognisable. The intensity of hue and interest in evanescent light not only placed Turner's work in the vanguard of English painting, but exerted an influence on art in France; the <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionists</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a>, carefully studied his techniques.</p>
<p>High levels of volcanic ash (from the eruption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Tambora" title="Mt. Tambora">Mt. Tambora</a>) in the atmosphere during 1816, the "<a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">Year Without a Summer</a>", led to unusually spectacular sunsets during this period, and were an inspiration for some of Turner's work.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> says in his "Notes" on Turner in March 1878, that an early patron, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Monro" title="Thomas Monro">Thomas Monro</a>, the Principal Physician of <a href="/wiki/Bethlem_Royal_Hospital" title="Bethlem Royal Hospital">Bedlam</a>, and also a collector and amateur artist, was a significant influence on Turner's style:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>His true master was Dr Monro; to the practical teaching of that first patron and the wise simplicity of method of watercolour study, in which he was disciplined by him and companioned by his friend <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Girtin" title="Thomas Girtin">Girtin</a>, the healthy and constant development of the greater power is primarily to be attributed; the greatness of the power itself, it is impossible to over-estimate.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Together with a number of young artists Turner was able, in the London house of Dr. Monro, to copy works of the major topographical draughtsmen of his time and perfect his skills in drawing. But the curious atmospherical effects and illusions of the watercolours of <a href="/wiki/John_Robert_Cozens" title="John Robert Cozens">John Robert Cozens</a>, some of which were present in Monro's house, went far further than the neat renderings of topography. The solemn grandeur of his Alpine views were an early revelation to the young artist and showed him the true potential of the watercolour medium, conveying mood instead of information.</p>
<p>On a trip to Europe, circa 1820, he met the Irish physician <a href="/wiki/Robert_James_Graves" title="Robert James Graves">Robert James Graves</a>. Graves was travelling in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diligence_(vehicle)" title="Diligence (vehicle)">diligence</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> when a man who looked like the mate of a ship got in, sat beside him, and soon took from his pocket a note-book across which his hand from time to time passed with the rapidity of lightning. Graves wondered if the man was insane, he looked, saw that the stranger had been noting the forms of clouds as they passed and that he was no common artist. The two travelled and sketched together for months. Graves tells that Turner would outline a scene, sit doing nothing for two or three days, then suddenly, "perhaps on the third day, he would exclaim 'there it is', and seizing his colours work rapidly till he had noted down the peculiar effect he wished to fix in his memory."</p>

<p>The first American to buy a Turner painting was <a href="/wiki/James_Lenox" title="James Lenox">James Lenox</a> of New York City, a private collector. Lenox wished to own a Turner and in 1845 bought one unseen through an intermediary, his friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._R._Leslie" title="C. R. Leslie">C. R. Leslie</a>. From among the paintings Turner had on hand and was willing to sell for £500, Leslie selected and shipped the 1832 atmospheric seascape <i>Staffa, Fingal's Cave</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Worried about the painting's reception by Lenox, who knew Turner's work only through etchings, Leslie wrote to Lenox that the quality of <i>Staffa</i>, "a most poetic picture of a steam boat" would become apparent in time. On receiving the painting Lenox was baffled, and "greatly disappointed" by what he called the painting's "indistinctness". When Leslie was forced to relay this opinion to Turner, Turner said "You should tell Mr Lenox that indistinctness is my forte." <i>Staffa, Fingal's Cave</i> is now owned by the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Center_for_British_Art" title="Yale Center for British Art">Yale Center for British Art</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Turner used or experimented with a wide variety of pigments, using several within a few years of their dates of discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Turner used pigments like <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">carmine</a> in his paintings, despite knowing that they were not long-lasting, and against the advice of contemporary experts to use more durable pigments. As a result, many of his colours have now faded. John Ruskin complained at how quickly Turner's work decayed; Turner was indifferent to posterity and chose materials that looked good when freshly applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> By 1930 there was concern that both his oils and his watercolours were fading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perth_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-perth-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Turner left a small fortune which he hoped would be used to support what he called "decayed artists". He planned and designed an almshouse for them at <a href="/wiki/Twickenham" title="Twickenham">Twickenham</a> with a gallery for some of his works. His will was contested and in 1856, after a court battle, his first cousins, including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Price_Turner" title="Thomas Price Turner">Thomas Price Turner</a>, received part of his fortune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monkhouse_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monkhouse-39">[39]</a></sup> Another portion went to the Royal Academy of Arts, which occasionally awards students the Turner Medal. His collection of finished paintings was bequeathed to the British nation, and he intended that a special gallery would be built to house them. This did not happen because of a failure to agree on a site, and the parsimony of British governments. Twenty-two years after his death, the British Parliament passed an act allowing his paintings to be lent to museums outside London, and so began the process of scattering the pictures which Turner had wanted to be kept together.</p>
<p>In 1910, the main part of the Turner Bequest, which includes unfinished paintings and drawings, was rehoused in the Duveen Turner Wing at the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">National Gallery of British Art</a> (now Tate Britain). In 1987, a new wing at the Tate, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clore_Gallery" title="Clore Gallery">Clore Gallery</a>, was opened to house the Turner bequest, though some of the most important paintings remain in the National Gallery in contravention of Turner's condition that they be kept and shown together. Increasingly paintings are lent abroad, ignoring Turner's provision that they remain constantly and permanently in Turner's Gallery.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Church,_Battersea" title="St. Mary's Church, Battersea">St. Mary's Church, Battersea</a> added a commemorative stained glass window for Turner, between 1976 and 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> St Paul's Cathedral, Royal Academy of Arts and Victoria &amp; Albert Museum all hold statues representing him. A portrait drawing by Cornelius Varley with his patent graphic telescope (Sheffield Museums &amp; Galleries) was compared with his death mask (National Portrait Gallery, London) by Kelly Freeman at Dundee University 2009–10 to ascertain whether it really depicts Turner. The city of Westminster unveiled a memorial plaque at the site of his birthplace at 21 Maiden Lane, <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a> 2 June 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Selby Whittingham founded The Turner Society at London and Manchester in 1975. After the society endorsed the Tate Gallery's Clore Gallery wing (on the lines of the Duveen wing of 1910), as the solution to the controversy of what should be done with the Turner Bequest, Selby Whittingham resigned and founded the Independent Turner Society. The Tate created the prestigious annual <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> art award in 1984, named in Turner's honour, and 20 years later the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_Painters_in_Water_Colours" title="Royal Institute of Painters in Water Colours">Royal Institute of Painters in Water Colours</a> founded the Winsor &amp; Newton Turner Watercolour Award. A major exhibition, "Turner's Britain", with material (including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fighting_Temeraire" title="The Fighting Temeraire">The Fighting Temeraire</a></i>) on loan from around the globe, was held at <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Museum_and_Art_Gallery" title="Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery">Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery</a> from 7 November 2003 to 8 February 2004. In 2005, Turner's <i>The Fighting Temeraire</i> was voted Britain's "greatest painting" in a public poll organised by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatest_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-greatest-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 April 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a> announced that Turner would appear on the next issue of the <a href="/wiki/Banknotes_of_the_pound_sterling" title="Banknotes of the pound sterling">£20 note</a>, as the result of a selection process to choose a representative from the field of visual arts. The Turner note is due to enter circulation in 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Portrayal_in_theatre,_television_and_film"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayal_in_theatre.2C_television_and_film">Portrayal in theatre, television and film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portrayal in theatre, television and film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leo_McKern" title="Leo McKern">Leo McKern</a> played Turner in <i>The Sun is God</i>, a 1974 <a href="/wiki/Thames_Television" title="Thames Television">Thames Television</a> production directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Darlow" title="Michael Darlow">Michael Darlow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The programme was shown on 17 December 1974, during the Turner Bicentenary Exhibition in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2011 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Painter_(play)" title="The Painter (play)">The Painter</a></i>, a biographical play on his life by <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Lenkiewicz" title="Rebecca Lenkiewicz">Rebecca Lenkiewicz</a>, premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Arcola_Theatre" title="Arcola Theatre">Arcola Theatre</a> in London.</p>
<p>British filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Mike_Leigh" title="Mike Leigh">Mike Leigh</a> wrote and directed <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Turner" title="Mr. Turner">Mr. Turner</a></i>, a biopic of Turner's later years, released in 2014. The film starred <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Spall" title="Timothy Spall">Timothy Spall</a> as Turner, Dorothy Atkinson, Marion Bailey and Paul Jesson, and premiered in competition for the Palme d'Or at the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>, with Spall taking the award for Best Actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-child_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-child-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ch4_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-ch4-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_works">Selected works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Selected works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>a.</b> <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_Anone"><a href="#ref_Anone"><b><sup>^</sup></b></a></span> Although Turner was known by his middle name, William, he is now generally referred to by his initials, in order to avoid confusion with another artist named <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(artist)" title="William Turner (artist)">William Turner</a>.</p>
<p><b>b.</b> <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_Bnone"><a href="#ref_Bnone"><b><sup>^</sup></b></a></span> Turner is believed to have been born some time between late April and early May 1775; his exact date of birth is unknown. The date of 23 April, which is both <a href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_Day" title="Saint George's Day">Saint George's Day</a> and the supposed birthday of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, was the date Turner himself claimed to have been born, but this claim has never been verified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shanes2008_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-shanes2008-3">[3]</a></sup> The first verifiable date is that Turner was baptised on 14 May. This casts some doubt on 23 April date, as high infant mortality rates meant parents would usually baptise their children shortly after birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>c.</b><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_Cnone"><a href="#ref_Cnone"><b><sup>^</sup></b></a></span> Mary Marshall died in 1804, after having been committed in 1799 to St Luke's Hospital and then to the <a href="/wiki/Bethlem_Royal_Hospital" title="Bethlem Royal Hospital">Bethlem Royal Hospital</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_asylum" title="Mental asylum">mental asylum</a>. Her illness possibly due in part to the early death of Turner's younger sister. Hamilton suggests that this "fit of illness" may have been an early sign of her madness.</p>
