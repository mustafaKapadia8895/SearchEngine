<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Vertical draft</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Downdraft&amp;redirect=no" title="Downdraft">Downdraft</a>)</span></div>

<p>An <b>updraft</b> is a small‐scale current of rising air, often within a cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Localized regions of warm or cool air will exhibit vertical movement. A mass of warm air will typically be less dense than the surrounding region, and so will rise until it reaches air that is either warmer or less dense than itself. The converse will occur for a mass of cool air, and is known as <a href="/wiki/Subsidence_(atmosphere)" title="Subsidence (atmosphere)">subsidence</a>. This movement of large volumes of air, especially when regions of hot, wet air rise, can create large <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>, and is the central source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a>. Drafts can also be conceived by low or high pressure regions. A low pressure region will attract air from the surrounding area, which will move towards the center and then rise, creating an updraft. A high pressure region will then attract air from the surrounding area, which will move towards the center and sink, spawning a <b>downdraft</b>.</p>
<p>Updrafts and downdrafts, along with <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> in general, are a major contributor to airplane crashes during takeoff and landing in a thunderstorm. Extreme cases, known as <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">microbursts</a> can be deadly and difficult to predict or observe. The crash of <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines_Flight_191" title="Delta Air Lines Flight 191">Delta Air Lines Flight 191</a> on its final approach before landing at <a href="/wiki/Dallas/Fort_Worth_International_Airport" title="Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport">Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport</a> in 1985 was presumably caused by a microburst, and prompted the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> (FAA) to research and deploy new storm detection radar stations at some of the major airports, notably the ones in the South, Midwest, and Northeast United States where wind shear affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_safety" title="Air safety">air safety</a>. Downbursts can cause extensive localized damage, similar to that caused by <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. Downburst damage can be differentiated from that of a tornado because the resulting destruction is circular and radiates away from the center. Tornado damage radiates inward, towards the center of the damage.</p>
<p>The term downdraft can also refer to a type of <a href="/wiki/Backdraft" title="Backdraft">backdraft</a> which occurs through chimneys which have fireplaces on the lowermost levels (such as basements) of multi-level buildings. It involves cold air coming down the chimney due to low air pressure, and makes it hard to light fires, and can push soot and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> into domiciles.</p>
