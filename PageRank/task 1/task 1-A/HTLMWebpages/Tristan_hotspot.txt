<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tristan hotspot</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Tristan hotspot</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> which is responsible for the volcanic activity which forms the volcanoes in the southern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. It is thought to have formed the island of <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Ridge" title="Walvis Ridge">Walvis Ridge</a> on the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African Plate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairhead-Wilson-539_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fairhead-Wilson-539-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_hotspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is also believed to be closely connected with the <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_and_Etendeka_traps" title="Paraná and Etendeka traps">Paraná and Etendeka</a> flood basalt provinces, which formed over the hotspot during the opening of the South Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Tristan and <a href="/wiki/Gough_Island" title="Gough Island">Gough</a> hotspots are widely spaced end-members of the volcanic system that generated the Walvis Ridge on the African plate and the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_Rise" title="Rio Grande Rise">Rio Grande Rise</a> on the South American plate beginning at <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=129–133">129 to 133</a> Ma</span> when the Paraná and Etendeka traps formed. Whether or not Tristan and Gough represent two distinct volcanic centres is still debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="#CITEREFFairheadWilson2005">Fairhead &amp; Wilson 2005</a> argued that the Walvis Ridge is not the product of a deep mantle plume or the Tristan hotspot, but that changes in the internal stress in the spreading African and South American plates trigger changes in the magmatic processes in the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. The Walvis Ridge should then have developed as a result of these periodic stress releases along the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairhead-Wilson-Abst_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fairhead-Wilson-Abst-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="#CITEREFO.27ConnorJokatle_RoexClass2012">O'Connor et al. 2012</a> found that the hotspot trails in the eastern South Atlantic (Tristan, Gough, Discovery, <a href="/wiki/Shona_hotspot" title="Shona hotspot">Shona</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Bouvet_Island" title="Bouvet Island">Bouvet</a>) started forming synchronously <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=44–41">44 to 41</a> Ma</span>. Older <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a> east of these hotspot trails formed at the edge of the <a href="/wiki/African_superswell" title="African superswell">African swell</a> where the oceanic crust was spreading apart and are not the product of hotspot volcanism. The Tristan-Gough trail switched from forming a series of aseismic ridges to a broader line of <a href="/wiki/Guyot" title="Guyot">guyots</a> and smaller volcanic ridges at about the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Connor-etal-2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Connor-etal-2012-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hotspots of the eastern South Atlantic formed along the plume generation zones (PGZs) of the African <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_Low_Shear_Velocity_Provinces" title="Large Low Shear Velocity Provinces">large low seismic velocity province</a> (LLSVP). LLSVPs are dense and stable structures, and most of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">plumes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kimberlite" title="Kimberlite">kimberlites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous provinces</a> (LIPs) on Earth can be rotated back to the PGZs. All major African LIPs (<a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_and_Etendeka_traps" title="Paraná and Etendeka traps">Paraná-Etendeka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Plateau" title="Agulhas Plateau">Agulhas</a>) formed at the PGZs of the African LLSVP but <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=132–100">132 to 100</a> Ma</span> the Tristan-Gough hotspot was the only hotspot located at a spreading boundary and therefore the only African hotspot that started to form a volcanic trail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Connor-etal-2012_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Connor-etal-2012-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeoclimatic_and_evolutionary_role">Palaeoclimatic and evolutionary role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_hotspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Palaeoclimatic and evolutionary role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on molecular estimates, <a href="#CITEREFArnasonGullbergSchweizer_BurgeteJanke2000">Arnason et al. 2000</a> found that the <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee%E2%80%93human_last_common_ancestor" title="Chimpanzee–human last common ancestor"><i>Homo</i>-<i>Pan</i> divergence</a> occurred <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=13–10.5">13 to 10.5</a> Ma</span> (in contrast to earlier estimates that suggested around 5 Ma).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Arnason et al. also estimated the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platyrrhini" title="Platyrrhini">Platyrrhini</a></i>-<i><a href="/wiki/Catarrhini" title="Catarrhini">Catarrhini</a></i> divergence to have occurred around <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=70">70</a> Ma</span> and found that the latter evolved in South America before their dispersal into Africa. Arnason et al. hypothesised that the Rio Grande-Walvis Ridge system was exposed above sea level and formed a chain of islands across the South Atlantic during the <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeocene" title="Palaeocene">Palaeocene</a> <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=70–65">70 to 65</a> Ma</span> allowing the primates to disperse across the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Other researchers dismiss the hypothesis of Arnason et al. instead favouring a less spectacular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rafting_event" title="Rafting event">rafting event</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other hints of transoceanic <a href="/wiki/Island_hopping" title="Island hopping">island hopping</a> in the fossil record is <i><a href="/wiki/Lavocatavis" title="Lavocatavis">Lavocatavis</a></i>, a flightless <a href="/wiki/Phorusrhacidae" title="Phorusrhacidae">terror bird</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> layers in Algeria, the only found outside South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
