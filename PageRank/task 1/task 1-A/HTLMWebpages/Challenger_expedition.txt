<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Challenger</i> expedition</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;"><a href="/wiki/HMS_Challenger_(1858)" title="HMS Challenger (1858)">HMS <i>Challenger</i></a> in 1874</div>

<p>The <b><i>Challenger</i> expedition</b> of 1872–76 was a scientific exercise that made many discoveries to lay the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>. The expedition was named after the mother vessel, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Challenger_(1858)" title="HMS Challenger (1858)">HMS <i>Challenger</i></a>.</p>
<p>Prompted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wyville_Thomson" title="Charles Wyville Thomson">Charles Wyville Thomson</a>—of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merchiston_Castle_School" title="Merchiston Castle School">Merchiston Castle School</a>—the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_London" title="Royal Society of London">Royal Society of London</a> obtained the use of <i>Challenger</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and in 1872 modified the ship for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_work" title="Scientific work">scientific work</a>, equipping her with separate laboratories for <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. The expedition, led by Captain <a href="/wiki/George_Nares" title="George Nares">George Nares</a>, sailed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_England" title="Portsmouth, England">Portsmouth</a>, England, on 21 December 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rice_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-1">[1]</a></sup> Other naval officers included Commander <a href="/wiki/John_Maclear" title="John Maclear">John Maclear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_list_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-crew_list-2">[2]</a></sup> Under the scientific supervision of Thomson himself, she traveled nearly 70,000 nautical miles (130,000 km) surveying and exploring. The result was the <i>Report Of The Scientific Results of the Exploring Voyage of H.M.S. Challenger during the years 1873-76</i> which, among many other discoveries, cataloged over 4,000 previously unknown species. <a href="/wiki/John_Murray_(oceanographer)" title="John Murray (oceanographer)">John Murray</a>, who supervised the publication, described the report as "the greatest advance in the knowledge of our planet since the celebrated discoveries of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries". <i>Challenger</i> sailed close to <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, but not within sight of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aga_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-aga-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Challenger_expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>As recently as the late 19th century, human knowledge of the oceans was confined to the topmost few <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathoms</a> of the water and a small amount of the bottom, mainly in shallow areas. Sailors and scientists knew almost nothing of the ocean depths. The Royal Navy's efforts to chart all of the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline">coastlines</a> in the mid-19th century reinforced the vague idea that most of the ocean was very deep, although little more was known. As exploration ignited both popular and scientific interest in the polar regions and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, so too did the mysteries of the unexplored oceans.</p>
<p>To enable her to probe the depths, <i>Challenger's</i> guns were removed and her spars reduced to make more space available. Laboratories, extra cabins and a special <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredging</a> platform were installed. She was loaded with specimen jars, filled with <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> for preservation of samples, microscopes and chemical apparatus, trawls and dredges, thermometers and water sampling bottles, <a href="/wiki/Depth_sounding" title="Depth sounding">sounding leads</a> and devices to collect sediment from the sea bed and great lengths of rope with which to suspend the equipment into the ocean depths. Because of the novelty of the expedition, some of the equipment was invented or specially modified for the occasion. In all, she was supplied with 181 miles (291 km) of Italian <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> for <a href="/wiki/Depth_sounding" title="Depth sounding">sounding</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expedition">Expedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Challenger_expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Challenger_negatives_box_Picture_0105.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of the original boxes containing the photographic negatives brought back from the expedition</div>

<p>On her 68,890-nautical-mile (127,580 km) journey circumnavigating the globe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rice_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-1">[1]</a></sup> 492 deep sea soundings, 133 bottom dredges, 151 open water trawls and 263 serial water temperature observations were taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Also about 4,700 new species of marine life were discovered.</p>
<p>The scientific work was conducted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wyville_Thomson" title="Wyville Thomson">Wyville Thomson</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Murray_(oceanographer)" title="John Murray (oceanographer)">John Murray</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Young_Buchanan" title="John Young Buchanan">John Young Buchanan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Nottidge_Moseley" title="Henry Nottidge Moseley">Henry Nottidge Moseley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_von_Willemoes-Suhm" title="Rudolf von Willemoes-Suhm">Rudolf von Willemoes-Suhm</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Evers_Beddard" title="Frank Evers Beddard">Frank Evers Beddard</a> was appointed <a href="/wiki/Prosector" title="Prosector">Prosector</a>. The official expedition artist was <a href="/wiki/John_James_Wild" title="John James Wild">John James Wild</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_list_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-crew_list-2">[2]</a></sup> As well as Nares and Maclear, others that were part of the naval crew included <a href="/wiki/Pelham_Aldrich" title="Pelham Aldrich">Pelham Aldrich</a>, Lord <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Granville_Campbell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Granville Campbell (page does not exist)">George Granville Campbell</a>, and Andrew Francis Balfour (one of the sons of Scottish botanist <a href="/wiki/John_Hutton_Balfour" title="John Hutton Balfour">John Hutton Balfour</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_list_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-crew_list-2">[2]</a></sup> Also among the officers was <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Tizard" title="Thomas Henry Tizard">Thomas Henry Tizard</a>, who had carried out important hydrographic observations on previous voyages. Though he was not among the civilian scientific staff, Tizard would later help write the official account of the expedition, and also become a Fellow of the Royal Society.</p>
<p>The original ship's complement included 21 officers and around 216 crew members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-5">[5]</a></sup> By the end of the voyage, this had reduced to 144 due to deaths, desertions, being left ashore due to illness, and planned departures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Challenger</i> reached <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> in December 1874, at which point Nares and Aldrich left the ship to take part in the <a href="/wiki/British_Arctic_Expedition" title="British Arctic Expedition">British Arctic Expedition</a>. The new captain was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Tourle_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frank Tourle Thomson (page does not exist)">Frank Tourle Thomson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_list_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-crew_list-2">[2]</a></sup> The second-in-command, and the most senior officer present throughout the entire expedition, was Commander <a href="/wiki/John_Maclear" title="John Maclear">John Maclear</a>. Willemoes-Suhm died and was buried at sea on the voyage to Tahiti. Lord Campbell and Balfour left the ship in Valparaiso, Chile, after being promoted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_list_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-crew_list-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first leg of the expedition took the ship from <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> (December 1872) south to <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> (January 1873) and then on to <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>. The next stops were <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> (both February 1873). The period from February to July 1873 was spent crossing the Atlantic westwards from the Canary Islands to the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>, then heading north to <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, east to the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, back to Madeira, and then south to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a>. During this period, there was a detour in April and May 1873, sailing from Bermuda north to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" title="City of Halifax">Halifax</a> and back, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> twice with the reverse journey crossing further to the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After leaving the Cape Verde Islands in August 1873, the expedition initially sailed south-east and then headed west to reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Rocks" title="St Paul's Rocks">St Paul's Rocks</a>. From here, the route went south across the equator to <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha" title="Fernando de Noronha">Fernando de Noronha</a> during September 1873, and onwards that same month to <a href="/wiki/Salvador,_Bahia" title="Salvador, Bahia">Bahia</a> (now called Salvador) in Brazil. The period from September to October 1873 was spent crossing the Atlantic from Bahia to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, touching at <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a> on the way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>December 1873 to February 1874 was spent sailing on a roughly south-eastern track from the Cape of Good Hope to the parallel of <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_south" title="60th parallel south">60 degrees south</a>. The islands visited during this period were the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Islands" title="Prince Edward Islands">Prince Edward Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crozet_Islands" title="Crozet Islands">Crozet Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen Islands</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heard_Island" title="Heard Island">Heard Island</a>. February 1874 was spent travelling south and then generally eastwards in the vicinity of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>, with sightings of icebergs, pack ice and whales. The route then took the ship north-eastward and away from the ice regions in March 1874, with the expedition reaching <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> in Australia later that month. The journey eastward along the coast from Melbourne to Sydney took place in April 1874, passing by <a href="/wiki/Wilsons_Promontory" title="Wilsons Promontory">Wilsons Promontory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Howe" title="Cape Howe">Cape Howe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>When the voyage resumed in June 1874, the route went east from Sydney to <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> in New Zealand, followed by a large loop north into the Pacific calling at <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, and then back westward to <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York</a> in Australia by the end of August. The ship arrived in New Zealand in late June and left in early July. Before reaching Wellington (on New Zealand's North Island), brief stops were made at Port Hardy (on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%27Urville_Island,_New_Zealand" title="D'Urville Island, New Zealand">d'Urville Island</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_Sound_(New_Zealand)" title="Queen Charlotte Sound (New Zealand)">Queen Charlotte Sound</a> (on New Zealand's South Island) and <i>Challenger</i> passed through the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Strait" title="Cook Strait">Cook Strait</a> to reach Wellington. The route from Wellington to Tonga went along the east coast of New Zealand's North Island, and then north and east into the open Pacific, passing by the <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Islands" title="Kermadec Islands">Kermadec Islands</a> en route to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongatabu" title="Tongatabu">Tongatabu</a>, the main island of the Tonga archipelago (then known as the Friendly Islands). The waters around the Fijian islands, a short distance to the north-west of Tonga, were surveyed during late July and early August 1874. The ship's course was then set westward, reaching <a href="/wiki/Raine_Island" title="Raine Island">Raine Island</a> (on the outer edge of the Great Barrier Reef) at the end of August and thence arriving at Cape York, at the tip of Australia's <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the following three months (September to November 1874), the expedition visited several islands and island groups while sailing from Cape York to China and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> (then a British colony). The first part of the route passed north and west over the <a href="/wiki/Arafura_Sea" title="Arafura Sea">Arafura Sea</a>, with <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> to the north-east and the Australian mainland to the south-west. The first islands visited were the <a href="/wiki/Aru_Islands" title="Aru Islands">Aru Islands</a>, followed by the nearby <a href="/wiki/Kai_Islands" title="Kai Islands">Kai Islands</a>. The ship then crossed the <a href="/wiki/Banda_Sea" title="Banda Sea">Banda Sea</a> touching at the <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda Islands</a>, to reach Amboina (<a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">Ambon Island</a>) in October 1874, and then continuing to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ternate_Island" title="Ternate Island">Ternate Island</a>. All these islands are now part of modern-day Indonesia. From Ternate, the route went north-westward towards the Philippines, passing east of Celebes (<a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>) into the <a href="/wiki/Celebes_Sea" title="Celebes Sea">Celebes Sea</a>. The expedition called at Samboangan (<a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_City" title="Zamboanga City">Zamboanga</a>) on Mindanao, and then <a href="/wiki/Iloilo" title="Iloilo">Iloilo</a> on the island of Panay, before navigating within the interior of the archipelago en route to the bay and harbour of <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> on the island of Luzon. The crossing north-westward from Manila to Hong Kong took place in November 1874.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After several weeks in Hong Kong, the expedition departed in early January 1875 to retrace their route south-east towards New Guinea. The first stop on this outward leg of the journey was Manila. From there, they continued on to Samboangan, but took a different route through the interior of the Philippines, this time touching at the island of Zebu (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cebu_(island)" title="Cebu (island)">Cebu</a>). From Samboangan the ship diverged from the inward route, this time passing south of Mindanao (in early February 1875). <i>Challenger</i> then headed east into the open sea, before turning to the south-east and making landfall at Humboldt Bay (now <a href="/wiki/Yos_Sudarso_Bay" title="Yos Sudarso Bay">Yos Sudarso Bay</a>) on the north coast of New Guinea. By March 1875, the expedition had reached the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_Islands" title="Admiralty Islands">Admiralty Islands</a> north-east of New Guinea. The final stage of the voyage on this side of the Pacific was a long journey across the open ocean to the north, passing mostly west of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolina_Islands" title="Carolina Islands">Carolina Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>, reaching port in <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a>, Japan, in April 1875.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Challenger</i> departed Japan in mid-June 1875, heading east across the Pacific to a point due north of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Sandwich Islands</a> (Hawaii), and then turning south, making landfall at the end of July at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honolulu,_Hawaii" title="Honolulu, Hawaii">Honolulu</a> on the Hawaiian island of <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a>. A couple of weeks later, in mid-August, the ship departed south-eastward, anchoring at <a href="/wiki/Hilo_Bay" title="Hilo Bay">Hilo Bay</a> off Hawaii's Big Island, before continuing to the south and reaching <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> in mid-September. The expedition left Tahiti in early October, swinging to the west and south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tubuai_Islands" title="Tubuai Islands">Tubuai Islands</a> and then heading to the south-east before turning east towards the South American coast. The route touched at the <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands" title="Juan Fernández Islands">Juan Fernández Islands</a> in mid-November 1875, with <i>Challenger</i> reaching the port of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valparaiso" title="Valparaiso">Valparaiso</a> in Chile a few days later. The next stage of the journey commenced the following month, with the route taking the ship south-westward back out into the Pacific, past the Juan Fernández Islands, before turning to the south-east and back towards South America, reaching Port Otway in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Penas" title="Gulf of Penas">Gulf of Penas</a> on 31 December 1875.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of January 1876 was spent navigating around the southern tip of South America, surveying and touching at many of the bays and islands of the Patagonian archipelago, the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>. Locations visited here include Hale Cove, Gray Harbour, Port Grappler, Tom Bay (all in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Wellington_Island" title="Wellington Island">Wellington Island</a>), Puerta Bueno (near <a href="/wiki/Hanover_Island" title="Hanover Island">Hanover Island</a>), Isthmus Bay (near the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Adelaide_Archipelago" title="Queen Adelaide Archipelago">Queen Adelaide Archipelago</a>), and Port Churruca (near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Ines_Island" title="Santa Ines Island">Santa Ines Island</a>). The final stops, before heading out into the Atlantic, were <a href="/wiki/Puerto_del_Hambre" title="Puerto del Hambre">Port Famine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandy_Point,_Chile" title="Sandy Point, Chile">Sandy Point</a>, and Elizabeth Island. <i>Challenger</i> reached the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> towards the end of January, calling at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Stanley" title="Port Stanley">Port Stanley</a> and then continuing northward, reaching <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a> in Uruguay in mid-February 1876. The ship left Montevideo at the end of February, heading first due east and then due north, arriving at <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension Island</a> at the end of March 1876. The period from early to mid-April was spent sailing from Ascension Island to the Cape Verde Islands (visited almost three years ago on the outward journey). From here, the route taken in late April and early May 1876 was a westward loop to the north out into the mid-Atlantic, eventually turning due east towards Europe to touch land at <a href="/wiki/Vigo" title="Vigo">Vigo</a> in Spain towards the end of May. The final stage of the voyage took the ship and its crew north-eastward from Vigo, skirting the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> to make landfall in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_charts_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-official_charts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Challenger</i> returned to <a href="/wiki/Spithead" title="Spithead">Spithead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, on 24 May 1876, having spent 713 days at sea out of the intervening 1,250.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rice_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-1">[1]</a></sup> The complete set of reports of the <i>Challenger</i> expedition, published in 50 volumes between 1877 and 1895, are available online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_objectives_and_observation_methods">Scientific objectives and observation methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Challenger_expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific objectives and observation methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> stated the voyage’s scientific goals as:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>To investigate the physical conditions of the deep sea in the great ocean basins (as far as the neighborhood of the Great Southern Ice Barrier) in regard to depth, temperature, circulation, <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> and penetration of light.</p>
<p>To determine the chemical composition of seawater at various depths from the surface to the bottom, the organic matter in solution and the particles in suspension.</p>
<p>To ascertain the physical and chemical character of deep-sea deposits and the sources of these deposits.</p>
To investigate the distribution of organic life at different depths and on the deep seafloor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HMS_Challenger:_Science_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HMS_Challenger:_Science-8">[8]</a></sup></blockquote>
<p>At each of the 360 stations the crew measured the bottom depth, temperature at different depths, observed weather and surface ocean conditions, and collected seafloor, water, and biota samples.</p>
<p>The <i>Challenger</i> crew used methods that were developed in prior small-scale expeditions to make observations. To measure depth, the <i>Challenger</i> crew would lower a line with a weight attached to it until it reached the sea floor. The line was marked in 25 fathom intervals with flags denoting depth. Because of this, the depth measurements from the <i>Challenger</i> were at best accurate to 25 fathoms, or approximately 45m. The sinker often had a small container attached to it that would allow for the collection of bottom sediment samples.</p>
<p>The crew used a variety of dredges and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawls</a> to collect biological samples. The dredges consisted of metal nets attached to a wooden plank and dragged across the sea floor. Mop heads attached to the wooden plank would sweep across the sea floor and release organisms from the ocean bottom to be caught in the nets. Trawls were large metal nets towed behind the ship to collect organisms at different depths of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Upon the retrieval of a dredge or trawl, <i>Challenger</i> crew would sort, rinse, and store the specimens for examination upon return. The specimens were often preserved in either brine or alcohol.</p>
<p>The primary thermometer used throughout the <i>Challenger</i> expedition was the <a href="/wiki/Miller-Casella_thermometer" title="Miller-Casella thermometer">Miller-Casella thermometer</a>, which contained two markers within a curved mercury tube to record the maximum and minimum temperature through which the instrument traveled. Several of these thermometers would be lowered at various depths for recording. However, this design assumed that the water closer to the surface of the ocean was always warmer than that below. During the voyage, <i>Challenger</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> crew tested the <a href="/wiki/Reversing_thermometer" title="Reversing thermometer">reversing thermometer</a>, which could measure temperature at specified depths. Afterwards, this type of thermometer was used extensively until the second half of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HMS_Challenger:_Science_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HMS_Challenger:_Science-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_Challenger_Deep">Discovery of Challenger Deep</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Challenger_expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Discovery of Challenger Deep">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 23, 1875, at sample station number 225 located in the southwest Pacific Ocean between Guam and Palau, the crew recorded a sounding of 4,475 fathoms, (8,184 meters) deep, which was confirmed by an additional sounding. As shown by later expeditions using modern equipment, this area represents the southern end of the Mariana trench and is one of the deepest known places on the ocean floor. Modern soundings to 10,994 meters have since been found near the site of the <i>Challenger</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> original sounding. <i>Challenger</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> discovery of this depth was a key finding of the expedition in broadening oceanographic horizons on the ocean’s depth and extent and now bears the vessel's name, the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Findings_and_legacy">Findings and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Challenger_expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Findings and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Findings from the <i>Challenger</i> expedition continued to be published until 1895, 19 years after the completion of its journey. The report contained 50 volumes and was over 29,500 pages in length. Specimens brought back by the <i>Challenger</i> were distributed to the world's foremost experts for examination, which greatly increased the expenses and time required to finalize the report. The report and specimens are currently held at the <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">British Natural History Museum</a> and the report has been made available online. Some specimens, many of which were the first discovered of their kind, are still examined by scientists today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A large number of scientists worked on categorising the material brought back from the expedition including the palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Warton_Lee" title="Gabriel Warton Lee">Gabriel Warton Lee</a>.</p>
<p>As the first true oceanographic cruise, the <i>Challenger</i> expedition laid the groundwork for an entire academic and research discipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-5">[5]</a></sup> The name of the <i>Challenger</i> was applied to such varied phenomena as the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Society_for_Marine_Science" title="Challenger Society for Marine Science">Challenger Society for Marine Science</a>, the oceanographic and marine geological survey ship <i><a href="/wiki/Glomar_Challenger" title="Glomar Challenger">Glomar Challenger</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger" title="Space Shuttle Challenger">Space Shuttle <i>Challenger</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
