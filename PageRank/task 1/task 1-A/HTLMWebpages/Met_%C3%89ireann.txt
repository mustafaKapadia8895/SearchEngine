<p><b>Met Éireann</b> (<small>Irish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[mʲɛtʲ ˈeːrʲən̪ˠ]</a></span>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Met</a> of Ireland") is the national <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> service in Ireland, part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Housing,_Planning,_Community_and_Local_Government" title="Department of Housing, Planning, Community and Local Government">Department of Housing, Planning, Community and Local Government</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_%C3%89ireann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Met_ireann_(Irish_Meteorological_Service)_(1387399963).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Met_ireann_%28Irish_Meteorological_Service%29_%281387399963%29.jpg/220px-Met_ireann_%28Irish_Meteorological_Service%29_%281387399963%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Met_ireann_%28Irish_Meteorological_Service%29_%281387399963%29.jpg/330px-Met_ireann_%28Irish_Meteorological_Service%29_%281387399963%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Met_ireann_%28Irish_Meteorological_Service%29_%281387399963%29.jpg/440px-Met_ireann_%28Irish_Meteorological_Service%29_%281387399963%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Met Éireann</div>

<p>The history of modern meteorology in Ireland dates back to 8 October 1860, when the first weather observations were transmitted from Valentia Observatory on <a href="/wiki/Valentia_Island" title="Valentia Island">Valentia Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">British Meteorological Office</a>. A network of weather stations was established around the coasts of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Irish independence</a> in 1922, the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> continued to rely on the British Met Office for weather services, and the Met Office continued to administer the weather stations around Ireland. With the advent of transatlantic air services in the 1930s, it was decided that Ireland needed its own weather service to provide detailed aviation weather reports.</p>
<p>In 1936, the <b>Irish Meteorological Service</b> was set up with its headquarters in St. Andrew's Street, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. In 1937 it took over the network of weather stations from the British Met Office. On the date of transfer, the Irish weather observation network consisted of the following stations:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Valentia Observatory, then the only permanently manned weather station</li>
<li>4 telegraphic stations (at <a href="/wiki/Malin_Head" title="Malin Head">Malin Head</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blacksod_Point&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blacksod Point (page does not exist)">Blacksod Point</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roches_Point&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roches Point (page does not exist)">Roches Point</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birr,_County_Offaly" title="Birr, County Offaly">Birr</a>)</li>
<li>18 climatological stations</li>
<li>172 rainfall stations</li>
</ul>
<p>At first, the new weather service was bolstered by Met Office employees seconded from London, but by 1941 they were able to depend on their own resources. During <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">the Emergency</a> (as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> was referred to in <a href="/wiki/Irish_neutrality" title="Irish neutrality">neutral Ireland</a>), the Irish Meteorological Service supplied the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> with weather information despite Ireland's neutrality. The decision to go ahead with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normandy_Landings" title="Normandy Landings">D-day landings</a> was made following a favourable weather report from the Blacksod Point weather station in <a href="/wiki/County_Mayo" title="County Mayo">County Mayo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The service expanded rapidly in the post-war years, with its headquarters relocating to <a href="/wiki/O%27Connell_Street" title="O'Connell Street">O'Connell Street</a>, opposite the <a href="/wiki/Gresham_Hotel" title="Gresham Hotel">Gresham Hotel</a> in Dublin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The service began supplying forecasts to <a href="/wiki/Raidi%C3%B3_Teilif%C3%ADs_%C3%89ireann" title="Raidió Teilifís Éireann">Radio Éireann</a> from 1948, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Irish_newspapers" title="List of Irish newspapers">daily newspapers</a> from 1952, to television from shortly after the first <a href="/wiki/Raidi%C3%B3_Teilif%C3%ADs_%C3%89ireann" title="Raidió Teilifís Éireann">Raidió Teilifís Éireann</a> television broadcasts in 1962, and recorded telephone forecasts in the 1960s. Ireland joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organisation" title="World Meteorological Organisation">World Meteorological Organisation</a> in the 1950s, and was a founder member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts</a> in 1975 and <a href="/wiki/EUMETSAT" title="EUMETSAT">EUMETSAT</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, the service moved to new purpose-built premises in <a href="/wiki/Glasnevin" title="Glasnevin">Glasnevin</a>, Dublin, and in 1996 it was renamed to <b>Met Éireann</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting_methodology">Forecasting methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_%C3%89ireann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forecasting methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Met Éireann uses the <a href="/wiki/HIRLAM" title="HIRLAM">HIRLAM</a> weather model for short-term forecasts (up to 48 hours). This model is developed cooperatively by multiple European weather services. Met Éireann runs a 10 km resolution HIRLAM model four times daily. The operational running of this model has been managed by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Centre_for_High-End_Computing" title="Irish Centre for High-End Computing">Irish Centre for High-End Computing</a> (ICHEC) since 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ichectt_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ichectt-4">[4]</a></sup> Met Éireann and ICHEC are working on bring a 5 km resolution HIRLAM model into production by the end of 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (October 2017)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>For medium-term forecasts (between 48 hours and 7 days), Ireland, along with 17 other European countries, relies on forecasts provided by the <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts</a> in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation_forecasting">Aviation forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_%C3%89ireann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aviation forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Ireland is strategically located on the busy air routes between North America and Europe, aviation forecasting is an important part of Met Éireann's work. In 2005, the service received payments of €7.8 million from the <a href="/wiki/Eurocontrol" title="Eurocontrol">Eurocontrol</a> air traffic control authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-report-5">[5]</a></sup> Aviation weather offices are located at <a href="/wiki/Casement_Aerodrome" title="Casement Aerodrome">Casement Aerodrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cork_Airport" title="Cork Airport">Cork Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland_West_Airport_Knock" title="Ireland West Airport Knock">Ireland West Airport Knock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Airport" title="Shannon Airport">Shannon Airport</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Met_%C3%89ireann&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the director of Met Éireann is Liam Campbell. According to the Met Éireann annual report for 2005, the service had a budget of €20.6 million for that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-report-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glamour">Glamour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_%C3%89ireann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Glamour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Met Éireann's female weather presenters are noted for appearing on television in less conservative clothing. Viewers have addressed numerous complaints about the attire of these female weather presenters, with one stating that the televised weather forecasts reports are more "glamour orientated" than focused on the weather and another stating: “One can hardly believe that we are having the coldest weather at the moment by the way the female presenters are dressed. It looks so ridiculous.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
