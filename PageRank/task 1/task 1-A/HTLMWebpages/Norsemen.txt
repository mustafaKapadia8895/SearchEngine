<p><b>Norsemen</b> are a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_people" title="Germanic people">Germanic people</a> who inhabited <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and spoke what is now called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse language</a> between <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 800 AD and c. 1300 AD. The language belongs to the <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> and is the predecessor of the modern Germanic languages of Scandinavia.</p>
<p><i>Norseman</i> means "man from the North" and applied primarily to Old Norse-speaking tribes living in southern and central <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. In history, "Norse" or "Norseman" could be any person from Scandinavia, even though <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> were different sets of people by the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.</p>
<p>The Norse Scandinavians established states and settlements in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">southern Italy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_with_Normandy">Connection with Normandy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norsemen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Connection with Normandy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Frankish" title="Old Frankish">Old Frankish</a> word <i>Nortmann</i> "Northman" was <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_of_names" title="Latinisation of names">Latinised</a> as <i>Normanni</i> and then entered <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> as <i>Normands</i>, whence the name of the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, which was settled by Norsemen in the 10th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_names">Other names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norsemen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> period, as today, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Vikings</a></i> was a common term for attacking Norsemen,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> especially in connection with raids and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monastic" title="Monastic">monastic</a> plundering by Norsemen in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. The Norse were also known as <i>Ascomanni</i>, <i>ashmen</i>, by the Germans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Lochlanach</i> (Norse) by the Gaels and <i>Dene</i> (Danes) by the Anglo-Saxons.</p>
<p>The Gaelic terms <i>Finn-Gall</i> (Norwegian Viking or Norwegian), <i>Dubh-Gall</i> (Danish Viking or Danish) and <i>Gall Goidel</i> (foreign Gaelic) were used for the people of Norse descent in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, who assimilated into the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Dubliners called them Ostmen, or East-people, and the name Oxmanstown (an area in central Dublin; the name is still current) comes from one of their settlements; they were also known as Lochlannaigh, or Lake-people.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the 8th century the inrush of the Vikings in force began to be felt all over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictland" title="Pictland">Pictland</a>. These Vikings were pagans and savages of the most unrestrained and pitiless type. They were composed of Finn-Gall or Norwegians, and of Dubh-Gall or Danes. The latter were a mixed breed, with a Hunnish strain in them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Archibald Black Scott, <i>The Pictish Nation, its People &amp; its Church</i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>However, British conceptions of the Vikings' origins were not quite correct. Those who plundered Britain lived in what is today Denmark, <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a>, the western coast of Sweden and Norway (up to almost the 70th parallel) and along the Swedish Baltic coast up to around the 60th latitude and Lake <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A4laren" title="Mälaren">Mälaren</a>. They also settled on the island of <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a>. The border between the Norsemen and more southerly Germanic tribes, the <a href="/wiki/Danevirke" title="Danevirke">Danevirke</a>, today is located about 50 kilometres (31 mi) south of the Danish-German border. The southernmost living Vikings lived no further north than <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, and travelled to Britain more from the east than from the north.</p>
<p>The northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">Scandinavian Peninsula</a> (with the exception of the Norwegian coast) was almost unpopulated, but the few who lived there were <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami</a>, the native people of northern Sweden and large areas of Norway, Finland and the <a href="/wiki/Kola_Peninsula" title="Kola Peninsula">Kola Peninsula</a> in today's <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantines</a> knew them as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rus%27_(people)" title="Rus' (people)">Rus'</a></i> or <i>Rhōs</i>, probably derived from various uses of <i>rōþs-</i>, i.e. "related to rowing", or from the area of <a href="/wiki/Roslagen" title="Roslagen">Roslagen</a> in east-central <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, where most of the Vikings who visited the Slavic lands originated. Archaeologists and historians of today believe that these Scandinavian settlements in the Slavic lands formed the names of the countries of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>.</p>
<p>The Slavs and the Byzantines also called them <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varangian" title="Varangian">Varangians</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">ON</a>: <i>Væringjar</i>, meaning "sworn men"), and the Scandinavian bodyguards of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> emperors were known as the <a href="/wiki/Varangian_Guard" title="Varangian Guard">Varangian Guard</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Scandinavian_usage">Modern Scandinavian usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norsemen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern Scandinavian usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Old Norse language, the term <i>norrœnir menn</i> (<i>northern men</i>), was used correspondingly to the modern English name <i>Norsemen</i>, referring to Swedes, Danes, Norwegians, Faroe Islanders, Icelanders, etc.</p>
<p>The modern Scandinavian languages have a common word for Norsemen: the word <i>nordbo</i>, (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Sw.</a>: <i>nordborna</i>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Da.</a>: <i>nordboerne</i>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">No.</a>: <i>nordboerne</i> or <i>nordbuane</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Definiteness" title="Definiteness">definite</a> <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plural</a>) is used for both ancient and modern people living in the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a> and speaking one of the Scandinavian Germanic languages.</p>
<p>The word Vikings: <i>Vikinger</i> in Danish and Norwegian Bokmål, and <i>Vikingar</i> in Swedish and Norwegian Nynorsk is not used as a word for Norsemen by natives, as "Viking" is the name for a specific activity/occupation (a "raid"), and not a demographic group. The Vikings were simply people (of any ethnicity, or origin) partaking in the raid (known as "going viking").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On occasions Finland is also mentioned as a "Scandinavian country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish language</a> is not <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a>, but Finland was for around six centuries a part of Sweden (late 12th century to 1809), and around 6% of the Finnish population still use Swedish as their first language. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%85land" title="Åland">Åland</a> islands Swedish is by far the dominant language, but elsewhere in Finland the share of Swedish-speaking people has been dropping ever since Finland gained independence in 1917,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> after the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>. Iceland, Greenland, and the Faroe Islands are also geographically separate from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_peninsula" title="Scandinavian peninsula">Scandinavian peninsula</a>. The term <i>Nordic countries</i> is therefore used to encompass <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">the Scandinavian countries</a>, Iceland, Greenland, the Faroes and Finland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
