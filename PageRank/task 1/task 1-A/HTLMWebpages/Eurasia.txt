<p><b>Eurasia</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ʊ/: 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="/ʒ/: 's' in 'pleasure'">ʒ</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a combined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_landmass" title="Continental landmass">continental landmass</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatlGeo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatlGeo-4">[4]</a></sup> The term is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of its constituent continents (Europe and Asia). Located primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">Eastern Hemispheres</a>, it is bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the west, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> to the east, the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> to the north, and by <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The division between Europe and Asia as two different continents is a historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_construct" title="Social construct">social construct</a>, with no clear physical separation between them; thus, in some parts of the world, Eurasia is recognized as the largest of five or six continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatlGeo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NatlGeo-4">[4]</a></sup> In geology, Eurasia is often considered as a single rigid megablock. However, the rigidity of Eurasia is debated based on the <a href="/wiki/Paleomagnetism" title="Paleomagnetism">paleomagnet</a> data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eurasia covers around 55,000,000 square kilometres (21,000,000 sq mi), or around 36.2% of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s total land area. The landmass contains around 5.0 billion people, equating to approximately 70% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population" title="Human population">human population</a>. Humans first settled in Eurasia between 60,000 and 125,000 years ago. Some major islands, including <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and those of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, are often included under the popular definition of Eurasia, in spite of being separate from the massive landmass.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">Physiographically</a>, Eurasia is a single continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatlGeo_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NatlGeo-4">[4]</a></sup> The concepts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> as distinct continents date back to <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a> and their borders are geologically arbitrary. In ancient times the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Marmara" title="Sea of Marmara">Sea of Marmara</a>, along with their associated straits, were seen as separating the continents, but today the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus</a> ranges are more seen as the main delimiters between the two. Eurasia is connected to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a>, and Eurasia is sometimes combined with Africa to make the largest contiguous landmass on Earth called <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">Afro-Eurasia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Due to the vast landmass and differences in latitude, Eurasia exhibits all types of climate under the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen classification</a>, including the harshest types of hot and cold temperatures, high and low precipitation and various types of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Eurasia" title="History of Eurasia">History of Eurasia</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_interactions_with_Europe" title="Foreign interactions with Europe">Foreign interactions with Europe</a></div>
<p>Eurasia formed 375 to 325 million years ago with the merging of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstania" title="Kazakhstania">Kazakhstania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a>, which was joined to <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a>, now North America, to form <a href="/wiki/Euramerica" title="Euramerica">Euramerica</a>. Chinese <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a> collided with Siberia's southern coast.</p>
<p>Eurasia has been the host of many ancient civilizations, including those based in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley" title="Indus Valley">Indus Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Axial_Age" title="Axial Age">Axial Age</a> (mid-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_millennium_BC" title="First millennium BC">first millennium BC</a>), a continuous belt of civilizations stretched through the Eurasian <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical zone</a> from the Atlantic to the Pacific. This belt became the mainstream of world history for two millennia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geopolitics">Geopolitics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geopolitics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally, “Eurasia” is a geographical notion: in this sense, it is simply the biggest continent; the combined landmass of Europe and Asia. However, geopolitically, the word has several different meanings, reflecting the specific geopolitical interests of each nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> “Eurasia” is one of the most important geopolitical concepts; as <a href="/wiki/Zbigniew_Brzezinski" title="Zbigniew Brzezinski">Zbigniew Brzezinski</a> observed:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>“... how America "manages" Eurasia is critical. A power that dominates “Eurasia” would control two of the world’s three most advanced and economically productive regions. A mere glance at the map also suggests that control “Eurasia” would almost automatically entail Africa’s subordination, rendering the Western Hemisphere and Oceania geopolitically peripheral to the world’s central continent. About 75 per cent of the world’s people live in “Eurasia”, and most of the world’s physical wealth is there as well, both in its enterprises and underneath its soil. “Eurasia” accounts for about three-fourths of the world’s known energy resources.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the moment one of the most prominent projects of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) is the <a href="/wiki/Russia%E2%80%93European_Union_relations" title="Russia–European Union relations">Russia - EU Four Common Spaces Initiative.</a> A political and economic union of former Soviet states named the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Economic_Union" title="Eurasian Economic Union">Eurasian Economic Union</a> was established in 2015, similar in concept to the EU.</p>
<p>The Russian concept of “Eurasia” corresponded initially more or less to the land area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a> in 1914, including parts of Eastern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> One of Russia's main geopolitical interests lies in ever closer integration with those countries that it considers part of “Eurasia.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This concept is further integrated with communist eschatology by author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Dugin" title="Alexander Dugin">Alexander Dugin</a> as the guiding principle of "self-sufficiency of a large space" during expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Every two years since 1996 a meeting of most Asian and European countries is organised as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia-Europe_Meeting" title="Asia-Europe Meeting">Asia-Europe Meeting</a> (ASEM).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_term">Use of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="History_of_the_Europe–Asia_division"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_Europe.E2.80.93Asia_division">History of the Europe–Asia division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of the Europe–Asia division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In ancient times, the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> classified Europe (derived from the mythological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenician</a> princess <a href="/wiki/Europa_(mythology)" title="Europa (mythology)">Europa</a>) and Asia (derived from <a href="/wiki/Asia_(mythology)" title="Asia (mythology)">Asia</a>, a woman in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>) as separate "lands". Where to draw the dividing line between the two regions is still a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundaries_between_continents" title="Boundaries between continents">matter of discussion</a>. Especially whether the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuma-Manych_Depression" title="Kuma-Manych Depression">Kuma-Manych Depression</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus Mountains</a> form the southeast boundary is disputed, since <a href="/wiki/Mount_Elbrus" title="Mount Elbrus">Mount Elbrus</a> would be part of Europe in the latter case, making it (and not <a href="/wiki/Mont_Blanc" title="Mont Blanc">Mont Blanc</a>) Europe's highest mountain. Most accepted is probably the boundary as defined by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Johan_von_Strahlenberg" title="Philip Johan von Strahlenberg">Philip Johan von Strahlenberg</a> in the 18th century. He defined the dividing line along the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dardanelles" title="Dardanelles">Dardanelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Marmara" title="Sea of Marmara">Sea of Marmara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus">Bosporus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuma%E2%80%93Manych_Depression" title="Kuma–Manych Depression">Kuma–Manych Depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ural_River" title="Ural River">Ural River</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">northern hemispheres</a>, Eurasia is considered a <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a>, part of the supercontinent of <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">Afro-Eurasia</a> or simply a continent in its own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a> includes Europe and most of Asia but not the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> or the area of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Chersky_Range" title="Chersky Range">Chersky Range</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Soviet_countries">Post-Soviet countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-Soviet countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Nineteenth-century Russian philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolai_Danilevsky" title="Nikolai Danilevsky">Nikolai Danilevsky</a> defined Eurasia as an entity separate from Europe and Asia, bounded by the Himalayas, the Caucasus, the Alps, the Arctic, the Pacific, the Atlantic, the Mediterranean, the Black Sea and the Caspian Sea, a definition that has been influential in Russia and other parts of the former Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Nowadays, partly inspired by this usage, the term Eurasia is sometimes used to refer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_space" title="Post-Soviet space">post-Soviet space</a> – in particular <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asian</a> republics, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Caucasus" title="South Caucasus">Transcaucasian</a> republics – and sometimes also adjacent regions such as <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>. This usage can be seen in the names of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasianet" title="Eurasianet">Eurasianet</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Journal of Eurasian Studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Slavic,_East_European,_and_Eurasian_Studies" title="Association for Slavic, East European, and Eurasian Studies">Association for Slavic, East European, and Eurasian Studies</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> as well as the titles of numerous academic programmes at US universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Another prominent example of this usage is in the name of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Economic_Community" title="Eurasian Economic Community">Eurasian Economic Community</a>, an organization including <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, Russia, and some of their neighbors, and headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, Russia, and <a href="/wiki/Astana" title="Astana">Astana</a>, the capital of Kazakhstan.</p>
<p>The word "Eurasia" is often used in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> to describe its location. Numerous Kazakh institutions have the term in their names, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lev_Gumilev" title="Lev Gumilev">L. N. Gumilev</a> Eurasian National University (<a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh</a>: <span lang="kk" xml:lang="kk"><a class="extiw" href="https://kk.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%95%D1%83%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B7%D0%B8%D1%8F_%D2%B1%D0%BB%D1%82%D1%82%D1%8B%D2%9B_%D1%83%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B5%D1%80%D1%81%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B5%D1%82%D1%96" title="kk:Еуразия ұлттық университеті">Л. Н. Гумилёв атындағы Еуразия Ұлттық университеті</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru"><a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%95%D0%B2%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B7%D0%B8%D0%B9%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%9D%D0%B0%D1%86%D0%B8%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%B0%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9_%D1%83%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B5%D1%80%D1%81%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B5%D1%82_%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8_%D0%9B._%D0%9D._%D0%93%D1%83%D0%BC%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%91%D0%B2%D0%B0" title="ru:Евразийский Национальный университет имени Л. Н. Гумилёва">Евразийский Национальный университет имени Л. Н. Гумилёва</a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lev_Gumilev" title="Lev Gumilev">Lev Gumilev</a>'s <a Suleimenov</a>), the Eurasian Media Forum,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> the Eurasian Cultural Foundation (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language"
