<p>A <b>frigate</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> is any of several types of <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warship</a>, the term having been used for ships of various sizes and roles over the last few centuries.</p>
<p>In the 17th century, this term was used for any warship built for speed and maneuverability, the description often used being "frigate-built". These could be warships carrying their principal batteries of carriage-mounted <a href="/wiki/Naval_artillery" title="Naval artillery">guns</a> on a single <a href="/wiki/Deck_(ship)" title="Deck (ship)">deck</a> or on two decks (with further smaller carriage-mounted guns usually carried on the forecastle and quarterdeck of the vessel). The term was generally used for ships too small to stand in the <a href="/wiki/Line_of_battle" title="Line of battle">line of battle</a>, although early line-of-battle ships were frequently referred to as frigates when they were built for speed.</p>
<p>In the 18th century, the term referred to ships that were usually as long as a <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">ship of the line</a> and were <a href="/wiki/Square_rig" title="Square rig">square-rigged</a> on all three masts (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_rigged" title="Full rigged">full rigged</a>), but were faster and with lighter armament, used for patrolling and escort. In the definition adopted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Admiralty" title="British Admiralty">British Admiralty</a>, they were <a href="/wiki/Rating_system_of_the_Royal_Navy" title="Rating system of the Royal Navy">rated ships</a> of at least 28 guns, carrying their principal armaments upon a single continuous deck — the upper deck — while ships of the line possessed two or more continuous decks bearing batteries of guns.</p>
<p>In the late 19th century (beginning about 1858 with the construction of prototypes by the British and French navies), the <b>armoured frigate</b> was a type of <a href="/wiki/Ironclad_warship" title="Ironclad warship">ironclad warship</a> that for a time was the most powerful type of vessel afloat. The term "frigate" was used because such ships still mounted their principal armaments on a single continuous upper deck.</p>
<p>In modern navies, frigates are used to protect other warships and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchant-marine" title="Merchant-marine">merchant-marine</a> ships, especially as <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a> (ASW) combatants for amphibious expeditionary forces, underway replenishment groups, and merchant <a href="/wiki/Convoy" title="Convoy">convoys</a>. Ship classes dubbed "frigates" have also more closely resembled <a href="/wiki/Corvette" title="Corvette">corvettes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a>, and even battleships. Some European navies such as the French, German or Spanish ones use the term "frigate" for both their destroyers and frigates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The rank "<a href="/wiki/Frigate_captain" title="Frigate captain">frigate captain</a>" derives from the name of this type of ship.</p>

<p>The term "frigate" (Italian: <i>fregata</i>; Spanish/Catalan/Portuguese/Sicilian: <i>fragata</i>; Dutch: <i>fregat</i>; French: <i>frégate</i>) originated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> in the late 15th century, referring to a lighter <a href="/wiki/Galleass" title="Galleass">galleass</a> type ship with oars, sails and a light armament, built for speed and maneuverability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson-1">[1]</a></sup> The etymology of the word is unknown, although it may have originated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corruption_(linguistic)" title="Corruption (linguistic)">corruption</a> of <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aphractus" title="wikt:aphractus">aphractus</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for an open vessel with no lower deck. <i>Aphractus</i> was, in turn, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> phrase ἄφρακτος ναῦς (<i>aphraktos naus</i>), or "undefended ship".</p>
<p>In 1583, during the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Eighty Years' War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Habsburg Spain</a> recovered the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Netherlands" title="Southern Netherlands">Southern Netherlands</a> from the rebellious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_(ethnic_group)" title="Dutch (ethnic group)">Dutch</a>. This soon led to the occupied ports being used as bases for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privateers" title="Privateers">privateers</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunkirker" title="Dunkirker">Dunkirkers</a>, to attack the shipping of the Dutch and their allies. To achieve this they developed small, maneuverable, sailing vessels that came to be referred to as frigates. The success of these Dunkirker vessels influenced the ship design of other navies contending with them but because most regular navies required ships of greater endurance than the Dunkirker frigates could provide, the term was soon applied less exclusively to any relatively fast and elegant sail-only war ship. In French, the term "frigate" became a verb, meaning 'to build long and low', and an adjective, adding further confusion. Even the huge English <a href="/wiki/HMS_Sovereign_of_the_Seas" title="HMS Sovereign of the Seas"><i>Sovereign of the Seas</i></a> could be described as "a delicate frigate" by a contemporary after her upper decks were reduced in 1651.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The navy of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> was the first navy to build the larger ocean-going frigates. The Dutch navy had three principal tasks in the struggle against Spain: to protect Dutch merchant ships at sea, to blockade the ports of Spanish-held <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> to damage trade and halt enemy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privateering" title="Privateering">privateering</a>, and to fight the Spanish fleet and prevent troop landings. The first two tasks required speed, shallowness of draft for the shallow waters around the Netherlands, and the ability to carry sufficient supplies to maintain a blockade. The third task required heavy armament, sufficient to fight against the Spanish fleet. The first of these larger battle-capable frigates were built around 1600 at <a href="/wiki/Hoorn" title="Hoorn">Hoorn</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_of_Holland" title="County of Holland">Holland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By the later stages of the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Eighty Years' War</a> the Dutch had switched entirely from the heavier ships still used by the English and Spanish to the lighter frigates, carrying around 40 guns and weighing around 300 tons.</p>
<p>The effectiveness of the Dutch frigates became most visible in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Downs" title="Battle of the Downs">Battle of the Downs</a> in 1639, encouraging most other navies, especially the English, to adopt similar designs.</p>
<p>The fleets built by the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a> in the 1650s generally consisted of ships described as "frigates", the largest of which were two-decker 'great frigates' of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_rate" title="Third rate">third rate</a>. Carrying 60 guns, these vessels were as big and capable as 'great ships' of the time; however, most other frigates at the time were used as '<a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a>': independent fast ships. The term "frigate" implied a long hull design, which relates directly to speed (see <a href="/wiki/Hull_speed" title="Hull speed">hull speed</a>) and also, in turn, helped the development of the <a href="/wiki/Broadside" title="Broadside">broadside</a> tactic in naval warfare.</p>

<p>At this time, a further design evolved, reintroducing oars to create the galley frigate such as <a href="/wiki/HMS_Charles_Galley_(1676)" title="HMS Charles Galley (1676)">HMS <i>Charles Galley</i></a> of 1676 which was rated as a 32-gun fifth rate but also had a bank of 40 oars set below the upper deck which could be used to propel the ship in the absence of a favourable wind.</p>
<p>In Danish, the word "fregat" is often applied to warships carrying as few as 16 guns, such as <a href="/wiki/HMS_Falcon_(1802)" title="HMS Falcon (1802)">HMS <i>Falcon</i></a> which the British classified as a sloop.</p>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Rating_system_of_the_Royal_Navy" title="Rating system of the Royal Navy">rating system of the Royal Navy</a>, by the middle of the 18th century, the term "frigate" was technically restricted to single-decked ships of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fifth_rate" title="Fifth rate">fifth rate</a>, though small 28-gun frigates were classed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sixth_rate" title="Sixth rate">sixth rate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The classic sailing frigate, well-known today for its role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleonic_wars" title="Napoleonic wars">Napoleonic wars</a>, can be traced back to French developments in the second quarter of the 18th century. The French-built <a href="/wiki/French_frigate_M%C3%A9d%C3%A9e_(1741)" title="French frigate Médée (1741)">Médée</a> of 1740 is often regarded as the first example of this type. These ships were square-rigged and carried all their main guns on a single continuous upper deck. The lower deck, known as the "gun deck", now carried no armament, and functioned as a "berth deck" where the crew lived, and was in fact placed below the waterline of the new frigates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breen_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Breen-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A total of fifty-nine French sailing frigates were built between 1777 and 1790, with a standard design averaging a hull length of 135 ft (41 m) and an average draught of 13 ft (4.0 m). The new frigates recorded sailing speeds of up to 14 knots (26 km/h; 16 mph), significantly faster than their predecessor vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breen_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Breen-4">[4]</a></sup> They were able to fight with all their guns when the seas were so rough that comparable two-deckers had to close the gun-ports on their lower decks (see the <a href="/wiki/Action_of_13_January_1797" title="Action of 13 January 1797">Action of 13 January 1797</a>, for an example when this was decisive). Like the larger 74 which was developed at the same time, the new frigates sailed well and were good fighting vessels due to a combination of long hulls and low upperworks compared to vessels of comparable size and firepower.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> captured a handful of the new French frigates during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a> (1740–1748) and were impressed by them, particularly for their inshore handling capabilities. They soon built copies and started to adapt the type to their own needs, setting the standard for other frigates as the leading naval power. The first British frigates carried 28 guns including an upper deck battery of twenty-four 9-pounder guns (the remaining four smaller guns were carried on the quarter deck) but soon developed into fifth-rate ships of 32 or 36 guns including an upper deck battery of twenty-six 12-pounder guns, with the remaining six or ten smaller guns carried on the quarter deck and forecastle. From around 1778, a larger "heavy" frigate was developed with a main battery of twenty-six or twenty-eight 18-pounder guns (again with the remaining ten smaller guns carried on the quarter deck and forecastle).</p>
<p>Both British and American frigates could (and usually did) additionally carry smaller carriage-mounted guns on their quarter decks and <a href="/wiki/Forecastle" title="Forecastle">forecastles</a> (the superstructures above the upper deck). Technically, rated ships with fewer than 28 guns could not be classed as frigates but as "<a href="/wiki/Post_ship" title="Post ship">post ships</a>"; however, in common parlance most post ships were often described as "frigates", the same casual misuse of the term being extended to smaller two-decked ships that were too small to stand in the line of battle.</p>
<p>Royal Navy frigates of the late 18th century included the 1780-vintage <i>Perseverance</i> class, which measured around 900 <a href="/wiki/Builder%27s_Old_Measurement" title="Builder's Old Measurement">tons burthen</a> and carried 36 guns; this successful class was followed by numerous other classes that measured over 1,000 tons burthen and carried 38 guns.</p>

<p>In 1797, three of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Original_six_frigates_of_the_United_States_Navy" title="Original six frigates of the United States Navy">first six major ships</a> were rated as 44-gun frigates (or "super-frigates"), which operationally carried fifty-six to sixty 24-pounder long guns and 32-pounder or 42-pounder <a href="/wiki/Carronade" title="Carronade">carronades</a> on two decks; by all regards they were exceptionally powerful and tough. These ships were so well-armed that they were often regarded as equal to ships of the line, and after a series of losses at the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, Royal Navy fighting instructions ordered British frigates (usually of 38 guns or less) to never engage American frigates at any less than a 2:1 advantage. <a href="/wiki/USS_Constitution" title="USS Constitution">USS <i>Constitution</i></a>, preserved as a <a href="/wiki/Museum_ship" title="Museum ship">museum ship</a> by the US Navy, is the oldest commissioned warship afloat, and is a surviving example of a frigate from the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>. <i>Constitution</i> and her sister ships <a href="/wiki/USS_President_(1800)" title="USS President (1800)"><i>President</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_United_States_(1797)" title="USS United States (1797)"><i>United States</i></a> were created in a response to deal with the Barbary Coast pirates and in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Act_of_1794" title="Naval Act of 1794">Naval Act of 1794</a>. The three big frigates, when built, had a distinctive building pattern which minimised "<a href="/wiki/Hogging_and_sagging" title="Hogging and sagging">hogging</a>" (in which the centre of the keel rises while both ends drop) and improves hydrodynamic efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archibald.2C_Roger_1997_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Archibald.2C_Roger_1997-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hull was designed so that all the weight from the guns was upon the keel itself. <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Humphreys" title="Joshua Humphreys">Joshua Humphreys</a> proposed that only <a href="/wiki/Live_oak" title="Live oak">live oak</a>, a tree that grew only in America, should be used to build these ships. The method was to use diagonal riders, eight on each side that sat at a 45 degree angle. These beams of live oak were about 2 feet (61 cm) wide and around 1 foot (30 cm) thick and helped to maintain the shape of the hull, serving also to reduce flexibility and to minimize impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archibald.2C_Roger_1997_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Archibald.2C_Roger_1997-5">[5]</a></sup> These ideas were considered revolutionary in the late 18th and early 19th century. A three-layer method was used in which the planks along the sides of the hull were laid horizontally across the <a href="/wiki/Frame_(nautical)" title="Frame (nautical)">frames</a>, making a crossing or checker board pattern. The sides of the ship could be as thick as 25 inches (64 cm), and were able to absorb substantial damage. The strength of this braced construction earned USS <i>Constitution</i> the nickname "Old Ironsides".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="621" data-file-width="474" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg/240px-20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg/360px-20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5c/20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20000_Abraham_Lincoln.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The fictitious, but representative, ironclad frigate USS <i>Abraham Lincoln</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Twenty_Thousand_Leagues_Under_the_Sea" title="Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea">Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea</a></i></div>

<p>Frigates were perhaps the hardest-worked of warship types during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>. While smaller than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship-of-the-line" title="Ship-of-the-line">ship-of-the-line</a>, they were formidable opponents for the large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Sloop" title="Sloop">sloops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gunboat" title="Gunboat">gunboats</a>, not to mention <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a> or merchantmen. Able to carry six months' stores, they had very long range; and vessels larger than frigates were considered too valuable to operate independently.</p>
<p>Frigates scouted for the fleet, went on commerce-raiding missions and patrols, and conveyed messages and dignitaries. Usually, frigates would fight in small numbers or singly against other frigates. They would avoid contact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ships-of-the-line" title="Ships-of-the-line">ships-of-the-line</a>; even in the midst of a fleet engagement it was bad etiquette for a ship of the line to fire on an enemy frigate which had not fired first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Frigates were involved in fleet battles, often as "repeating frigates". In the smoke and confusion of battle, <a href="/wiki/Naval_flag_signalling" title="Naval flag signalling">signals</a> made by the fleet commander, whose <a href="/wiki/Flagship" title="Flagship">flagship</a> might be in the thick of the fighting, might be missed by the other ships of the fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB-7">[7]</a></sup> Frigates were therefore stationed to windward or leeward of the main <a href="/wiki/Line_of_battle" title="Line of battle">line of battle</a>, and had to maintain a clear line of sight to the commander's flagship. Signals from the flagship were then repeated by the frigates, which themselves standing out of the line and clear from the smoke and disorder of battle, could be more easily seen by the other ships of the fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB-7">[7]</a></sup> If damage or loss of masts prevented the flagship from making clear conventional signals, the repeating frigates could interpret them and hoist their own in the correct manner, passing on the commander's instructions clearly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>For officers in the Royal Navy, a frigate was a desirable posting. Frigates often saw action, which meant a greater chance of glory, promotion, and <a href="/wiki/Prize_money" title="Prize money">prize money</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike larger ships that were placed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserve_Fleet" title="Reserve Fleet">in ordinary</a>, frigates were kept in service in peacetime as a cost-saving measure and to provide experience to frigate captains and officers which would be useful in wartime. Frigates could also carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(military)" title="Marine (military)">marines</a> for boarding enemy ships or for operations on shore; in 1832, the frigate <a href="/wiki/USS_Potomac_(1822)" title="USS Potomac (1822)">USS <i>Potomac</i></a> landed a party of 282 sailors and Marines ashore in the US Navy's <a href="/wiki/First_Sumatran_expedition" title="First Sumatran expedition">first Sumatran expedition</a>.</p>
<p>Common armament was one gundeck with 24–30 long guns, from 8- to 24-pounders (3.6 to 11 kg), with up to a dozen light guns or <a href="/wiki/Carronade" title="Carronade">carronades</a> on the quarterdeck and forecastle above.</p>
<p>Frigates remained a crucial element of navies until the mid-19th century. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ironclad" title="Ironclad">ironclads</a> were classified as "frigates" because of the number of guns they carried. However, terminology changed as iron and steam became the norm, and the role of the frigate was assumed first by the <a href="/wiki/Protected_cruiser" title="Protected cruiser">protected cruiser</a> and then by the <a href="/wiki/Light_cruiser" title="Light cruiser">light cruiser</a>.</p>
<p>Frigates are often the vessel of choice in historical naval novels due to their relative freedom compared to ships of the line (kept for fleet actions) and smaller vessels (generally assigned to a home port and less widely ranging). For example, the <a href="/wiki/Patrick_O%27Brian" title="Patrick O'Brian">Patrick O'Brian</a> <a href="/wiki/Aubrey%E2%80%93Maturin_series" title="Aubrey–Maturin series">Aubrey–Maturin series</a>, <a href="/wiki/C._S._Forester" title="C. S. Forester">C. S. Forester</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Hornblower" title="Horatio Hornblower">Horatio Hornblower</a> series and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Reeman" title="Douglas Reeman">Alexander Kent</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Bolitho" title="Richard Bolitho">Richard Bolitho</a> series. The motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Master_and_Commander:_The_Far_Side_of_the_World" title="Master and Commander: The Far Side of the World">Master and Commander: The Far Side of the World</a></i> features a reconstructed historic frigate, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Surprise_(replica_ship)" title="HMS Surprise (replica ship)">HMS <i>Rose</i></a>, to depict Aubrey's frigate HMS <i>Surprise</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_of_steam">Age of steam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Age of steam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Vessels classed as frigates continued to play a great role in navies with the adoption of steam power in the 19th century. In the 1830s, navies experimented with large <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">paddle steamers</a> equipped with large guns mounted on one deck, which were termed "paddle frigates".</p>
<p>From the mid-1840s on, frigates which more closely resembled the traditional sailing frigate were built with steam engines and screw <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propellers</a>. These "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screw_frigate" title="Screw frigate">screw frigates</a>", built first of wood and later of <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">iron</a>, continued to perform the traditional role of the frigate until late in the 19th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armoured_frigate">Armoured frigate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Armoured frigate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1859, armour was added to ships based on existing frigate and <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">ship of the line</a> designs. The additional weight of the armour on these first <a href="/wiki/Ironclad_warship" title="Ironclad warship">ironclad warships</a> meant that they could have only one gun deck, and they were technically frigates, even though they were more powerful than existing ships-of-the-line and occupied the same strategic role. The phrase "armoured frigate" remained in use for some time to denote a sail-equipped, broadside-firing type of ironclad.</p>
<p>After 1875, the term "frigate" fell out of use. Vessels with armoured sides were designated as "<a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleships</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Armored_cruiser" title="Armored cruiser">armoured cruisers</a>", while "<a href="/wiki/Protected_cruiser" title="Protected cruiser">protected cruisers</a>" only possessed an armoured deck, and unarmoured vessels, including frigates and sloops, were classified as "unprotected cruisers".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Modern frigates are related to earlier frigates only by name. The term "frigate" was readopted during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> by the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> to describe an <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine</a> escort vessel that was larger than a <a href="/wiki/Corvette" title="Corvette">corvette</a>, while smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a>. Equal in size and capability to the American <a href="/wiki/Destroyer_escort" title="Destroyer escort">destroyer escort</a>, frigates are usually less expensive to build and maintain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Anti-submarine escorts had previously been classified as <a href="/wiki/Sloop-of-war" title="Sloop-of-war">sloops</a> by the Royal Navy, and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Swan-class_sloop" title="Black Swan-class sloop"><i>Black Swan</i>-class</a> sloops of 1939–1945 were as large as the new types of frigate, and more heavily armed. Twenty-two of these were reclassified as frigates after the war, as were the remaining 24 smaller <a href="/wiki/Castle-class_corvette" title="Castle-class corvette">Castle-class</a> corvettes.</p>
<p>The frigate was introduced to remedy some of the shortcomings inherent in the corvette design: limited armament, a hull form not suited to open-ocean work, a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driveshaft" title="Driveshaft">shaft</a> which limited speed and maneuverability, and a lack of range. The frigate was designed and built to the same mercantile construction standards (<a href="/wiki/Scantling" title="Scantling">scantlings</a>) as the corvette, allowing manufacture by yards unused to warship construction. The first frigates of the <a href="/wiki/River-class_frigate" title="River-class frigate">River class</a> (1941) were essentially two sets of corvette machinery in one larger hull, armed with the latest <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog_(weapon)" title="Hedgehog (weapon)">Hedgehog</a> anti-submarine weapon.</p>
<p>The frigate possessed less offensive firepower and speed than a <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a>, but such qualities were not required for anti-submarine warfare. Submarines were slow while submerged, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASDIC" title="ASDIC">ASDIC</a> sets did not operate effectively at speeds of over 20 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (23 <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">mph</a>; 37 <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a>). Rather, the frigate was an austere and weatherly vessel suitable for mass-construction and fitted with the latest innovations in anti-submarine warfare. As the frigate was intended purely for convoy duties, and not to deploy with the fleet, it had limited range and speed.</p>
<p>The contemporary <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> <i><a href="/wiki/F-class_escort_ship" title="F-class escort ship">Flottenbegleiter</a></i> ("fleet escorts"), also known as "F-Boats", were essentially frigates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They were based on a pre-war <i><a href="/wiki/Oberkommando_der_Marine" title="Oberkommando der Marine">Oberkommando der Marine</a></i> concept of vessels which could fill roles such as fast minesweeper, minelayer, merchant escort and anti-submarine vessel. Because of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> their displacement was officially limited to 600 tons, although in reality they exceeded this by about 100 tons. F-boats had two stacks and two 105 mm gun turrets. The design was flawed because of its narrow beam, sharp bow and unreliable high pressure steam turbines. F-boats were succeeded in operational duties by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torpedo_boat_type_35" title="Torpedo boat type 35">Type 35</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elbing_class_torpedo_boat" title="Elbing class torpedo boat">Elbing class torpedo boats</a>. <i>Flottenbegleiter</i> remained in service as advanced training vessels.</p>
<p>It was not until the Royal Navy's <a href="/wiki/Bay-class_frigate" title="Bay-class frigate">Bay class</a> of 1944 that a British design classified as a "frigate" was produced for fleet use, although it still suffered from limited speed. These <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">anti-aircraft</a> frigates, built on incomplete <a href="/wiki/Loch-class_frigate" title="Loch-class frigate">Loch-class</a> frigate hulls, were similar to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Destroyer_escort" title="Destroyer escort">destroyer escorts</a> (DE), although the latter had greater speed and offensive armament to better suit them to fleet deployments. The destroyer escort concept came from design studies by the <a href="/wiki/General_Board_of_the_United_States_Navy" title="General Board of the United States Navy">General Board of the United States Navy</a> in 1940, as modified by requirements established by a British commission in 1941<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> prior to the American entry into the war, for deep-water escorts. The American-built destroyer escorts serving in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> were rated as <a href="/wiki/Captain-class_frigate" title="Captain-class frigate">Captain-class</a> frigates. The U.S. Navy's two <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a>-built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asheville-class_frigate" title="Asheville-class frigate"><i>Asheville</i>-class</a> and 96 British-influenced, American-built <a href="/wiki/Tacoma-class_frigate" title="Tacoma-class frigate"><i>Tacoma</i>-class</a> frigates that followed originally were classified as "patrol <a href="/wiki/Gunboat" title="Gunboat">gunboats</a>" (PG) in the U.S. Navy but on 15 April 1943 were all reclassified as <b>patrol frigates (PF)</b>.</p>

<p>The introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> after World War II made relatively small ships effective for anti-aircraft warfare: the "guided missile frigate." In the USN, these vessels were called "<a href="/wiki/Ocean_escort" title="Ocean escort">ocean escorts</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Hull_classification_symbol" title="Hull classification symbol">designated</a> "DE" or "DEG" until 1975 – a holdover from the World War II <a href="/wiki/Destroyer_escort" title="Destroyer escort">destroyer escort</a> or "DE". The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Navy" title="Royal Canadian Navy">Royal Canadian Navy</a> and British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> maintained the use of the term "frigate"; likewise, the French Navy refers to missile-equipped ship, up to cruiser-sized ships (<a href="/wiki/Suffren-class_frigate" title="Suffren-class frigate"><i>Suffren</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Tourville-class_frigate" title="Tourville-class frigate"><i>Tourville</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Horizon-class_frigate" title="Horizon-class frigate"><i>Horizon</i> classes</a>), by the name of "frégate", while smaller units are named <i><a href="/wiki/Aviso" title="Aviso">aviso</a></i>. The Soviet Navy used the term "guard-ship" (<i>сторожевой корабль</i>).</p>
<p>From the 1950s to the 1970s, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> commissioned ships classed as guided missile frigates (<a href="/wiki/Hull_classification_symbol" title="Hull classification symbol">hull classification symbol</a> DLG or DLGN, literally meaning guided missile <a href="/wiki/Destroyer_leader" title="Destroyer leader">destroyer leaders</a>), which were actually anti-aircraft warfare <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a> built on <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a>-style hulls. These had one or two twin launchers per ship for the <a href="/wiki/RIM-2_Terrier" title="RIM-2 Terrier">RIM-2 Terrier</a> missile, upgraded to the <a href="/wiki/RIM-67_Standard" title="RIM-67 Standard">RIM-67 Standard</a> ER missile in the 1980s. This type of ship was intended primarily to defend aircraft carriers against anti-ship <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise missiles</a>, augmenting and eventually replacing converted World War II cruisers (CAG/CLG/CG) in this role. The guided missile frigates also had an anti-submarine capability that most of the World War II cruiser conversions lacked. Some of these ships — <a href="/wiki/USS_Bainbridge_(CGN-25)" title="USS Bainbridge (CGN-25)"><i>Bainbridge</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Truxtun_(CGN-35)" title="USS Truxtun (CGN-35)"><i>Truxtun</i></a> along with the <a href="/wiki/California-class_cruiser" title="California-class cruiser"><i>California</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia-class_cruiser" title="Virginia-class cruiser"><i>Virginia</i> classes</a> — were <a href="/wiki/United_States_naval_reactors" title="United States naval reactors">nuclear-powered</a> (DLGN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These "frigates" were roughly mid-way in size between cruisers and destroyers. This was similar to the use of the term "frigate" during the age of sail during which it referred to a medium-sized warship, but it was inconsistent with conventions used by other contemporary navies which regarded frigates as being smaller than destroyers. During the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_1975_ship_reclassification" title="United States Navy 1975 ship reclassification">1975 ship reclassification</a>, the large American frigates were redesignated as guided missile cruisers or destroyers (CG/CGN/DDG), while <a href="/wiki/Ocean_escort" title="Ocean escort">ocean escorts</a> (the American classification for ships smaller than destroyers, with hull symbol DE/DEG (<a href="/wiki/Destroyer_escort" title="Destroyer escort">destroyer escort</a>)) were reclassified as frigates (FF/FFG), sometimes incorrectly called "fast frigates". In the late 1970s the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> introduced the 51-ship <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Hazard_Perry-class_frigate" title="Oliver Hazard Perry-class frigate"><i>Oliver Hazard Perry</i>-class</a> guided missile frigates (FFG), the last of which was decommissioned in 2015, although some serve in other navies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By 1995 the older guided missile cruisers and destroyers were replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Ticonderoga-class_cruiser" title="Ticonderoga-class cruiser"><i>Ticonderoga</i>-class cruisers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arleigh_Burke-class_destroyer" title="Arleigh Burke-class destroyer"><i>Arleigh Burke</i>-class destroyers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most successful post-1945 designs was the British <a href="/wiki/Leander-class_frigate" title="Leander-class frigate"><i>Leander</i>-class</a> frigate, which was used by several navies. Laid down in 1959, the <i>Leander</i>s were based on the previous <a href="/wiki/Whitby-class_frigate" title="Whitby-class frigate">Type 12 anti-submarine frigate</a> but equipped for anti-aircraft use as well. They were used by the UK into the 1990s, at which point some were sold onto other navies. The <i>Leander</i> design, or improved versions of it, were licence-built for other navies as well.</p>
<p>Nearly all modern frigates are equipped with some form of offensive or defensive missiles, and as such are rated as guided-missile frigates (FFG). Improvements in surface-to-air missiles (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Eurosam" title="Eurosam">Eurosam</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MBDA_Aster" title="MBDA Aster">Aster 15</a>) allow modern guided-missile frigates to form the core of many modern navies and to be used as a fleet defence platform, without the need for specialised anti-air warfare frigates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Royal Navy <a href="/wiki/Salisbury-class_frigate" title="Salisbury-class frigate">Type 61 <i>Salisbury</i> class</a> were <a href="/wiki/Radar_picket" title="Radar picket">"air direction"</a> frigates equipped to track aircraft. To this end they had reduced armament compared to the <a href="/wiki/Leopard-class_frigate" title="Leopard-class frigate">Type 41 <i>Leopard</i>-class</a> air-defence frigates built on the same hull.</p>
<p>Multi-role frigates like the <a href="/wiki/MEKO_200" title="MEKO 200">MEKO 200</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anzac-class_frigate" title="Anzac-class frigate"><i>Anzac</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Halifax-class_frigate" title="Halifax-class frigate"><i>Halifax</i> classes</a> are designed for navies needing warships deployed in a variety of situations that a general frigate class would not be able to fulfill and not requiring the need for deploying <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-submarine_role">Anti-submarine role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anti-submarine role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Somerset_(F82).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1309" data-file-width="1745" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/HMS_Somerset_%28F82%29.jpg/220px-HMS_Somerset_%28F82%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/HMS_Somerset_%28F82%29.jpg/330px-HMS_Somerset_%28F82%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/HMS_Somerset_%28F82%29.jpg/440px-HMS_Somerset_%28F82%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Somerset_(F82).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/HMS_Somerset_(F82)" title="HMS Somerset (F82)">HMS <i>Somerset</i></a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Type_23_frigate" title="Type 23 frigate">Type 23 frigates</a> were built for <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a> but are capable multi-purpose ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the opposite end of the spectrum, some frigates are specialised for <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a>. Increasing submarine speeds towards the end of World War II (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Type_XXI_submarine" title="German Type XXI submarine">German Type XXI submarine</a>) greatly reduced the margin of speed superiority of frigate over submarine. The frigate could no longer be slow and powered by mercantile machinery and consequently postwar frigates, such as the <a href="/wiki/Type_12_frigate" title="Type 12 frigate"><i>Whitby</i> class</a>, were faster.</p>
<p>Such ships carry improved <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> equipment, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sonar#Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Sonar">variable depth sonar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Towed_array_sonar" title="Towed array sonar">towed array</a>, and specialised weapons such as <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedoes</a>, forward-throwing weapons such as <a href="/wiki/Limbo_(weapon)" title="Limbo (weapon)">Limbo</a> and missile-carried anti-submarine torpedoes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASROC" title="ASROC">ASROC</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ikara_(missile)" title="Ikara (missile)">Ikara</a>. Surface-to-air missiles such as <a href="/wiki/AIM-7_Sparrow#Sea_Sparrow" title="AIM-7 Sparrow">Sea Sparrow</a> and surface-to-surface missiles such as <a href="/wiki/Exocet" title="Exocet">Exocet</a> give them defensive and offensive capabilities. The Royal Navy's original <a href="/wiki/Type_22_frigate" title="Type 22 frigate">Type 22 frigate</a> is an example of a specialised anti-submarine warfare frigate.</p>
<p>Especially for anti-submarine warfare, most modern frigates have a <a href="/wiki/Helipad" title="Helipad">landing deck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hangar" title="Hangar">hangar</a> aft to operate <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, eliminating the need for the frigate to close with unknown sub-surface threats, and using fast helicopters to attack nuclear submarines which may be faster than surface warships. For this task the helicopter is equipped with sensors such as <a href="/wiki/Sonobuoy" title="Sonobuoy">sonobuoys</a>, wire-mounted dipping sonar and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anomaly_detector" title="Magnetic anomaly detector">magnetic anomaly detectors</a> to identify possible threats, and torpedoes or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depth-charge" title="Depth-charge">depth-charges</a> to attack them.</p>
<p>With their onboard radar helicopters can also be used to reconnoitre over-the-horizon targets and, if equipped with <a href="/wiki/Anti-ship_missile" title="Anti-ship missile">anti-ship missiles</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penguin_missile" title="Penguin missile">Penguin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sea_Skua" title="Sea Skua">Sea Skua</a>, to attack them. The helicopter is also invaluable for <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> operation and has largely replaced the use of <a href="/wiki/Dinghy" title="Dinghy">small boats</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Underway_replenishment" title="Underway replenishment">jackstay rig</a> for such duties as transferring personnel, mail and cargo between ships or to shore. With helicopters these tasks can be accomplished faster and less dangerously, and without the need for the frigate to slow down or change course.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Stealth_technology" title="Stealth technology">Stealth technology</a> has been introduced in modern frigate design by the French <a href="/wiki/La_Fayette-class_frigate" title="La Fayette-class frigate"><i>La Fayette</i> class</a> design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Frigate shapes are designed to offer a minimal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_cross_section" title="Radar cross section">radar cross section</a>, which also lends them good air penetration; the maneuverability of these frigates has been compared to that of sailing ships. Examples are the French <a href="/wiki/Horizon-class_frigate" title="Horizon-class frigate"><i>Horizon</i> class</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aster_15" title="Aster 15">Aster 15</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aster_30" title="Aster 30">Aster 30</a> missile for anti-missile capabilities, the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F125-class_frigate" title="F125-class frigate">F125</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sachsen-class_frigate" title="Sachsen-class frigate"><i>Sachsen</i>-class</a> frigates, the <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkish</a> <a href="/wiki/TF2000-class_frigate" title="TF2000-class frigate">TF2000</a> type frigates with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MK-41_VLS" title="MK-41 VLS">MK-41 VLS</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Shivalik-class_frigate" title="Shivalik-class frigate"><i>Shivalik</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Talwar-class_frigate" title="Talwar-class frigate"><i>Talwar</i> classes</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmos_missile" title="Brahmos missile">Brahmos missile</a> system.</p>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a> applies the term first-class frigate and second-class frigate to both destroyers and frigates in service. Pennant numbers remain divided between F-series numbers for those ships internationally recognised as frigates and D-series pennant numbers for those more traditionally recognised as destroyers. This can result in some confusion as certain classes are referred to as frigates in French service while similar ships in other navies are referred to as destroyers. This also results in some recent classes of French ships such as the <a href="/wiki/Horizon-class_frigate" title="Horizon-class frigate"><i>Horizon</i> class</a> being among the largest in the world to carry the rating of frigate.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/German_Navy" title="German Navy">German Navy</a>, frigates were used to replace aging destroyers; however in size and role the new German frigates exceed the former class of destroyers. The future German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F125-class_frigate" title="F125-class frigate">F125-class frigate</a> will be the largest class of frigates worldwide with a displacement of more than 7,200 tons. The same was done in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Navy" title="Spanish Navy">Spanish Navy</a>, which went ahead with the deployment of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegis_combat_system" title="Aegis combat system">Aegis</a> frigates, the <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Baz%C3%A1n-class_frigate" title="Álvaro de Bazán-class frigate"><i>Álvaro de Bazán</i>-class</a> frigates.</p>
<h3><span id="Littoral_Combat_Ship_(LCS)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Littoral_Combat_Ship_.28LCS.29">Littoral Combat Ship (LCS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frigate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Littoral Combat Ship (LCS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="874" data-file-width="1309" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USS_Independence_LCS-2_at_pierce_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/USS_Independence_(LCS-2)" title="USS Independence (LCS-2)">USS <i>Independence</i></a>, an <a href="/wiki/Independence-class_littoral_combat_ship" title="Independence-class littoral combat ship"><i>Independence</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Littoral_combat_ship" title="Littoral combat ship">littoral combat ship</a> of the United States Navy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some new classes of ships similar to <a href="/wiki/Corvette" title="Corvette">corvettes</a> are optimized for high-speed deployment and combat with small craft rather than combat between equal opponents; an example is the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Littoral_Combat_Ship" title="Littoral Combat Ship">Littoral Combat Ship</a> (LCS). As of 2015, all <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Hazard_Perry-class_frigate" title="Oliver Hazard Perry-class frigate"><i>Oliver Hazard Perry</i>-class</a> frigates in the United States Navy have been decommissioned, and their role partially being assumed by the new LCS. While the LCS class ships are smaller than the frigate class they will replace, they offer a similar degree of weaponry while requiring less than half the crew complement and offering a top speed of over 40 knots (74 km/h; 46 mph). A major advantage for the LCS ships is that they are designed around specific mission modules allowing them to fulfill a variety of roles. The modular system also allows for most upgrades to be performed ashore and installed later into the ship, keeping the ships available for deployment for the maximum time.</p>
<p>The latest U.S. deactivation plans means that this is the first time that the U.S. Navy has been without a frigate class of ships since 1943 (technically <a href="/wiki/USS_Constitution" title="USS Constitution">USS <i>Constitution</i></a> is rated as a frigate and is still in commission, but does not count towards Navy force levels).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remaining 20 LCSs to be acquired from 2019 and onwards that will be enhanced will be designated as frigates, and existing ships given modifications may also have their classification changed to <i>FF</i> as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
