<p>The <b>Great Spirit</b>, called <b><a href="/wiki/Wakan_Tanka" title="Wakan Tanka">Wakan Tanka</a></b> among the <a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">Sioux</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ostler_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ostler-1">[1]</a></sup> and <b><a href="/wiki/Gitche_Manitou" title="Gitche Manitou">Gitche Manitou</a></b> in <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a>, is a conception of universal spiritual force, or supreme being, prevalent among some <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Nation" title="First Nation">First Nation</a> cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-2">[2]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a> activist <a href="/wiki/Russell_Means" title="Russell Means">Russell Means</a>, a better translation of Wakan Tanka is the <b>Great Mystery</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Means_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Means-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Spirit was sometimes imagined as an “anthropomorphic celestial deity who personally ruled the world and intervened regularly with human affairs” (Cave, 3). He is the God of creation, history and eternity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> There are many different prophets of the Great Spirit. Each prophet aims at restoration and preservation of a way of Native American life that is diminishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> The Great Spirit is used to guide individuals and their communities with the lessons through the prophets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Great Spirit is expressed through multiple Native American Tribes which is why each story regarding the Great Spirit is different- they are products of various cultures. Some prophets call for war, while others call for peace. Certain prophets call for acculturation between the Native Americans and whites, while others call for complete segregation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prophets">Prophets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prophets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two of the most well known prophets' prophecies took place in the early 1800s. <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">The Shawnee Prophet</a> occurred in 1824. Tenskwatawa, a religious and political leader of the Shawnee tribe, warned The Governor of Michigan, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Cass" title="Lewis Cass">Lewis Cass</a>, that the children of the Shawnees tribe would carry the “sacred flame”. This flame would end the world as it was between the Native Americans and Whites. Once the destruction was complete, the Great Spirit would restructure and repopulate the world in the way it was believed that it should be.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another well-known story happened in 1827 and involves <a href="/wiki/William_Clark" title="William Clark">William Clark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kennekuk" title="Kennekuk">Kennekuk</a>, a spiritual leader of the <a href="/wiki/Kickapoo_people" title="Kickapoo people">Kickapoo</a> nation. This is known as the Kickapoo Prophet. Kennekuk informed Clark that he must be careful while exploring the land that is now Illinois. This warning was so that the relocation of Kennekuk’s tribe would be delayed. He proclaimed that the Great Spirit would give a sign when it was safe to continue traveling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other popular prophets include <a href="/wiki/Neolin" title="Neolin">The Delaware Prophet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muscogee" title="Muscogee">The Red Sticks Prophet.</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Great_Spirit_according_to_Native_Americans">The Great Spirit according to Native Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Great Spirit according to Native Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Spirit is portrayed in Native American history as a powerful force that guides the people in wisdom and survival. In the various tribes, The Great Spirit might be called Earthgrasper, Earthmaker, Gisha Munetoa, Gitchi Manido, The Great Spirit is also referred to as the “Creator”.</p>
<p>An Algonquin legend speaks of a Delaware Indian called Eroneniera that travels to meet The Great Spirit. Upon meeting, The Great Spirit tells Eroneniera that he is the “Maker of Heaven and Earth…because I love you…the land on which you are, I have made for you” (Schoolcraft.1856). The Great Spirit teaches him a prayer to share with his people that they should repeat it every morning and night. The stories of the Native American helped explain mysteries and abstract ideas. The stories also explained weather, animals, and land formations.</p>
<p>The Great Spirit and many other deities are mentioned in the mythology of the Native American. These spirits were characters in stories that were told to “amuse, to instruct, to distract from the cold, hunger, and threats outside the warm circle of the family, band, and culture. Stories explained how the world had been created and how to behave in it” (Brehm. 2011).</p>
<p>Chief Mononcue, of the <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Ohio Wendat</a> a nation of Christianized tribes, spoke to a group of white <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodists</a> in the 1820s. He pointed out that both Native Americans and the white men had been taught to do good. “The Great Spirit has taught you and us both one thing- that we should love one another and fear him. He has taught us by his Spirit and you white men by the Good Book, which is all one.” Mononcue tells the gathered crowd that the white men say that they love the tribes but they give them whiskey and this causes evil and that the white man cheats the Indian and treats him as if he is less than the white man. “Now, your Good Book forbids all this. Why not then, do what it tells you? Then Indians would do right too….Now, brothers, let us all do right; Then our Great Father will be pleased and make us happy in this world, and after death we shall all live together in his house above and always be happy” (Brehm. 2011.)</p>
<p>The Christian missionaries often used the similarities of the two beliefs to teach the tribes about Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myths_about_the_Great_Spirit">Myths about the Great Spirit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Myths about the Great Spirit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Story_of_the_Sleeping_Bear_Dunes">The Story of the Sleeping Bear Dunes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Story of the Sleeping Bear Dunes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chippewa" title="Chippewa">Chippewa</a> legend a forest fire on the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> shoreline forced a mother bear and her two cubs into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>. The cubs became tired and fell behind their mother and eventually drowned within sight of the shoreline. The mother made it to the shore and climbed to the top of a dune to look for her cubs, but they were gone. The mother waited there for days in hopes that her cubs would appear. The Great Spirit was moved by the mother’s devotion and commitment to find her cubs and covered the mother in a blanket of sand so she would have a final resting place and be able to reunite with her cubs. It is said that the mother is still lying in this spot today waiting for her cubs to appear. The Great Spirit also created two islands to mark the spot where the cubs had drowned. The two islands are known today as the North and <a href="/wiki/South_Manitou_Island" title="South Manitou Island">South Manitou Islands</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sleeping_Bear_Dunes" title="Sleeping Bear Dunes">Sleeping Bear Dunes</a> are located on the northwest coast of the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Peninsula_of_Michigan" title="Lower Peninsula of Michigan">Lower Peninsula of Michigan</a> in the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leelanau" title="Leelanau">Leelanau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Story_of_the_Strawberry_and_Blackberry">Story of the Strawberry and Blackberry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Story of the Strawberry and Blackberry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Great Spirit created man and woman and they "lived in happiness for a time, but as husbands and wives have done ever since, they soon began to quarrel". The story explains that the wife leaves the husband and sets off walking toward "the setting sun". The Great Spirit sees that the man is unhappy and creates berries along her path; she ignores the huckleberries, cherries and blackberries along the way. Then The Great Spirit creates strawberries and the woman stops to gather some and the man is able to catch up to her, she shares them with him and they return home together. The berries are then named Odamin, meaning heart berry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
