<p><b>Behavioral modernity</b> is a suite of behavioral and cognitive traits that distinguishes current <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> from other <a href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_human" title="Anatomically modern human">anatomically modern humans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominins" title="Hominins">hominins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>. Although often debated, most scholars agree that modern human behavior can be characterized by abstract thinking, planning depth, <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolic</a> behavior (e.g., art, ornamentation, music), exploitation of large <a href="/wiki/Game_(hunting)" title="Game (hunting)">game</a>, and blade technology, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup> Underlying these behaviors and technological innovations are cognitive and cultural foundations that have been documented experimentally and ethnographically. Some of these human universal patterns are cumulative cultural adaptation, social norms, language, and extensive help and cooperation beyond close kin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill_et_al_2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hill_et_al_2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These traits have been viewed as largely responsible for the human replacement of <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a>, along with the climatic conditions of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, and the peopling of the rest of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_1998_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_1998-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arising from differences in the archaeological record, a debate continues as to whether anatomically modern humans were behaviorally modern as well. There are many theories on the evolution of behavioral modernity. These generally fall into two camps: gradualist and cognitive approaches. The Later Upper Paleolithic Model refers to the idea that modern human behavior arose through cognitive, genetic changes abruptly around 40,000–50,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup> Other models focus on how modern human behavior may have arisen through gradual steps; the archaeological signatures of such behavior only appearing through demographic or subsistence-based changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marean_et_al_2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marean_et_al_2007-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_et_al_2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_et_al_2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Premo_Kuhn_2010_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Premo_Kuhn_2010-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>In order to classify what traits should be included in modern human behavior, it is necessary to define behaviors that are universal among living human groups. Some examples of these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_universals" title="Human universals">human universals</a> are abstract thought, planning, trade, cooperative labor, body decoration, control and use of fire. Along with these traits, humans possess a heavy reliance on social learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culture_and_the_Evo_Process_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_and_the_Evo_Process-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakahashi_2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nakahashi_2013-11">[11]</a></sup> This cumulative cultural change or cultural "ratchet" separates human culture from <a href="/wiki/Social_learning_in_animals" title="Social learning in animals">social learning in animals</a>. As well, a reliance on social learning may be responsible in part for humans' rapid adaptation to many environments outside of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Since cultural universals are found in all cultures including some of the most isolated indigenous groups, these traits must have evolved or have been invented in Africa prior to the exodus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Archaeologically a number of empirical traits have been used as indicators of modern human behavior. While these are often debated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shea_2011_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shea_2011-16">[16]</a></sup> a few are generally agreed upon. Archaeological evidence of behavioral modernity are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleolithic_burial" title="Paleolithic burial">burial</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fishing#History" title="Fishing">fishing</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Art_of_the_Upper_Paleolithic" title="Art of the Upper Paleolithic">figurative art</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave paintings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dendroglyph" title="Dendroglyph">dendroglyphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus_figurines" title="Venus figurines">figurines</a>)</li>
<li>systematic use of <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Ochre" title="Ochre">ochre</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry#Early_history" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a> for decoration or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-ornamentation" title="Self-ornamentation">self-ornamentation</a></li>
<li>Using bone material for tools</li>
<li>Transport of resources over long distances</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blade_(archaeology)" title="Blade (archaeology)">Blade technology</a></li>
<li>Diversity, standardization, and regionally distinct artifacts</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">Hearths</a></li>
<li>Composite tools</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques">Critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several critiques have been placed against the traditional concept of behavioral modernity, both methodologically and philosophically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shea_2011_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shea_2011-16">[16]</a></sup> Shea (2011) outlines a variety of problems with this concept, arguing instead for "behavioral variability", which, according to the author, better describes the archaeological record. The use of trait lists, according to Shea (2011), runs the risk of taphonomic bias, where some sites may yield more artifacts than others despite similar populations; as well, trait lists can be ambiguous in how behaviors may be empirically recognized in the archaeological record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shea_2011_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shea_2011-16">[16]</a></sup> Shea (2011) in particular cautions that population pressure, cultural change, or optimality models, like those in <a href="/wiki/Human_behavioral_ecology" title="Human behavioral ecology">human behavioral ecology</a>, might better predict changes in tool types or subsistence strategies than a change from "archaic" to "modern" behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shea_2011_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shea_2011-16">[16]</a></sup> Some researchers argue that a greater emphasis should be placed on identifying only those artifacts which are unquestionably, or purely, symbolic as a metric for modern human behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_and_Models">Theories and Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theories and Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id='Late_Upper_Paleolithic_Model_or_"Revolution"'></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Upper_Paleolithic_Model_or_.22Revolution.22">Late Upper Paleolithic Model or "Revolution"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: Late Upper Paleolithic Model or "Revolution"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Late Upper Paleolithic Model, or Upper Paleolithic Revolution, refers to the idea that, though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" title="Anatomically modern humans">anatomically modern humans</a> first appear around 150,000 years ago, they were not cognitively or behaviorally "modern" until around 50,000 years ago, leading to their expansion into Europe and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffecker_2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffecker_2009-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tattersall_2009_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tattersall_2009-18">[18]</a></sup> These authors note that traits used as a metric for behavioral modernity do not appear as a package until around 40–50,000 years ago. Klein (1995) specifically describes evidence of fishing, bone shaped as a tool, hearths, significant artifact diversity, and elaborate graves are all absent before this point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup> Although assemblages before 50,000 years ago show some diversity the only distinctly modern tool assemblages appear in Europe at 48,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffecker_2009_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffecker_2009-17">[17]</a></sup> According to these authors, art only becomes common beyond this switching point, signifying a change from archaic to modern humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup> Most researchers argue that a neurological or genetic change, perhaps one enabling complex language, such as <a href="/wiki/FOXP2" title="FOXP2">FOXP2</a>, caused this revolutionary change in our species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tattersall_2009_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tattersall_2009-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_Models">Alternative Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternative Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contrasted with this view of a spontaneous leap in cognition among ancient humans, some authors like <a href="/wiki/Alison_S._Brooks" title="Alison S. Brooks">Alison S. Brooks</a>, primarily working in African archaeology, point to the gradual accumulation of "modern" behaviors, starting well before the 50,000 year benchmark of the Upper Paleolithic Revolution models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foley_Lahr_1997_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foley_Lahr_1997-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Howiesons_Poort" title="Howiesons Poort">Howiesons Poort</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blombos" title="Blombos">Blombos</a>, and other South African archaeological sites, for example, show evidence of marine resource acquisition, trade, and abstract ornamentation at least by 80,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marean_et_al_2007_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marean_et_al_2007-7">[7]</a></sup> Given evidence from Africa and the Middle East, a variety of hypotheses have been put forth to describe an earlier, gradual transition from simple to more complex human behavior. Some authors have pushed back the appearance of fully modern behavior to around 80,000 years ago in order to incorporate the South African data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foley_Lahr_1997_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foley_Lahr_1997-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others focus on the slow accumulation of different technologies and behaviors across time. These researchers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_Marean_2003_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_Marean_2003-2">[2]</a></sup> describe how anatomically modern humans could have been cognitively the same and what we define as behavioral modernity is just the result of thousands of years of cultural adaptation and learning. D'Errico and others have looked at <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a> culture rather than early human behavior for clues into behavioral modernity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_1998_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_1998-5">[5]</a></sup> Noting that Neanderthal assemblages often portray traits similar to those listed for modern human behavior, researchers stress that the foundations for behavioral modernity may in fact lie deeper in our hominin ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_2003_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_2003-20">[20]</a></sup> If both modern humans and Neanderthals express abstract art and complex tools then "modern human behavior" cannot be a derived trait for our species. They argue that the original 'human revolution' theory reflects a profound Eurocentric bias. Recent archaeological evidence, they argue, proves that humans evolving in Africa some 300,000 or even 400,000 years ago were already becoming cognitively and behaviourally 'modern'. These features include blade and microlithic technology, bone tools, increased geographic range, specialized hunting, the use of aquatic resources, long distance trade, systematic processing and use of pigment, and art and decoration. These items do not occur suddenly together as predicted by the ‘‘human revolution’’ model, but at sites that are widely separated in space and time. This suggests a gradual assembling of the package of modern human behaviours in Africa, and its later export to other regions of the Old World.</p>
<p>Between these extremes is the view – currently supported by archaeologists Chris Henshilwood,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Curtis Marean,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Ian Watts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and others – that there was indeed some kind of 'human revolution' but that it occurred in Africa and spanned tens of thousands of years. The term 'revolution' in this context would mean not a sudden mutation but a historical development along the lines of 'the industrial revolution' or 'the Neolithic revolution'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In other words, it was a relatively accelerated process, too rapid for ordinary Darwinian 'descent with modification' yet too gradual to be attributed to a single genetic or other sudden event. These archaeologists point in particular to the relatively explosive emergence of ochre crayons and shell necklaces apparently used for cosmetic purposes. These archaeologists see symbolic organisation of human social life as the key transition in modern human evolution. Recently discovered at sites such as Blombos Cave and Pinnacle Point, South Africa, pierced shells, pigments and other striking signs of personal ornamentation have been dated within a time-window of 70,000 – 160,000 years ago in the African Middle Stone Age, suggesting that the emergence of <i>Homo sapiens</i> coincided, after all, with the transition to modern cognition and behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> While viewing the emergence of language as a 'revolutionary' development, this school of thought generally attributes it to cumulative social, cognitive and cultural evolutionary processes as opposed to a single genetic mutation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>A further view, taken by archaeologists such as Francesco D'Errico<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and João Zilhão,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> is a multi-species perspective arguing that evidence for symbolic culture in the form of utilised pigments and pierced shells are also found in Neanderthal sites, independently of any 'modern' human influence.</p>
<p>Cultural evolutionary models may also shed light on why although evidence of behavioral modernity exists before 50,000 years ago it is not expressed consistently until that point. With small population sizes, human groups would have been affected by demographic and cultural evolutionary forces that may not have allowed for complex cultural traits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_et_al_2009_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_et_al_2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Premo_Kuhn_2010_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Premo_Kuhn_2010-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culture_and_the_Evo_Process_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_and_the_Evo_Process-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakahashi_2013_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nakahashi_2013-11">[11]</a></sup> According to some authors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_et_al_2009_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_et_al_2009-8">[8]</a></sup> until population density became significantly high, complex traits could not have been maintained effectively. It is worth noting that some genetic evidence supports a dramatic increase in population size before human migration out of Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tattersall_2009_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tattersall_2009-18">[18]</a></sup> High local extinction rates within a population also can significantly decrease the amount of diversity in neutral cultural traits, regardless of cognitive ability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Premo_Kuhn_2010_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Premo_Kuhn_2010-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Highly speculatively -- <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicameral_mind" title="Bicameral mind">bicameral mind</a> theory argues for an additional, and cultural rather than genetic, shift from selfless to self-perceiving forms of human cognition and behavior very late in human history, in the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. This is based on a literary analysis of Bronze Age texts which claims to show the first appearances of the concept of <a href="/wiki/Self" title="Self">self</a> around this time, replacing the voices of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a> as the primary form of recorded human cognition. This non-mainstream theory is not widely accepted but does receive serious academic interest from time to time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_Evidence">Archaeological Evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Archaeological Evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the "Out of Africa" theory was generally accepted, there was no consensus on where the human species evolved and, consequently, where modern human behavior arose. Now, however, African archaeology has become extremely important in discovering the origins of humanity. Since human expansion into Europe around 48,000 years ago is generally accepted as already "modern",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffecker_2009_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffecker_2009-17">[17]</a></sup> the question becomes whether behavioral modernity appeared in Africa well before 50,000 years ago, as a late Upper Paleolithic "revolution" which prompted migration out of Africa, or arose outside Africa and diffused back.</p>
<p>A variety of evidence of abstract imagery, widened subsistence strategies, and other "modern" behaviors have been discovered in Africa, especially South Africa. The <a href="/wiki/Blombos_Cave" title="Blombos Cave">Blombos Cave</a> site in South Africa, for example, is famous for rectangular slabs of ochre engraved with geometric designs. Using multiple dating techniques, the site was confirmed to be around 77,000 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henshilwood_2002_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henshilwood_2002-29">[29]</a></sup> Beads and other personal ornamentation have been found from Morocco which might be as old as 130,000 years old; as well, the Cave of Hearths in South Africa has yielded a number of beads significantly before 50,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Expanding subsistence strategies beyond big-game hunting and the consequential diversity in tool types has been noted as signs of behavioral modernity. A number of South African sites have shown an early reliance on aquatic resources from fish to shellfish. Pinnacle Point, in particular, shows exploitation of marine resources as early as 120,000 years ago, perhaps in response to more arid conditions inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marean_et_al_2007_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marean_et_al_2007-7">[7]</a></sup> Establishing a reliance on predictable shellfish deposits, for example, could reduce mobility and facilitate complex social systems and symbolic behavior. Blombos Cave and Site 440 in Sudan both show evidence of fishing as well. Taphonomic change in fish skeletons from Blombos Cave have been interpreted as capture of live fish, clearly an intentional human behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrearty_Brooks_2000_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-McBrearty_Brooks_2000-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While traditionally described as evidence for the later Upper Paleolithic Model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1995_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Klein_1995-6">[6]</a></sup> European archaeology has shown that the issue is more complex. A variety of stone tool technologies are present at the time of human expansion into Europe and show evidence of modern behavior. Despite the problems of conflating specific tools with cultural groups, the <a href="/wiki/Aurignacian" title="Aurignacian">Aurignacian</a> tool complex, for example, is generally taken as a purely modern human signature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joris_Street_2008_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joris_Street_2008-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anikovich_2007_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anikovich_2007-31">[31]</a></sup> The discovery of "transitional" complexes, like "proto-Aurignacian", have been taken as evidence of human groups progressing through "steps of innovation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joris_Street_2008_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joris_Street_2008-30">[30]</a></sup> If, as this might suggest, human groups were already migrating into eastern Europe around 40,000 years and only afterward show evidence of behavioral modernity, then either the cognitive change must have diffused back into Africa or was already present before migration.</p>
<p>In light of a growing body of evidence of Neanderthal culture and tool complexes some researchers have put forth a "multiple species model" for behavioral modernity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_1998_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_1998-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_2003_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_2003-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abadia_2010_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abadia_2010-32">[32]</a></sup> Neanderthals were often cited as being an evolutionary dead-end, apish cousins who were less advanced than their human contemporaries. Personal ornaments were relegated as trinkets or poor imitations compared to the cave art produced by <i>H. sapiens</i>. Despite this, European evidence has shown a variety of personal ornaments and artistic artifacts produced by Neanderthals; for example, the Neanderthal site of <a href="/wiki/Grotte_du_Renne" title="Grotte du Renne">Grotte du Renne</a> has produced grooved bear, wolf, and fox incisors, ochre and other symbolic artifacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abadia_2010_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abadia_2010-32">[32]</a></sup> Though burials are few and controversial, there has been circumstantial evidence of Neanderthal ritual burials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_2003_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_2003-20">[20]</a></sup> There are two options to describe this symbolic behavior among Neanderthals: they copied cultural traits from arriving modern humans or they had their own cultural traditions comparative with behavioral modernity. If they just copied cultural traditions, which is debated by several authors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_1998_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_1998-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Errico_2003_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Errico_2003-20">[20]</a></sup> they still possessed the capacity for complex culture described by behavioral modernity. As discussed above, if Neanderthals also were "behaviorally modern" then it cannot be a species-specific derived trait.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavioral_modernity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most debates surrounding behavioral modernity have been focused on Africa or Europe but an increasing amount of focus has been placed on East Asia. This region offers a unique opportunity to test hypotheses of multi-regionalism, replacement, and demographic effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_Jin_2009_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_Jin_2009-33">[33]</a></sup> Unlike Europe, where initial migration occurred around 50,000 years ago, human remains have been dated in China to around 100,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu_et_al._2010_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu_et_al._2010-34">[34]</a></sup> This early evidence of human expansion calls into question behavioral modernity as an impetus for migration.</p>
<p>Stone tool technology is particularly of interest in East Asia. Following <a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a> migrations out of Africa, <a href="/wiki/Acheulean" title="Acheulean">Acheulean</a> technology never seems to appear beyond present-day India and into China. Analogously, Mode 3, or <a href="/wiki/Levallois_technique" title="Levallois technique">Levallois</a> technology, is not apparent in China following later hominin dispersals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_Bae_2008_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_Bae_2008-35">[35]</a></sup> This lack of more advanced technology has been explained by serial founder effects and low population densities out of Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lycett_Norton_2010_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lycett_Norton_2010-36">[36]</a></sup> Though tool complexes comparative to Europe are missing or fragmentary, other archaeological evidence shows behavioral modernity. For example, the peopling of the Japanese archipelago offers an opportunity to investigate the early use of watercraft. Though one site, Kanedori in Honshu, does suggest the use of watercraft as early as 84,000 years ago, there is no other evidence of hominins in Japan until 50,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_Jin_2009_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_Jin_2009-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zhoukoudian" title="Zhoukoudian">Zhoukoudian</a> cave system near Beijing has been excavated since the 1930s and has yielded precious data on early human behavior in East Asia. Though disputed, there is evidence of possible human burials and interred remains in the cave dated to around 34-20,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_Jin_2009_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_Jin_2009-33">[33]</a></sup> These remains have associated personal ornaments in the form of beads and worked shell, suggesting symbolic behavior. Along with possible burials, numerous other symbolic objects like punctured animal teeth and beads, some dyed in red <a href="/wiki/Ochre" title="Ochre">ochre</a>, have all been found at Zhoukoudian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_Jin_2009_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_Jin_2009-33">[33]</a></sup> Though fragmentary, the archaeological record of eastern Asia shows evidence of behavioral modernity before 50,000 years ago but, like the African record, it is not fully apparent until that time.</p>
