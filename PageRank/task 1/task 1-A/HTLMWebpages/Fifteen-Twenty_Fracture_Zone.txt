<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fifteen-Twenty Fracture Zone</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Fifteen-Twenty</b> or <b>15°20' Fracture Zone</b> (<b>FTFZ</b>), also known as the <b>Cabo Verde Fracture Zone</b>, is s <a href="/wiki/Fracture_zone" title="Fracture zone">fracture zone</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> (MAR) in the central <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> between 14–16°N. It is the current location of the migrating <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">triple junction</a> marking the boundaries between the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American</a>, and <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">Nubian</a> plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujiwara-bathy_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fujiwara-bathy-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujiwara-intro_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fujiwara-intro-2">[2]</a></sup> The FTFZ is roughly parallel to the North and South America—Africa spreading direction and has a broad axial valley produced over the last ten million years by the northward-migrating triple junction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujiwara-bathy_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fujiwara-bathy-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujiwara-intro_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fujiwara-intro-2">[2]</a></sup> Offsetting the MAR by some 175 km, the FTFZ is located on one of the slowest portions of the MAR where the full spreading rate is 25 km/Myr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Godard-414_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Godard-414-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_setting">Geological setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifteen-Twenty_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geological setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>North and south of the FTFZ the axis of the MAR is near-perpendicular to the spreading direction and the spreading rate is 2.6 mm/yr. The axial valley south of the FTFZ is composed of short axial volcanic ridges separated by 8–18 km-long en echelon deeps, while north of the FTFZ the axial ridges are much longer and more linear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EscCan-p415_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EscCan-p415-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>North and south of the FTFZ the ocean floor is relatively smooth with long abyssal hills, probably <a href="/wiki/Detachment_fault" title="Detachment fault">detachment faults</a>, aligned near-parallel to the ridge axis. In contrast, close to the FTFZ the terrain is more rugged and adorned with short, oblique <a href="/wiki/Fault_scarp" title="Fault scarp">fault scarps</a>. Associated with the transition between these two types of terrains (at about 15°50'N and 14°30'N respectively) are V-shaped, south-propagating structures. These transitional structures disappear away from the ridge. Within the rugged terrain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentinized" title="Serpentinized">serpentinized</a> <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a> are capped with a thin layer of extrusive basalt. In the smooth areas the lithosphere is more magmatic in composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EscCan-p415_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-EscCan-p415-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The FTFZ is flanked by two negative <a href="/wiki/Gravity_anomaly" title="Gravity anomaly">gravity anomalies</a> associated with the accretion of igneous crust. The anomaly south of the FTFZ is twice as large as the northern one. There are also geochemical variations across the fracture zone. On the southern side basalts are enriched MORB (mid-ocean ridge basalt) but on the northern side basalts change from enriched to depleted away from the FTFZ. <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">Peridotites</a> collected from south of the FTFZ have an uncommon composition ascribed to a H<sub>2</sub>O-rich or hot mantle source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Godard-414_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Godard-414-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megamullions">Megamullions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifteen-Twenty_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Megamullions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Corrugated surfaces known as megamullions or <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_core_complex" title="Oceanic core complex">oceanic core complexes</a> measure 25 km along-axis and 10–15 km across. When found along other <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridges</a> such structures occur at the inside corners of ridge discontinuities, but at the FTFZ they occur on both sides of the ridge away from any non-transform discontinuities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EscCan-p415_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-EscCan-p415-4">[4]</a></sup> These structures and ultramafic rocks outcropping on either side of the MAR (in contrast to other parts of the ridge) indicate considerably reduced magma supply near the FTFZ. Paradoxically, geochemical analyses of basalts near the FTFZ instead suggest an enriched mantle source and the presence of a mantle hotspot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujiwara-intro_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fujiwara-intro-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two models can explain these contradictions. A westward ridge jump could relocate an older megamullion on the original western flank to the opposite flank after which a new megamullion start to form on the new western flank. Near the FTFZ this would place the older megamullion in an outside corner while the younger develop in an inside corner. Alternatively, an eastward ridge jump or migration could turn a west-dipping detachment fault into an east-dipping fault, which would also result in an older abandoned and a youger active megamullion. Which is the case is currently not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Superimposed on the larger corrugated surfaces are two systems of smaller scale corrugations: one on a 1–3 km-scale, roughly 200 m high, and another finer about 100–500 m wide. The latter occur up to 1 km from the ridge and is covered by elevated ridges running parallel to the spreading direction, about 10 m wide, hundreds of metres long, and 10 m tall. These in turn are covered with cm-scale striations running in the same direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triple_junction">Triple junction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifteen-Twenty_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Triple junction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The North American–South American–African triple junction is associated with the initial opening of the Atlantic Ocean and has had a complex tectonic history. It probably migrated from near 10°N to its current location near the FTFZ between 72.5 and 35.5 Ma. Both its location and that of the North America–South-American–Caribbean triple junction are debated, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The initiation and evolution of triple junctions is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plumes</a>, but, if this is the case near the FTFZ, the limited supply of magma suggest an embryonic plume or a local, anomalous mantle composition. The relative movement between the North American and South American plates is very small, but the resulting deformation could possibly explain both off-axis seismicity and the odd mantle composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
