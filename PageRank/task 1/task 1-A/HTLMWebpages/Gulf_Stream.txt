<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gulf Stream</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream_(disambiguation)" title="Gulf Stream (disambiguation)">Gulf Stream (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Golfstrom.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="363" data-file-width="363" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Golfstrom.jpg/300px-Golfstrom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Golfstrom.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Golfstrom.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_temperature" title="Surface temperature">Surface temperatures</a> in the western North Atlantic. The North American landmass is black and dark blue (cold), while the Gulf Stream is red (warm). Source: <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Gulf Stream</b>, together with its northern extension the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a>, is a warm and swift <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean current</a> that originates in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and stretches to the tip of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and follows the eastern coastlines of the United States and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> before crossing the Atlantic Ocean. The process of <a href="/wiki/Boundary_current" title="Boundary current">western intensification</a> causes the Gulf Stream to be a northward accelerating current off the east coast of North America. At about <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gulf_Stream&amp;params=40_0_N_30_0_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°0′N</span> <span class="longitude">30°0′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.000°N 30.000°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">40.000; -30.000</span></span></span></a></span>, it splits in two, with the northern stream, the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a>, crossing to Northern Europe and the southern stream, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Current" title="Canary Current">Canary Current</a>, recirculating off West Africa.</p>
<p>The Gulf Stream influences the climate of the east coast of North America from <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, and the west coast of Europe. Although there has been recent debate, there is consensus that the climate of Western Europe and Northern Europe is warmer than it would otherwise be due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_drift" title="North Atlantic drift">North Atlantic drift</a> which is the northeastern section of the Gulf Stream. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Gyre" title="North Atlantic Gyre">North Atlantic Gyre</a>. Its presence has led to the development of strong <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> of all types, both within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and within the ocean. The Gulf Stream is also a significant potential source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_power" title="Renewable power">renewable power</a> generation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Gulf Stream may be <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">slowing down as a result of climate change</a>.</p>
<p>The Gulf Stream is typically 100 kilometres (62 mi) wide and 800 metres (2,600 ft) to 1,200 metres (3,900 ft) deep. The current velocity is fastest near the surface, with the maximum speed typically about 2.5 metres per second (5.6 mph).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Franklingulfstream.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4761" data-file-width="5868" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Franklingulfstream.jpg/300px-Franklingulfstream.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Franklingulfstream.jpg/450px-Franklingulfstream.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Franklingulfstream.jpg/600px-Franklingulfstream.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Franklingulfstream.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>'s map of the Gulf Stream</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>European discovery of the Gulf Stream dates to the 1512 expedition of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de León">Juan Ponce de León</a>, after which it became widely used by Spanish ships sailing from the Caribbean to Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A summary of Ponce de León's voyage log, on April 22, 1513, noted, "A current such that, although they had great wind, they could not proceed forward, but backward and it seems that they were proceeding well; at the end it was known that the current was more powerful than the wind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Its existence was also known to <a href="/wiki/Peter_Martyr_d%27Anghiera" title="Peter Martyr d'Anghiera">Peter Martyr d'Anghiera</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> became interested in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a> circulation patterns. In 1768, while in England, Franklin heard a curious complaint from the Colonial Board of Customs: Why did it take British packets several weeks longer to reach New York from England than it took an average American merchant ship to reach <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, despite the merchant ships leaving from London and having to sail down the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and then the length of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> before they sailed across the Atlantic, while the packets left from <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> in Cornwall?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuchman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tuchman-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Franklin asked Timothy Folger, his cousin twice removed (Nantucket Historical Society), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nantucket_Island" title="Nantucket Island">Nantucket Island</a> whaling captain, for an answer. Folger explained that merchant ships routinely crossed the then-unnamed Gulf Stream—identifying it by whale behavior, measurement of the water's temperature and the speed of bubbles on its surface, and changes in the water's color—while the mail packet captains ran against it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuchman_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tuchman-3">[3]</a></sup> Franklin worked with Folger and other experienced ship captains, learning enough to chart the Gulf Stream and giving it the name by which it is still known today. He offered this information to Anthony Todd, secretary of the British Post Office, but it was ignored by British sea captains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuchman_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-tuchman-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Franklin's Gulf Stream chart was published in 1770 in England, where it was mostly ignored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Subsequent versions were printed in France in 1778 and the U.S. in 1786.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ocean_explorer_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ocean_explorer-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gulf Stream proper is a <a href="/wiki/Boundary_current#Western_intensification" title="Boundary current">western-intensified</a> current, driven largely by wind stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_current" title="North Atlantic current">North Atlantic Drift</a>, in contrast, is largely <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>–driven. In 1958 the oceanographer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stommel" title="Henry Stommel">Henry Stommel</a> noted that "very little water from the Gulf of Mexico is actually in the Stream".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> By carrying warm water northeast across the Atlantic, it makes <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> warmer than it otherwise would be.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DRIFT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-DRIFT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the extent of its contribution to the actual temperature differential between North America and Europe is a matter of dispute, as there is a recent minority opinion within the science community that this temperature difference (beyond that caused by contrasting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_climate" title="Maritime climate">maritime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climates</a>) is mainly due to atmospheric waves created by the Rocky Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_behavior">Formation and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Golfstream.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="745" data-file-width="1024" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Golfstream.jpg/275px-Golfstream.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Golfstream.jpg/413px-Golfstream.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Golfstream.jpg/550px-Golfstream.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Golfstream.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Evolution of the Gulf Stream to the west of Ireland continuing as the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A river of sea water, called the <a href="/wiki/North_Equatorial_Current" title="North Equatorial Current">Atlantic North Equatorial Current</a>, flows westward off the coast of Central Africa. When this current interacts with the northeastern coast of South America, the current forks into two branches. One passes into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, while a second, the <a href="/wiki/Antilles_Current" title="Antilles Current">Antilles Current</a>, flows north and east of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">West Indies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These two branches rejoin north of the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Florida" title="Straits of Florida">Straits of Florida</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> blow westward in the tropics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> blow eastward at mid-latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This wind pattern applies a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> to the subtropical ocean surface with negative <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a> across the north Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sverdrup_transport" title="Sverdrup transport">Sverdrup transport</a> is equatorward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of conservation of <a href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity" title="Potential vorticity">potential vorticity</a> caused by the northward-moving winds on the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>'s western periphery and the increased relative vorticity of northward moving water, transport is balanced by a narrow, accelerating poleward current, which flows along the western boundary of the ocean basin, outweighing the effects of friction with the western boundary current known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labrador_current" title="Labrador current">Labrador current</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The conservation of potential vorticity also causes bends along the Gulf Stream, which occasionally break off due to a shift in the Gulf Stream's position, forming separate warm and cold eddies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This overall process, known as western intensification, causes currents on the western boundary of an ocean basin, such as the Gulf Stream, to be stronger than those on the eastern boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a consequence, the resulting Gulf Stream is a strong ocean current. It transports water at a rate of 30 million cubic meters per second (30 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">sverdrups</a>) through the Florida Straits. As it passes south of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, this rate increases to 150 million cubic metres per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The volume of the Gulf Stream dwarfs all rivers that empty into the Atlantic combined, which barely total 0.6 million cubic metres per second. It is weaker, however, than the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Given the strength and proximity of the Gulf Stream, beaches along the East Coast of the United States may be more vulnerable to large sea-level anomalies, which significantly impact rates of <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Gulf Stream is typically 100 kilometres (62 mi) wide and 800 metres (2,600 ft) to 1,200 metres (3,900 ft) deep. The current velocity is fastest near the surface, with the maximum speed typically about 2.5 metres per second (5.6 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> As it travels north, the warm water transported by the Gulf Stream undergoes evaporative cooling. The cooling is wind-driven: Wind moving over the water causes <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, cooling the water and increasing its <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> and density. When sea ice forms, salts are left out of the ice, a process known as brine exclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> These two processes produce water that is denser and colder (or, more precisely, water that is still liquid at a lower temperature). In the North Atlantic Ocean, the water becomes so dense that it begins to sink down through less salty and less dense water. (The <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> action is not unlike that of a <a href="/wiki/Lava_lamp" title="Lava lamp">lava lamp</a>.) This downdraft of cold, dense water becomes a part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>, a southgoing stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Very little <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> lies within the current, although seaweed lies in clusters to its east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Localized_effects">Localized effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Localized effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Florida" title="Climate of Florida">Climate of Florida</a></div>
<p>The Gulf Stream is influential on the climate of the Florida peninsula. The portion off the Florida coast, referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Current" title="Florida Current">Florida current</a>, maintains an average water temperature at or above 24 °C (75 °F) during the winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> East winds moving over this warm water move warm air from over the Gulf Stream inland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> helping to keep temperatures milder across the state than elsewhere across the Southeast during the winter. Also, the Gulf Stream's proximity to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> adds to its <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, as it is the northern limit for southern varieties of plant life, and the southern limit for northern plant species, Nantucket being warmer during winter than the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> of the Gulf Stream, along with similar warm air currents, helps keep Ireland and the western coast of Great Britain a couple of degrees warmer than the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, the difference is most dramatic in the western coastal islands of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A noticeable effect of the Gulf Stream and the strong westerly winds (driven by the warm water of the Gulf Stream) on Europe occurs along the Norwegian coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DRIFT_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-DRIFT-8">[8]</a></sup> Northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> lie close to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> zone, most of which is covered with ice and snow in winter. However, almost all of Norway's coast remains free of ice and snow throughout the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Weather systems warmed by the Gulf Stream drift into Northern Europe, also warming the climate behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_mountains" title="Scandinavian mountains">Scandinavian mountains</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_cyclone_formation">Effect on cyclone formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effect on cyclone formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The warm water and temperature contrast along the edge of the Gulf Stream often increase the intensity of cyclones, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> or otherwise. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclone generation</a> normally requires water temperatures in excess of 26.5 °C (79.7 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-31">[31]</a></sup> Tropical cyclone formation is common over the Gulf Stream, especially in the month of July. Storms travel westward through the Caribbean and then either move in a northward direction and curve toward the eastern coast of the United States or stay on a north-westward track and enter the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-32">[32]</a></sup> Such storms have the potential to create strong winds and extensive damage to the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">United States' Southeast Coastal Areas</a>.</p>
<p>Strong <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> have been shown to deepen significantly along a shallow <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal zone</a>, forced by the Gulf Stream itself during the cold season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclones</a> also tend to generate near the Gulf Stream. 75 percent of such systems documented between 1951 and 2000 formed near this warm water current, with two annual peaks of activity occurring during the months of May and October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Cyclones within the ocean form under the Gulf Stream, extending as deep as 3,500 metres (11,500 ft) beneath the ocean's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_renewable_power_source">Possible renewable power source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Possible renewable power source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The theoretical maximum energy dissipation from Gulf Stream by turbines is in the range of 20-60 GW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> One idea, which would supply the equivalent power of several nuclear power plants, would deploy a field of underwater turbines placed 300 meters (980 ft) under the center of the core of the Gulf Stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">Ocean thermal energy</a> could also be harnessed to produce electricity using the temperature difference between cold deep water and warm surface water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
