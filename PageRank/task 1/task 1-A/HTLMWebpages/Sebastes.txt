<p><i><b>Sebastes</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Sebastidae" title="Sebastidae">Sebastidae</a> (though some include this in <a href="/wiki/Scorpaenidae" title="Scorpaenidae">Scorpaenidae</a>), most of which have the common name of <b>rockfish</b>. A few are called <b>ocean perch</b>, <b>sea perch</b> or <b>redfish</b> instead. Most of the <i>Sebastes</i> species live in the north <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, although two (<i>Sebastes capensis</i> and <i>Sebastes oculatus</i>) live in the south Pacific/<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and four (<i>Sebastes fasciatus</i>, <i>Sebastes mentella</i>, <i>Sebastes norvegicus</i> and <i>Sebastes viviparus</i>) live in the north Atlantic. The coast off South <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> is the area of highest rockfish <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">diversity</a>, with 56 species living in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Bight" title="Southern California Bight">Southern California Bight</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> of rockfish goes back to the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>, from California and Japan (although fossil <a href="/wiki/Otolith" title="Otolith">otoliths</a> from <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, "<i>Sebastes</i>" <i>weileri</i>, may push the record back as far as the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>).</p>
<p>Rockfish are an important sport and commercial fish, and many species have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overfished" title="Overfished">overfished</a>. As a result, seasons are tightly controlled in many areas. <i>Sebastes</i> are sometimes fraudulently substituted for the more expensive <i>Lutjanus campechanus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rockfish range from the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a> to almost 3,000 metres (9,800 ft) deep, usually living <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthically</a> on various substrates, often (as the name suggests) around rock outcrops. Some rockfish species are very long-lived, amongst the <a href="/wiki/List_of_longest-living_organisms" title="List of longest-living organisms">longest-living</a> fish on earth, with several species known to surpass 100 years of age, and a maximum reported age of 205 years for <i><a href="/wiki/Rougheye_rockfish" title="Rougheye rockfish">Sebastes aleutianus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Ecotoxicology,_radioecology"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecotoxicology.2C_radioecology">Ecotoxicology, radioecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecotoxicology, radioecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like all carnivores, these fish can bioaccumulate some pollutants or <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cesium" title="Cesium">cesium</a>. Highly radioactive rockfish have been caught in a port near <a href="/wiki/Fukushima,_Fukushima" title="Fukushima, Fukushima">Fukushima</a> city, Japan, not far from the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant">Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant</a>, nearly two years after the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">nuclear disaster</a> (ex: 107000 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">Bq</a>/kg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tepco2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tepco2013-6">[6]</a></sup> (2013-02-12) ; 116000 Bq/kg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tepco2013_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tepco2013-6">[6]</a></sup> (2013-02-13) and 132000Bq/kg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tepco2013_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tepco2013-6">[6]</a></sup> (2013-02-13), respectively 1070, 1160 and 1320 times more than the maximum allowed by Japanese authorities (as updated on April 1, 2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tepco2013_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tepco2013-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
