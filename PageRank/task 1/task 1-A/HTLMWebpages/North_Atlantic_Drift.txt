<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">North Atlantic Current</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>North Atlantic Current</b> (<b>NAC</b>), also known as <b>North Atlantic Drift</b> and <b>North Atlantic Sea Movement</b>, is a powerful warm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_boundary_current" title="Western boundary current">western boundary current</a> that extends the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> north-eastward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossby-Abst_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rossby-Abst-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The NAC originates from where the Gulf Stream turns north at the Southeast Newfoundland Rise, a submarine ridge that stretches south-east from the Grand Banks. The NAC flows northward east of the Grand Banks, from 40°N to 51°N, before turning sharply east to cross the Atlantic. It transports more warm tropical water to northern latitudes than any other boundary current; more than 40 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sv</a> in the south and 20 Sv as it crosses the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. It reaches speeds of 2 knots near the North American coast. Directed by topography, the NAC meanders heavily but, in contrast to the meanders of the Gulf Stream, the NAC meanders remain stable without breaking off into eddies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossby-Abst_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rossby-Abst-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colder parts of the Gulf Stream turn northward near the "tail" of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland" title="Grand Banks of Newfoundland">Grand Banks</a> at 50°W where the <a href="/wiki/Azores_Current" title="Azores Current">Azores Current</a> branches off to flow south of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. From there the NAC flows north-eastward east of the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Cap" title="Flemish Cap">Flemish Cap</a> (47°N, 45°W). Approaching the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>, it then turns eastward and becomes much broader and more diffuse. It then splits into a colder, north-eastern branch and a warmer eastern branch. As the warmer branch turns southward most of the subtropical component of the Gulf Stream has been diverted southward and, as a consequence, the North Atlantic is mostly supplied by subpolar waters, including a contribution from the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Current" title="Labrador Current">Labrador Current</a> recirculated into the NAC at 45°N.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>West of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a>, it splits into two major branches. One branch goes southeast, later to become the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Current" title="Canary Current">Canary Current</a> as it passes northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and turns southwest. The other major branch continues north along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Europe" title="Northwestern Europe">Northwestern Europe</a>. Other branches include the <a href="/wiki/Irminger_Current" title="Irminger Current">Irminger Current</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Current" title="Norwegian Current">Norwegian Current</a>. Driven by the global <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> (THC), the North Atlantic Current is part of the wind-driven Gulf Stream, which goes further east and north from the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> coast across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>.</p>
<p>The North Atlantic Current, together with the Gulf Stream, have a long-lived reputation for having a considerable warming influence on European climate. The principal cause for differences in winter climate between North America and Europe is, however, winds rather than ocean currents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
