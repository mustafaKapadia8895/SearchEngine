<p>The <b>windshield</b> (<a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>) or <b>windscreen</b> (<a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">Commonwealth</a> English) of an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">car</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorbike" title="Motorbike">motorbike</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram</a> is the front <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">window</a>. Modern windshields are generally made of <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">laminated safety glass</a>, a type of treated glass, which consists of two (typically) curved sheets of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> layer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laminate" title="Laminate">laminated</a> between them for safety, and are bonded into the window frame. Motorbike windshields are often made of high-impact <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Poly(methyl_methacrylate)" title="Poly(methyl methacrylate)">acrylic</a> plastic.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="700" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg/220px-Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg/330px-Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg/440px-Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raked_windshield_1952_DeSoto.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Split and raked windshield on a 1952 DeSoto. Note the panes of glass are flat.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Windshields protect the vehicle's occupants from wind and flying debris such as dust, insects, and rocks, and provide an aerodynamically formed window towards the front. UV coating <a href="/wiki/Windshield_sun_shades" title="Windshield sun shades">may be applied</a> to screen out harmful ultraviolet radiation. However, this is usually unnecessary since most auto windshields are made from <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">laminated safety glass</a>. The majority of UV-B is absorbed by the glass itself, and any remaining UV-B together with most of the UV-A is absorbed by the PVB bonding layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On motorbikes their main function is to shield the rider from wind, though not as completely as in a car, whereas on sports and racing motorcycles the main function is reducing drag when the rider assumes the optimal aerodynamic configuration with his or her body in unison with the machine, and does not shield the rider from wind when sitting upright.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Windshield-spiderweb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="655" data-file-width="1349" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Windshield-spiderweb.jpg/220px-Windshield-spiderweb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Windshield-spiderweb.jpg/330px-Windshield-spiderweb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Windshield-spiderweb.jpg/440px-Windshield-spiderweb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Windshield-spiderweb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Automobile windshield displaying "spiderweb" cracking typical of laminated safety glass.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early windshields were made of ordinary window glass, but that could lead to serious injuries in the event of a crash. A series of crashes led up to the development of stronger windshields. The most notable example of this is the <i>Pane vs. Ford</i> case of 1917 that decided against Pane in that he was only injured through <a href="/wiki/Reckless_driving" title="Reckless driving">reckless driving</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They were replaced with windshields made of <a href="/wiki/Toughened_glass" title="Toughened glass">toughened glass</a> and were fitted in the frame using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a> <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mechanical)" title="Seal (mechanical)">seal</a>. The hardened glass shattered into many mostly harmless fragments when the windshield broke. These windshields, hovever, could shatter from a simple stone chip. In 1919, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> solved the problem of flying debris by using the new French technology of <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">glass laminating</a>. Windshields made using this process were two layers of glass with a cellulose inner layer. This inner layer held the glass together when it fractured. Between 1919 and 1929, Ford ordered the use of laminated glass on all of his vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern, glued-in windshields contribute to the vehicle's rigidity, but the main force for innovation has historically been the need to prevent injury from sharp glass fragments. Almost all nations now require windshields to stay in one piece even if broken, except if pierced by a strong force. Properly installed automobile windshields are also essential to safety; along with the roof of the car, they provide protection to the vehicle's occupants in the case of a roll-over accident<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Today’s windshields are a safety device just like seat belts and airbags. The installation of the auto glass is done with an automotive grade urethane designed specifically for automobiles. The adhesive creates a molecular bond between the glass and the vehicle. If the adhesive bond fails at any point on the glass it can reduce the effectiveness of the air bag and substantially compromise the structural integrity of the roof.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The urethane sealant is protected from UV in sunlight by a band of dark dots around the edge of the windshield. The darkened edge transitions to the clear windshield with smaller dots to minimize thermal stress in manufacturing. The same band of darkened dots is often expanded around the rear view mirror to act as a sunshade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>In many places, laws restrict the use of heavily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Window_tinting" title="Window tinting">tinted glass</a> in vehicle windshields; generally, laws specify the maximum level of tint permitted. Some vehicles have noticeably more tint in the uppermost part of the windshield to block <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution#Glare" title="Light pollution">sunglare</a>.</p>
<p>In aircraft windshields, an electric current is applied through a conducting layer of <a href="/wiki/Tin_dioxide" title="Tin dioxide">tin(IV) oxide</a> to generate <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> to prevent <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">icing</a>. A similar system for automobile windshields, introduced on <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> vehicles as "<a href="/wiki/Quickclear" title="Quickclear">Quickclear</a>" in Europe ("InstaClear" in North America) in the 1980s and through the early 1990s, used this conductive metallic coating applied to the inboard side of the outer layer of glass. Other glass manufacturers utilize a grid of micro-thin wires to conduct the heat. These systems are more typically utilized by European auto manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Cars" title="Jaguar Cars">Jaguar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a>.</p>
<p>The use of thermal glass prevents some <a href="/wiki/Automotive_navigation_system" title="Automotive navigation system">navigation systems</a> from functioning correctly, as the embedded metal blocks the satellite signal.The RF signal tends to flow along the metal wires or layer so very little radiation can pass. This can be resolved by using an external antenna. Mobile telephones can also have problems; thermal glass typically allows only 0.0001 (1‰, or one per mille) of the signal to pass, whereas a concrete wall with rebars allows up to 0.0100 (10%, or 100‰) of the signal to pass.</p>

<p>The term <i>windshield</i> is used generally throughout North America. The term <i>windscreen</i> is the usual term in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> for all vehicles. In the US <i>windscreen</i> refers to the mesh or foam placed over a <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> to minimize wind noise, while a <i>windshield</i> refers to the front window of a car. In the UK, the terms are reversed, although generally, the foam screen is referred to as a microphone shield, and not a windshield.</p>
<p>Sports or racing cars would sometimes have <b>aero screens</b>, which were small semi-circular or rectangular windshields. These were often mounted in pairs behind a foldable flat windshield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Aero screens are usually less than 20 cm (8 in) in height. They are known as aero screens because they only deflect the wind. The twin aeroscreen setup (often called <a href="/wiki/Brooklands" title="Brooklands">Brooklands</a>) was popular among older sports and modern cars in vintage style.</p>
<p>A <i>wiperless windshield</i> is a windshield that uses a mechanism other than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windshield_wiper" title="Windshield wiper">wipers</a> to remove snow and rain from the windshield. The concept car <a href="/wiki/Acura_TL" title="Acura TL">Acura TL</a> features a wiperless windshield using a series of jet <a href="/wiki/Nozzle" title="Nozzle">nozzles</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cowling" title="Cowling">cowl</a> to blow <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressurized air</a> onto the windshield.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Repair_of_stone-chip_and_crack_damage">Repair of stone-chip and crack damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Repair of stone-chip and crack damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the US National Windshield Repair Association, many types of <a href="/wiki/Stone_damage" title="Stone damage">stone damage</a> can be successfully repaired. Whether the windshield can be repaired always depends upon four factors: the size, type, depth and location of the damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_depth">Size and depth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Size and depth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Repair of cracks up to 6.1 cm (2.4 in) is within permissible limits; automobile glass with more severe damage needs to be replaced. However, this is dependent on local laws. Unless it goes to the edge of the window. It would then compromise the structural integrity of the windshield.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Type">Type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Circular bullseyes, linear cracks, crack chips, dings, pits and star-shaped breaks can be repaired without removing the glass, eliminating the risk of leaking or bonding problems sometimes associated with replacement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some damages are very difficult to repair, or cannot be repaired:</p>
<ul>
<li>on inside of the windshield</li>
<li>deep damage on both layers of glass</li>
<li>damage over rain sensor or internal radio antenna</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>complex multiple cracks</li>
<li>very long cracks (i.e. over 18–24 inches (46–61 cm) long)</li>
<li>contaminated cracks</li>
<li>edge cracks</li>
</ul>
<p>In cracked windshield repair, air is removed from the damaged area on the windshield with a specified vacuum injection pump. Then using the injection pump, clear adhesive resin is injected to replace the air in the windshield crack. The resin is then cured with an ultraviolet light. When done properly, the damaged area’s strength is restored, as is 90–95% of the clarity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacement">Replacement<span id="Windshield_replacement"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Windshields that cannot be repaired have to be replaced. Replacement of a windshield typically takes less than an hour. To ensure the vehicle is safe to drive, time values called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safe_drive_away_time" title="Safe drive away time">Safe Drive Away Time</a> have been established. Windshields which have been replaced must cure or bond sufficiently until they are able to withstand the forces of a crash. Knowing the minimum time needed to cure the glass bonding adhesives is therefore important. This <b>safe drive away time</b> (SDAT) or <b>minimum drive away time</b> (MDAT) refers to the time required until a windshield installation or glass replacement is considered safe to drive again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Criteria are specified in <a href="/wiki/National_Highway_Traffic_Safety_Administration" title="National Highway Traffic Safety Administration">U.S. Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards</a> 212/208 (see <a href="/wiki/Federal_Motor_Vehicle_Safety_Standards" title="Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards">FMVSS</a>) to ensure the reliability of adhesive systems. Typically the SDAT is verified with <a href="/wiki/Crash_test" title="Crash test">crash tests</a> as well as with high speed laboratory test methods.</p>
<p>Consumers may be unaware that the MDAT or SDAT time is focused on safety and not necessarily on the quality, durability, or warranty of the installation. Care must be taken not to drive the vehicle prior to the SDAT/MDAT. If a vehicle is released to be driven before the SDAT and the adhesive used to set the new windshield has not had appropriate cure time, the occupants will not be properly protected in the event of a collision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Airbag" title="Airbag">Airbags</a> deploy at speeds up to 200 mph (320 km/h; 89 m/s) and in some cases exert tremendous force on the windshield. Occupants can impact the airbag just 50 ms after initial deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Depending on vehicle design, airbag deployment and/or occupant impact into the airbag may increase forces on the windshield, dramatically in some cases. Forces of occupants on the airbags - and hence the potential forces on the windshield - are lower for belted occupants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As consequence, <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesive</a> suppliers usually inform their customers about the level of security achieved:</p>
<p>1) Example: Security exceeding FMVSS 212/208 belted</p>
<p>2) Example: Security exceeding FMVSS 212/208 unbelted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the advent of quick cure adhesives, mobile windshield replacements have become more prevalent. Often the temperature and humidity cannot be controlled for mobile installations. For most common glass adhesives the ideal environment is 70 °F (21 °C) and 50% <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. Variations from the ideal curing environment can increase the time needed for a sufficiently safe bond to form. A controlled environment always ensures the performance and safety of the windshield installation.</p>
<p>If not properly cured, the adhesive bond could fail during a crash, allowing occupants to be injured, ejected, or causing other harm. Because of the variables and difficulties involved in mobile windshield replacement, many vehicle manufacturers do not recommend this method of installation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disposal">Disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windshield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" title="Waste disposal">Waste disposal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">laminated glass</a> is no longer permitted in landfill in most European countries as the <a href="/wiki/End_of_Life_Vehicles_Directive" title="End of Life Vehicles Directive">End of Life Vehicles Directive</a> (ELV) is implemented. A study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrey_University" title="Surrey University">Surrey University</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilkington_Glass" title="Pilkington Glass">Pilkington Glass</a> proposes that waste laminated glass be placed into a separating device such as a rolling mill where the glass is fragmented and the larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cullet" title="Cullet">cullet</a> is mechanically detached from the inner film. The application of heat then melts the laminating plastic, usually Polyvinyl Butyral "PVB" enabling both the glass and the interior film to be recycled. The PVB recycling process is a simple procedure of melting and reshaping it.</p>
