<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Central American Seaway</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Central American Seaway</b>, also known as the <b>Panamanic</b> <b>Inter-American</b> and <b>Proto-Caribbean Seaway</b>, was a body of water that once separated <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. It formed in the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> (200–154 <a href="/wiki/Year#Abbreviations_yr_and_ya" title="Year">Ma</a>) during the separation of the <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaean</a> supercontinent, and closed when the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> was formed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> activity in the late <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> (2.76–2.54 Ma).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1052" data-file-width="900" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg/220px-Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg/330px-Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg/440px-Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_American_Biotic_Interchange_examples.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>Figure 1. The Great American Interchange</b></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The closure of the Central American Seaway had tremendous effects on <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">oceanic circulation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> of the adjacent seas, isolating many species and triggering <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> and diversification of tropical and sub-tropical marine fauna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lessios_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lessios-1">[1]</a></sup> The inflow of <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">nutrient-rich water of deep Pacific origin</a> into the Caribbean was blocked, so local species had to adapt to an environment of lower productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jain_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jain-2">[2]</a></sup> It had an even larger impact on terrestrial life. The seaway had isolated South America for much of the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>, allowing the evolution of a wholly unique diverse mammalian fauna there; when it closed, a <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Interchange" title="Great American Interchange">faunal exchange</a> with North America ensued, leading to the extinction of many of the native South American forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GGSimpson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GGSimpson-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LandMammals_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LandMammals-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence">Evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Seaway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The evidence for when the Central American landmass emerged and the closing of the Central American Seaway can be divided into three categories. The first is the direct geologic observation of crustal thickening and submarine deposits in Central America. The second is the <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Interchange" title="Great American Interchange">Great American Interchange</a> of vertebrates between North and South America which required a continuous land bridge across the two areas for the organisms to travel along with a climate that was very different than the climate today. Lastly is the development of differences in marine assemblages and their isotopic signatures in the Caribbean from those in the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Great American Interchange gives climate change the role as an arid Central American environment was needed in order for vertebrates to go from the savannas across the Panama region. The Central American Seaway was closed by the elevation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_American_Isthmus" title="Central American Isthmus">Central American Isthmus</a> which is proposed to have occurred three and a half to five million years ago. The closing of the Central American Seaway is also supported by the evolution of taxa on different sides of the Central American Isthmus along with the different histories of the oceans on either side of the isthmus.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Seaway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The closing of the seaway allowed a major migration of land mammals between North and South America or The <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Interchange" title="Great American Interchange">Great American Interchange</a>. This allowed the entrance of species of mammals such as sloths, cats, horses, elephants and camels to migrate from North America to South America. It is believed that the animals that were able to cross the land bridge were savannah animals as the rain forest animals would not be able to cross Central America. There is much controversy about glacial and interglacial climates in South America. Research shows that vegetation in most of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon basin</a> has changed a very small amount since glacial times. It is also believed that the area was more of a savannah during glacial times, but it is believed that the area is quite the same. Predicted connections between a closed seaway and glaciation are causation for a very different North Atlantic Ocean circulation having an effect on the surrounding atmospheric temperatures thus permitting South American organisms to tolerate the North American Climate. The emergence of South American organisms is due to the widening of that organisms' <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a>. The niche concept is that if an area supports an organism's need for survival then it is more likely to live there. The <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Panama Isthmus</a> is believed to have emerged due to the appearance of glaciation in the Northern Hemisphere. The appearance of the isthmus is then credited to changing the current therefore affecting the atmosphere causing the recurrence of ice sheets in the Northern Hemisphere and is believed to be the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">Ice Age</a>. The emergence of the isthmus caused a reflection of the westward-flowing North Equatorial Current northward and enhanced the northward-flowing Gulf Stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
