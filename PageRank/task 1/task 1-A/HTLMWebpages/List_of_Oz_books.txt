<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of Oz books</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Oz books</b> form a <a href="/wiki/Book_series" title="Book series">book series</a> that begins with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i> (1900) and relate the fictional history of the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Oz" title="Land of Oz">Land of Oz</a>. Oz was created by author <a href="/wiki/L._Frank_Baum" title="L. Frank Baum">L. Frank Baum</a>, who went on to write fourteen full-length Oz books. All of the Baum written books are in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Even while he was alive, Baum was styled as "the Royal Historian of Oz" to emphasize the concept that Oz is an actual place. The illusion created was that characters such as <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Ozma" title="Princess Ozma">Princess Ozma</a> related their adventures in Oz to Baum themselves, by means of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_telegraph" title="Wireless telegraph">wireless telegraph</a>.</p>

Gottschalk">Louis F. Gottschalk</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tik-Tok_Man_of_Oz" title="The Tik-Tok Man of Oz">The Tik-Tok Man of Oz</a></i>. Also in 1909, he wrote a play called <i>The Girl from Oz</i>. The manuscript is held in the archives at <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_University" title="Syracuse University">Syracuse University</a>, but apparently its relation to Oz is little more than nominal (it is also known as <i>The Girl from Tomorrow</i> and was later adapted for radio by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Joslyn_Baum" title="Frank Joslyn Baum">Frank Joslyn Baum</a>), as is also the case with the short story, "The Littlest Giant", a rather brutal tale designated in two lines to be in the Gillikin country of Oz. With Gottschalk writing the music, he wrote an unproduced stage version of <i>The Patchwork Girl of Oz</i> in November 1913, that was developed into the film scenario.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_Oz_books_by_other_writers">Subsequent Oz books by other writers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Oz_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent Oz books by other writers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Oz books that were written subsequent to Baum's death can be classified into two categories:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The books that were founded on L. Frank Baum's original canon</i>: Publishers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reilly_%26_Lee" title="Reilly &amp; Lee">Reilly &amp; Lee</a>, who had printed 13 out of Baum's 14 Oz novels, printed an additional 26 Oz books after Baum's death, over the ensuing decades - 19 by <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Plumly_Thompson" title="Ruth Plumly Thompson">Ruth Plumly Thompson</a>, three by <a href="/wiki/John_R._Neill" title="John R. Neill">John R. Neill</a>, two by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Snow_(writer)" title="Jack Snow (writer)">Jack Snow</a> and one each by <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Cosgrove_Payes" title="Rachel Cosgrove Payes">Rachel Cosgrove Payes</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Eloise_Jarvis_McGraw" title="Eloise Jarvis McGraw">Eloise Jarvis McGraw</a> &amp; her daughter. Other publishers have since printed a plethora of additional Oz books, written by the aforementioned authors as well as many other fans (more below).</li>
<li><i>The books that do not follow L. Frank Baum's original canon, but create an alternate Oz</i>: Most notable among such books are Gregory Maguire's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked:_The_Life_and_Times_of_the_Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked: The Life and Times of the Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked</a></i> (more below).</li>
</ul>
<p>The Oz books of <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Smith" title="Sherwood Smith">Sherwood Smith</a>, published in 2005 and 2006, are officially recognized as canon by The Baum Trust.</p>
<h3><span id="Subsequent_books_by_other_writers_published_by_Reilly_&amp;_Lee"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_books_by_other_writers_published_by_Reilly_.26_Lee">Subsequent books by other writers published by Reilly &amp; Lee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Oz_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subsequent books by other writers published by Reilly &amp; Lee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ruth Plumly Thompson's style was markedly different from Baum's. Her tales harked back to more traditional <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a>. She often included a small kingdom, with a prince or princess who saves his or her kingdom and regains the throne or saves Oz from invasion. Thompson even respelled Baum's respelling "<a href="/wiki/Nome_King" title="Nome King">Nome</a>" as the more traditional "<a href="/wiki/Gnome" title="Gnome">Gnome</a>".</p>

<p>Illustrator John R. Neill's vision of Oz is more manic than Thompson or Baum's. Houses often get up and do battle, and everything can be alive. His entries take Oz's color scheme (blue for <a href="/wiki/Munchkin_Country" title="Munchkin Country">Munchkin Country</a>, red for <a href="/wiki/Quadling_Country" title="Quadling Country">Quadling Country</a>, etc.) to an extreme, extending it to sky and skin colors.</p>

Based upon the writings of L. Frank Baum, it is a <a href="/wiki/Historical_revisionism" title="Historical revisionism">revisionist</a> look at the land and characters of Oz, drawing primarily from Baum's 1900 novel <i>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</i> and the 1939 film <i>The Wizard of Oz</i>.
<p>The novel presents events, characters and situations from Baum's books and the film in new ways, with several differences between the L. Frank Baum series and the Wicked Cycle. These differences arise from the original Oz functioning as a mirror-image of Kansas in a cultural and economic framework: Oz was wealthy, prosperous and had excellent agricultural yields while Kansas was characterized by economic hardship, environmental difficulties and poor harvests. The social strife described in the Wicked Cycle indicates that the two series are set in similar and internally consistent but distinctly separate visions of Oz.</p>
<p>The novel focuses on the life of the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a>, whom Maguire gives the name <a href="/wiki/Elphaba" title="Elphaba">Elphaba</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike the popular 1939 movie and Baum's writings, this novel is not directed at children, and contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_language" title="Adult language">adult language</a> and content.</p>
