<p>The transition between the end of the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> is marked by large-scale extinction and floral and faunal turnover (although minor in comparison to the <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">largest mass extinctions</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most of the affected organisms were marine or aquatic in nature. They included the last of the ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetaceans" title="Cetaceans">cetaceans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archaeoceti" title="Archaeoceti">Archaeoceti</a>.</p>
<p>This was a time of major <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climatic change</a>, especially cooling, not obviously linked with any single major impact or any catastrophic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> One cause of the extinction event is speculated to be extended volcanic activity. Another speculation is that the extinctions are related to several large <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a> impacts that occurred about this time. One such event caused the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay_impact_crater" title="Chesapeake Bay impact crater">Chesapeake Bay impact crater</a> (40 km), and another at the <a href="/wiki/Popigai_crater" title="Popigai crater">Popigai crater</a> (100 km) of central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, scattering debris perhaps as far as Europe. New dating of the Popigai meteor suggests it may be a cause of the mass extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A leading scientific theory on climate cooling at this time is decrease in atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, which slowly declined in the mid to late Eocene and possibly reached some threshold approximately 34 million years ago. This boundary is closely linked with the Oligocene Oi-1 event, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope" title="Oxygen isotope">oxygen isotope</a> excursion that marks the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> coverage on Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grande_Coupure">Grande Coupure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eocene%E2%80%93Oligocene_extinction_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Grande Coupure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Grande Coupure</i>, or "great break" in continuity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with a major European turnover in mammalian fauna about 33.5 Ma, marks the end of the last phase of Eocene assemblages, the <a href="/wiki/Priabonian" title="Priabonian">Priabonian</a>, and the arrival in Europe of Asian species. The Grande Coupure is characterized by widespread extinctions and <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">allopatric speciation</a> in small isolated <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">relict populations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was given its name in 1910 by the <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Hans_Georg_Stehlin" title="Hans Georg Stehlin">Hans Georg Stehlin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> to characterise the dramatic turnover of European mammalian fauna, which he placed at the Eocene-Oligocene boundary. A comparable turnover in Asian fauna has since been called the "Mongolian Remodelling".</p>
<p>The Grande Coupure marks a break between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> European faunas before the break and mixed faunas with a strong Asian component afterwards. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._J._Hooker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. J. Hooker (page does not exist)">J. J. Hooker</a> and his team summarized the break:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooker_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hooker-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>"Pre-Grande Coupure faunas are dominated by the perissodact family <a href="/wiki/Palaeotheriidae" title="Palaeotheriidae">Palaeotheriidae</a> (distant <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a> relatives), six families of artiodactyls (cloven-hoofed mammals) (<a href="/wiki/Anoplotheriidae" title="Anoplotheriidae">Anoplotheriidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xiphodontidae" title="Xiphodontidae">Xiphodontidae</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Choeropotamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Choeropotamidae (page does not exist)">Choeropotamidae</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cebochoeridae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cebochoeridae (page does not exist)">Cebochoeridae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dichobunidae" title="Dichobunidae">Dichobunidae</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Amphimerycidae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Amphimerycidae (page does not exist)">Amphimerycidae</a>), the rodent family <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudosciuridae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pseudosciuridae (page does not exist)">Pseudosciuridae</a>, the primate families <a href="/wiki/Omomyidae" title="Omomyidae">Omomyidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adapiformes" title="Adapiformes">Adapidae</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Archonta" title="Archonta">archontan</a> family <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nyctitheriidae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nyctitheriidae (page does not exist)">Nyctitheriidae</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>"Post-Grande Coupure faunas include the true rhinoceros (family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhinocerotidae" title="Rhinocerotidae">Rhinocerotidae</a>), three artiodactyl families (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entelodontidae" title="Entelodontidae">Entelodontidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthracotheriidae" title="Anthracotheriidae">Anthracotheriidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gelocidae" title="Gelocidae">Gelocidae</a>) related respectively to <a href="/wiki/Suidae" title="Suidae">pigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> families <a href="/wiki/Eomyidae" title="Eomyidae">Eomyidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricetidae" title="Cricetidae">Cricetidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hamster" title="Hamster">hamsters</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Castoridae" title="Castoridae">Castoridae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beavers</a>), and the lipotyphlan family <a href="/wiki/Erinaceidae" title="Erinaceidae">Erinaceidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a>). The speciose genus <a href="/wiki/Palaeotherium" title="Palaeotherium">Palaeotherium</a> plus <i><a href="/wiki/Anoplotherium" title="Anoplotherium">Anoplotherium</a></i> and the families Xiphodontidae and Amphimerycidae were observed to disappear completely.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>"Only the <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupial</a> family <a href="/wiki/Herpetotheriidae" title="Herpetotheriidae">Herpetotheriidae</a>, the artiodactyl family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cainotheriidae" title="Cainotheriidae">Cainotheriidae</a>, and the rodent families <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theridomyidae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theridomyidae (page does not exist)">Theridomyidae</a> and Gliridae (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dormice" title="Dormice">dormice</a>) crossed the faunal divide undiminished."</dd>
</dl>
<p>It has been suggested that this was caused by <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> associated with the earliest polar glaciations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and a major fall in sea levels, or by competition with taxa dispersing from Asia. However, few argue for an <a href="/wiki/Oligocene#Climate" title="Oligocene">isolated single cause</a>. Other possible causes are related to the impact of one or more large bolides in northern hemisphere at <a href="/wiki/Popigai_crater" title="Popigai crater">Popigai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toms_Canyon_impact_crater" title="Toms Canyon impact crater">Toms Canyon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay_impact_crater" title="Chesapeake Bay impact crater">Chesapeake Bay</a>. Improved correlation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Europe" title="Northwest Europe">northwest European</a> successions to global events<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooker_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hooker-9">[9]</a></sup> confirms the Grande Coupure as occurring in the earliest Oligocene, with a hiatus of about 350 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennia" title="Millennia">millennia</a> prior to the first record of post-Grande Coupure Asian immigrant taxa.</p>
<p>An element of the paradigm of the Grande Coupure was the apparent <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of all European primates at the Coupure: the recent discovery<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> of a <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mouse</a>-sized early Oligocene <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omomyid" title="Omomyid">omomyid</a>, reflecting the better survival chances of small mammals, further undercut the Grand Coupure <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a>.</p>
<p>Evidence in the world’s ocean current system indicates an abrupt cooling from 34.1 to 33.6 Ma across the Eocene–Oligocene boundary at 33.9 Ma. The remarkable cooling period in the ocean is correlated with pronounced mammalian faunal replacement within continental Asia as well. The Asian biotic reorganization events are comparable to the Grande Coupure in Europe and the Mongolian Remodeling of mammalian communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The global cooling is also correlated with marked drying conditions in low-latitudes Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
