<p>The <b>TORRO tornado intensity scale</b> (or <b>T-Scale</b>) is a scale measuring <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> intensity between T0 and T11. It was proposed by Terence Meaden of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornado_and_Storm_Research_Organisation_(TORRO)" title="Tornado and Storm Research Organisation (TORRO)">Tornado and Storm Research Organisation (TORRO)</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> organisation in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, as an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_derivation_from_Beaufort_scale">History and derivation from Beaufort scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TORRO_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and derivation from Beaufort scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scale was tested from 1972 to 1975 and was made public at a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Meteorological_Society" title="Royal Meteorological Society">Royal Meteorological Society</a> in 1975. The scale sets T0 as the equivalent of 8 on the Beaufort scale and is related to the Beaufort scale (B) by the formula:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>B</i> = 2 (<i>T</i> + 4)</dd>
</dl>
<p>and conversely:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>T</i> = (<i>B</i>/2 - 4)</dd>
</dl>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a> was first introduced in 1805, and in 1921 quantified. It expresses the wind speed (v) by the formula:</p>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">v</a> = 0.837 <i>B</i><sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="TORRO_scale_formula">TORRO scale formula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TORRO_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: TORRO scale formula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most UK tornadoes are T6 or below with the strongest known UK tornado being a T8. For comparison, the strongest detected winds in a United States tornado (during the <a href="/wiki/1999_Oklahoma_tornado_outbreak" title="1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak">1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak</a>) would be T11 using the following formulae:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">v</a></i> = 2.365 (<i>T</i>+4)<sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a></dd>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">v</a></i> = 8.511 (<i>T</i>+4)<sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a></dd>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">v</a></i> = 5.289 (<i>T</i>+4)<sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">mph</a></dd>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">v</a></i> = 4.596 (<i>T</i>+4)<sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a></dd>
</dl>
<p>where <i>v</i> is <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> and <i>T</i> is TORRO intensity number. Wind speed is defined as a 3-second gust at 10 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_ground_level" title="Above ground level">AGL</a>.</p>
<p>Alternatively, the T-Scale formula may be expressed as:</p>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">v</a> = 0.837 (2<i>T</i>+8)<sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a></dd>
</dl>
<p>or</p>
<dl>

<dd>or</dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rating_process_and_comparisons_to_Fujita_scale">Rating process and comparisons to Fujita scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TORRO_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rating process and comparisons to Fujita scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>TORRO claims it differs from the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">Fujita scale</a> in that it is "purely" a wind speed scale, whereas the Fujita scale relies on damage for classification, but in practice, damage is utilised almost exclusively in both systems to infer intensity. That is because such a proxy for intensity is usually all that is available, although users of both scales would prefer direct, objective, quantitative measurements. The scale is primarily used in the United Kingdom whereas the Fujita scale is the primary scale used in North America, continental Europe, and the rest of the world.</p>
<p>At the 2004 European Conference on Severe Storms, Dr. Meaden proposed a unification of the TORRO and Fujita scales as the Tornado Force or TF Scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2007 in the United States, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_Scale" title="Enhanced Fujita Scale">Enhanced Fujita Scale</a> replaced the original Fujita Scale from 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It made substantial improvements in standardizing damage descriptors through expanding and refining damage indicators and associated degrees of damage, as well as calibrated tornado wind speeds to better match the associated damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the EF Scale is biased to US construction practices,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. As of 2014, only the United States and Canada have adopted the EF scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike with the F scale, no analyses have been undertaken at all to establish the veracity and accuracy of the T scale damage descriptors. The scale was written in the early 1970s, and does not take into account changes such as the growth in weight of vehicles or the great reduction in numbers and change of type of railway locomotives,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and was written in an environment where tornadoes of F2 or stronger are extremely rare, so little or no first-hand investigation of actual damage at the upper end of the scale was possible. The TORRO scale has more graduations than the F scale which makes it arguably more useful for tornadoes on the lower end of the scale<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>; however, such accuracy and precision are not typically attainable in practice. <a href="/wiki/Harold_E._Brooks" title="Harold E. Brooks">Brooks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Doswell_III" title="Charles A. Doswell III">Doswell</a> stated that "the problems associated with damage surveys and uncertainties associated with estimating wind speed from observed damage make highly precise assignments dubious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In survey reports, Fujita ratings sometimes also have extra qualifications added ("minimal F2" or "upper-end F3 damage"), made by investigators who have experience of many similar tornadoes and relating to the fact that the F scale is a damage scale, not a wind speed scale.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Tornadoes are rated after they have passed and have been examined, not whilst in progress. In rating the intensity of a tornado, both direct measurements and inferences from empirical observations of the effects of a tornado are used. Few <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometers</a> are struck by a tornado, and even fewer survive, so there are very few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ" title="In-situ">in-situ</a> measurements. Therefore, almost all ratings are obtained from <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> techniques or as <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxies</a> from damage surveys. <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Weather radar</a> is used when available, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Photogrammetry" title="Photogrammetry">photogrammetry</a> or <a href="/wiki/Videogrammetry" title="Videogrammetry">videogrammetry</a> estimates wind speed by measuring tracers in the vortex. In most cases, aerial and ground damage surveys of structures and vegetation are utilised, sometimes with engineering analysis. Also sometimes available are ground swirl patterns (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cycloidal" title="Cycloidal">cycloidal</a> marks) left in the wake of a tornado. If an on site analysis is not possible, either for retrospective ratings or when personnel cannot reach a site, photographs, videos, or descriptions of damage may be utilised.</p>
