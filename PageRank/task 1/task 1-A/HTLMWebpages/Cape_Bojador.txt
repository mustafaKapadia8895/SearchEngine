<p><b>Cape Bojador</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">رأس بوجادور</span>‎‎, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_transliteration" title="Arabic transliteration">trans.</a> <i>Rā's Būjādūr</i>; <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>: <span lang="ber" xml:lang="ber">ⴱⵓⵊⴷⵓⵔ</span>, <i>Bujdur</i>; <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>Cabo Bojador</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Cap Boujdour</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">headland</a> on the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>, at 26° 07' 37"N, 14° 29' 57"W (various sources give various locations: this is from the <i>Sailing Directions</i> for the region), as well as the name of the large nearby town with a population of 41,178.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Population-1">[1]</a></sup> The name of the surrounding province also derives its name from the cape (Bojador Province).</p>
<p>It is shown on nautical charts with the original Portuguese name "Cabo Bojador", but is sometimes spelled "Cape Boujdour" in some media and academic research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is said that it is also known as the "Bulging Cape", although no references to this usage are to be found in standard geographical references. The Cape's name in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> is "Abu Khatar", meaning "the father of danger".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cape is not prominent on maps but may be located by looking 220 km (120 nautical miles) due south of the south-western point of the hook of <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_significance">Historical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Bojador&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The discovery of a passable route around Cape Bojador, in 1434, by the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> mariner <a href="/wiki/Gil_Eanes" title="Gil Eanes">Gil Eanes</a> was considered a major breakthrough for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">European</a> explorers and traders en route to Africa and later to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Eanes had made a previous attempt in 1433 which resulted in failure, but tried again under orders of Prince Henry the Navigator, who first sent him in 1424. He was successful after the second expedition. The disappearance of numerous European vessels that had made prior attempts to round the Cape despite its violent seas, led some to suggest the presence of sea monsters. The mythic importance of the cape for Portugal was captured in <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a>'s early 20th century work "Mensagem". In famous stanzas from this longer poem Pessoa wrote of the enormous costs of the Portuguese explorations to the nation. Capturing the symbolic importance to the nation of rounding Cape Bojador, Pessoa wrote: "Who wants to pass beyond Bojador / Must also pass beyond pain." (<i>"Quem quer passar além do Bojador / Tem que passar além da dor."</i>) They thought the ocean was burning past Cape Bojador, but Henry's men went past it.</p>
<p>The reason for the fearsome reputation of the cape is not immediately obvious from maps, where it appears as the south-western point of a slight hump in the coastline, bounded at its other end by Cabo Falso Bojador, ten nautical miles to the northeast. Nor does what is said in the <i>Sailing Directions</i> sound terribly formidable: "Cabo Falso Bojador is formed by several tall sand dunes ... A rocky shoal, with a least depth of 4.8m, extends up to 3 miles N of the cape. A rocky patch, with a least depth of 8m, lies about 2 miles W of the cape. The coast between Cabo Falso Bojador and Cabo Bojador, 10 miles SW, consists of a sandy beach fringed by rocks. Clumps of scrub top the sand dunes which stand about 0.5 mile inland of this beach. Heavy breakers have been observed along this coast at all times. Cabo Bojador, a very low point, is located 9.5 miles SW of Cabo Falso Bojador and is bordered on the S side by black rocks. From the N, the cape appears as a mass of red sand with a gradual slope towards the sea. From the W, the cape is difficult to identify, but from the S its extremity appears as a reef which dries in places and is marked by breakers even in calm weather."</p>
<p>Examining the <i>Pilot Charts</i> for this area, however, it becomes clear that the main concern lies in the changes in winds that occur at about the point at which Cape Bojador is passed in sailing down the coast. It is here that the winds start to blow strongly from the northeast at all seasons. Together with the half-knot set of current down the coast, these conditions would naturally alarm an ignorant and superstitious Medieval mariner used to sailing close to the land and having no knowledge of what lay ahead. In the end it was discovered that by sailing well out to sea—far out of sight of land—a more favorable wind could be picked up.</p>
<p>In addition, this is also believed to be the site where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_James_Riley" title="Captain James Riley">Captain James Riley</a> and the crew of the U.S. brig <a href="/wiki/Commerce_(ship)" title="Commerce (ship)"><i>Commerce</i></a>, sailing at the time from Gibraltar towards the Cape Verde Islands, shipwrecked in August 1815. This tragedy is recounted in the <i><a href="/wiki/Skeletons_on_the_Zahara" title="Skeletons on the Zahara">Skeletons on the Zahara</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Dean_King" title="Dean King">Dean King</a>, which is set in this region of the African coast. Dean reports that any coastal map of Western Sahara is inaccurate because of the ever-changing physical features, due to the harsh conditions of the Sahara. It also mentions that the depth of the water surrounding Cape Bojador is deceptively shallow, and the color of the sand underneath the water is a "fearful sight". The cape had a fearsome reputation among mariners even prior to the wreck of the <i>Commerce</i>, as there had been at least thirty known shipwrecks between 1790 and 1806.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sailors' fears were founded in what they saw, and the phenomena witnessed by the sailors of those days can be seen today. Any ship that has to pass those places makes sure to give a wide berth in order to avoid accidents. Cape Bojador and its surrounding coast extends into the sea in the form of an underwater reef, and, when the waves break after crashing into unseen gullies, the water spouts furiously into high foamy clouds that look like steam, even on calm days. The sea next to the Cape, and for approximately 3 miles seaward from the coast, is no more than two metres deep. Fish are abundant in the area, and shoals of sardines rise to the surface during the feeding times of larger fish. When this happens, the sea seems to bubble violently as if boiling, and, observed from a distance, the hissing sound produced by the fish flicking their tails on the water's surface adds to the impression. The stifling air wafted westerly on lazy breezes from the desert heightens the impression of extreme temperature, while the desert dust helps to create a mysterious darkness. Worse, the ferrous rocks make compass needles whirl erratically. As recently as 2004, the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s publication <i>Africa Pilot</i> warns that nautical charts of the coastline in the area of Cape Bojador are "reported to be inaccurate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_significance">Ecological significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Bojador&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecological significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Spanish interest in the desert coast of Western Africa was the result of fishing activities carried out from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> by Spanish fishermen.</p>
<p>Spanish fishers were seal fur traders and hunters, fishers and whalers off the Sahara coast with several enclaves in Cabo Bojador, <a href="/wiki/Dakhla,_Western_Sahara" title="Dakhla, Western Sahara">Dakhla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ras_Nouadhibou" title="Ras Nouadhibou">Ras Nouadhibou</a> from 1500 to present, extending from the west coast of Africa to hunting <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">humpback whales</a> and whale calves, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, and the Gulf of Guinea in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annobon" title="Annobon">Annobon</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">São Tomé and Príncipe</a> islands just to 1940. These fishing activities have had a negative impact on wildlife causing the disappearance or endangerment of many species, particularly marine mammals and birds. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The former range of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_monk_seal" title="Mediterranean monk seal">Mediterranean monk seal</a> (<i>Monachus monachus</i>) extended throughout the Northwest Atlantic coast of Africa and the Mediterranean and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coastlines, including all offshore islands of the Mediterranean, and into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and its islands: Canary Islands, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilhas_Desertas" title="Ilhas Desertas">Ilhas Desertas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porto_Santo" title="Porto Santo">Porto Santo</a>, and others as far west as the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">Vagrants</a> could be found as far south as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia">Gambia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands, and as far north as continental Portugal and Atlantic France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_modern_times">In modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Bojador&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Spanish originally claimed the land from 20° 51' N (near Cap Blanc) to 26° 8' N (near Cape Bojador) in 1885. This would be a protectorate governed from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> in 1887. <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> would later claim the Western Sahara. The boundary was settled in a joint French-Spanish convention in 1900 to divide the area between <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sahara" title="Spanish Sahara">Spanish Sahara</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_West_Africa" title="French West Africa">French West Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> claimed a protectorate over the coastal region from Cap Blanc, far to the south of Cape Bojador, to a point about 200 km to the north in 1884. In 1975, as Spain pulled out following the Madrid Accords, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> sought to gain control over the area, leading to disputes between Morocco and the <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">Polisario Front</a>, the organization which proclaimed the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a> in February 1976. In January 2016 it was announced that the Canary Association of Victims of Terrorism (ACAVITE) intended to sue the Polisario Front for committing "crimes against humanity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Tindouf region of Algeria, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daira_de_Bojador" title="Daira de Bojador">Daira de Bojador</a> is a refugee camp for Sahrawis named after Cape Bojador.</p>
<p>In December 2015, the bodies of 11 drowned migrants were found 147 kilometers off Cape Bojador. Earlier that same month, the Spanish coastguard rescued 47 African migrants in a boat of the coast of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
