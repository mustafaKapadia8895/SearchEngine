<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ertebølle culture</h1>

<p>The <b>Ertebølle culture</b> (ca 5300 BC – 3950 BC) (<small>Danish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Danish" title="Help:IPA/Danish">[ˈæɐ̯dəˌbølə]</a></span>) is the name of a <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> and fisher, pottery-making <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_culture" title="Archaeological culture">culture</a> dating to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> period. The culture was concentrated in Southern Scandinavia, but genetically linked to strongly related cultures in Northern Germany and the Northern Netherlands. It is named after the type site, a location in the small village of Ertebølle on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limfjorden" title="Limfjorden">Limfjorden</a> in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>. In the 1890s, the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Denmark" title="National Museum of Denmark">National Museum of Denmark</a> excavated heaps of <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> shells there, mixed with <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, bones and bone, antler and flint artifacts, which were evaluated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitchen_midden" title="Kitchen midden">kitchen middens</a> (Danish <i>køkkenmødding</i>), or refuse dumps. Accordingly, the culture is less commonly named the <b>Kitchen Midden</b>. As it is approximately identical to the Ellerbek culture of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>, the combined name, <b>Ertebølle-Ellerbek</b> is often used. The Ellerbek culture (German <i>Ellerbek Kultur</i>) is named after a type site in Ellerbek, a community on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Kiel" title="Kiel">Kiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1960s and 1970s another closely related culture was found in the (now dry) <a href="/wiki/Noordoostpolder" title="Noordoostpolder">Noordoostpolder</a> in the Netherlands, near the village Swifterbant and the former island of <a href="/wiki/Urk" title="Urk">Urk</a>. Named the <a href="/wiki/Swifterbant_culture" title="Swifterbant culture">Swifterbant culture</a> (5300 – 3400 BC) they show a transition from hunter-gatherer to both animal husbandry, primarily cows and pigs, and cultivation of barley and emmer wheat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> During the formative stages contact with nearby <a href="/wiki/Linear_Pottery_culture" title="Linear Pottery culture">Linear Pottery culture</a> settlements in <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Netherlands)" title="Limburg (Netherlands)">Limburg</a> has been detected. Like the Ertebølle culture, they lived near open water, in this case creeks, riverdunes and bogs along post-glacial banks of the Overijsselse <a href="/wiki/Vechte" title="Vechte">Vecht</a>. Recent excavations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kooijmans_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kooijmans-2">[2]</a></sup> show a local continuity going back to (at least) 5600 BC, when burial practices resembled the contemporary gravefields in Denmark and South Sweden "in all details", suggesting only part of a diverse ancestral "Ertebølle"-like heritage was locally continued into the later (Middle Neolithic) Swifterbant tradition (4200 – 3400 BC).</p>
<p>The Ertebølle culture was roughly contemporaneous with the <a href="/wiki/Linear_Pottery_culture" title="Linear Pottery culture">Linear Pottery culture</a>, food-producers whose northernmost border was located just to the south. The Ertebølle did not practice agriculture but it did utilize domestic grain in some capacity, which it must have obtained from the south.</p>
<p>The Ertebølle culture replaced the earlier <a href="/wiki/Kongemose_culture" title="Kongemose culture">Kongemose culture</a> of Denmark. It was limited to the north by the Scandinavian <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B8stvet_and_Lihult_cultures" title="Nøstvet and Lihult cultures">Nøstvet and Lihult cultures</a>. It is divided into an early phase ca 5300 BC-ca 4500 BC, and a later phase ca 4500 BC-3950 BC. Shortly after 4100 BC the Ertebølle began to expand along the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> coast at least as far as <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCgen" title="Rügen">Rügen</a>. Shortly thereafter it was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Funnelbeaker_culture" title="Funnelbeaker culture">Funnelbeaker culture</a>.</p>
<p>In recent years archaeologists have found the acronym <b>EBK</b> most convenient, parallel to LBK for German Linearbandkeramik (<a href="/wiki/Linear_Pottery_culture" title="Linear Pottery culture">Linear Pottery culture</a>) and TRB for German Trichterbecher, Danish Tragtbæger (<a href="/wiki/Funnelbeaker_culture" title="Funnelbeaker culture">Funnelbeaker culture</a>) and Dutch trechterbekercultuur. Ostensibly for Ertebølle Kultur, EBK could be either German or Danish and has the added advantage that Ellerbek also begins with E.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ertebølle culture falls within the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(period)" title="Atlantic (period)">Atlantic</a> climate period and the <a href="/wiki/Littorina_Sea" title="Littorina Sea">Littorina Sea</a> phase of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> basin; that is, climate was warmer and moister than today, deciduous forests covered Europe, and the Baltic was at higher levels than today, and was a salt sea, rather than a brackish one or a lake. The Baltic coastline was often flooded to a level of 5m-6m higher than now. <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>. Marshes were extensive, with tracts of shallow water rich in fish. The environment itself thus invited settlement.</p>
<p>The Ertebølle population settled on promontories, near or on beaches, on islands and along rivers and estuaries away from the dense forests. The environment most like the then range of the Ertebølle is the <a href="/wiki/Wadden_Sea" title="Wadden Sea">Wadden Sea</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> to <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. Due to chance fluctuations in the sea level during Ertebølle occupation of the coast and subsequently, many of the culture sites are currently under 3m-4m of water. Some have been excavated by underwater archaeology. The artifacts are in an excellent state of preservation, having been protected by anaerobic mud. On the disadvantage side, water movements have disrupted many sites.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_remains">Cultural remains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural remains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ertebølle population derived its living from a variety of means, but chiefly from the sea. They prospered, grew healthy and multiplied on a diet of fish. They were masters of the inland waters, which they traversed in paddled dugouts. Like many peoples known in history, they were able to hunt whales and seals from their dugouts. Their materials were mainly wood, with bone, antler and flint for functions requiring harder surfaces. Homes were constructed of brush or light wood. The materials encourage us to view them as transitory. They were, nevertheless, able to place the dead in longer-used cemeteries. Perhaps the dwelling-places were transitory, but the territories were not.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_anthropology">Physical anthropology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical anthropology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Skeletal remains are relatively meager. They have been studied and described in great detail from an anthropometric, or "man-measuring", point of view. Without resorting to this specialized language, the main conclusions are as follows. The Ertebølle and preceding Kongemose populations were of mixed race. On the one hand they did not differ from the current inhabitants of Denmark in skeleton. Soft tissue features, being known through reconstruction only, leave some space for variation.</p>
<p>On the other hand, many skulls evidence facial features or dimensions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cro-magnon" title="Cro-magnon">Cro-magnon</a> man. The latter type prevailed in Late <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> times in Europe, supplanting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neanderthal_man" title="Neanderthal man">Neanderthal man</a> there. The Cro-magnon skull is dolichocephalic (long), the jaw prognathous (protruding), the nose flat and the supraorbital ridges (brow ridges) pronounced. Genetic analysis by scientists from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ferrara" title="University of Ferrara">University of Ferrara</a> (Italy) indicates that the Cro-magnons were ancestral to the current population of Europe. There is no evidence of superficial features such as coloration or details of facial tissue, and nothing to suggest that the Cro-magnons of Europe were the only ancestors of Europeans or that features considered Europoid were not found elsewhere.</p>
<p>Two hypotheses concerning the origin of the Ertebølle population are therefore possible and have been proposed. One is that in the remains we are seeing an intermediate phase in the evolution of the Nordics. The second is that the Ertebølle population were an admixture of agrarian southerners with indigenous Cro-magnons over a permeable border. Both views are supported by the evidence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_of_conflict">Evidence of conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evidence of conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some evidence of conflict between Ertebølle settlements: an arrowhead in a pelvis at Skateholm, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>; a bone point in a throat at <a href="/wiki/Vedb%C3%A6k" title="Vedbæk">Vedbæk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zealand_(Denmark)" title="Zealand (Denmark)">Zealand</a>; a bone point in the chest at Stora Biers, Sweden. More significant is evidence of cannibalism at Dyrholmen, <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>, and Møllegabet on <a href="/wiki/%C3%86r%C3%B8" title="Ærø">Ærø</a>. There human bones were broken open to obtain the marrow. As <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism" title="Cannibalism">cannibalism</a> is not practiced to obtain food, the next most likely explanation is that the warlike Ertebølle population ritually devoured its enemies in order to ingest their powers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_cultures">Similar cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Similar cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ertebølle culture is of a general type called Late Mesolithic, of which other examples can be found in <a href="/wiki/Swifterbant_culture" title="Swifterbant culture">Swifterbant culture</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zedmar_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zedmar culture (page does not exist)">Zedmar culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narva_culture" title="Narva culture">Narva culture</a> and in Russia. Some would include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%B8stvet_culture" title="Nøstvet culture">Nøstvet culture</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lihult_culture" title="Lihult culture">Lihult culture</a> to the north as well. The various locations seem fragmented and isolated, but that characteristic may be an accident of discovery. Perhaps if all the submarine sites were known, a continuous coastal culture would appear from the Netherlands to the lakes of Russia, but it has yet to be demonstrated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ertebølle peoples lived primarily on seafood.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing_industry">Fishing industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fishing industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mainstay of Ertebølle economy was fish. Three main methods of fishing are supported by the evidence, such as the boats and other equipment found in fragmentary form at Tybrind Vig and elsewhere: trapping, angling and spearing.</p>
<p>To trap fish, the fishermen constructed fish fences, or weirs, of approximately 4m-long <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazel</a> sticks set upright in the mud at the bottom of shallow water. The fish must have been corralled by some method and then harvested at will. Wickerwork traps were also used.</p>
<p>Ertebølle fishermen angled with hooks made of red deer bone, of which at least one example has been found with line attached. They spear-fished with spears made of shafts to which hazel tines were attached. Boats were dugouts a few feet wide propelled by paddles constructed of shafts to which leaf-shaped or heart-shaped blades were attached. At one end a layer of clay spread on the bottom supported hot coals, an indispensable source of heat if you were going to spend much time in the boat.</p>
<p>Dozens of species of fish have been found in the middens. Some of the most common are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pike_(fish)" title="Pike (fish)">pike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_whitefish" title="Freshwater whitefish">whitefish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_ling" title="Common ling">ling</a> at Østenkaer, anchovy (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engraulis_encrasicolus" title="Engraulis encrasicolus">Engraulis encrasicolus</a></i>), three-spined stickleback (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasterosteus_aculeatus" title="Gasterosteus aculeatus">Gasterosteus aculeatus</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/European_eel" title="European eel">eel</a> at Krabbesholm. The oldest site, Yderhede, featured remains of <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a> and sharks: <a href="/wiki/Porbeagle" title="Porbeagle">porbeagle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topeshark" title="Topeshark">topeshark</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smoothhound" title="Smoothhound">smoothhound</a> and at Lystrup Enge <a href="/wiki/Spurdog" title="Spurdog">spurdog</a>. At Egsminde <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyprinid" title="Cyprinid">cyprinids</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_perch" title="European perch">European perch</a> were found. The presence of deep-sea fish and sharks probably indicates the Ertebølle fishermen often ventured out on deep water. Whether they did so in their marshland dugouts or also owned larger, ocean-going ones is an answer that waits for more evidence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Whaling_and_sealing_industry">Whaling and sealing industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Whaling and sealing industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Eg%C3%A5_Engs%C3%B8#Archaeological_finds" title="Egå Engsø">Lystrup Enge</a>, Yderhede and other places the bones of <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">cetaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">pinnipeds</a> have been found; specifically, of <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">killer whales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White-beaked_dolphin" title="White-beaked dolphin">white-beaked dolphin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin" title="Bottlenose dolphin">bottlenose dolphin</a> among the Cetaceans. These are not animals requiring whaling voyages on the high seas. They could have been washed onto the shore or hunted in shallow waters.</p>
<p>The seals are the <a href="/wiki/Ringed_seal" title="Ringed seal">ringed seal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Harp_seal" title="Harp seal">harp seal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grey_seal" title="Grey seal">grey seal</a>. These animals for the most part were common in the <a href="/wiki/Littorina_Sea" title="Littorina Sea">Littorina Sea</a> but are not found in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> now. Again, they could have been taken on land or in the shallows.</p>
<p>The species found raise the question of whether a whaling or sealing industry existed as such or whether the bones came from opportunistic scavenging. There is no direct evidence of voyaging out in dugouts to harpoon whales that could kill the voyagers in an instant. However, one of the two main types of pottery used was the blubber lamp, a small, oval deep dish in which you ignited a chunk of blubber or even oil with a wick. The widespread use of this lamp implies a widespread industry to obtain blubber; i.e., professional whale and seal hunting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunting_industry">Hunting industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hunting industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Judging from the remains of animal bones at their sites, the Ertebølle people hunted mainly three types of land animals: large forest browsers, fur animals and maritime birds.</p>
<p>The forest mammals are the <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roe_deer" title="Roe deer">roe deer</a>, which were dietary staples, and the <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a>, less frequently the <a href="/wiki/Aurochs" title="Aurochs">aurochs</a>, and a rare <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a>, believed to have been wild. Only a left foreleg from Østenkær remains. It offers definitive proof that horses lived in the forests of Europe. On the plains to the east they are only found in association with man. The boar were supplemented by swine with mixed European and Near Eastern ancestry, obtained through their Neolithic farming neighbors, as early as 4600 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fur animals are fairly widespread: the <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beaver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_polecat" title="European polecat">polecat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">fox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynx</a>. Furs might have served as a currency and may have been traded to some degree, but this is speculation.</p>
<p>Maritime birds must have been easily taken in the marshes and ponds of the region: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-throated_diver" title="Red-throated diver">red-throated diver</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-throated_diver" title="Black-throated diver">black-throated diver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalmatian_pelican" title="Dalmatian pelican">Dalmatian pelican</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capercaille" title="Capercaille">capercaille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grebe" title="Grebe">grebe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a>.</p>
<p>In addition are a few others: the <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a>, and two snakes, the common <a href="/wiki/Grass_snake" title="Grass snake">grass snake</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aesculapian_snake" title="Aesculapian snake">Aesculapian snake</a>. As snakes do not appear in the art, it is impossible to say what cultural impact they had, if any.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_use">Plant use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Plant use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The EBK gathered berries for consumption and also prepared a number of wild plants, judging from the seed remains of plants that could not be consumed without preparation. Of the berries that have been found are <a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">raspberry</a> (<i>Rubus idaeus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Dewberry" title="Dewberry">dewberry</a> (<i>Rubus caesius</i>), wild <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberry</a>, and the somewhat less palatable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogwood" title="Dogwood">dogwood</a> (<i>Cornus sanguinea</i>), hawthorn (<i><a href="/wiki/Crataegus_monogyna" title="Crataegus monogyna">Crataegus monogyna</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Crataegus_oxyacantha" title="Crataegus oxyacantha">C. oxyacantha</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rowanberry" title="Rowanberry">rowanberry</a> (<i>Sorbus aucuparia</i>), crab <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">rose</a> hips.</p>
<p>Some seeds usually made into gruel in historical times are acorn and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manna_grass" title="Manna grass">manna grass</a> (<i>Glyceria fluitans</i>). Roots of the <a href="/wiki/Sea_beet" title="Sea beet">sea beet</a>, Beta maritima, were prepared as well. That species is ancestral to modern domestic beets. Greens could have been boiled from nettle (<i><a href="/wiki/Urtica_dioica" title="Urtica dioica">Urtica dioica</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orache" title="Orache">orache</a> (<i>Atriplex</i>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goosefoot" title="Goosefoot">goosefoot</a> (<i>Chenopodium album</i>).</p>
<p>Some of the pottery evidences grain impressions, which some interpret as the use of food imported from the south. Certainly, they did not need to import food and were probably better nourished than the southerners. Analysis of charred remains in one pot indicates that it at least was used for fermenting a mixture of blood and nuts. Some have therefore guessed that fermentation of grain was used to produce beer.</p>
<p>Finally, fragments of textiles from Tybrind Vig were woven in the needle-netting technique from spun plant fibers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_and_art">Tools and art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tools and art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement_life">Settlement life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Settlement life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The many settlements on the coast and in the hinterland vary between large all-year-round settlements and smaller seasonal settlements. A settlement consisted of huts, probably brush supported by posts. The huts were in no special order. Fire pits located outside the huts indicate that most village functions were performed outdoors, with the dwellings used perhaps for storage and sleeping. At the time winters were mild.</p>
<p>An external fireplace from Ronaes Skae was constructed as a perimeter of stones surrounding a mud and clay hearth on which charred wood was found in a spoke pattern. The wood was collected from the shore. Fungus was used for tinder.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pottery">Pottery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pottery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2192" data-file-width="2120" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG/220px-Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG/330px-Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG/440px-Arch%C3%A4ologisches_Landesmuseum_Schloss_Gottorf_023.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Pottery</div>

<p>Pottery was manufactured from native clays tempered with sand, crushed stone and organic material. The EBK pot was made by coil technique, being fired on the open bed of hot coals. It was not like the neighbouring Neolithic Linearbandkeramik and appears related instead to a pottery type that first appears in Europe in the Samara region of Russia c. 7000 cal BC, and spread up the Volga to the Eastern Baltic and then westward along the shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two main types are found, a beaker and a lamp. The beaker is a pot-bellied pot narrowing at the neck, with a flanged, outward turning rim. The bottom was typically formed into a point or bulb (the "funnel") of some sort that supported the pot when it was placed in clay or sand. One can imagine a sort of mobile pantry consisting of rows of jars set now in the hut, now by the fire, now in the clay layer at the bottom of a dugout.</p>
<p>The beaker came in various sizes from 8 to 50 cm high and from 5 to 20 cm in diameter. Decoration filled the entire surface with horizontal bands of fingertip or fingernail impressions. It must have been in the decoration phase that grains of wheat and barley left their impression in the clay. Late in the period technique and decoration became slightly more varied and sophisticated: the walls were thinner and different motifs were used in the impressions: chevrons, cord marks, and punctures made with animal bones. Handles are sometimes added and the rims may turn in instead of out.</p>
<p>The blubber lamp was molded from a single piece of clay. The use of such lamps suggests some household activity in the huts after dark.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tool_kit">Tool kit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tool kit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flintstone</a> industry evolved a high and unified standard with small and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flake_axe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flake axe (page does not exist)">flake axes</a>, long <a href="/wiki/Lithic_flake" title="Lithic flake">lithic flakes</a> (knives) and arrow heads. However, tools of many materials were in use: wood prongs and points, antler parts, carved bone tools.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Paddles from Tybrind Vig show traces of highly developed and artistic woodcarving. This is an example of the embellishment of functional pieces. The population also polished and engraved non-functional or not obviously functional pieces of bone or antler. Motifs were predominantly geometric with some anthropomorphic or zoomorphic forms. Also in evidence (for example, at Fanø) are polished amber representations of animals, such as birds, boars, and bears. Jewelry was made of animal teeth or decorative shells. To what extent any of these pieces were symbolic of wealth and status is not clear.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funerary_customs">Funerary customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erteb%C3%B8lle_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Funerary customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cemeteries, such as the ones at <a href="/wiki/Vedb%C3%A6k" title="Vedbæk">Vedbæk</a> and Skateholm, give a "sedentary" character to the settlements. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_ochre" title="Red ochre">Red ochre</a> and deer antlers were placed in some graves, but not others. Some social distinctions may therefore have been made. There was some appreciation of sexual dimorphism: the women wore necklaces and belts of animal teeth and shells. No special body position was used. Both burial and cremation were practiced. At Møllegabet, an individual was buried in a dugout, which some see as the beginning of Scandinavian boat burials.</p>
<p>Skateholm contained also a dog cemetery. Dog graves were prepared and gifted the same as human, with ochre, antler, and grave goods. In either history or prehistory the dog is an invaluable animal and is often treated as a person.</p>
