<p>The <b>Carpathian Mountains</b> or <b>Carpathians</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/iː/ or /ɪ/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>) are a <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a> system forming an arc roughly 1,500 km (932 mi) long across <a href="/wiki/Central_and_Eastern_Europe" title="Central and Eastern Europe">Central and Eastern Europe</a>, making them the second-longest mountain range in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (after the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Mountains" title="Scandinavian Mountains">Scandinavian Mountains</a>, 1,700 km (1,056 mi)).</p>
<p>They provide the habitat for the largest European populations of <a href="/wiki/Brown_bear" title="Brown bear">brown bears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_wolf" title="Eurasian wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">chamois</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a>, with the highest concentration in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> as well as over one third of all European plant species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Carpathians and their <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">foothills</a> also have many <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">thermal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral waters</a>, with Romania having one-third of the European total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Romania is likewise home to the second-largest surface of <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">virgin forests</a> in Europe after Russia, totaling 250,000 hectares (65%), most of them in the Carpathians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Carpathians" title="Southern Carpathians">Southern Carpathians</a> constituting Europe's largest unfragmented forested area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Carpathians consist of a chain of mountain ranges that stretch in an arc from the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> (3%) in the northwest through <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> (17%), <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> (10%), <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> (4%) and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> (11%), and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> (51%). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-visiteurope.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-visiteurope.com-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carpathianconvention.org_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-carpathianconvention.org-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhmbeo.rs_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhmbeo.rs-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.rs_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.rs-12">[12]</a></sup> The highest range within the Carpathians is the <a href="/wiki/Tatra_Mountains" title="Tatra Mountains">Tatras</a>, on the border of Slovakia and Poland, where the highest peaks exceed 2,600 m (8,530 ft). The second-highest range is the Southern Carpathians in Romania, where the highest peaks exceed 2,500 m (8,202 ft).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Carpathians" title="Divisions of the Carpathians">divisions of the Carpathians</a> are usually in three major sections:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carpathians.pl_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-carpathians.pl-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Western_Carpathians" title="Western Carpathians">Western Carpathians</a> — Austria, Czech Republic, Poland, and Slovakia</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Carpathians" title="Eastern Carpathians">Eastern Carpathians</a> — southeastern Poland, eastern Slovakia, Ukraine, and Romania</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Southern_Carpathians" title="Southern Carpathians">Southern Carpathians</a> — Romania and Serbia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-visiteurope.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-visiteurope.com-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carpathianconvention.org_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-carpathianconvention.org-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.rs_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.rs-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_Carpathians" title="Outer Carpathians">Outer Carpathians</a> is frequently used to describe the northern rim of the Western and Eastern Carpathians.</p>
<p>The most important cities in or near the Carpathians are: <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%A1ice" title="Košice">Košice</a> in Slovakia, <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a> in Poland, <a href="/wiki/Cluj-Napoca" title="Cluj-Napoca">Cluj-Napoca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sibiu" title="Sibiu">Sibiu</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bra%C5%9Fov" title="Braşov">Braşov</a> in Romania, and <a href="/wiki/Uzhhorod" title="Uzhhorod">Uzhhorod</a> in Ukraine.</p>

<p>In modern times, the range is called <i>Karpaty</i> in <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a> and <i>Карпати (Karpaty)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>, <i>Carpați</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Romanian" title="Help:IPA/Romanian">[karˈpat͡sʲ]</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Ro-Carpa%C8%9Bi.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlazovich1994332-15">[15]</a></sup> Although the toponym was recorded already by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> in the second century CE, <sup <i>Havasok</i> ("Snowy Mountains") was its medieval <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> name; Russian chronicles referred to it as "Hungarian Mountains". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlazovich1994332_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlazovich1994332-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoldovanu201018_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoldovanu201018-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The name "Carpates" is highly associated with the old Dacian tribes called "Carpes" or "Carpi" who lived in a large area from the east, north-east of the Black Sea to Transylvanian plains on the present day Romania and Moldova. The name <i>Carpates</i> may ultimately be from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto Indo-European</a> root <i>*sker-</i>/<i>*ker-</i>, from which comes the <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a> word <i>karpë</i> (rock), and the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> word <i>skála</i> (rock, cliff), perhaps via a <a href="/wiki/Dacian_language" title="Dacian language">Dacian</a> cognate<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2011)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> which meant mountain, rock, or rugged (cf. Germanic root <i>*skerp-</i>, Old Norse <i>harfr</i> "harrow", Gothic <i>skarpo</i>, Middle Low German <i>scharf</i> "potsherd", and Modern High German <i>Scherbe</i> "shard", Old English <i>scearp</i> and English <i>sharp</i>, Lithuanian <i>kar~pas</i> "cut, hack, notch", Latvian <i>cìrpt</i> "to shear, clip"). The archaic Polish word <i>karpa</i> meant "rugged irregularities, underwater obstacles/rocks, rugged roots, or trunks". The more common word <i>skarpa</i> means a sharp cliff or other vertical terrain. The name may instead come from Indo-European *<i>kwerp</i> "to turn", akin to Old English <i>hweorfan</i> "to turn, change" (English warp) and Greek <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">καρπός</span> <i>karpós</i> "wrist", perhaps referring to the way the mountain range bends or veers in an L-shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> documents, the Eastern Carpathian Mountains were referred to as <i>Montes Sarmatici</i> (meaning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatian" title="Sarmatian">Sarmatian</a> Mountains).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Western Carpathians were called <i>Carpates</i>, a name that is first recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographia" title="Geographia">Geographia</a></i> (second century AD).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the Scandinavian <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hervarar_saga" title="Hervarar saga">Hervarar saga</a></i>, which relates ancient Germanic legends about <a href="/wiki/Hl%C3%B6%C3%B0skvi%C3%B0a" title="Hlöðskviða">battles</a> between <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>, the name <i>Karpates</i> appears in the predictable Germanic form as <i>Harvaða fjöllum</i> (see <a href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_law" title="Grimm's law">Grimm's law</a>).</p>
<p>"<i>Inter Alpes Huniae et Oceanum est Polonia</i>" by <a href="/wiki/Gervase_of_Tilbury" title="Gervase of Tilbury">Gervase of Tilbury</a>, has described in his <i>Otia Imperialia</i> ("Recreation for an Emperor") in 1211.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNB_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-19">[19]</a></sup> Thirteenth- to fifteenth-century Hungarian documents named the mountains <i>Thorchal</i>, <i>Tarczal</i>, or less frequently <i>Montes Nivium</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNB_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>The northwestern Carpathians begin in Slovakia and southern Poland. They surround <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">Transcarpathia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> in a large semicircle, sweeping towards the southeast, and end on the Danube near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Or%C5%9Fova" title="Orşova">Orşova</a> in Romania. The total length of the Carpathians is over 1,500 km (932 mi) and the mountain chain's width varies between 12 and 500 km (7 and 311 mi). The highest altitudes of the Carpathians occur where they are widest. The system attains its greatest breadth in the Transylvanian plateau and in the southern <a href="/wiki/Tatra_Mountains" title="Tatra Mountains">Tatra Mountains</a> group – the highest range, in which <a href="/wiki/Gerlachovsk%C3%BD_%C5%A1t%C3%ADt" title="Gerlachovský štít">Gerlachovský štít</a> in Slovakia is the highest peak at 2,655 m (8,711 ft) above sea level. The Carpathians cover an area of 190,000 km<sup>2</sup> (73,359 sq mi), and after the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, form the next-most extensive mountain system in Europe.</p>

<p>Although commonly referred to as a mountain chain, the Carpathians do not actually form an uninterrupted chain of mountains. Rather, they consist of several <a href="/wiki/Orography" title="Orography">orographically</a> and geologically distinctive groups, presenting as great a structural variety as the Alps. The Carpathians, which attain an altitude over 2,500 m (8,202 ft) in only a few places, lack the bold peaks, extensive snowfields, large <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, high waterfalls, and numerous large lakes that are common in the Alps. It was believed that no area of the Carpathian range was covered in snow all year round and there were no glaciers, but recent research by Polish scientists discovered one permafrost and glacial area in the Tatra Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Carpathians at their highest altitude are only as high as the middle region of the Alps, with which they share a common appearance, climate, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a>. The Carpathians are separated from the Alps by the Danube. The two ranges meet at only one point: the <a href="/wiki/Leitha_Mountains" title="Leitha Mountains">Leitha Mountains</a> at Bratislava. The river also separates them from the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Mountains" title="Balkan Mountains">Balkan Mountains</a> at Orşova in Romania. The valley of the <a href="/wiki/Morava_(river)" title="Morava (river)">March</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oder" title="Oder">Oder</a> separates the Carpathians from the <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravian</a> chains, which belong to the middle wing of the great Central Mountain System of Europe. Unlike the other wings of the system, the Carpathians, which form the watershed between the northern seas and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, are surrounded on all sides by plains, namely the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pannonian_plain" title="Pannonian plain">Pannonian plain</a> to the southwest, the plain of the Lower Danube (Romania) to the south, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galicia_(Central_Europe)" title="Galicia (Central Europe)">Galician</a> plain to the northeast.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_passes">Mountain passes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpathian_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mountain passes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Romanian part of the main chain of the Carpathians, the most important <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pass" title="Mountain pass">mountain passes</a> are (starting from the Ukrainian border): the <a href="/wiki/Prislop_Pass" title="Prislop Pass">Prislop Pass</a>, Rodna Pass, <a href="/wiki/Tihu%C8%9Ba_Pass" title="Tihuța Pass">Tihuța Pass</a> (also known as Borgo Pass), Tulgheș Pass, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicaz_Canyon" title="Bicaz Canyon">Bicaz Canyon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghime%C8%99_Pass" title="Ghimeș Pass">Ghimeș Pass</a>, Uz Pass and Oituz Pass, <a href="/wiki/Buz%C4%83u_Pass" title="Buzău Pass">Buzău Pass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Predeal_Pass" title="Predeal Pass">Predeal Pass</a> (crossed by the railway from Braşov to <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a>), <a href="/wiki/Turnu_Ro%C8%99u_Pass" title="Turnu Roșu Pass">Turnu Roșu Pass</a> (1,115 ft., running through the narrow gorge of the <a href="/wiki/Olt_River" title="Olt River">Olt River</a> and crossed by the railway from Sibiu to Bucharest), <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Pass" title="Vulcan Pass">Vulcan Pass</a>, Teregova Pass and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_Gate_(Danube)" title="Iron Gate (Danube)">Iron Gate</a> (both crossed by the railway from <a href="/wiki/Timi%C8%99oara" title="Timișoara">Timișoara</a> to <a href="/wiki/Craiova" title="Craiova">Craiova</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpathian_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg/220px-Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg/330px-Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg/440px-Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Slovakia_Vratna_11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Vr%C3%A1tna_dolina" title="Vrátna dolina">Vrátna dolina</a>, Slovakia</div>

</div>
<p>The area now occupied by the Carpathians was once occupied by smaller ocean basins. The Carpathian mountains were formed during the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_orogeny" title="Alpine orogeny">Alpine orogeny</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> by moving the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ALCAPA_(tectonic_plate)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ALCAPA (tectonic plate) (page does not exist)">ALCAPA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tisza_Plate" title="Tisza Plate">Tisza</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dacia_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dacia Plate (page does not exist)">Dacia</a> plates over <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducting</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> (<a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=rCzu3YsGshAC&amp;lpg=PA18&amp;dq=alcapa%20tisza&amp;pg=PA18#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">see maps</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The mountains take the form of a <a href="/wiki/Fold_and_thrust_belt" title="Fold and thrust belt">fold and thrust belt</a> with generally north <a href="/wiki/Vergence_(geology)" title="Vergence (geology)">vergence</a> in the western segment, northeast to east vergence in the eastern portion and southeast vergence in the southern portion.</p>
<p>The external, generally northern, portion of the orogenic belt is a Tertiary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretionary_prism" title="Accretionary prism">accretionary prism</a> of a so-called <a href="/wiki/Flysch" title="Flysch">Flysch belt</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Flysch_Belt" title="Carpathian Flysch Belt">Carpathian Flysch Belt</a>) created by rocks scraped off the sea bottom and thrust over the North-European plate. The Carpathian <a href="/wiki/Accretionary_wedge" title="Accretionary wedge">accretionary wedge</a> is made of several thin skinned <a href="/wiki/Nappe" title="Nappe">nappes</a> composed of Cretaceous to Paleogene <a href="/wiki/Turbidite" title="Turbidite">turbidites</a>. Thrusting of the Flysch nappes over the Carpathian foreland caused the formation of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carpathian_foreland_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carpathian foreland basin (page does not exist)">Carpathian foreland basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The boundary between the Flysch belt and internal zones of the orogenic belt in the western segment of the mountain range is marked by the <a href="/wiki/Pieniny_Klippen_Belt" title="Pieniny Klippen Belt">Pieniny Klippen Belt</a>, a narrow complicated zone of polyphase compressional deformation, later involved in a supposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strike-slip" title="Strike-slip">strike-slip</a> zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Internal zones in western and eastern segments contain older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variscan" title="Variscan">Variscan</a> igneous massifs reworked in Mesozoic <a href="/wiki/Thick-skinned_deformation" title="Thick-skinned deformation">thick</a> and thin-skinned nappes. During the Middle <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> this zone was affected by intensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calc-alkaline" title="Calc-alkaline">calc-alkaline</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P.C3.A1cskay.2C_Z._2006.2C_pp._511_-_530_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-P.C3.A1cskay.2C_Z._2006.2C_pp._511_-_530-25">[25]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_volcanism" title="Arc volcanism">arc volcanism</a> that developed over the subduction zone of the flysch basins. At the same time, the internal zones of the orogenic belt were affected by large extensional structure<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> of the back-arc <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Basin" title="Pannonian Basin">Pannonian Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The last volcanic activity occurred at <a href="/wiki/Ciomadul" title="Ciomadul">Ciomadul</a> about 30,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P.C3.A1cskay.2C_Z._2006.2C_pp._511_-_530_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-P.C3.A1cskay.2C_Z._2006.2C_pp._511_-_530-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iron, gold and silver were found in great quantities in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Carpathians" title="Western Carpathians">Western Carpathians</a>. After the Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a>'s conquest of Dacia, he brought back to Rome over 165 tons of gold and 330 tons of silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions_of_the_Carpathians">Divisions of the Carpathians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpathian_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Divisions of the Carpathians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Carpathians" title="Divisions of the Carpathians">Divisions of the Carpathians</a></div>

<p>The largest range is the <a href="/wiki/Tatra_Mountains" title="Tatra Mountains">Tatras</a> in Poland and Slovakia. A major part of the western and northeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_Carpathians" title="Outer Carpathians">Outer Carpathians</a> in Poland, Ukraine, and Slovakia is traditionally called the <a href="/wiki/Beskids" title="Beskids">Beskids</a>.</p>
<p>The geological border between the Western and Eastern Carpathians runs approximately along the line (south to north) between the towns of <a href="/wiki/Michalovce" title="Michalovce">Michalovce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bardejov" title="Bardejov">Bardejov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nowy_S%C4%85cz" title="Nowy Sącz">Nowy Sącz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tarn%C3%B3w" title="Tarnów">Tarnów</a>. In older systems the border runs more in the east, along the line (north to south) along the rivers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_River" title="San River">San</a> and <a href="/wiki/Os%C5%82awa" title="Osława">Osława</a> (Poland), the town of <a href="/wiki/Snina" title="Snina">Snina</a> (Slovakia) and river Tur'ia (Ukraine). Biologists, however, shift the border even further to the east.</p>
<p>The border between the eastern and southern Carpathians is formed by the <a href="/wiki/Predeal" title="Predeal">Predeal</a> Pass, south of Braşov and the <a href="/wiki/Prahova_Valley" title="Prahova Valley">Prahova Valley</a>.</p>
<p>Ukrainians sometimes denote as "Eastern Carpathians" only the Ukrainian Carpathians (or Wooded Carpathians), meaning the part situated largely on their territory (i.e., to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Prislop_Pass" title="Prislop Pass">Prislop Pass</a>), while Romanians sometimes denote as "Eastern (Oriental) Carpathians" only the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Carpathians" title="Romanian Carpathians">Romanian Carpathians</a> part which lies on their territory (i.e., from the Ukrainian border or from the Prislop Pass to the south), which they subdivide into three simplified geographical groups (north, center, south), instead of Outer and Inner Eastern Carpathians. These are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Carpathians of Maramureș and Bukovina (Romanian: <i>Carpaţii Maramureșului și ai Bucovinei</i>)</li>
<li>Moldavian-Transylvanian Carpathians (Romanian: <i>Carpaţii Moldo-Transilvani</i>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curvature_Carpathians" title="Curvature Carpathians">Curvature Carpathians</a> (Romanian: <i>Carpaţii Curburii, Carpaţii de Curbură</i>)</li>
</ul>
