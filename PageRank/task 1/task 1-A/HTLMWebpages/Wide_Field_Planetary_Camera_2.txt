<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Wide Field and Planetary Camera 2</h1>

<p>The <b>Wide Field and Planetary Camera 2</b> (<b>WFPC2</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a> formerly installed on the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>. The camera was built by the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> and is roughly the size of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_grand_piano" title="Baby grand piano">baby grand piano</a>. It was installed by servicing mission 1 (<a href="/wiki/STS-61" title="STS-61">STS-61</a>) in 1993, replacing the telescope's original <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_and_Planetary_Camera" title="Wide Field and Planetary Camera">Wide Field and Planetary Camera</a> (WF/PC). WFPC2 was used to image the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Deep_Field" title="Hubble Deep Field">Hubble Deep Field</a> in 1995, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hourglass_Nebula" title="Hourglass Nebula">Hourglass Nebula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egg_Nebula" title="Egg Nebula">Egg Nebula</a> in 1996, and the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Deep_Field_South" title="Hubble Deep Field South">Hubble Deep Field South</a> in 1998. During <a href="/wiki/STS-125" title="STS-125">STS-125</a>, WFPC2 was removed and replaced with the <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_Camera_3" title="Wide Field Camera 3">Wide Field Camera 3</a> as part of the mission's first spacewalk on May 14, 2009. After returning to Earth, the camera was displayed briefly at the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> before returning to its final home at the Smithsonian's <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithsonian_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonian-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nest_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nest-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wide_Field_and_Planetary_Camera_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WFPC2 was built by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a>, which also built the predecessor <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_and_Planetary_Camera" title="Wide Field and Planetary Camera">WF/PC</a> camera launched with Hubble in 1990. WFPC2 contains internal corrective optics to fix the spherical aberration in the Hubble telescope's primary mirror.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">charge-coupled devices</a> (CCDs) in the WFPC2 (designed at JPL and manufactured by Loral) detected <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> in a range from 120 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a> to 1000 nm. This included the 380 nm to 780 nm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_spectrum" title="Optical spectrum">visible spectrum</a>, all of the near <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (and a small part of the extreme ultraviolet band) and most of the near <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> band. The sensitivity distribution of these CCDs is roughly <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">normal</a>, with a peak around 700 nm and concomitantly very poor sensitivity at the extremes of the CCDs' operating range. WFPC2 featured four identical CCD detectors, each 800x800 <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a>. Three of these, arranged in an L-formation, comprise Hubble's Wide Field Camera (WFC). Adjacent to them is the Planetary Camera (PC), a fourth CCD with different (narrower-focused) optics. This afforded a more detailed view over a smaller region of the visual field. WFC and PC images are typically combined, producing the WFPC2's characteristic stairstep image. When distributed as non-scientific <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEG</a> files the PC portion of the image is shown with the same resolution as the WFC portions, but astronomers receive a raw scientific image package which presents the PC image in its native, higher detail.</p>
<p>To allow scientists to view specific parts of the electromagnetic spectrum the WFPC2 featured a rotating wheel which moves different optical filters into the lightpath (between the WFPC2's aperture and the CCD detectors). The 48 filter elements included:</p>
<ul>
<li>A set of standard wideband photometric filters.</li>
<li>A graduated filter, featuring a wide range of very narrowband filters. By positioning the target object at a precise part of the field, the operator can use an accurately picked narrowband filter.</li>
<li>A number of narrowband optical filters tuned to the wavelengths of various atomic emission lines.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wide_Field_and_Planetary_Camera_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>As predicted, over the course of its mission the WFPC2 experienced degradation of the CCDs, resulting in defective ("hot") pixels. The telescope's operators perform monthly calibration tests to catalog these; with the WFPC's <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a> closed a number of long exposures are taken, and pixels which differ significantly from near black are flagged. To avoid false positives caused by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> tripping a given pixel, the output of different calibration shots are compared. Pixels which are consistently "hot" are recorded, and astronomers who analyse raw WFPC2 images receive a list of these pixels. Typically astronomers adjust their photo-processing software to ignore these bad pixels.</p>
<p>WFPC2 was largely superseded for broad-band imaging by the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Camera_for_Surveys" title="Advanced Camera for Surveys">Advanced Camera for Surveys</a>, installed during servicing mission 3B in 2002. However, the early 2007 failure of ACS resulted in WFPC2 returning to its role as Hubble's primary visible light camera. WFPC2 was removed from HST during Servicing Mission 4 in May 2009, for return to Earth and eventual museum display. It was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_Camera_3" title="Wide Field Camera 3">Wide Field Camera 3</a>, which features two UV/visible detecting CCDs, each 2048x4096 pixels, and a separate IR CCD of 1024 x 1024, capable of receiving infrared radiation up to 1700 nm.</p>
