<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">African Plate</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>African Plate</b> is a major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> straddling the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">prime meridian</a>. It includes much of the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, as well as oceanic crust which lies between the continent and various surrounding ocean ridges. Between <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=60">60</a> million years ago</span> and <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=10">10</a> million years ago</span>, the <a href="/wiki/Somali_Plate" title="Somali Plate">Somali Plate</a> began <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rift_(geology)" title="Rift (geology)">rifting</a> from the African Plate along the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since the continent of Africa consists of crust from both the African and the Somali plates, some literature refers to the African Plate as the <b>Nubian Plate</b> to distinguish it from the continent as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries">Boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The western edge of the African Plate is a <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">divergent boundary</a> with the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a> to the north and the <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plate</a> to the south which forms the central and southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>. The African plate is bounded on the northeast by the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Plate" title="Arabian Plate">Arabian Plate</a>, the southeast by the <a href="/wiki/Somali_Plate" title="Somali Plate">Somali Plate</a>, the north by the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea_Plate" title="Aegean Sea Plate">Aegean Sea Plate</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_Plate" title="Anatolian Plate">Anatolian Plate</a>, and on the south by the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Plate" title="Antarctic Plate">Antarctic Plate</a>. All of these are divergent or spreading boundaries with the exception of the northern boundary and a short segment near the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Terceira_Rift" title="Terceira Rift">Terceira Rift</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The African Plate includes several <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a>, stable blocks of old crust with deep roots in the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">subcontinental lithospheric mantle</a>, and less stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terranes" title="Terranes">terranes</a>, which came together to form the African continent during the assembly of the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pangea" title="Pangea">Pangea</a> around 550 million years ago. The cratons are, from south to north, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalahari_craton" title="Kalahari craton">Kalahari craton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo_craton" title="Congo craton">Congo craton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanzania_craton" title="Tanzania craton">Tanzania craton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_African_craton" title="West African craton">West African craton</a>. The cratons were widely separated in the past, but came together during the <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_orogeny" title="Pan-African orogeny">Pan-African orogeny</a> and stayed together when Gondwana split up. The cratons are connected by <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenic belts</a>, regions of highly deformed rock where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plates" title="Tectonic plates">tectonic plates</a> have engaged. The <a href="/wiki/Saharan_Metacraton" title="Saharan Metacraton">Saharan Metacraton</a> has been tentatively identified as the remains of a craton that has become detached from the subcontinental lithospheric mantle, but alternatively may consist of a collection of unrelated crustal fragments swept together during the Pan-African orogeny.</p>
<p>In some areas, the cratons are covered by <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basins</a>, such as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tindouf_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tindouf basin (page does not exist)">Tindouf basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taoudeni_basin" title="Taoudeni basin">Taoudeni basin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo_basin" title="Congo basin">Congo basin</a>, where the underlying archaic crust is overlaid by more recent <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a> sediments. The plate includes <a href="/wiki/Shear_zone" title="Shear zone">shear zones</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Shear_Zone" title="Central African Shear Zone">Central African Shear Zone</a> (CASZ) where, in the past, two sections of the crust were moving in opposite directions, and rifts such as the <a href="/wiki/Anza_trough" title="Anza trough">Anza trough</a> where the crust was pulled apart, and the resulting depression filled with more modern sediment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_movements">Modern movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The African Plate is rifting in the eastern interior of the African continent along the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>. This rift zone separates the African Plate to the west from the Somali Plate to the east. One <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> proposes the existence of a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a> beneath the <a href="/wiki/Afar_Triangle" title="Afar Triangle">Afar region</a>, whereas an opposing hypothesis asserts that the rifting is merely a zone of maximum weakness where the African Plate is deforming as plates to its east are moving rapidly northward.</p>
<p>The African Plate's speed is estimated at around 2.15 cm (0.85 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It has been moving over the past 100 million years or so in a general northeast direction. This is drawing it closer to the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, causing <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> where <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> is converging with <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> (e.g. portions of the central and eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>). In the western Mediterranean, the relative motions of the Eurasian and African plates produce a combination of lateral and compressive forces, concentrated in a zone known as the <a href="/wiki/Azores%E2%80%93Gibraltar_Transform_Fault" title="Azores–Gibraltar Transform Fault">Azores–Gibraltar Fault Zone</a>. Along its northeast margin, the African Plate is bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea_Rift" title="Red Sea Rift">Red Sea Rift</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Plate" title="Arabian Plate">Arabian Plate</a> is moving away from the African Plate.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_England_hotspot" title="New England hotspot">New England hotspot</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> has probably created a short line of mid- to late-<a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> age <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a> on the African Plate but appears to be currently inactive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SE_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SE-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
