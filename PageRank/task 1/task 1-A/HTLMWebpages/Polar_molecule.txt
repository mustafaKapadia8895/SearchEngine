<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <b>polarity</b> is a separation of <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> leading to a molecule or its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_group" title="Chemical group">chemical groups</a> having an electric <a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">dipole</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multipole_moments" title="Multipole moments">multipole</a> moment.</p>
<p>Polar molecules must contain polar <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bonds</a> due to a difference in <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> between the bonded atoms. A polar molecule with two or more polar bonds must have a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_geometry" title="Molecular geometry">geometry</a> which is asymmetric in at least one direction, so that the <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">bond dipoles</a> do not cancel each other.</p>
<p>Polar molecules interact through dipole–dipole <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonds" title="Hydrogen bonds">hydrogen bonds</a>. Polarity underlies a number of physical properties including <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a>, and melting and boiling points.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polarity_of_bonds">Polarity of bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Polarity of bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;max-width:204px">
<div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="506" data-file-width="1100" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png/200px-Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png/300px-Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png/400px-Hydrogen-fluoride-3D-balls.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div>

<div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="883" data-file-width="1100" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png/200px-Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png/300px-Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png/400px-Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div>

<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">In a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF), the more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronegative" title="Electronegative">electronegative</a> atom (<a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>) is shown in yellow. Because the electrons spend more time by the fluorine atom in the H−F bond, the red represents partially negatively charged regions, while blue represents partially positively charged regions.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Not all atoms attract electrons with the same force. The amount of "pull" an atom exerts on its electrons is called its <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a>. Atoms with high electronegativities – such as <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> – exert a greater pull on electrons than atoms with lower electronegativities such as <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metals</a>. In a bond, this leads to unequal sharing of electrons between the atoms, as electrons will be drawn closer to the atom with the higher electronegativity.</p>
<p>Because electrons have a negative charge, the unequal sharing of electrons within a bond leads to the formation of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_dipole" title="Electric dipole">electric dipole</a>: a separation of positive and negative electric charge. Because the amount of charge separated in such dipoles is usually smaller than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_charge" title="Fundamental charge">fundamental charge</a>, they are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_charges" title="Partial charges">partial charges</a>, denoted as δ+ (<a href="/wiki/Delta_(letter)" title="Delta (letter)">delta</a> plus) and δ− (delta minus). These symbols were introduced by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Kelk_Ingold" title="Christopher Kelk Ingold">Christopher Kelk Ingold</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edith_Hilda_Ingold&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edith Hilda Ingold (page does not exist)">Edith Hilda Ingold</a> in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">bond dipole moment</a> is calculated by multiplying the amount of charge separated and the distance between the charges.</p>
<p>These dipoles within molecules can interact with dipoles in other molecules, creating <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force#Dipole-dipole_interactions" title="Intermolecular force">dipole-dipole intermolecular forces</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bonds can fall between one of two extremes – being completely nonpolar or completely polar. A completely nonpolar bond occurs when the electronegativities are identical and therefore possess a difference of zero. A completely polar bond is more correctly called an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bond</a>, and occurs when the difference between electronegativities is large enough that one atom actually takes an electron from the other. The terms "polar" and "nonpolar" are usually applied to <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a>, that is, bonds where the polarity is not complete. To determine the polarity of a covalent bond using numerical means, the difference between the electronegativity of the atoms is used.</p>
<p>Bond polarity is typically divided into three groups that are loosely based on the difference in electronegativity between the two bonded atoms. According to the <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity#Pauling_electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">Pauling scale</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Nonpolar bonds</i> generally occur when the difference in <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> between the two atoms is less than 0.5</li>
<li><i>Polar bonds</i> generally occur when the difference in electronegativity between the two atoms is roughly between 0.5 and 2.0</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bonds" title="Ionic bonds">Ionic bonds</a></i> generally occur when the difference in electronegativity between the two atoms is greater than 2.0</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Pauling</a> based this classification scheme on the <i>partial ionic character</i> of a bond, which is an approximate function of the difference in electronegativity between the two bonded atoms. He estimated that a difference of 1.7 corresponds to 50% ionic character, so that a greater difference corresponds to a bond which is predominantly ionic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Introduction_to_quantum_mechanics" title="Introduction to quantum mechanics">quantum-mechanical</a> description, Pauling proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> for a polar molecule AB is a <a href="/wiki/Linear_combination" title="Linear combination">linear combination</a> of wave functions for covalent and ionic molecules: ψ = aψ(A:B) + bψ(A<sup>+</sup>B<sup>−</sup>). The amount of covalent and ionic character depends on the values of the squared coefficients a<sup>2</sup> and b<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polarity_of_molecules">Polarity of molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Polarity of molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Dipole#Molecular_dipoles" title="Dipole">dipole § Molecular dipoles</a>.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>While the molecules can be described as "polar covalent", "nonpolar covalent", or "ionic", this is often a relative term, with one molecule simply being <i>more polar</i> or <i>more nonpolar</i> than another. However, the following properties are typical of such molecules.</p>
<p>A molecule is composed of one or more chemical bonds between <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">molecular orbitals</a> of different atoms. A molecule may be polar either as a result of polar bonds due to differences in <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> as described above, or as a result of an asymmetric arrangement of nonpolar covalent bonds and non-bonding pairs of electrons known as a full <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">molecular orbital</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_molecules">Polar molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polar molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dipoli_acqua.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="712" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dipoli_acqua.png/200px-Dipoli_acqua.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dipoli_acqua.png/300px-Dipoli_acqua.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dipoli_acqua.png/400px-Dipoli_acqua.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dipoli_acqua.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The water molecule is made up of oxygen and hydrogen, with respective electronegativities of 3.44 and 2.20. The dipoles from each of the two bonds (red arrows) add together to make the overall molecule polar.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A polar molecule has a net <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a> as a result of the opposing charges (i.e. having partial positive and partial negative charges) from polar bonds arranged asymmetrically. <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water" title="Properties of water">Water</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O) is an example of a polar molecule since it has a slight positive charge on one side and a slight negative charge on the other. The dipoles do not cancel out resulting in a net dipole. Due to the polar nature of the water molecule itself, polar molecules are generally able to dissolve in water. Other examples include sugars (like <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>), which have many polar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">oxygen–hydrogen</a> (−OH) groups and are overall highly polar.</p>
<p>If the <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">bond dipole moments</a> of the molecule do not cancel, the molecule is polar. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_molecule" title="Water molecule">water molecule</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O) contains two polar O−H bonds in a <a href="/wiki/Bent_molecular_geometry" title="Bent molecular geometry">bent</a> (nonlinear) geometry. The <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">bond dipole moments</a> do not cancel, so that the molecule forms a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipoles#Molecular_dipoles" title="Dipoles">molecular dipole</a> with its negative pole at the oxygen and its positive pole midway between the two hydrogen atoms. In the figure each bond joins the central O atom with a negative charge (red) to an H atom with a positive charge (blue).</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, HF, molecule is polar by virtue of polar covalent bonds – in the covalent bond electrons are displaced toward the more electronegative fluorine atom. <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">Ammonia</a>, NH<sub>3</sub>, molecule the three N−H bonds have only a slight polarity (toward the more electronegative nitrogen atom). The molecule has two lone electrons in an orbital, that points towards the fourth apex of the approximate tetrahedron, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VSEPR" title="VSEPR">VSEPR</a>). This orbital is not participating in covalent bonding; it is electron-rich, which results in a powerful dipole across the whole ammonia molecule.</p>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png" title="Resonance Lewis structures of the ozone molecule"><img alt="Resonance Lewis structures of the ozone molecule" data-file-height="295" data-file-width="1100" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png/300px-Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png/450px-Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png/600px-Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (O<sub>3</sub>) molecules, the two O−O bonds are nonpolar (there is no electronegativity difference between atoms of the same element). However, the distribution of other electrons is uneven – since the central atom has to share electrons with two other atoms, but each of the outer atoms has to share electrons with only one other atom, the central atom is more deprived of electrons than the others (the central atom has a <a href="/wiki/Formal_charge" title="Formal charge">formal charge</a> of +1, while the outer atoms each have a formal charge of −<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>). Since the molecule has a bent geometry, the result is a dipole across the whole ozone molecule.</p>
<p>When comparing a polar and nonpolar molecule with similar molar masses, the polar molecule in general has a higher boiling point, because the dipole–dipole interaction between polar molecules results in stronger intermolecular attractions. One common form of polar interaction is the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bond</a>, which is also known as the H-bond. For example, water forms H-bonds and has a molar mass M = 18 and a boiling point of +100 °C, compared to nonpolar <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> with M = 16 and a boiling point of –161 °C.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonpolar_molecules">Nonpolar molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nonpolar molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A molecule may be nonpolar either when there is an equal sharing of electrons between the two atoms of a diatomic molecule or because of the symmetrical arrangement of polar bonds in a more complex molecule. For example, <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a> (BF<sub>3</sub>) has a trigonal planar arrangement of three polar bonds at 120°. This results in no overall dipole in the molecule.</p>

<p>Not every molecule with polar bonds is a polar molecule. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) has two polar C=O bonds, but the geometry of CO<sub>2</sub> is linear so that the two bond dipole moments cancel and there is no net molecular dipole moment; the molecule is nonpolar.</p>

In <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, the bonds are arranged symmetrically (in a tetrahedral arrangement) so there is no overall dipole.</div>

<p>Examples of household nonpolar compounds include fats, oil, and petrol/gasoline. Therefore, most nonpolar molecules are water-insoluble (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a>) at room temperature. Many nonpolar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvent" title="Organic solvent">organic solvents</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Turpentine" title="Turpentine">turpentine</a>, are able to dissolve polar substances.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> molecule (CH<sub>4</sub>) the four C−H bonds are arranged tetrahedrally around the carbon atom. Each bond has polarity (though not very strong). However, the bonds are arranged symmetrically so there is no overall dipole in the molecule. The diatomic <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> molecule (O<sub>2</sub>) does not have polarity in the covalent bond because of equal electronegativity, hence there is no polarity in the molecule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amphiphilic_molecules">Amphiphilic molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Amphiphilic molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Large molecules that have one end with polar groups attached and another end with nonpolar groups are described as <a href="/wiki/Amphiphile" title="Amphiphile">amphiphiles</a> or <i>amphiphilic</i> molecules. They are good <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactants</a> and can aid in the formation of stable emulsions, or blends, of water and fats. Surfactants reduce the interfacial tension between oil and water by <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorbing</a> at the liquid–liquid interface.</p>

<p>Determining the <a href="/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions" title="Point groups in three dimensions">point group</a> is a useful way to predict polarity of a molecule. In general, a molecule will not possess dipole moment, if the individual bond dipole moments of the molecule cancel each other out. This is because dipole moments are <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">euclidean vector</a> quantities with magnitude and direction, and a two equal vectors who oppose each other will cancel out.</p>
<p>Any molecule with a centre of inversion ("i") or a horizontal mirror plane ("σ<sub>h</sub>") will not possess dipole moments. Likewise, a molecule with more than one C<sub><i>n</i></sub> axis will not possess dipole moment because dipole moments cannot lie in more than one <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a>. As a consequence of that constraint, all molecules with D symmetry (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sch%C3%B6nflies_notation" title="Schönflies notation">Schönflies notation</a>) will, therefore, not have dipole moment because, by definition, D point groups have two or multiple C<sub><i>n</i></sub> axis.</p>
<p>Since C<sub>1</sub>, C<sub>s</sub>,C<sub>∞h</sub> C<sub><i>n</i></sub> and C<sub><i>n</i>v</sub> <a href="/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions" title="Point groups in three dimensions">point groups</a> do not have a centre of inversion, horizontal mirror planes or multiple C<sub><i>n</i></sub> axis, molecules in one of those point groups will have dipole moment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_deflection_of_water">Electrical deflection of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_polarity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electrical deflection of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Contrary to popular misconception, the electrical deflection of a stream of water from a charged object is not based on polarity. The deflection occurs because of electrically charged droplets in the stream, which the charged object induces. A stream of water can also be deflected in a uniform electrical field, which cannot exert force on polar molecules. Additionally, after a stream of water is grounded, it can no longer be deflected. Weak deflection is even possible for nonpolar liquids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
