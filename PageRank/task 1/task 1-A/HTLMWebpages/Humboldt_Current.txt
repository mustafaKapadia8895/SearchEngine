<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Humboldt Current</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Humboldt Current System (HCS),</b> also called <b>the Peru Current,</b> is a major Eastern Boundary Upwelling Ecosystem that flows north along the western coast of South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> It is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_boundary_current" title="Eastern boundary current">eastern boundary current</a> flowing in the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, and extend 500-1,000 kilometers offshore. The Humboldt Current is named after the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>. In 1846, von Humboldt reported measurements of the cold-water current in his book Cosmos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The current extends from the southern <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> (~45’S) to northern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> (~4’S) where cold, upwelled waters intersect warm tropical waters to form the Equatorial Front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Sea surface temperatures off the coast of Peru, around 5’S, reach temperatures as low as 16’C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> This is highly uncharacteristic of tropical waters, as most other regions have temperatures measuring above 25’C. Upwelling brings nutrients to the surface, which support phytoplankton and ultimately increase biological productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">Upwelling</a> within the current is not uniform across the entire system. Three notable upwelling subsystems are produced by this current: i) seasonal upwelling in Chile, ii) upwelling “shadow” that is less productive, but still large in northern Chile and Southern Peru, and iii) year-round upwelling in Peru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Humboldt Current is considered a Class I, highly productive (&gt;300 gC/m2-yr), ecosystem. It is the most productive eastern boundary current system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It accounts for roughly 18-20% of the total worldwide marine fish catch. The species are mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_mackerel" title="Jack mackerel">jack mackerel</a>. The system’s high productivity supports other important fishery resources as well as marine mammals (<a href="/wiki/Eared_seal" title="Eared seal">eared seals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">cetaceans</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>. The cold, nutrient-rich water brought to the surface by upwelling drives the system’s extraordinary productivity.</p>

</div>
<p>Periodically, the upwelling that drives the system’s productivity is disrupted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) event. When this occurs, fish abundance and distribution are significantly affected, often leading to stock crashes and cascading social and economic impacts. These events have led to sequential changes, where sardines and anchovies have replaced each other periodically as the dominant species in the ecosystem. These species changes can have negative consequences for the fishing industry and the economies of the countries that fish the system.</p>
<p>The Humboldt has a considerable cooling influence on the <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Chile" title="Climate of Chile">climate of Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Peru" title="Climate of Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Ecuador" title="Climate of Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. It is also largely responsible for the aridity of <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte_Grande,_Chile" title="Norte Grande, Chile">northern Chile</a> and coastal areas of Peru and also of the aridity of southern Ecuador. Marine air is cooled by the current and thus is not conducive to generating precipitation (although clouds and fog are produced).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_Oceanography">Physical Oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humboldt_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical Oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> are the primary drivers of the Humboldt Current circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Variability in this system is driven by latitudinal shifts between the Intertropical Convergent Zone and the trade winds in the north. Shifts within the South Pacific High at mid-latitudes, as well as cyclonic storms and movement of the Southern <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a> southward also contribute to system changes. Atmospheric variability off central Chile is enhanced by the aggravation of coastal low pressure systems trapped between the marine boundary layer and the coastal mountains. This is prominent poleward from 27’S to 42’S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Humboldt current, occupying the upper ocean, flows equatorward carrying fresh, cold Sub-Antarctic surface water northward, along the outskirts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_gyre" title="Subtropical gyre">subtropical gyre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The main flow of the current veers offshore in southern Peru, as a weaker limb continues to flow equatorward. Around 18’S the fresh, cold waters begin to mix with the warm, high salinity Subtropical Surface waters. This collision causes partial <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subductions</a>. Within this region, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_Undercurrent" title="Equatorial Undercurrent">equatorial undercurrent</a> (EUC) flows eastward along the equator, feeding the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peru-Chile_undercurrent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peru-Chile undercurrent (page does not exist)">Peru-Chile undercurrent</a> (PCU) that moves poleward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Off the coast of central Chile, there is a coastal transition zone (CTZ), which is characterized by high eddy kinetic energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> This energy forms mesoscale eddies which extend 600–800 km offshore. The CTZ has three distinct regions within its boundaries: 1) high chlorophyll-a concentrations in wide regions off the coast of Peru (10-15’S), 2) high chlorophyll-a concentrations in wide regions off the coast of Chile (30’S), and 3) high chlorophyll-a concentrations in narrow regions off the coast of northern Chile (Montecino and Lange 2008). High chlorophyll-a concentrations are generally found within 50 km of the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The limb of the HCS that veers off the coast of Peru creates a decrease in ventilation within the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> This lack of ventilation is the primary driver of an intense <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_minimum_zone" title="Oxygen minimum zone">oxygen minimum zone</a> (OMZ) which is formed in the sub-surface to intermediate depths. In the north, the EUC ventilates the OMZ, and in the south the PCU <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advects</a> low oxygen waters southward towards northern Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> This OMZ is the fourth largest permeant hypoxic zone in the world’s oceans. It occupies an area about 2.18 +/- 0.66 x 10<sup>6</sup> km<sup>3</sup>. The core of this zone is centered off Peru, creating a shallow upper boundary that reaches from about 100 m down to 600 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Another factor contributing to the OMZ is sinking and decay of primary productive resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Consequently, the OMZ forces many organisms to stay near the surface where nutrients and oxygen are obtainable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> The presence of a shallow OMZ restricts the migration of <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> within the water column. Between 0 and 600 m, many species of zooplankton occupy this space within the OMZ. This allows for a substantial exchange of carbon between the euphotic layer and the OMZ. 75% of the total zooplankton biomass move in and out of the OMZ. The OMZ also serves as a refuge for organisms that can live in <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxic</a> conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coastal upwelling is the main factor contributing to the high biological productivity of the Humboldt current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Upwelling within the current is not uniform across the entire system. Three notable upwelling subsystems are produced by this current: i) seasonal upwelling in Chile only during the spring and summer, because of the displacement of the subtropical center of high pressure during the winter (January–March), ii) upwelling “shadow” that is less productive, but still large in northern Chile and Southern Peru, and iii) highly productive year-round upwelling in Peru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The upwelling shadow identified between 35’S and 15’S is caused by the oligotrophic subtropical gyre impinging on the coast. This creates a narrow, but highly productive, upwelling zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_Productivity">Biological Productivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humboldt_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biological Productivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the upwelling zones within the Humboldt current, biological diversity is extremely high. The current hosts a wide range of organisms including multiple species of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, fish, and marine mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The food web starts with the phytoplankton. The conditions of the Humboldt current are prime for these organisms to thrive. This causes a cascade effect in which larger and larger organisms are drawn to the area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humboldt_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Humboldt current produces some of the most successful commercial fisheries in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The major catches include: sardines, anchovies, mackerel, hake, and squid. Three major stocks of anchoveta are distributed between 4’S and 42’S within the HCS. North-Central Peru’s fishery is primarily composed of one stock of anchoveta. Sardines, chub mackerel, and bonito are also common catches, but not as prominent, in Peru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Southern Peru and Northern Chile host a major sardine fishery. Other common stocks include: a second anchoveta stock, jack mackerel, tuna, and swordfish. Anchoveta, jack mackerel, and sardines are the primary commercial stocks in central Chile.</p>
<p>Anchoveta are found in more recently upwelled waters, close to the coast. Sardines, on the other hand, are typically found farther offshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-20"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Seasonal upwelling plays a major role in the spawning behaviors of both sardines and anchoveta. By spawning at the end of winter, egg and larval survival in greatly enhanced. This is due to the moderate upwelling, which causes lower turbulence, as well as a weakened Ekman drift offshore. These two species experience population shifts related to climate changes and environmental events such as El Nino. This is due to changes in the availability of each species habitat. Anchoveta are an important component in the diets of marine mammals, seabirds, and larger fish. Shifts in these populations ultimately cause a shift in the energy processing within the HCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-21"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jack mackerel (jurel) is the second largest fishery in the HCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-22"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> As with the anchoveta in Peru, this species is believed to be composed of a single stock. Jurel are a straddling species. This means the species is found both within and outside of the 200-mile economic exclusive zone. Jurel became an important fishery in the 1970s to alleviate the pressure put on the anchoveta stock. During the 1980s, however, the jurel decreased in population size due to poor recruitment and overfishing. Restrictions of jurel fishing were imposed in 1998 which led to regrowth of the population. Since 2002, the jurel population is now under full exploitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-23"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the last 15 years, the hake fishery in Peru has declined significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-24"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> This is due to overfishing, environmental stress, and decreased reproductive capacity. The Chilean hake population in central-south Chile catch exceeded 100,000 tons, and has since dropped to 40,000 tons in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-25"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Influence_of_El_Niño"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_El_Ni.C3.B1o">Influence of El Niño</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humboldt_Current&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence of El Niño">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The productivity of the HCS is strongly effected by El Niño and La Niña events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-26"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> During an El Niño event, the thermocline and upper region of the OMZ deepen to greater than 600 m. This causes a loss of nitrogen and decrease in export of carbon. El Niño also causes poleward currents to increase in velocity. During non-El Niño years, productivity is very high due to the high nutrient contents, nitrogen recycling through processes such as denitrification, increased carbon export, and remineralization <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-27"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The anchoveta fishery in Peru was booming during the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1970, catches were reported to exceed 12 million tons per year. This accounted for 20% of the world’s catches. An El Niño event occurred during 1972 and caused the anchoveta population to collapse. However, sardine populations saw a dramatic increase in the next 15–20 years. Consequently, sardine fisheries grew in this “regime shift”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
