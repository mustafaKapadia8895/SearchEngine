<p><b>Thunder</b> is the sound caused by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>. Depending on the distance and nature of the lightning, thunder can range from a sharp, loud crack to a long, low rumble (brontide). The sudden increase in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> from lightning produces rapid <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">expansion of the air</a> surrounding and within a bolt of lightning. In turn, this expansion of air creates a sonic shock wave, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a>, which produces the sound of thunder, often referred to as a <i>clap</i>, <i>crack</i>, <i>peal of thunder</i>, or <i>boom</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cause of thunder has been the subject of centuries of speculation and scientific <a href="/wiki/Inquiry" title="Inquiry">inquiry</a>. The first recorded theory is attributed to the Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> in the fourth century BC, and an early speculation was that it was caused by the collision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds" title="Clouds">clouds</a>. Subsequently, numerous other theories were proposed. By the mid-19th century, the accepted theory was that lightning produced a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>.</p>
<p>In the 20th century a consensus evolved that thunder must begin with a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> in the air due to the sudden thermal expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> in the lightning channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007-1">[1]</a></sup> The temperature inside the lightning channel, measured by <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectral analysis</a>, varies during its 50 <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">μs</a> existence, rising sharply from an initial temperature of about 20,000 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> to about 30,000 K, then dropping away gradually to about 10,000 K. The average is about 20,400 K (20,100 °C; 36,300 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This heating causes a rapid outward expansion, impacting the surrounding cooler air at a speed faster than sound would otherwise travel. The resultant outward-moving pulse is a shock wave,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">[3]</a></sup> similar in principle to the shock wave formed by an <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosion</a>, or at the front of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>.</p>
<p>Experimental studies of simulated lightning have produced results largely consistent with this model, though there is continued debate about the precise physical mechanisms of the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007-1">[1]</a></sup> Other causes have also been proposed, relying on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamic" title="Electrodynamic">electrodynamic</a> effects of the massive current acting on the plasma in the bolt of lightning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The shockwave in thunder is sufficient to cause injury, such as internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contusion" title="Contusion">contusion</a>, to individuals nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Inversion thunder results when lightning strikes between cloud and ground occur during a temperature inversion. In such an inversion, the air near the ground is cooler than the higher air. The sound energy is prevented from dispersing vertically as it would in a non-inversion and is thus concentrated in the near-ground layer. Inversions often occur when warm moist air passes above a cold front; the resulting thunder sound is significantly louder than it would be if heard at the same distance in a non-inversion condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>d</i> in Modern English <i>thunder</i> (from earlier <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>þunor</i>) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epenthetic" title="Epenthetic">epenthetic</a>, and is now found as well in Modern Dutch <i>donder</i> (cp <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> <i>donre</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Eorr" title="Þorr">þorr</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Frisian" title="Old Frisian">Old Frisian</a> <i>þuner</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>donar</i> descended from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>þunraz</i>). In <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> the term was <i>tonare</i> "to thunder". The name of the Germanic god <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a> comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> word for thunder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shared <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_root" title="Proto-Indo-European root">root</a> is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:List_of_Proto-Indo-European_roots#t" title="wiktionary:Appendix:List of Proto-Indo-European roots">*<i>tón-r̥</i></a> or <i>*<span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language">tar-</span></i>, also found Gaulish <i><a href="/wiki/Taranis" title="Taranis">Taranis</a></i> and Hittite <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarhunt" title="Tarhunt">Tarhunt</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_distance">Calculating distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Calculating distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A flash of lightning, followed after some time by a rumble of thunder, illustrates the fact that <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> travels significantly slower than <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. Using this difference, one can estimate how far away the bolt of lightning is by timing the interval between seeing the flash and hearing thunder. The <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> in dry air is approximately 343 m/s or 1,127 ft/s or 768 mph (1,236 km/h) at 20 °C (68 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This translates to approximately 5 seconds per mile (or 3 seconds per kilometer).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> is high enough that it can be taken as <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinite</a> in this calculation because of the relatively small distance involved. Therefore, the lightning is approximately one kilometer distant for every 3 seconds that elapse between the visible flash and the first sound of thunder (or one mile for every 5 seconds). In the same five seconds, the light could have traveled the Lunar distance four times. (In this calculation, the initial shock wave, which travels at a rate faster than the speed of sound, but only extends outward for the first 30 feet, is ignored.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Thunder is seldom heard at distances over 20 kilometers (12 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">[3]</a></sup> A very bright flash of lightning and an almost simultaneous sharp "crack" of thunder, a <i>thundercrack</i>, therefore indicates that the lightning strike was very near.</p>
