<p><b>Sand</b> is a naturally occurring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granular" title="Granular">granular</a> material composed of finely divided <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> particles. It is defined by size, being finer than <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> and coarser than <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>. Sand can also refer to a <a href="/wiki/Soil_texture" title="Soil texture">textural class</a> of soil or soil type; i.e., a soil containing more than 85 percent sand-sized particles by mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The composition of sand varies, depending on the local rock sources and conditions, but the most common constituent of sand in inland continental settings and non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal" title="Coastal">coastal</a> settings is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> (silicon dioxide, or SiO<sub>2</sub>), usually in the form of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>. The second most common type of sand is <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, for example, <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>, which has mostly been created, over the past half billion years, by various forms of life, like <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>. For example, it is the primary form of sand apparent in areas where reefs have dominated the ecosystem for millions of years like the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.</p>
<p>Sand is a non-renewable resource over human timescales, and sand suitable for making concrete is in high demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The exact definition of sand varies. The scientific <a href="/wiki/Unified_Soil_Classification_System" title="Unified Soil Classification System">Unified Soil Classification System</a> used in engineering and geology corresponds to US Standard Sieves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and defines sand as particles with a diameter of between 0.074 and 4.75 millimeters. By another definition, in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">particle size</a> as used by <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a>, sand particles range in diameter from 0.0625 mm (or <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>16</sub></span> mm) to 2 mm. An individual particle in this range size is termed a <i>sand grain</i>. Sand grains are between <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> (with particles ranging from 2 mm up to 64 mm by the latter system, and from 4.75 mm up to 75 mm in the former) and <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> (particles smaller than 0.0625 mm down to 0.004 mm). The size specification between sand and gravel has remained constant for more than a century, but particle diameters as small as 0.02 mm were considered sand under the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Atterberg" title="Albert Atterberg">Albert Atterberg</a> standard in use during the early 20th century. A 1953 engineering standard published by the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_State_Highway_and_Transportation_Officials" title="American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials">American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials</a> set the minimum sand size at 0.074 mm. A 1938 specification of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> was 0.05 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Sand feels gritty when rubbed between the fingers. <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">Silt</a>, by comparison, feels like <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> 14688 grades sands as fine, medium, and coarse with ranges 0.063 mm to 0.2 mm to 0.63 mm to 2.0 mm. In the United States, sand is commonly divided into five sub-categories based on size: very fine sand (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>16</sub></span> – <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> mm diameter), fine sand (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> mm – <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> mm), medium sand (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> mm – <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> mm), coarse sand (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> mm – 1 mm), and very coarse sand (1 mm – 2 mm). These sizes are based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">Krumbein phi scale</a>, where size in Φ = -log<sub>2</sub>D; D being the particle size in mm. On this scale, for sand the value of Φ varies from −1 to +4, with the divisions between sub-categories at whole numbers.</p>

<p>The most common constituent of sand, in inland continental settings and non-tropical coastal settings, is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> (silicon dioxide, or SiO<sub>2</sub>), usually in the form of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, which, because of its chemical inertness and considerable hardness, is the most common <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> resistant to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>.</p>
<p>The composition of mineral sand is highly variable, depending on the local rock sources and conditions. The bright <a href="/wiki/Coral_sand" title="Coral sand">white sands</a> found in tropical and subtropical coastal settings are eroded <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and may contain <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">shell</a> fragments in addition to other <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organic</a> or organically derived fragmental material, suggesting sand formation depends on living organisms, too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> sand dunes of the <a href="/wiki/White_Sands_National_Monument" title="White Sands National Monument">White Sands National Monument</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> are famous for their bright, white color. <a href="/wiki/Arkose" title="Arkose">Arkose</a> is a sand or <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> with considerable <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> content, derived from <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> of a (usually nearby) <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granitic</a> rock outcrop. Some sands contain <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorite_group" title="Chlorite group">chlorite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glauconite" title="Glauconite">glauconite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>. Sands rich in <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> are dark to black in color, as are sands derived from volcanic <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chlorite_group" title="Chlorite group">Chlorite</a>-<a href="/wiki/Glauconite" title="Glauconite">glauconite</a> bearing sands are typically green in color, as are sands derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basaltic" title="Basaltic">basaltic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>) with a high <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> content. Many sands, especially those found extensively in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a>, have <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> impurities within the quartz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystals" title="Crystals">crystals</a> of the sand, giving a deep yellow color. Sand deposits in some areas contain <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnets</a> and other resistant minerals, including some small <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstones</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Study">Study</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The study of individual grains can reveal much historical information as to the origin and kind of transport of the grain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quartz_sand" title="Quartz sand">Quartz sand</a> that is recently weathered from <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gneiss" title="Gneiss">gneiss</a> quartz crystals will be angular. It is called <a href="/wiki/Grus_(geology)" title="Grus (geology)">grus</a> in geology or <i>sharp sand</i> in the building trade where it is preferred for concrete, and in gardening where it is used as a soil amendment to loosen clay soils. Sand that is transported long distances by water or wind will be rounded, with characteristic abrasion patterns on the grain surface. Desert sand is typically rounded.</p>
<p>People who collect sand as a hobby are known as <a href="/wiki/Arenophile" title="Arenophile">arenophiles</a>. Organisms that thrive in sandy environments are psammophiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sand_sorting_tower.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="944" data-file-width="854" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sand_sorting_tower.jpg/220px-Sand_sorting_tower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sand_sorting_tower.jpg/330px-Sand_sorting_tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sand_sorting_tower.jpg/440px-Sand_sorting_tower.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sand_sorting_tower.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sand sorting tower at a <a href="/wiki/Gravel_pit" title="Gravel pit">gravel pit</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a>: Sandy <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> are ideal for crops such as watermelons, peaches and peanuts, and their excellent drainage characteristics make them suitable for intensive dairy farming.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">Aquaria</a>: Sand makes a low cost aquarium base material which some believe is better than gravel for home use. It is also a necessity for saltwater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reef_tank" title="Reef tank">reef tanks</a>, which emulate environments composed largely of <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> sand broken down from <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_reefs" title="Artificial reefs">Artificial reefs</a>: <a href="/wiki/Geotextile" title="Geotextile">Geotextile</a> bagged sand can serve as the foundation for new reefs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">Artificial islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beach_nourishment" title="Beach nourishment">Beach nourishment</a>: Governments move sand to <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a> where tides, storms or deliberate changes to the shoreline erode the original sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">Brick</a>: Manufacturing plants add sand to a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> and other materials for manufacturing bricks.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cob_(material)" title="Cob (material)">Cob</a>: Coarse sand makes up as much as 75% of cob.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">Concrete</a>: Sand is often a principal component of this critical construction material.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">Glass</a>: Sand rich in silica is the principal component in common glasses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">Hydraulic fracturing</a>: A drilling technique for natural gas, which uses rounded silica sand as a "proppant", a material to hold open cracks that are caused by the hydraulic fracturing process.</li>
<li>Landscaping: Sand makes small hills and slopes (golf courses would be an example).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">Mortar</a>: Sand is mixed with masonry cement or Portland cement and lime to be used in masonry construction.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">Paint</a>: Mixing sand with paint produces a <a href="/wiki/Texture_(crystalline)" title="Texture (crystalline)">textured finish</a> for walls and ceilings or non-slip floor surfaces.</li>
<li>Railroads: <a href="/wiki/Railroad_engineer" title="Railroad engineer">Engine drivers and rail transit operators</a> use sand to improve the traction of wheels on the rails.</li>
<li>Recreation. Playing with sand is a favorite beach time activity. One of the most beloved uses of sand is to make sometimes intricate, sometimes simple structures known as sand castles. Such structures are well known for their impermanence. Sand is also used in children's play. Special play areas enclosing a significant area of sand, known as sandboxes, are common on many public playgrounds, and even at some single family homes.</li>
<li>Roads: Sand improves traction (and thus traffic safety) in icy or snowy conditions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sand_animation" title="Sand animation">Sand animation</a>: <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">Performance artists</a> draw images in sand. Makers of animated films use the same term to describe their use of sand on frontlit or backlit glass.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sand_casting" title="Sand casting">Sand casting</a>: Casters moisten or oil <a href="/wiki/Molding_sand" title="Molding sand">molding sand</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a> sand and then shape it into molds into which they pour molten material. This type of sand must be able to withstand high temperatures and pressure, allow gases to escape, have a uniform, small grain size and be non-reactive with metals.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_castle" title="Sand castle">Sand castles</a>: Shaping sand into castles or other miniature buildings is a popular beach activity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sandbag" title="Sandbag">Sandbags</a>: These protect against floods and gunfire. The inexpensive bags are easy to transport when empty, and unskilled volunteers can quickly fill them with local sand in emergencies.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandblasting" title="Sandblasting">Sandblasting</a>: Graded sand serves as an <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasive</a> in cleaning, preparing, and polishing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Early_thermal_weapons" title="Early thermal weapons">Thermal weapon</a>: While not in widespread use anymore, sand used to be heated and poured on invading troops in the classical and medieval time periods.</li>
<li>Water filtration: <a href="/wiki/Media_filter" title="Media filter">Media filters</a> use sand for filtering water.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wu%E1%B8%8D%C5%AB%CA%BE" title="Wuḍūʾ">Wuḍūʾ</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> procedure for washing parts of the body.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoanthid" title="Zoanthid">Zoanthid</a> "skeletons": Animals in this order of marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cnidaria" title="Cnidaria">cnidarians</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_anemone" title="Sea anemone">sea anemones</a>, incorporate sand into their <a href="/wiki/Mesoglea" title="Mesoglea">mesoglea</a> for structural strength, which they need because they lack a true skeleton.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resources_and_environmental_concerns">Resources and environmental concerns</span></h2>
<p>Only some sands are suitable for the construction industry, for example for making <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>. Because of the growth of population and of cities and the consequent construction activity there is a huge demand for these special kinds of sand, and natural sources are running low. In 2012 French director <a href="/wiki/Denis_Delestrac" title="Denis Delestrac">Denis Delestrac</a> made a documentary called "Sand Wars" about the impact of the lack of construction sand. It shows the ecological and economic effects of both legal and illegal trade in construction sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sand's many uses require a significant <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredging</a> industry, raising environmental concerns over fish depletion, landslides, and flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Countries such as China, Indonesia, Malaysia and Cambodia ban sand exports, citing these issues as a major factor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It is estimated that the annual consumption of sand and gravel is 40 billion tons and sand is a US$70 billion global industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span></h2>
<p>While sand is generally non-toxic, sand-using activities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandblasting" title="Sandblasting">sandblasting</a> require precautions. Bags of silica sand used for sandblasting now carry labels warning the user to wear respiratory protection to avoid breathing the resulting fine silica <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>. <a href="/wiki/Safety_data_sheet" title="Safety data sheet">Safety data sheets</a> for silica sand state that "excessive inhalation of crystalline silica is a serious health concern".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In areas of high <a href="/wiki/Pore_water_pressure" title="Pore water pressure">pore water pressure</a>, sand and salt water can form <a href="/wiki/Quicksand" title="Quicksand">quicksand</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogel" title="Hydrogel">hydrogel</a> that behaves like a liquid. Quicksand produces a considerable barrier to escape for creatures caught within, who often die from exposure (not from submersion) as a result.</p>
