Continental-continental divergent/constructive boundary</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, a <b>divergent boundary</b> or <b>divergent plate boundary</b> (also known as a <b>constructive boundary</b> or an <b>extensional boundary</b>) is a linear feature that exists between two <a href="/wiki/List_of_tectonic_plates" title="List of tectonic plates">tectonic plates</a> that are moving away from each other. Divergent boundaries within <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> initially produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rift_(geology)" title="Rift (geology)">rifts</a> which eventually become <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valleys</a>. Most active divergent plate boundaries occur between <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic plates</a> and exist as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-oceanic_ridge" title="Mid-oceanic ridge">mid-oceanic ridges</a>. Divergent boundaries also form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_islands" title="Volcanic islands">volcanic islands</a> which occur when the plates move apart to produce gaps which molten lava rises to fill.</p>
<p>Current research indicates that complex <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> allows material to rise to the base of the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> beneath each divergent plate boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This supplies the area with vast amounts of heat and a reduction in pressure that melts <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> (or upper mantle) beneath the rift area forming large <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalt</a> or lava flows. Each eruption occurs in only a part of the plate boundary at any one time, but when it does occur, it fills in the opening gap as the two opposing plates move away from each other.</p>
<p>Over millions of years, tectonic plates may move many hundreds of kilometers away from both sides of a divergent plate boundary. Because of this, rocks closest to a boundary are younger than rocks further away on the same plate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Divergent_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg/220px-Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg/330px-Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg/440px-Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bridge_across_continents_iceland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bridge across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%81lfagj%C3%A1" title="Álfagjá">Álfagjá</a> <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> in southwest <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, that is part of the boundary between the Eurasian and North American continental tectonic plates.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At divergent boundaries, two plates move apart from each other and the space that this creates is filled with new crustal material sourced from molten <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> that forms below. The origin of new divergent boundaries at <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction" title="Triple junction">triple junctions</a> is sometimes thought to be associated with the phenomenon known as <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a>. Here, exceedingly large convective cells bring very large quantities of hot asthenospheric material near the surface and the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> is thought to be sufficient to break apart the lithosphere. The hot spot which may have initiated the Mid-Atlantic Ridge system currently underlies Iceland which is widening at a rate of a few centimeters per year.</p>
<p>Divergent boundaries are typified in the oceanic lithosphere by the rifts of the oceanic ridge system, including the Mid-Atlantic Ridge and the <a href="/wiki/East_Pacific_Rise" title="East Pacific Rise">East Pacific Rise</a>, and in the continental lithosphere by rift valleys such as the famous East African <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">Great Rift Valley</a>. Divergent boundaries can create massive fault zones in the oceanic ridge system. Spreading is generally not uniform, so where spreading rates of adjacent ridge blocks are different, massive transform faults occur. These are the <a href="/wiki/Fracture_zone" title="Fracture zone">fracture zones</a>, many bearing names, that are a major source of <a href="/wiki/Submarine_earthquake" title="Submarine earthquake">submarine earthquakes</a>. A sea floor map will show a rather strange pattern of blocky structures that are separated by <a href="/wiki/File:World_Distribution_of_Mid-Oceanic_Ridges.gif" title="File:World Distribution of Mid-Oceanic Ridges.gif">linear features</a> perpendicular to the ridge axis. If one views the sea floor between the fracture zones as conveyor belts carrying the ridge on each side of the rift away from the spreading center the action becomes clear. Crest depths of the old ridges, parallel to the current spreading center, will be older and deeper... (from thermal contraction and <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is at mid-ocean ridges that one of the key pieces of evidence forcing acceptance of the seafloor spreading hypothesis was found. Airborne <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">geomagnetic</a> surveys showed a strange pattern of symmetrical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_reversal" title="Magnetic reversal">magnetic reversals</a> on opposite sides of ridge centers. The pattern was far too regular to be coincidental as the widths of the opposing bands were too closely matched. Scientists had been studying <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">polar reversals</a> and the link was made by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Morley" title="Lawrence Morley">Lawrence W. Morley</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_Vine" title="Fred Vine">Frederick John Vine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drummond_Matthews" title="Drummond Matthews">Drummond Hoyle Matthews</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morley%E2%80%93Vine%E2%80%93Matthews_hypothesis" title="Morley–Vine–Matthews hypothesis">Morley–Vine–Matthews hypothesis</a>. The magnetic banding directly corresponds with the Earth's polar reversals. This was confirmed by measuring the ages of the rocks within each band. The banding furnishes a map in time and space of both spreading rate and polar reversals.</p>
