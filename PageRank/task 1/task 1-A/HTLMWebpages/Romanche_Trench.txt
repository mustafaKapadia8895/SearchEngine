<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Romanche Trench</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Romanche Trench</b>, also called the <i>Romanche Furrow</i> or <i>Romanche Gap</i>, is the third deepest of the major <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">trenches</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Sandwich_Trench" title="South Sandwich Trench">South Sandwich Trench</a>. It bisects the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> (MAR) just north of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> at the narrowest part of the Atlantic between Brazil and West Africa, extending from 2°N to 2°S and from 16°W to 20°W. The trench has been formed by the actions of the <b>Romanche Fracture Zone</b>, a portion of which is an active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transform_boundary" title="Transform boundary">transform boundary</a> offsetting sections of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlitzer-etal-_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schlitzer-etal--1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanography">Oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanche_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The trench has a depth of 7,761 m (25,463 ft), is 300 km (190 mi) long and has an average width of 19 km (12 mi) and allows for a major circulation of deep ocean basin water from the west Atlantic to the east Atlantic basins. Deep water flow through the trench is from west to east with a rate of 3.6 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sverdrups</a> (million m³/s) of 1.57 °C (34.83 °F) water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlitzer-etal-_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schlitzer-etal--1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lower <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a> (LNADW) is found around 3,600–4,000 metres (11,800–13,100 ft) below sea level and flows from the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Sea" title="Greenland Sea">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Seas</a>; it brings high salinity, oxygen, and freon concentrations towards the equator. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_Bottom_Water" title="Antarctic Bottom Water">Antarctic Bottom Water</a> (AABW) flows below the LNADW and reaches down to the seafloor. Formed around Antarctica, the AABW is cold, has low salinity, and high silicate concentration. As it flows north, it is constrained by numerous obstacles on the seafloor. In the basin on the eastern side of the MAR the <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Ridge" title="Walvis Ridge">Walvis Ridge</a> blocks the northward passage. For LNADW and AABW, the Romanche and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_Fracture_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chain Fracture Zone (page does not exist)">Chain Fracture Zones</a> (just south of the equator) are the only deep passages in the MAR where interbasin exchange is possible. As AABW flows through the Romanche Fracture Zone, salinity and temperature increase significantly. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanche_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Romanche Fracture Zone offsets the Mid-Atlantic ridge by 900 km (560 mi), making it the largest equatorial fracture zone in the Atlantic. According to the normal scenario for the opening of the South Atlantic, it is spreading at a rate of 1.75 cm/year (0.69 in/year) and began forming about <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=50">50</a> Ma</span>. North of and parallel to the fracture zone is a transverse ridge which is particularly prominent over hundreds of kilometres east and west of the MAB of the South Atlantic. The western part of the transverse ridge consists of fragments of uplifted oceanic crust and upper mantle. The summit of the transverse ridge is capped by Miocene shallow water limestones that reached above sea level 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaannum" title="Megaannum">Ma</a> before subsiding abnormally fast. The eastern part of the transverse ridge, however, consists of a thick sequence of stratified material called the Romanche Sedimentary Sequence (RSS). The RSS includes pelagic material from the early Cretaceous (140 Ma) which, coupled with the thickness of the sequence, does not fit with the normal scenario for the opening of the South Atlantic around the <a href="/wiki/Aptian" title="Aptian">Aptian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Albian" title="Albian">Albian</a> (125–100 Ma).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The transverse ridge separates the present trench from an 800 km (500 mi)-long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aseismic" title="Aseismic">aseismic</a> valley where the Romanche transform was located until about 10–8 Ma. This transform migration was preceded by a process 25–17 Ma during which the Miocene shallow-water platform mentioned above reached sea level as the transverse ridge was first <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">uplifted</a>, then deformed, and finally buried under the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanche_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a> of the MAR support many life forms. The Romanche and Chain Fracture Zones creates a huge gap in the MAR and can act as a "Subsea Berlin Wall" segregating the North Atlantic communities from those in other oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Swarms of hydrothermal shrimps reminiscent of those found from northern MAR vent-sites have been found on the northern part of the southern MAR. Bivalve communities have been reported around vents further south. These species await a formal description and it is not known whether or not they represent communities distinct from those on the northern MAR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The flow of NADW through the Romanche and Chain Fracture Zones may serve as a conduit for larval transport from the western North Atlantic to the eastern South Atlantic. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Caridea" title="Caridea">caridean</a> shrimp <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alvinocaris_muricola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alvinocaris muricola (page does not exist)">Alvinocaris muricola</a></i> inhabits cold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seep" title="Seep">seeps</a> in both the Gulf of Mexico and the Gulf of Guinea, which clearly suggests a gene flow across the Atlantic, but the amount of data is very limited and dispersal pathways are not fully understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
