<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Iapetus Ocean</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Positions_of_ancient_continents,_550_million_years_ago.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="820" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg/300px-Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg/450px-Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg/600px-Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Positions_of_ancient_continents,_550_million_years_ago.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reconstruction of how the Iapetus Ocean and surrounding continents might have been arranged during the late <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a> <a href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">period</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Iapetus Ocean</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was an <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> that existed in the late <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> <a href="/wiki/Era" title="Era">eras</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_timescale" title="Geologic timescale">geologic timescale</a> (between 600 and 400 million years ago). The Iapetus Ocean was situated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a>, between the paleocontinents of <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a>. The ocean disappeared with the <a href="/wiki/Acadian_orogeny" title="Acadian orogeny">Acadian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Caledonian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taconic_orogeny" title="Taconic orogeny">Taconic</a> <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenies</a>, when these three continents joined to form one big landmass called <a href="/wiki/Euramerica" title="Euramerica">Euramerica</a>. The "southern" Iapetus Ocean has been proposed to have closed with the <a href="/wiki/Famatinian_orogeny" title="Famatinian orogeny">Famatinian</a> and Taconic orogenies, meaning a collision between <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Western Gondwana</a> and Laurentia.</p>
<p>Because the Iapetus Ocean was positioned between continental masses that would at a much later time roughly form the opposite shores of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, it can be seen as a sort of precursor of the Atlantic. The Iapetus Ocean was therefore named for the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">titan</a> <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(mythology)" title="Iapetus (mythology)">Iapetus</a>, who in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> was the father of <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">Atlas</a>, after whom the Atlantic Ocean was named.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[A]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_history">Research history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iapetus_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Research history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg/300px-Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg/450px-Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg/600px-Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geological_fault_at_Niarbyl_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107854.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Geological fault at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niarbyl" title="Niarbyl">Niarbyl</a>. The narrow white diagonal line near centre of picture is the only known remaining visible indication of the Iapetus Ocean</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the start of the 20th century, American paleontologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Doolittle_Walcott" title="Charles Doolittle Walcott">Charles Walcott</a> noticed differences in early Paleozoic <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic</a> <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">trilobites</a> of <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Olenellidae" title="Olenellidae">Olenellidae</a>, the so-called "Pacific fauna"), as found in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> and those of Baltica (such as <a href="/wiki/Paradoxididae" title="Paradoxididae">Paradoxididae</a>, often called the "Atlantic fauna"), as found in the southern parts of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and eastern Newfoundland. Geologists of the early 20th century presumed that a large trough, a so-called <a href="/wiki/Geosyncline" title="Geosyncline">geosyncline</a>, had existed between Scotland and England in the early Paleozoic, keeping both sides separated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalziel-1997_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalziel-1997-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the development of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> in the 1960s, geologists such as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Holmes" title="Arthur Holmes">Arthur Holmes</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Tuzo_Wilson" title="John Tuzo Wilson">John Tuzo Wilson</a> concluded that the Atlantic Ocean must have had a precursor before the time of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. Wilson also noticed that the Atlantic had opened at roughly the same place where its precursor ocean had closed. This led him to his <a href="/wiki/Wilson_cycle" title="Wilson cycle">Wilson cycle</a> hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalziel-1997_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dalziel-1997-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geodynamic_history">Geodynamic history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iapetus_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geodynamic history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neoproterozoic_origin">Neoproterozoic origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iapetus_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neoproterozoic origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many spots in Scandinavia <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dikes</a> are found with ages between 670 and 650 million years. These are interpreted as evidence that by that time, <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rifting</a> had started that would form the Iapetus Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torsvik-etal-1996_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Torsvik-etal-1996-5">[4]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Long_Range_dikes" title="Long Range dikes">Long Range dikes</a> are also thought to have formed during the formation of the Iapetus Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> It has been proposed that both the <a href="/wiki/Fen_Complex" title="Fen Complex">Fen Complex</a> in Norway and the <a href="/wiki/Aln%C3%B6_Complex" title="Alnö Complex">Alnö Complex</a> in Sweden formed as consequence to mild <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">extensional tectonics</a> in the ancient continent of <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a> that followed the opening of the Iapetus Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The southern Iapetus Ocean opened between Laurentia and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> (now South America) about 550 Ma in the <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a>–<a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> transition. At the time it did so the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adamastor_Ocean" title="Adamastor Ocean">Adamastor Ocean</a> further east <a href="/wiki/Suture_(geology)" title="Suture (geology)">closed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaucheretal2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaucheretal2010-8">[7]</a></sup> The opening of the Iapetus Ocean probably postdates the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Puncoviscana_Formation" title="Puncoviscana Formation">Puncoviscana Ocean</a> with the Iapetus Ocean being separated from the Puncoviscana Ocean by the ribbon-shaped <a href="/wiki/Arequipa-Antofalla" title="Arequipa-Antofalla">Arequipa-Antofalla</a> <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terrane</a>. However, the formation of both oceans seems unrelated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-escayolartal_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-escayolartal-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iapetus_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="574" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg/300px-Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg/450px-Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg/600px-Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iapetus_fossil_evidence_EN.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Position of the continents after the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Caledonian orogeny</a> (<a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> to <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> times). Differences in fossil faunas on both sides of the red line (the <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Suture" title="Iapetus Suture">Iapetus Suture</a>) are evidence for the existence of an ocean between the two sides in the time before the continents were joined in the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Southwest of the Iapetus, a volcanic <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arc</a> evolved from the early <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> (540 million years ago) onward. This volcanic arc was formed above a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">subduction zone</a> where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_lithosphere" title="Oceanic lithosphere">oceanic lithosphere</a> of the Iapetus Ocean subducted southward under other oceanic lithosphere. From Cambrian times (about 550 million years ago) the western Iapetus Ocean began to grow progressively narrower due to this subduction. The same happened further north and east, where Avalonia and Baltica began to move towards Laurentia from the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> (488-444 million years ago) onward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torsvik-etal-1996_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Torsvik-etal-1996-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">Trilobite</a> <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">faunas</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelves</a> of Baltica and Laurentia are still very different in the Ordovician, but <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> faunas show progressive mixing of species from both sides, because the continents moved closer together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the west, the Iapetus Ocean closed with the <a href="/wiki/Taconic_orogeny" title="Taconic orogeny">Taconic orogeny</a> (480-430 million years ago), when the volcanic island arc <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">collided</a> with Laurentia. Some authors consider the oceanic basin south of the island arc also a part of the Iapetus, this branch closed during the later <a href="/wiki/Acadian_orogeny" title="Acadian orogeny">Acadian orogeny</a>, when Avalonia collided with Laurentia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It has been suggested that the southern Iapetus Ocean closed during a <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">continental collision</a> between <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Western Gondwana</a> (South America). If factual the <a href="/wiki/Taconic_orogeny" title="Taconic orogeny">Taconic orogen</a> would be the northward continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Famatinian_orogeny" title="Famatinian orogeny">Famatinian orogen</a> exposed in Argentina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[B]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, the eastern parts had closed too: the <a href="/wiki/Tornquist_Sea" title="Tornquist Sea">Tornquist Sea</a> between Avalonia and Baltica already during the late Ordovician,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> the main branch between Baltica-Avalonia and Laurentia during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grampian_phase" title="Grampian phase">Grampian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandian_phase" title="Scandian phase">Scandian phases</a> of the Caledonian orogeny (440-420 million years ago).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At the end of the Silurian period (c. 420 million years ago) the Iapetus Ocean had completely disappeared and the combined mass of the three continents formed the "new" continent of <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup> which would itself be the northern component of the singular supercontinent of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
