<p>The <b>1966 Atlantic hurricane season</b> featured the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> with the longest track in the Atlantic basin – <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faith" title="Hurricane Faith">Hurricane Faith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Also during the year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">Weather Office</a> was re-designated the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>. The season officially began on June 1, and lasted until November 30. These dates conventionally delimit the period of each year when most tropical cyclones form in the Atlantic basin. It was a near average season in terms of tropical storms, with a total of 11 <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storms</a>. The first system, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alma_(1966)" title="Hurricane Alma (1966)">Hurricane Alma</a>, developed over eastern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on June 4. Alma brought severe flooding to <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> and later to <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, after crossing the western <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. The storm also brought relatively minor impact to the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a>. Alma caused 91 deaths and about $210.1 million (1966 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> in damage.</p>
<p>Hurricanes Becky, Celia, and Dorothy, and Tropical Storm Ella all resulted in minimal or no impact on land. The next system, Hurricane Faith, developed near Cape Verde on August 21. It tracked westward across the Atlantic Ocean until north of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. After paralleling the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>, Faith moved northeastward across the open Atlantic and later became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> near <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> on September 6. Overall, Faith traveled about 6,850 mi (11,020 km) across the Atlantic. Although it never made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>, the storm generated rough seas that resulted in five deaths. The two next tropical storms – Greta and Hallie – caused negligible impact.</p>
<p>The strongest tropical cyclone of the season was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Inez" title="Hurricane Inez">Hurricane Inez</a>, a powerful Category 4 hurricane that devastated a large majority of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>, and parts of Mexico. Throughout its path, the storm caused about $226.5 million in damage and more than 1,000 deaths. Tropical Storm Judith left only minor impacts in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>. The final system, Hurricane Lois, developed east of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on November 4. Later in its duration, Lois passed west of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, bringing gale-force winds to <a href="/wiki/Corvo_Island" title="Corvo Island">Corvo Island</a>. The storm became extratropical northeast of the islands on November 11. A possible tropical cyclone in June and July and another in July brought minor damage to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, respectively. Overall, the storms of this season collectively caused at least 1,096 fatalities and about $436.6 million in damage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_summary">Season summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Season summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<center>

</center>
<p>The Atlantic hurricane season officially began on June 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seasondates_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-seasondates-3">[2]</a></sup> During the year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">Weather Office</a> was re-designated the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> It was a near average season in which eleven tropical storms formed, compared with the 1966–2009 average of 11.3 named storms. Seven of these reached hurricane status, slightly above of the 1966–2009 average of 6.2. Furthermore, three storms reached major hurricane status,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[nb 2]</a></sup> with the 1950-2000 mean being 2.3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Three hurricanes and one tropical storm made landfall during the season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> causing at least 1,096 deaths and $436.6 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> Hurricane Faith also caused fatalities, despite remaining well offshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> The season officially ended on November 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seasondates_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-seasondates-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first storm, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alma_(1966)" title="Hurricane Alma (1966)">Hurricane Alma</a>, developed over eastern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on June 4. Alma crossed the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and struck <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. The storm made another landfall in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> as a hurricane on June 9. This marked the earliest United States hurricane landfall since a hurricane in May and June of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1820-1829_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons#1825_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1820-1829 Atlantic hurricane seasons">1825</a>. Alma continued northeastward across the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a> until becoming extratropical offshore <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> on June 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Later that month, another tropical depression developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> The month of July was highly active, with four named storms – Becky, Celia, Dorothy, and Ella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Additionally, a tropical depression developed in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> then halted for more than three weeks, until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faith" title="Hurricane Faith">Hurricane Faith</a> developed on August 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> On average, three or four named storms form in August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Four tropical cyclones developed in September, including tropical storms Greta, Hallie and Judith, as well as Hurricane Inez. Peaking as a strong Category 4 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a> with winds of 150 mph (240 km/h), Inez was the strongest tropical cyclone of the season. Although Inez persisted into October, no other system developed that month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Two named storms usually form in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> The final tropical cyclone, Hurricane Lois, existed from November 4 to November 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season's activity was reflected with an <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 145.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACE_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-13">[11]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. It is only calculated for full advisories on tropical systems at or exceeding 39 mph (63 km/h), which is tropical storm strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Alma">Hurricane Alma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hurricane Alma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alma_(1966)" title="Hurricane Alma (1966)">Hurricane Alma (1966)</a></div>
<p>In early June, a dissipating <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> extended southward into the western Caribbean Sea. A surface <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">circulation</a> formed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> and thus, a tropical depression developed over eastern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on June 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> While moving through <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, it dropped heavy rainfall that killed at least 73 people in the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael,_Lempira" title="San Rafael, Lempira">San Rafael</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Offshore northern Honduras, the system produced heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Swan_Islands,_Honduras" title="Swan Islands, Honduras">Swan Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami-15">[13]</a></sup> The depression moved northeastward and intensified into Tropical Storm Alma on June 6, and a hurricane six hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Alma crossed western Cuba, causing heavy crop damage and water shortages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-16">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mn_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-mn-17">[15]</a></sup> Over 1,000 houses were destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-spok-18">[16]</a></sup> and damage was estimated around $200 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> The storm killed 12 people in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-spok-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After crossing Cuba, Alma intensified further to reach winds of 125 mph (205 km/h) in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> The hurricane passed west of <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West, Florida</a>, causing a power outage and flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-spt-19">[17]</a></sup> Alma dropped heavy rainfall and produced winds across most of Florida, which damaged crops and caused scattered power outages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-day-20">[18]</a></sup> The hurricane weakened before moving ashore near <a href="/wiki/Apalachee_Bay" title="Apalachee Bay">Apalachee Bay</a> on June 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> This was the earliest date of landfall in the United States since 1825. Damage in Florida was estimated at $10 million, and there were six deaths in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Alma crossed southeastern <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> as a tropical storm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> damaging a few houses and causing light damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-tol-21">[19]</a></sup> The storm re-intensified into a hurricane over the western Atlantic Ocean, and its outer rainbands dropped heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Alma encountered colder water temperatures and transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> on June 13. Its remnants dissipated a day later over Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical depression formed 300 mi (485 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on July 1 at 18:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> as confirmed by ESSA 2 satellite. The depression intensified while heading northeastward under an upper-level trough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> At 06:00 UTC on July 2, the system became Tropical Storm Becky and reached hurricane status only six hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-13"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Around that time, Becky attained its peak intensity with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 75 mph (120 km/h) and a minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 985 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.1 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-14"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> After coming under the influence of a <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold low</a>, Becky turned to the northwest toward <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> on July 3. Becky encountered cooler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a>, and became extratropical near <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> developed into a tropical depression at 12:00 UTC on July 13, while located about 200 mi (320 km) northeast of the Leeward Islands. While moving northwestward, a <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> flight observed tropical storm force winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Thus, the depression was upgraded to Tropical Storm Celia at 00:00 UTC on July 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-15"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Curving westward, Celia weakened to a tropical depression around midday on July 15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-16"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> and was operationally believed to have degenerated into a remnant low pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Between July 17 and July 19, the storm moved across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, before turning northeastward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-17"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>By July 20, the system re-intensified into a tropical storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-18"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Several hours later, a reconnaissance aircraft observed sustained winds of 80 mph (130 km/h). As a result, Celia was upgraded to a hurricane at 18:00 UTC on July 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Early the next day, the storm attained its minimum barometric pressure of 995 mbar (29.4 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-19"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Celia accelerated northeast in advance of a frontal trough and began losing tropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Around the time of landfall in eastern Nova Scotia at 18:00 UTC on July 21, the storm became extratropical. The remnants weakened and struck <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> before dissipating the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-20"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Only light rainfall was observed in Atlantic Canada and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In late July, a low-level disturbance situated over the central Atlantic Ocean encountered a vigorous <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">shortwave trough</a> and developed into a surface low pressure area on July 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> At 18:00 UTC, a tropical depression formed about 800 mi (1,300 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores)" title="Flores Island (Azores)">Flores Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-21"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Initially, the depression had extratropical features and lacked tropical characteristics, such as a warm core and a well-developed <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a>. A weather ship in the area indicated an influx of <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinity</a>, suggesting that Dorothy derived its energy through non-tropical processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dorothy_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-dorothy-23">[21]</a></sup> Around 12:00 UTC on July 23, the depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Dorothy. Thereafter, it moved in quasi-stationary motion to the northwest and continued to intensify, reaching hurricane status late on July 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-22"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon reaching hurricane intensity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Dorothy possessed tropical characteristics, with evidence of a weak warm core beginning on July 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dorothy_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-dorothy-23">[21]</a></sup> The hurricane moved in a semi-circular path, and at 12:00 UTC the next day, Dorothy attained its minimum barometric pressure of 989 mbar (29.2 inHg). Later on July 26, the system curved north-northeastward. Further strengthening occurred and at 00:00 UTC on July 28, Dorothy attained its maximum sustained wind speed of 85 mph (140 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-23"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Moving across colder sea surface temperatures, the hurricane began weakening and fell to tropical storm status early on July 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Dorothy continued to weaken and became extratropical around 18:00 UTC the following day, while located about 610 mi (980 km) north-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Corvo_Island" title="Corvo Island">Corvo Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. The remnants continued northwestward and dissipated on July 31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-24"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Faith">Hurricane Faith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hurricane Faith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faith" title="Hurricane Faith">Hurricane Faith</a></div>
<p>An area of disturbed weather emerged into the Atlantic Ocean from the west coast of Africa in mid-August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-23"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> It developed into a tropical depression while located between Cape Verde and the west coast of Africa on August 21. Tracking westward, the depression intensified and became Tropical Storm Faith on the following day. Moving westward across the Atlantic Ocean, it continued to slowly strengthen, reaching hurricane status early on August 23. About 42 hours later, Faith reached an initial peak with winds of 105 mph (165 km/h), before weakening slightly on August 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-29"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Located near the Lesser Antilles, the outer bands of Faith produced gale-force winds in the region, especially <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-24"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Minor damage to boats and jetties occurred as far south as <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>By August 28, the storm began to re-intensify, after curving north-northwestward near The Bahamas. At 00:00 UTC on the following day, Faith peaked with winds of 125 mph (205 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 950 mbar (28 inHg). Eventually, the storm weakened back to a Category 2 hurricane and re-curved to the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-30"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> One person drowned in the western Atlantic after his ship sank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-25"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall and strong winds pelted Bermuda, though no damage occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> The storm maintained nearly the same intensity for several days, while tracking northeastward into the far North Atlantic Ocean. Faith weakened while north of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and became extratropical near the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> on September 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-31"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Three other drowning deaths occurred in the North Sea near <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-26"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> A fifth death occurred after a man succumbed to injuries sustained during a boating incident related to the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ej_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ej-26">[24]</a></sup> The remnants of Faith moved across <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> for the next several days. In <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, heavy rainfall from the storm caused record high glacier melting, resulting in "large" flooding in some areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> Crossing into <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the remnants of Faith remained identifiable until reaching <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Land" title="Franz Josef Land">Franz Josef Land</a> on September 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-27"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>A weather report from the <i>SS San Marcial</i> and Nimbus 2 satellite imagery showed that a circulation developed within a cloud mass to the east of the Lesser Antilles on September 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-28"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> As a result, a tropical depression formed about 745 mi (1,199 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> at 12:00 UTC. The depression moved west-northwestward and remained weak for a few days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-32"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Based on reconnaissance aircraft flight observing sustained winds of 58 mph (93 km/h), the depression was upgraded to Tropical Storm Greta at 18:00 UTC on September 4. Early the next day, Greta attained its peak intensity with maximum sustained winds of 60 mph (95 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 1,004 mbar (29.6 inHg). The storm began weakening on September 6 and fell to tropical depression intensity around midday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-33"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Late on September 7, Greta merged with a pre-<a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal</a> cloud mass between the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> and Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-29"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Satellite imagery from ESSA 2 indicated that a large area of disturbed weather began merging with a frontal band in the southwestern Gulf of Mexico. After an increase in convective activity and satellite imagery revealing a closed circulation on September 20, the system was classified as a tropical depression. By early on the following day, the depression was upgraded to Tropical Storm Hallie. After initially remaining stationary, Hallie eventually began a southwestward drift toward <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-30"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Around 12:00 UTC on September 21, the storm made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Nautla,_Veracruz" title="Nautla, Veracruz">Nautla, Veracruz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-34"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Due to cool, dry air, as well as land interaction with the mountainous terrain of Mexico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-31"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Hallie rapidly weakened inland and dissipated by 00:00 UTC on September 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-35"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> While passing near Nautla, winds of 40 mph (65 km/h) and heavy rainfall were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-32"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Inez" title="Hurricane Inez">Hurricane Inez</a></div>
<p>A tropical wave developed into a tropical depression well east of the Lesser Antilles on September 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-33"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> It moved slowly westward and strengthened into Tropical Storm Inez on September 24. The storm strengthened into a hurricane and was quickly intensifying when it struck the French overseas region of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on September 27. After entering the Caribbean, Inez briefly weakened before restrengthening, attaining peak sustained winds of 150 mph (240 km/h) on September 28. Continuing westward, Inez made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Barahona_Province" title="Barahona Province">Barahona Peninsula</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>. Inez then struck southwestern <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-36"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> where it was considered the worst hurricane since the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-lodi-28">[26]</a></sup> Inez weakened quickly over Hispaniola, although it reintensified into a major hurricane before striking southeastern Cuba on September 30. The hurricane moved slowly over Cuba for two days before emerging into the Atlantic Ocean near the Bahamas. Inez stalled and later resumed its previous westward path. Between October 4 and October 5, the storm moved west-southwestward across the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>. Entering the Gulf of Mexico, Inez began to slowly re-strengthen. On October 10, Inez made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a> as a Category 3 hurricane. The storm weakened rapidly and dissipated over <a href="/wiki/Guanajuato" title="Guanajuato">Guanajuato</a> on October 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-37"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Guadeloupe, Inez severely damaged the island's banana and sugar crops, and thousands of homes were damaged, leaving 10,000 people homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt930_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-spt930-29">[27]</a></sup> There were 40 deaths and damage totaled approximately $50 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-34"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-lodi-28">[26]</a></sup> The storm flooded many rivers and destroyed over 800 houses in Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-victoria_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-30">[28]</a></sup> There were about 100 deaths and $12 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-35"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> In Haiti, as many as 1,000 people were killed, and 60,000 people were left homeless. Damage totaled $20.35 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-re121_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-re121-31">[29]</a></sup> About 125,000 people were forced to evacuate in Cuba,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-spok2-32">[30]</a></sup> and there were three deaths and $20 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-36"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> In the Bahamas, heavy rainfall and high tides caused flooding, which killed five people and left $15.5 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-37"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Hurricane-force winds were observed in the Florida Keys, where 160 homes and 190 trailers were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap107_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap107-33">[31]</a></sup> Salt spray damaged crops in the region, and there was $5 million in damage and four deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-38"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-tol2-34">[32]</a></sup> In the Straits of Florida, Inez capsized a boat of Cuban refugees, killing 45 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-39"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> In the northern Gulf of Mexico, a helicopter crashed after carrying evacuees from an oil rig, killing 11 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> Inez produced flooding and caused some power outages in the Yucatán Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis2_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis2-36">[34]</a></sup> At its final landfall, Inez flooded portions of <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> and cut off roads to <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> About 84,000 people were left homeless,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1015_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap1015-38">[36]</a></sup> and the hurricane destroyed at least 2,500 houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup> Damage was estimated at $104 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi1013_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-upi1013-40">[38]</a></sup> and there were 74 deaths in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1015_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap1015-38">[36]</a></sup></p>

<td>September 27 – September 30</td>

<td>50 mph (85 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  1007 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<p>On September 26 and September 27, satellite imagery and ships monitored an area of disturbed weather located to the east of the Lesser Antilles and following Hurricane Inez. Based on reports of a circulation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-40"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> the system developed into a tropical depression at 00:00 UTC on September 27. The depression intensified slowly and became Tropical Storm Judith around midday on September 28. While centered north of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> the next day, Judith attained its peak intensity with maximum sustained winds of 50 mph (85 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 1,007 mbar (29.7 inHg),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-38"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> both of which were observed by a reconnaissance aircraft flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-41"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the storm crossed through the Windward Islands and weakened to a tropical depression, possibly due to entering the outflow of Inez. At 12:00 UTC on September 30, Judith dissipated over the eastern Caribbean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-39"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Winds up to 37 mph (60 km/h) were observed on <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-42"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Lois">Hurricane Lois</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hurricane Lois">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">
<tr>
<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold; background: #ffffcc">Category 1 hurricane (SSHWS)</td>

<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center">

<td>November 4 – November 11</td>

<th scope="row">Peak intensity</th>
<td>80 mph (130 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  986 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<p>A vortex within an area of low pressure developed into a tropical depression at 12:00 UTC on November 4, while located about 965 mi (1,555 km) east-southeast of Bermuda. Initially, the depression featured cold temperatures near the center and did not completely possess tropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-43"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-40"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> During the next few days, the depression organized further and acquired a warmer center of circulation while heading west-southwestward and then to the east-southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-44"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-41"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> Late on November 6, the depression was upgraded to Tropical Storm Lois,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-42"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> based on reconnaissance aircraft flight observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-45"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> assing west of the Azores on November 10, a sustained wind speed of 50 mph (80 km/h) was observed on <a href="/wiki/Corvo_Island" title="Corvo Island">Corvo Island</a>. Moving over colder ocean temperature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-46"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> Lois gradually lost tropical characteristics and transitioned into an extratropical cyclone at 00:00 UTC on November 12, while located about 640 mi (1,030 km) north of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Miguel_Island" title="São Miguel Island">São Miguel Island</a>. The remnants curved southeastward and weakened until dissipating a few hundred miles offshore <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> on November 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-43"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the 11 tropical cyclones and Kendra, the <a href="/wiki/Monthly_Weather_Review" title="Monthly Weather Review">Monthly Weather Review</a> indicates the existence of two tropical depressions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-47"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> though neither are included in the Atlantic basin best track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-44"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup> The first such system reportedly developed in the northwestern Caribbean Sea on June 28. The depression moved slowly northward and remained poorly defined throughout its duration, with a few radar reports indicating no evidence of an eye formation. Despite this, the wind field was described as well organized, especially in the northeastern quadrant. During the next four days, the depression crossed Cuba and later made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Cross_City,_Florida" title="Cross City, Florida">Cross City, Florida</a>. After striking Florida, the system curved northeastward and soon dissipated over southeastern Georgia on July 2. The depression spawned two tornadoes, one of which destroyed two aircraft at <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_International_Airport" title="Palm Beach International Airport">Palm Beach International Airport</a>; the other touched down in <a href="/wiki/Vero_Beach,_Florida" title="Vero Beach, Florida">Vero Beach</a> and caused minimal effects. The depression dropped heavy rainfall in some areas of Florida, with a peak total of 10 in (250 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Everglades_City,_Florida" title="Everglades City, Florida">Everglade City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville</a>. The precipitation in Jacksonville resulted in $50,000 in damage to roadways. Additionally, the depression brought "beneficial rains" to <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-48"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The other tropical depression was reported to have existed in late July. A tropical low pressure area moved across Florida and entered the northeastern Gulf of Mexico on July 24. By the following day, coastal radars indicated a relatively well-defined circulation. As a result, it is estimated that the system became a tropical depression later on July 25. After minimal intensification, the depression made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Boothville-Venice,_Louisiana" title="Boothville-Venice, Louisiana">Boothville, Louisiana</a> early on July 26. The depression then curved westward and dissipated on the next day. Other than heavy thunderstorms and a brief suspension of fishing activities, no other effects were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-49"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 9, a cyclone 200 mi (320 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> was named Kendra and operationally classified as a tropical storm, but post-analysis found the system actually remained an extratropical gale center. This makes Kendra the only system in the Atlantic basin to be named and not considered a tropical cyclone (pending reanalysis);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_8-50"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-8">[6]</a></sup> previously another such system was Mike of <a href="/wiki/1950_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1950 Atlantic hurricane season">1950</a>, but that storm was later re-added into the database as a tropical storm after reanalysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_9-45"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_names">Storm names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Storm names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>The following names were used for named storms (tropical storms and hurricanes) that formed in the North Atlantic in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup> Storms were named Dorothy, Faith, Hallie, Inez, Kendra and Lois for the first time in 1966. At the 1967 hurricane warning conference it was decided to use 1966's list of names for 1970, with the name Faith was substituted for Francelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1967_minutes_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-1967_minutes-42">[40]</a></sup> The name Inez was retired after the 1969 hurricane warning conference, despite not having a significant effect on the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup> Names that were not assigned are marked in gray.</p>
