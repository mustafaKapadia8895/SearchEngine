<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Antonio Vivaldi</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Antonio Lucio Vivaldi</b> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[anˈtɔːnjo ˈluːtʃo viˈvaldi]</a></span>; 4 March 1678 – 28 July 1741) was an <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> composer, <a href="/wiki/Virtuoso" title="Virtuoso">virtuoso</a> violinist, teacher and <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">cleric</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, he is recognized as one of the greatest Baroque composers, and his influence during his lifetime was widespread across Europe. He composed many instrumental <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">concertos</a>, for the violin and a variety of other instruments, as well as sacred choral works and more than forty <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>. His best-known work is a series of <a href="/wiki/Violin_concerto" title="Violin concerto">violin concertos</a> known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Four_Seasons_(Vivaldi)" title="The Four Seasons (Vivaldi)">The Four Seasons</a></i>.</p>
<p>Many of his compositions were written for the female music ensemble of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ospedale_della_Piet%C3%A0" title="Ospedale della Pietà">Ospedale della Pietà</a></i>, a home for abandoned children where Vivaldi (who had been ordained as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">Catholic priest</a>) was employed from 1703 to 1715 and from 1723 to 1740. Vivaldi also had some success with expensive stagings of his operas in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. After meeting the <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles VI</a>, Vivaldi moved to Vienna, hoping for preferment. However, the Emperor died soon after Vivaldi's arrival, and Vivaldi himself died less than a year later in poverty.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3957" data-file-width="5519" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg/220px-Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg/330px-Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg/440px-Chiesa_di_San_Giovanni_in_Bragora_-_Venezia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Antonio Lucio Vivaldi was born on 4 March 1678 in Venice,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalbotGrove_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalbotGrove-3">[3]</a></sup> then the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a>. He was <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptized</a> immediately after his birth at his home by the midwife, which led to a belief that his life was somehow in danger. Though not known for certain, the child's immediate baptism was most likely due either to his poor health or to an <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> that shook the city that day. In the trauma of the earthquake, Vivaldi's mother may have dedicated him to the priesthood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Vivaldi's official church baptism took place two months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vivaldi's parents were Giovanni Battista Vivaldi and Camilla Calicchio, as recorded in the register of <a href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_in_Bragora" title="San Giovanni in Bragora">San Giovanni in Bragora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Vivaldi had eight siblings: Iseppo Santo Vivaldi, Iseppo Gaetano Vivaldi, Bonaventura Tomaso Vivaldi, Margarita Gabriela Vivaldi, Cecilia Maria Vivaldi , Gerolama Michela Vivaldi, Francesco Gaetano Vivaldi, and Zanetta Anna Vivaldi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Giovanni Battista, who was a barber before becoming a professional violinist, taught Antonio to play the violin and then toured Venice playing the violin with his young son. Antonio was probably taught at an early age, judging by the extensive musical knowledge he had acquired by the age of 24, when he started working at the Ospedale della Pietà.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Giovanni Battista was one of the founders of the <i>Sovvegno dei musicisti di Santa Cecilia</i>, an association of musicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The president of the <i>Sovvegno</i> was <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Legrenzi" title="Giovanni Legrenzi">Giovanni Legrenzi</a>, an early Baroque composer and the <i>maestro di cappella</i> at <a href="/wiki/St_Mark%27s_Basilica" title="St Mark's Basilica">St Mark's Basilica</a>. It is possible that Legrenzi gave the young Antonio his first lessons in composition. The Luxembourg scholar Walter Kolneder has discerned the influence of Legrenzi's style in Vivaldi's early liturgical work <i>Laetatus sum</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ryom-Verzeichnis" title="Ryom-Verzeichnis">RV</a> Anh 31), written in 1691 at the age of thirteen. Vivaldi's father may have been a composer himself: in 1689, an opera titled <i>La Fedeltà sfortunata</i> was composed by a Giovanni Battista Rossi—the name under which Vivaldi's father had joined the Sovvegno di Santa Cecilia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vivaldi's health was problematic. His symptoms, <i>strettezza di petto</i> ("tightness of the chest"), have been interpreted as a form of <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-5">[5]</a></sup> This did not prevent him from learning to play the violin, composing or taking part in musical activities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-5">[5]</a></sup> although it did stop him from playing wind instruments. In 1693, at the age of fifteen, he began studying to become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">priest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was ordained in 1703, aged 25, and was soon nicknamed <i>il Prete Rosso</i>, "The Red Priest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rosso" title="wikt:rosso">Rosso</a></i> is Italian for "red", and would have referred to the color of his hair, a family trait.)</p>
<p>Not long after his ordination, in 1704, he was given a dispensation from celebrating <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> because of his ill health. Vivaldi only said Mass as a priest a few times and appeared to have withdrawn from priestly duties, though he remained a priest.</p>
<h3><span id="At_the_Conservatorio_dell'Ospedale_della_Pietà"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="At_the_Conservatorio_dell.27Ospedale_della_Piet.C3.A0">At the <i>Conservatorio dell'Ospedale della Pietà</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: At the Conservatorio dell'Ospedale della Pietà">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1703, Vivaldi became <i>maestro di violino</i> (master of violin) at an <a href="/wiki/Orphanage" title="Orphanage">orphanage</a> called the Pio <a href="/wiki/Ospedale_della_Piet%C3%A0" title="Ospedale della Pietà">Ospedale della Pietà</a> (Devout Hospital of Mercy) in Venice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalbotGrove_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-TalbotGrove-3">[3]</a></sup> While Vivaldi is most famous as a composer, he was regarded as an exceptional technical violinist as well. The German architect Johann Friedrich Armand von Uffenbach referred to Vivaldi as "the famous composer and violinist" and said that "Vivaldi played a solo accompaniment excellently, and at the conclusion he added a free fantasy [an improvised cadenza] which absolutely astounded me, for it is hardly possible that anyone has ever played, or ever will play, in such a fashion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Vivaldi was only 25 when he started working at the Ospedale della Pietà. Over the next thirty years he composed most of his major works while working there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> There were four similar institutions in Venice; their purpose was to give shelter and education to children who were abandoned or orphaned, or whose families could not support them. They were financed by funds provided by the Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The boys learned a trade and had to leave when they reached 15. The girls received a musical education, and the most talented stayed and became members of the Ospedale's renowned orchestra and choir.</p>
<p>Shortly after Vivaldi's appointment, the orphans began to gain appreciation and esteem abroad, too. Vivaldi wrote concertos, cantatas and sacred vocal music for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> These sacred works, which number over 60, are varied: they included solo motets and large-scale choral works for soloists, double chorus, and orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1704, the position of teacher of <i><a href="/wiki/Lyra_viol" title="Lyra viol">viola all'inglese</a></i> was added to his duties as violin instructor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landon26_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landon26-18">[18]</a></sup> The position of <i>maestro di coro</i>, which was at one time filled by Vivaldi, required a lot of time and work. He had to compose an oratorio or concerto at every feast and teach the orphans both music theory and how to play certain instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>His relationship with the board of directors of the Ospedale was often strained. The board had to take a vote every year on whether to keep a teacher. The vote on Vivaldi was seldom unanimous, and went 7 to 6 against him in 1709.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-20">[20]</a></sup> After a year as a freelance musician, he was recalled by the Ospedale with a unanimous vote in 1711; clearly during his year's absence the board had realized the importance of his role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-20">[20]</a></sup> He became responsible for all of the musical activity of the institution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> when he was promoted to <i>maestro de' concerti</i> (music director) in 1716.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1705, the first collection (<i>Connor Cassara</i>) of his works was published by Giuseppe Sala:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> his Opus 1 is a collection of 12 <a href="/wiki/Sonata" title="Sonata">sonatas</a> for two violins and basso continuo, in a conventional style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landon26_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Landon26-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1709, a second collection of 12 sonatas for violin and basso continuo appeared, his Opus 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A real breakthrough as a composer came with his first collection of 12 concerti for one, two, and four violins with strings, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%27estro_Armonico" title="L'estro Armonico">L'estro armonico</a></i> Opus 3, which was published in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> in 1711 by <a href="/wiki/Estienne_Roger" title="Estienne Roger">Estienne Roger</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinando_(III)_de%27_Medici" title="Ferdinando (III) de' Medici">Grand Prince Ferdinand of Tuscany</a>. The prince sponsored many musicians including <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Scarlatti" title="Alessandro Scarlatti">Alessandro Scarlatti</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">George Frideric Handel</a>. He was a musician himself, and Vivaldi probably met him in Venice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-26">[26]</a></sup> <i>L'estro armonico</i> was a resounding success all over Europe. It was followed in 1714 by <i><a href="/wiki/La_stravaganza" title="La stravaganza">La stravaganza</a></i> Opus 4, a collection of concerti for solo violin and strings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michael_Talbot_1978_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michael_Talbot_1978-27">[27]</a></sup> dedicated to an old violin student of Vivaldi's, the Venetian noble Vettor Dolfin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 1711, Vivaldi and his father traveled to <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>, where his setting of the <a href="/wiki/Stabat_Mater" title="Stabat Mater">Stabat Mater</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stabat_Mater_(Vivaldi)" title="Stabat Mater (Vivaldi)">RV 621</a>) was played as part of a religious festival. The work seems to have been written in haste: the string parts are simple, the music of the first three movements is repeated in the next three, and not all the text is set. Nevertheless, perhaps in part because of the forced essentiality of the music, the work is one of his early masterpieces.</p>
<p>Despite his frequent travels from 1718, the Pietà paid him 2 <a href="/wiki/Sequin_(coin)" title="Sequin (coin)">sequins</a> to write two concerti a month for the orchestra and to rehearse with them at least five times when in Venice. The Pietà's records show that he was paid for 140 concerti between 1723 and 1733.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opera_impresario">Opera impresario</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opera impresario">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In early 18th-century Venice, opera was the most popular musical entertainment. It proved most profitable for Vivaldi. There were several theaters competing for the public's attention. Vivaldi started his career as an opera composer as a sideline: his first opera, <i><a href="/wiki/Ottone_in_villa" title="Ottone in villa">Ottone in villa</a></i> (RV 729) was performed not in Venice, but at the Garzerie Theater in <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a> in 1713.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The following year, Vivaldi became the <a href="/wiki/Impresario" title="Impresario">impresario</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_San_Angelo" title="Teatro San Angelo">Teatro San Angelo</a> in Venice, where his opera <i><a href="/wiki/Orlando_finto_pazzo" title="Orlando finto pazzo">Orlando finto pazzo</a></i> (RV 727) was performed. The work was not to the public's taste, and it closed after a couple of weeks, being replaced with a repeat of a different work already given the previous year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1715, he presented <i><a href="/wiki/Nerone_fatto_Cesare" title="Nerone fatto Cesare">Nerone fatto Cesare</a></i> (RV 724, now lost), with music by seven different composers, of which he was the leader. The opera contained eleven <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">arias</a>, and was a success. In the late season, Vivaldi planned to put on an opera composed entirely by him, <i><a href="/wiki/Arsilda,_regina_di_Ponto" title="Arsilda, regina di Ponto">Arsilda, regina di Ponto</a></i> (RV 700), but the state censor blocked the performance. The main character, Arsilda, falls in love with another woman, Lisea, who is pretending to be a man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-26">[26]</a></sup> Vivaldi got the censor to accept the opera the following year, and it was a resounding success.</p>
<p>At this period, the <i>Pietà</i> commissioned several liturgical works. The most important were two <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorios</a>. <i>Moyses Deus Pharaonis</i>, (RV 643) is lost. The second, <i><a href="/wiki/Juditha_triumphans" title="Juditha triumphans">Juditha triumphans</a></i> (RV 644), celebrates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Corfu_(1716)" title="Siege of Corfu (1716)">victory of the Republic of Venice</a> against the Turks and the recapture of the island of <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a>. Composed in 1716, it is one of his sacred masterpieces. All eleven singing parts were performed by girls of the Pietà, both the female and male roles. Many of the <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">arias</a> include parts for solo instruments—recorders, oboes, violas d'amore, and mandolins—that showcased the range of talents of the girls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1716, Vivaldi wrote and produced two more operas, <i><a href="/wiki/L%27incoronazione_di_Dario" title="L'incoronazione di Dario">L'incoronazione di Dario</a></i> (RV 719) and <i>La costanza trionfante degli amori e degli odi</i> (RV 706). The latter was so popular that it performed two years later, re-edited and retitled <i>Artabano re dei Parti</i> (RV 701, now lost). It was also performed in Prague in 1732. In the following years, Vivaldi wrote several operas that were performed all over Italy.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Teatro_alla_moda.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="760" data-file-width="554" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Teatro_alla_moda.jpg/220px-Teatro_alla_moda.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Teatro_alla_moda.jpg/330px-Teatro_alla_moda.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Teatro_alla_moda.jpg/440px-Teatro_alla_moda.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>His progressive operatic style caused him some trouble with more conservative musicians, like <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Marcello" title="Benedetto Marcello">Benedetto Marcello</a>, a magistrate and amateur musician who wrote a <a href="/wiki/Pamphlet" title="Pamphlet">pamphlet</a> denouncing him and his operas. The pamphlet, <i><a href="/wiki/Il_teatro_alla_moda" title="Il teatro alla moda">Il teatro alla moda</a></i>, attacks Vivaldi without mentioning him directly. The cover drawing shows a boat (the Sant'Angelo), on the left end of which stands a little angel wearing a priest's hat and playing the violin. The Marcello family claimed ownership of the Teatro Sant'Angelo, and a long legal battle had been fought with the management for its restitution, without success. The obscure writing under the picture mentions non-existent places and names: <i>ALDIVIVA</i> is an anagram of <i>A. Vivaldi</i>.</p>
<p>In a letter written by Vivaldi to his patron Marchese Bentivoglio in 1737, he makes reference to his "94 operas". Only around 50 operas by Vivaldi have been discovered, and no other documentation of the remaining operas exists. Although Vivaldi may have exaggerated, in his dual role of composer and <i>impresario</i> it is plausible that he may either have written or been responsible for the production of as many as 94 operas during a career which by then had spanned almost 25 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> While Vivaldi certainly composed many operas in his time, he never reached the prominence of other great composers like <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Scarlatti" title="Alessandro Scarlatti">Alessandro Scarlatti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Adolph_Hasse" title="Johann Adolph Hasse">Johann Adolph Hasse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Leo" title="Leonardo Leo">Leonardo Leo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baldassare_Galuppi" title="Baldassare Galuppi">Baldassare Galuppi</a>, as evidenced by his inability to keep a production running for any extended period of time in any major opera house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heller114_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heller114-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mantua_and_the_Four_Seasons">Mantua and the <i>Four Seasons</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mantua and the Four Seasons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1717 or 1718, Vivaldi was offered a new prestigious position as <i>Maestro di Cappella</i> of the court of prince <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Hesse-Darmstadt" title="Philip of Hesse-Darmstadt">Philip of Hesse-Darmstadt</a>, governor of <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He moved there for three years and produced several operas, among which was <i><a href="/wiki/Tito_Manlio" title="Tito Manlio">Tito Manlio</a></i> (RV 738). In 1721, he was in Milan, where he presented the pastoral drama <i><a href="/wiki/La_Silvia" title="La Silvia">La Silvia</a></i> (RV 734, 9 arias survive). He visited Milan again the following year with the oratorio <i>L'adorazione delli tre re magi al bambino Gesù</i> (RV 645, also lost). In 1722 he moved to Rome, where he introduced his operas' new style. The new <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XIII" title="Pope Benedict XIII">pope Benedict XIII</a> invited Vivaldi to play for him. In 1725, Vivaldi returned to Venice, where he produced four operas in the same year.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vivaldi_caricature.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="3000" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Vivaldi_caricature.png/170px-Vivaldi_caricature.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Vivaldi_caricature.png/255px-Vivaldi_caricature.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Vivaldi_caricature.png/340px-Vivaldi_caricature.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vivaldi_caricature.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Caricature" title="Caricature">Caricature</a> by P. L. Ghezzi, Rome (1723)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During this period Vivaldi wrote the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Four_Seasons_(Vivaldi)" title="The Four Seasons (Vivaldi)">Four Seasons</a></i>, four violin concertos depicting scenes appropriate for each season. Three of the concerti are of original conception, while the first, "Spring", borrows motifs from a Sinfonia in the first act of his contemporaneous opera <i>Il Giustino</i>. The inspiration for the concertos was probably the countryside around Mantua. They were a revolution in musical conception: in them Vivaldi represented flowing creeks, singing birds (of different species, each specifically characterized), barking dogs, buzzing mosquitoes, crying shepherds, storms, drunken dancers, silent nights, hunting parties from both the hunters' and the prey's point of view, frozen landscapes, ice-skating children, and warming winter fires. Each concerto is associated with a <a href="/wiki/Sonnet" title="Sonnet">sonnet</a>, possibly by Vivaldi, describing the scenes depicted in the music. They were published as the first four concertos in a collection of twelve, <i><a href="/wiki/Il_cimento_dell%27armonia_e_dell%27inventione" title="Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione">Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione</a></i>, Opus 8, published in Amsterdam by <a href="/wiki/Michel-Charles_Le_C%C3%A8ne" title="Michel-Charles Le Cène">Michel-Charles Le Cène</a> in 1725.</p>
<p>During his time in Mantua, Vivaldi became acquainted with an aspiring young singer <a href="/wiki/Anna_Gir%C3%B2" title="Anna Girò">Anna Tessieri Girò</a> who was to become his student, protégée, and favorite prima donna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Anna, along with her older half-sister Paolina, became part of Vivaldi's entourage and regularly accompanied him on his many travels. There was speculation about the nature of Vivaldi's and Giro's relationship, but no evidence to indicate anything beyond friendship and professional collaboration. Although Vivaldi's relationship with Anna Girò was questioned, he adamantly denied any romantic relationship in a letter to his patron Bentivoglio dated 16 November 1737.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the height of his career, Vivaldi received commissions from European nobility and royalty. The <i>serenata</i> (cantata) <i>Gloria e Imeneo</i> (RV 687) was commissioned in 1725 by the French <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassador</a> to Venice in celebration of the marriage of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XV_of_France" title="Louis XV of France">Louis XV</a>. The following year, another <i>serenata</i>, <i>La Sena festeggiante</i> (RV 694), was written for and premiered at the French embassy as well, celebrating the birth of the French royal princesses, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Henriette_of_France" title="Princess Henriette of France">Henriette</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louise_%C3%89lisabeth_of_France" title="Louise Élisabeth of France">Louise Élisabeth</a>. Vivaldi's Opus 9, <i><a href="/wiki/La_cetra_(Vivaldi)" title="La cetra (Vivaldi)">La cetra</a></i>, was dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles VI</a>. In 1728, Vivaldi met the emperor while the emperor was visiting <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a> to oversee the construction of a new port. Charles admired the music of the Red Priest so much that he is said to have spoken more with the composer during their one meeting than he spoke to his ministers in over two years. He gave Vivaldi the title of <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>, a gold medal and an invitation to Vienna. Vivaldi gave Charles a manuscript copy of <i>La cetra</i>, a set of concerti almost completely different from the set of the same title published as Opus 9. The printing was probably delayed, forcing Vivaldi to gather an improvised collection for the emperor.</p>
<p>Accompanied by his father, Vivaldi traveled to Vienna and Prague in 1730, where his opera <i><a href="/wiki/Farnace" title="Farnace">Farnace</a></i> (RV 711) was presented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <i>Farnace</i> garnered six revivals later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heller114_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heller114-33">[33]</a></sup> Some of his later operas were created in collaboration with two of Italy's major writers of the time. <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Olimpiade" title="L'Olimpiade">L'Olimpiade</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Catone_in_Utica" title="Catone in Utica">Catone in Utica</a></i> were written by <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Metastasio" title="Pietro Metastasio">Pietro Metastasio</a>, the major representative of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Arcadia" title="Academy of Arcadia">Arcadian</a> movement and court poet in Vienna. <i><a href="/wiki/Griselda_(Vivaldi)" title="Griselda (Vivaldi)">La Griselda</a></i> was rewritten by the young <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Goldoni" title="Carlo Goldoni">Carlo Goldoni</a> from an earlier libretto by <a href="/wiki/Apostolo_Zeno" title="Apostolo Zeno">Apostolo Zeno</a>.</p>
<p>Like many composers of the time, the final years of Vivaldi's life found him in financial difficulties. His compositions were no longer held in such high esteem as they once were in Venice; changing musical tastes quickly made them outmoded. In response, Vivaldi chose to sell off sizeable numbers of his manuscripts at paltry prices to finance his migration to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The reasons for Vivaldi's departure from Venice are unclear, but it seems likely that, after the success of his meeting with Emperor Charles VI, he wished to take up the position of a composer in the imperial court. On his way to Vienna, Vivaldi may have stopped in Graz to see Anna Girò.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also likely that Vivaldi went to Vienna to stage operas, especially as he took up residence near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%A4rntnertortheater" title="Kärntnertortheater">Kärntnertortheater</a>. Shortly after his arrival in Vienna, Charles VI died, which left the composer without any royal protection or a steady source of income. Soon afterwards, Vivaldi became impoverished<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and died during the night of 27/28 July 1741, aged 63,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> of "internal infection", in a house owned by the widow of a Viennese saddlemaker. On 28 July he was buried in a simple grave in a burial ground that was owned by the public hospital fund. Vivaldi's funeral took place at <a href="/wiki/St._Stephen%27s_Cathedral,_Vienna" title="St. Stephen's Cathedral, Vienna">St. Stephen's Cathedral</a>, but contrary to popular legend, the young <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a> had nothing to do with this burial, since no music was performed on that occasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The cost of his funeral with a 'Kleingeläut' was 19 Gulden 45 Kreuzer which was rather expensive for the lowest class of peal of bells.</p>
<p>He was buried next to <a href="/wiki/Karlskirche" title="Karlskirche">Karlskirche</a>, in an area which is now part of the site of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna_University_of_Technology" title="Vienna University of Technology">Technical Institute</a>. The house where he lived in Vienna has since been destroyed; the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Sacher" title="Hotel Sacher">Hotel Sacher</a> is built on part of the site. Memorial plaques have been placed at both locations, as well as a Vivaldi "star" in the Viennese Musikmeile and a monument at the Rooseveltplatz.</p>
<p>Only two, possibly three original portraits of Vivaldi are known to survive: an engraving, an ink sketch and an oil painting. The engraving, which was the basis of several copies produced at some later time by other artists, was made in 1725 by François Morellon La Cave for the first edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Il_cimento_dell%27armonia_e_dell%27inventione" title="Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione">Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione</a></i>, and shows Vivaldi holding a sheet of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The ink sketch, a <a href="/wiki/Caricature" title="Caricature">caricature</a>, was done by <a href="/wiki/Pier_Leone_Ghezzi" title="Pier Leone Ghezzi">Ghezzi</a> in 1723 and shows Vivaldi's head and shoulders in profile. It exists in two versions: a first jotting kept at the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Library" title="Vatican Library">Vatican Library</a>, and a much lesser-known, slightly more detailed copy recently discovered in Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The oil painting, which can be seen in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Museum_and_Library_of_Music_of_Bologna" title="International Museum and Library of Music of Bologna">International Museum and Library of Music of Bologna</a>, is anonymous, and is thought to be of Vivaldi due to its strong resemblance to the La Cave engraving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>Vivaldi's music was innovative. He brightened the formal and rhythmic structure of the concerto, in which he looked for harmonic contrasts and innovative melodies and themes; many of his compositions are flamboyantly, almost playfully, exuberant.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a> was deeply influenced by Vivaldi's concertos and arias (recalled in his <i><a href="/wiki/St_John_Passion" title="St John Passion">St John Passion</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/St_Matthew_Passion" title="St Matthew Passion">St Matthew Passion</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Bach_cantata" title="Bach cantata">cantatas</a>). Bach transcribed six of Vivaldi's concerti for solo keyboard, three for organ, and one for four harpsichords, strings, and <a href="/wiki/Figured_bass" title="Figured bass">basso continuo</a> (BWV 1065) based upon the concerto for four violins, two violas, cello, and basso continuo (<a href="/wiki/Ryom-Verzeichnis" title="Ryom-Verzeichnis">RV</a> 580).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_reputation">Posthumous reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Posthumous reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his lifetime, Vivaldi was popular in many countries, including France, but after his death the composer's popularity dwindled. After the Baroque period, Vivaldi's published concerti became relatively unknown and were largely ignored. Even Vivaldi's most famous work, <i>The Four Seasons</i>, was unknown in its original edition during the <a href="/wiki/Classical_period_(music)" title="Classical period (music)">Classical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic</a> periods.</p>
<p>During the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Kreisler" title="Fritz Kreisler">Fritz Kreisler</a>'s Concerto in C, in the Style of Vivaldi (which he passed off as an original Vivaldi work) helped revive Vivaldi's reputation. This spurred the French scholar <a href="/wiki/Marc_Pincherle" title="Marc Pincherle">Marc Pincherle</a> to begin an academic study of Vivaldi's oeuvre. Many Vivaldi manuscripts were rediscovered, which were acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Turin_National_University_Library" title="Turin National University Library">Turin National University Library</a> as a result of the generous sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turinese</a> businessmen Roberto Foa and Filippo Giordano, in memory of their sons. This led to a renewed interest in Vivaldi by, among others, Mario Rinaldi, <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Casella" title="Alfredo Casella">Alfredo Casella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olga_Rudge" title="Olga Rudge">Olga Rudge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Chute" title="Desmond Chute">Desmond Chute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Arturo Toscanini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schering" title="Arnold Schering">Arnold Schering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Kaufman" title="Louis Kaufman">Louis Kaufman</a>, all of whom were instrumental in the Vivaldi revival of the 20th century.</p>
<p>In 1926, in a monastery in <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>, researchers discovered fourteen folios of Vivaldi's work that were previously thought to have been lost during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>. Some missing volumes in the numbered set were discovered in the collections of the descendants of the Grand Duke Durazzo, who had acquired the monastery complex in the 18th century. The volumes contained 300 concertos, 19 operas and over 100 vocal-instrumental works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The resurrection of Vivaldi's unpublished works in the 20th century is mostly due to the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Casella" title="Alfredo Casella">Alfredo Casella</a>, who in 1939 organized the historic Vivaldi Week, in which the rediscovered <a href="/wiki/Gloria_(Vivaldi)" title="Gloria (Vivaldi)">Gloria</a> (RV 589) and l'Olimpiade were revived. Since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Vivaldi's compositions have enjoyed wide success. <a href="/wiki/Historically_informed_performance" title="Historically informed performance">Historically informed performances</a>, often on "original instruments", have increased Vivaldi's fame still further.</p>
<p>Recent rediscoveries of works by Vivaldi include two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psalm" title="Psalm">psalm</a> settings of <i>Nisi Dominus</i> (RV 803, in eight movements) and <i>Dixit Dominus</i> (RV 807, in eleven movements). These were identified in 2003 and 2005 respectively, by the Australian scholar Janice Stockigt. The Vivaldi scholar <a href="/wiki/Michael_Talbot_(musicologist)" title="Michael Talbot (musicologist)">Michael Talbot</a> described RV 807 as "arguably the best nonoperatic work from Vivaldi's pen to come to light since... ...the 1920s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Vivaldi's lost 1730 opera <i><a href="/wiki/Argippo" title="Argippo">Argippo</a></i> (RV 697) was rediscovered in 2006 by the <a href="/wiki/Harpsichordist" title="Harpsichordist">harpsichordist</a> and conductor Ondřej Macek, whose Hofmusici orchestra performed the work at <a href="/wiki/Prague_Castle" title="Prague Castle">Prague Castle</a> on 3 May 2008, its first performance since 1730.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/List_of_compositions_by_Antonio_Vivaldi" title="List of compositions by Antonio Vivaldi">List of compositions by Antonio Vivaldi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_operas_by_Vivaldi" title="List of operas by Vivaldi">List of operas by Vivaldi</a></div>
<p>A composition by Vivaldi is identified by <a href="/wiki/Ryom-Verzeichnis" title="Ryom-Verzeichnis">RV number</a>, which refers to its place in the "Ryom-Verzeichnis" or "Répertoire des oeuvres d'Antonio Vivaldi", a catalog created in the 20th century by the musicologist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ryom" title="Peter Ryom">Peter Ryom</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Four_Seasons_(Vivaldi)" title="The Four Seasons (Vivaldi)"><i>Le quattro stagioni</i> (The Four Seasons)</a> of 1723 is his most famous work. Part of <i><a href="/wiki/Il_cimento_dell%27armonia_e_dell%27inventione" title="Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione">Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione</a></i> ("The Contest between Harmony and Invention"), it depicts moods and scenes from each of the four seasons. This work has been described as an outstanding instance of pre-19th century <a href="/wiki/Program_music" title="Program music">program music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vivaldi wrote more than 500 other concertos. About 350 of these are for solo instrument and strings, of which 230 are for violin, the others being for <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viola_d%27amore" title="Viola d'amore">viola d'amore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">recorder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lute" title="Lute">lute</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a>. About forty concertos are for two instruments and strings and about thirty are for three or more instruments and strings.</p>
<p>As well as about 46 operas, Vivaldi composed a large body of sacred choral music. Other works include <a href="/wiki/Sinfonia" title="Sinfonia">sinfonias</a>, about 90 <a href="/wiki/Sonata" title="Sonata">sonatas</a> and chamber music.</p>
<p>Some sonatas for flute, published as <i>Il Pastor Fido</i>, have been erroneously attributed to Vivaldi, but were composed by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Ch%C3%A9deville" title="Nicolas Chédeville">Nicolas Chédeville</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalogs_of_Vivaldi_works">Catalogs of Vivaldi works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Catalogs of Vivaldi works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vivaldi's works attracted cataloging efforts befitting a major composer. Scholarly work intended to increase the accuracy and variety of Vivaldi performances also supported new discoveries which made old catalogs incomplete. Works still in circulation today may be numbered under several different systems (some earlier catalogs are mentioned <a href="/wiki/Ryom-Verzeichnis" title="Ryom-Verzeichnis">here</a>).</p>
<p>Because the simply consecutive Complete Edition (CE) numbers did not reflect the individual works (Opus numbers) into which compositions were grouped, Fanna numbers were often used in conjunction with CE numbers. Combined Complete Edition (CE)/Fanna numbering was especially common in the work of Italian groups driving the mid-20th century revival of Vivaldi, such as Gli Accademici di Milano under Piero Santi. For example, the Bassoon Concerto in B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span> major, "La Notte" RV 501, became CE 12, F. VIII,1</p>
<p>Despite the awkwardness of having to overlay Fanna numbers onto the Complete Edition number for meaningful grouping of Vivaldi's oeuvre, these numbers displaced the older <a href="/wiki/Marc_Pincherle" title="Marc Pincherle">Pincherle</a> numbers as the (re-)discovery of more manuscripts had rendered older catalogs obsolete.</p>
<p>This cataloging work was led by the Istituto Italiano Antonio Vivaldi, where <a href="/wiki/Gian_Francesco_Malipiero" title="Gian Francesco Malipiero">Gian Francesco Malipiero</a> was both the director and the editor of the published scores (Edizioni G. Ricordi). His work built on that of Antonio Fanna, a Venetian businessman and the Institute's founder, and thus formed a bridge to the scholarly catalog dominant today.</p>
<p>Compositions by Vivaldi are identified today by <a href="/wiki/Ryom-Verzeichnis" title="Ryom-Verzeichnis">RV number</a>, the number assigned by Danish musicologist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ryom" title="Peter Ryom">Peter Ryom</a> in works published mostly in the 1970s, such as the "Ryom-Verzeichnis" or "Répertoire des oeuvres d'Antonio Vivaldi". Like the Complete Edition before it, the RV does not typically assign its single, consecutive numbers to "adjacent" works that occupy one of the composer's single opus numbers. Its goal as a modern catalog is to index the manuscripts and sources that establish the existence and nature of all known works. These several numbering systems are cross-referenced at <a class="external text" href="http://www.classical.net/music/composer/works/vivaldi/index.php" rel="nofollow">classical.net</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Vivaldi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movie <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vivaldi,_a_Prince_in_Venice&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vivaldi, a Prince in Venice (page does not exist)">Vivaldi, a Prince in Venice</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi,_un_prince_%C3%A0_Venise" title="fr:Antonio Vivaldi, un prince à Venise">fr</a>)</span></i> was completed in 2005 as an Italian-French co-production under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Guillermou" title="Jean-Louis Guillermou">Jean-Louis Guillermou</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Australian Broadcasting Corporation">ABC</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio_National" title="Radio National">Radio National</a> commissioned a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_play" title="Radio play">radio play</a> about Vivaldi, which was written by <a href="/wiki/Sean_Riley_(playwright)" title="Sean Riley (playwright)">Sean Riley</a>. Entitled <i>The Angel and the Red Priest</i>, the play was later adapted for the stage and was performed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adelaide_Festival_of_the_Arts" title="Adelaide Festival of the Arts">Adelaide Festival of the Arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Janice_Jordan_Shefelman" title="Janice Jordan Shefelman">Janice Jordan Shefelman</a> wrote a children's book detailing the life of Vivaldi entitled <i>I, Vivaldi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Classical Kids Music Education produces live theatre productions, and has issued a CD entitled Vivaldi's Ring of Mystery <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> an original fictional story about an orphan girl in the early 1700s who is in search of her roots. A central clue to the mystery is a missing Stradivarius violin that the Duke of Cremona has brought to Vivaldi’s school of the Pieta in the hopes of finding his long lost grandchild. Vivaldi music is featured throughout the story to support the narrative.</p>
