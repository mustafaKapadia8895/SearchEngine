<p>The <b>World Ocean</b> or <b>global ocean</b> (colloquially <b>the sea</b> or <b>the ocean</b>) is the interconnected system of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">waters</a>, and comprises the bulk of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, covering 361,132,000 square kilometres (139,434,000 sq mi) (70.8%) of Earth's surface, with a total volume of 1,332,000,000 cubic kilometres (320,000,000 cu mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The unity and continuity of the World Ocean, with relatively free interchange among its parts, is of fundamental importance to <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is divided into a number of principal oceanic areas that are delimited by the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> and various oceanographic features: these divisions are the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> (sometimes considered a <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> of the Atlantic), <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> (often considered instead as just the southern portions of the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans but according to Commodore John Leech of <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> (IHO), this situation has no scientific sense because of El Nino and a wider interest in global warming, has a different ecosystem or life from other Earth's oceans, and is located on a southern part of the planet, hence the name of an ocean).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>. In turn, oceanic waters are interspersed by many smaller seas, gulfs, and bays.</p>
<p>A global ocean has existed in one form or another on Earth for eons, and the notion dates back to <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a>. The contemporary concept of the <i>World Ocean</i> was coined in the early 20th century by the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuly_Shokalsky" title="Yuly Shokalsky">Yuly Shokalsky</a> to refer to the continuous ocean that covers and encircles most of Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>If viewed from the southern pole of Earth, the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans can be seen as lobes extending northward from the Southern Ocean. Farther north, the Atlantic opens into the Arctic Ocean, which is connected to the Pacific by the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>, forming a continuous expanse of water.</p>
<ul>
<li>The Pacific Ocean, the largest of the oceans, also reaches northward from the Southern Ocean to the Arctic Ocean. It spans the gap between <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. The Pacific Ocean meets the Atlantic Ocean south of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a>.</li>
<li>The Atlantic Ocean, the second largest, extends from the Southern Ocean between the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, to the Arctic Ocean. The Atlantic Ocean meets the Indian Ocean south of Africa at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agulhas" title="Cape Agulhas">Cape Agulhas</a>.</li>
<li>The Indian Ocean, the third largest, extends northward from the Southern Ocean to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australia</a>. The Indian Ocean joins the Pacific Ocean to the east, near Australia.</li>
<li>The Arctic Ocean is the smallest of the five. It joins the Atlantic Ocean near <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and joins the Pacific Ocean at the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>. It overlies the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a>, touching <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">Eastern Hemisphere</a>. The Arctic Ocean is partially covered in <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a>, the extent of which varies according to the season.</li>
<li>The Southern Ocean is a proposed <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> surrounding <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a>, generally the ocean south of sixty degrees south latitude. The Southern Ocean is partially covered in <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a>, the extent of which varies according to the season. The Southern Ocean is the second smallest of the five named oceans.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> continually change the coastline and structure of the world ocean.</p>
