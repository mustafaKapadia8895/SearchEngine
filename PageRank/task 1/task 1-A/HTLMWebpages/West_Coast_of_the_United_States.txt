<p>The <b>West Coast</b> or <b>Pacific Coast</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline">coastline</a> along which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_48" title="Lower 48">contiguous</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">Western United States</a> meets the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Ocean" title="North Pacific Ocean">North Pacific Ocean</a>. As a region, this term most often refers to the <a href="/wiki/Coastal_states" title="Coastal states">coastal states</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. More specifically, it refers to an area defined on the east by the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a>, and on the west by the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Census" title="U.S. Census">U.S. Census</a> groups the five states of California, Oregon, Washington, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> together as the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_States" title="Pacific States">Pacific States</a> division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eia1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-eia1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 Census</a>, the estimated population of the Census Bureau's Pacific Region was approximately 47.8 million (56.9 million if Nevada and Arizona are included) – about 15.3% (18.2% with Nevada and Arizona) of US population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The largest city on the west coast of the United States is Los Angeles.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Models_of_migration_to_the_New_World" title="Models of migration to the New World">Models of migration to the New World</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Mesoamerica" title="History of Mesoamerica">History of Mesoamerica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans in the United States</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_west_coast_of_North_America" title="History of the west coast of North America">history of the West Coast</a> begins with the arrival of the earliest known humans of the Americas, <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a>, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a> from <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> into North America over a land bridge, <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a>, that existed between 45,000 BCE and 12,000 BCE (47,000–14,000 years ago). Small isolated groups of <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a> migrated alongside herds of large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivores" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a> far into <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. Between 16,500 BCE and 13,500 BCE (18,500–15,500 years ago), ice-free corridors developed along the Pacific coast and valleys of North America and possibly by sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alaska_Natives" title="Alaska Natives">Alaska Natives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Pacific_Northwest_Coast" title="Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast">indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas#California" title="Classification of indigenous peoples of the Americas">California indigenous peoples</a> eventually descended from the <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a>. They developed various languages and established <a href="/wiki/Category:Native_American_trails_in_the_United_States" title="Category:Native American trails in the United States">trade routes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Later, Spanish, British, French, Russian, and American explorers and settlers <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">began colonizing the area</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WestCoast_koppen.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1530" data-file-width="1080" height="425" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/WestCoast_koppen.svg/300px-WestCoast_koppen.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/WestCoast_koppen.svg/450px-WestCoast_koppen.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/WestCoast_koppen.svg/600px-WestCoast_koppen.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WestCoast_koppen.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Köppen climate types of the US West Coast</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The West Coast of the United States has an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> in its Northern edge towards the Canada–US border, but from the California border towards the Mexico–US border the climate is <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">mediterranean</a>. The coastline sees significantly mild temperatures when compared to the inland areas during summer. In far Northern California there is a difference of 17 °C (30 °F) between <a href="/wiki/Eureka,_California" title="Eureka, California">Eureka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willow_Creek,_California" title="Willow Creek, California">Willow Creek</a> in spite of only 25 miles (40 km) separating the locations and Willow Creek being located at a 500 metres (1,600 ft) elevation. Slightly narrower fluctuations can be seen all through the coastline, and could partially be explained by the cold currents in the Pacific Ocean moderating coastal temperatures and the mountain ranges blocking the maritime air from moving farther inland than its foothills during summer. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_fog" title="Coastal fog">Coastal fog</a> is also prevalent in keeping shoreline temperatures cool. This does not only occur in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>, but it also affects <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a>, with very little yearly temperature differences but with cool summers similar to those expected in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a>. A short journey inland and summer temperatures are compatible with the rest of the United States on the same latitudes, sometimes warmer due to prevailing winds from the <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_desert_climate" title="Hot desert climate">hot desert climate</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the West Coast has been populated by immigrants more recently than the East Coast, its culture is considerably younger. Additionally, its demographic composition underlies its cultural difference from the rest of the United States. California's history first as a major Spanish colony, and later Mexican territory, has given the lower West Coast a distinctive Hispanic tone, which it also shares with the rest of the Southwest. Similarly, two of the three cities in which Asian Americans have concentrated, San Francisco and Los Angeles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> are located on the West Coast, with significant populations in other West Coast cities. San Francisco's <a href="/wiki/Chinatown" title="Chinatown">Chinatown</a>, the oldest in North America, is a vibrant cultural center<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>The West Coast also has a proportionally large share of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_cities" title="Green cities">green cities</a> within the United States, which manifests itself in different cultural practices such as bicycling and organic gardening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, the French writer <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Faye" title="Guillaume Faye">Guillaume Faye</a>, comparing California to Europe, wrote that "as super-America, California stands out as the absolute antithesis of authentic Europe[...] California has set itself up as the world centre of the simulacrum and the inauthentic, as the absolute synthesis of "cool" Stalinism. An hysterical land; focus and meeting-place for the rootless, California is the land of non-history, of the non-event, but at the same time the site of the constant swirl, the uninterrupted rhythm of fashion, that is to say, the site of tremors going nowhere, those tremors which so obsess it, constantly threatened as it is by earthquakes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other writers, like <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baudrillard" title="Jean Baudrillard">Jean Baudrillard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Davis_(scholar)" title="Mike Davis (scholar)">Mike Davis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a>, have made related statements on Californian culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Northwest</a>, Portland and Seattle are both considered among the coffee capitals of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> While Starbucks originated in Seattle, both towns are known for small-scale coffee roasters and independent coffeeshops. In the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> at large, which includes the Canadian west coast, the culture has been significantly shaped by the environment, especially by its forests, mountains, and rain. This may account for the fact that the Northwest has many high-quality libraries and bookshops (most notably <a href="/wiki/Powell%27s_Books" title="Powell's Books">Powell's Books</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Central_Library" title="Seattle Central Library">Seattle Central Library</a>) and a "bibliophile soul".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The region also has a marginal, but growing independence movement based on <a href="/wiki/Bioregionalism" title="Bioregionalism">bioregionalism</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_(independence_movement)" title="Cascadia (independence movement)">Cascadian</a> identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Doug_flag" title="Doug flag">Cascadian flag</a> has become a popular image at <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Sounders_FC" title="Seattle Sounders FC">Seattle Sounders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portland_Timbers" title="Portland Timbers">Portland Timbers</a> games.</p>
