<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Turbulence</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the turbulence felt on an <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplane</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Clear-air_turbulence" title="Clear-air turbulence">Clear-air turbulence</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Turbulence_(disambiguation)" title="Turbulence (disambiguation)">Turbulence (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Turbulence</b> or <b>turbulent flow</b> is a flow regime in <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> characterized by <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> changes in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">flow velocity</a>. It is in contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar flow</a> regime, which occurs when a fluid flows in parallel layers, with no disruption between those layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Turbulence is commonly observed in everyday phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Breaking_wave" title="Breaking wave">surf</a>, fast flowing rivers, billowing storm clouds, or smoke from a chimney, and most fluid flows occurring in nature and created in engineering applications are turbulent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ting-surf_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ting-surf-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tennekes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tennekes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:2</sup> Turbulence is caused by excessive kinetic energy in parts of a fluid flow, which overcomes the damping effect of the fluid's viscosity. For this reason turbulence is easier to create in low viscosity fluids, but more difficult in highly viscous fluids. In general terms, in turbulent flow, unsteady <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vortices" title="Vortices">vortices</a> appear of many sizes which interact with each other, consequently <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> due to friction effects increases. This would increase the energy needed to pump fluid through a pipe, for instance. However this effect can also be exploited by such as aerodynamic <a href="/wiki/Spoiler_(aeronautics)" title="Spoiler (aeronautics)">spoilers</a> on aircraft, which deliberately "spoil" the laminar flow to increase drag and reduce lift.</p>
<p>The onset of turbulence can be predicted by a dimensionless constant called the <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a>, which calculates the balance between kinetic energy and viscous damping in a fluid flow. However, turbulence has long resisted detailed physical analysis, and the interactions within turbulence creates a very complex situation. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a> has described turbulence as the most important unsolved problem of classical physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eames-quoting-feynman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-eames-quoting-feynman-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<ul>
<li>Smoke rising from a <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarette</a> is mostly turbulent flow. However, for the first few centimeters the flow is <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar</a>. The smoke <a href="/wiki/Plume_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Plume (fluid dynamics)">plume</a> becomes turbulent as its <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a> increases, due to its flow velocity and characteristic length increasing.</li>
<li>Flow over a <a href="/wiki/Golf_ball" title="Golf ball">golf ball</a>. (This can be best understood by considering the golf ball to be stationary, with air flowing over it.) If the golf ball were smooth, the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> flow over the front of the sphere would be laminar at typical conditions. However, the boundary layer would separate early, as the pressure gradient switched from favorable (pressure decreasing in the flow direction) to unfavorable (pressure increasing in the flow direction), creating a large region of low pressure behind the ball that creates high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Form_drag" title="Form drag">form drag</a>. To prevent this from happening, the surface is dimpled to perturb the boundary layer and promote transition to turbulence. This results in higher skin friction, but moves the point of boundary layer separation further along, resulting in lower form drag and lower overall drag.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clear-air_turbulence" title="Clear-air turbulence">Clear-air turbulence</a> experienced during airplane flight, as well as poor <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">astronomical seeing</a> (the blurring of images seen through the atmosphere.)</li>
<li>Most of the terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a></li>
<li>The oceanic and atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed layers</a> and intense oceanic currents.</li>
<li>The flow conditions in many industrial equipment (such as pipes, ducts, precipitators, gas <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_scraped_surface_heat_exchanger" title="Dynamic scraped surface heat exchanger">dynamic scraped surface heat exchangers</a>, etc.) and machines (for instance, <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>).</li>
<li>The external flow over all kind of vehicles such as cars, airplanes, ships and submarines.</li>
<li>The motions of matter in stellar atmospheres.</li>
<li>A jet exhausting from a nozzle into a quiescent fluid. As the flow emerges into this external fluid, shear layers originating at the lips of the nozzle are created. These layers separate the fast moving jet from the external fluid, and at a certain critical <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a> they become unstable and break down to turbulence.</li>
<li>Biologically generated turbulence resulting from swimming animals affects ocean mixing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snow_fence" title="Snow fence">Snow fences</a> work by inducing turbulence in the wind, forcing it to drop much of its snow load near the fence.</li>
<li>Bridge supports (piers) in water. In the late summer and fall, when river flow is slow, water flows smoothly around the support legs. In the spring, when the flow is faster, a higher Reynolds Number is associated with the flow. The flow may start off laminar but is quickly separated from the leg and becomes turbulent.</li>
<li>In many geophysical flows (rivers, atmospheric boundary layer), the flow turbulence is dominated by the coherent structure activities and associated turbulent events. A turbulent event is a series of turbulent fluctuations that contain more energy than the average flow turbulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Narasimha_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Narasimha-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trevethan_Chanson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trevethan_Chanson-7">[7]</a></sup> The turbulent events are associated with coherent flow structures such as eddies and turbulent bursting, and they play a critical role in terms of sediment scour, accretion and transport in rivers as well as contaminant mixing and dispersion in rivers and estuaries, and in the atmosphere.</li>
<li>In the medical field of <a href="/wiki/Cardiology" title="Cardiology">cardiology</a>, a stethoscope is used to detect <a href="/wiki/Heart_sounds" title="Heart sounds">heart sounds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruits" title="Bruits">bruits</a>, which are due to turbulent blood flow. In normal individuals, heart sounds are a product of turbulent flow as heart valves close. However, in some conditions turbulent flow can be audible due to other reasons, some of them pathological. For example, in advanced <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a>, bruits (and therefore turbulent flow) can be heard in some vessels that have been narrowed by the disease process.</li>
<li>Recently, turbulence in porous media became a highly debated subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>Turbulence is characterized by the following features:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Irregularity </dt>
<dd>Turbulent flows are always highly irregular. For this reason, turbulence problems are normally treated statistically rather than deterministically. Turbulent flow is chaotic. However, not all chaotic flows are turbulent.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Diffusivity </dt>
<dd>The readily available supply of energy in turbulent flows tends to accelerate the homogenization (mixing) of fluid mixtures. The characteristic which is responsible for the enhanced mixing and increased rates of mass, momentum and energy transports in a flow is called "diffusivity".</dd>
</dl>
<p>Turbulent diffusion is usually described by a turbulent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffusion_coefficient" title="Diffusion coefficient">diffusion coefficient</a>. This turbulent diffusion coefficient is defined in a phenomenological sense, by analogy with the molecular diffusivities, but it does not have a true physical meaning, being dependent on the flow conditions, and not a property of the fluid itself. In addition, the turbulent diffusivity concept assumes a constitutive relation between a turbulent <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> and the gradient of a mean variable similar to the relation between flux and gradient that exists for molecular transport. In the best case, this assumption is only an approximation. Nevertheless, the turbulent diffusivity is the simplest approach for quantitative analysis of turbulent flows, and many models have been postulated to calculate it. For instance, in large bodies of water like oceans this coefficient can be found using <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson" title="Lewis Fry Richardson">Richardson</a>'s four-third power law and is governed by the <a href="/wiki/Random_walk" title="Random walk">random walk</a> principle. In rivers and large ocean currents, the diffusion coefficient is given by variations of Elder's formula.</p>
<dl>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotationality" title="Rotationality">Rotationality</a> </dt>
<dd>Turbulent flows have non-zero vorticity and are characterized by a strong three-dimensional vortex generation mechanism known as <a href="/wiki/Vortex_stretching" title="Vortex stretching">vortex stretching</a>. In fluid dynamics, they are essentially vortices subjected to stretching associated with a corresponding increase of the component of vorticity in the stretching direction—due to the conservation of angular momentum. On the other hand, vortex stretching is the core mechanism on which the turbulence energy cascade relies to establish the structure function.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In general, the stretching mechanism implies thinning of the vortices in the direction perpendicular to the stretching direction due to volume conservation of fluid elements. As a result, the radial length scale of the vortices decreases and the larger flow structures break down into smaller structures. The process continues until the small scale structures are small enough that their kinetic energy can be transformed by the fluid's molecular viscosity into heat. This is why turbulence is always rotational and three dimensional. For example, atmospheric cyclones are rotational but their substantially two-dimensional shapes do not allow vortex generation and so are not turbulent. On the other hand, oceanic flows are dispersive but essentially non rotational and therefore are not turbulent.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">Dissipation</a> </dt>
<dd>To sustain turbulent flow, a persistent source of energy supply is required because turbulence dissipates rapidly as the kinetic energy is converted into internal energy by viscous shear stress. Turbulence causes the formation of <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddies</a> of many different length scales. Most of the kinetic energy of the turbulent motion is contained in the large-scale structures. The energy "cascades" from these large-scale structures to smaller scale structures by an inertial and essentially <a href="/wiki/Inviscid_flow" title="Inviscid flow">inviscid</a> mechanism. This process continues, creating smaller and smaller structures which produces a hierarchy of eddies. Eventually this process creates structures that are small enough that molecular diffusion becomes important and viscous dissipation of energy finally takes place. The scale at which this happens is the <a href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_microscales" title="Kolmogorov microscales">Kolmogorov length scale</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Via this <a href="/wiki/Energy_cascade" title="Energy cascade">energy cascade</a>, turbulent flow can be realized as a superposition of a spectrum of flow velocity fluctuations and eddies upon a <a href="/wiki/Mean_flow" title="Mean flow">mean flow</a>. The eddies are loosely defined as coherent patterns of flow velocity, vorticity and pressure. Turbulent flows may be viewed as made of an entire hierarchy of eddies over a wide range of length scales and the hierarchy can be described by the energy spectrum that measures the energy in flow velocity fluctuations for each length scale (<a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a>). The scales in the energy cascade are generally uncontrollable and highly non-symmetric. Nevertheless, based on these length scales these eddies can be divided into three categories.</p>

<dt><a href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_microscales" title="Kolmogorov microscales">Kolmogorov length scales</a> </dt>
<dd>Smallest scales in the spectrum that form the viscous sub-layer range. In this range, the energy input from nonlinear interactions and the energy drain from viscous dissipation are in exact balance. The small scales have high frequency, causing turbulence to be locally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> and homogeneous.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Taylor_microscale" title="Taylor microscale">Taylor microscales</a> </dt>
<dd>The intermediate scales between the largest and the smallest scales which make the inertial subrange. Taylor microscales are not dissipative scale but pass down the energy from the largest to the smallest without dissipation. Some literatures do not consider Taylor microscales as a characteristic length scale and consider the energy cascade to contain only the largest and smallest scales; while the latter accommodate both the inertial subrange and the viscous sublayer. Nevertheless, Taylor microscales are often used in describing the term “turbulence” more conveniently as these Taylor microscales play a dominant role in energy and momentum transfer in the wavenumber space.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Although it is possible to find some particular solutions of the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a> governing fluid motion, all such solutions are unstable to finite perturbations at large Reynolds numbers. Sensitive dependence on the initial and boundary conditions makes fluid flow irregular both in time and in space so that a statistical description is needed. The <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Kolmogorov" title="Andrey Kolmogorov">Andrey Kolmogorov</a> proposed the first statistical theory of turbulence, based on the aforementioned notion of the energy cascade (an idea originally introduced by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson" title="Lewis Fry Richardson">Richardson</a>) and the concept of <a href="/wiki/Self-similarity" title="Self-similarity">self-similarity</a>. As a result, the <a href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_microscales" title="Kolmogorov microscales">Kolmogorov microscales</a> were named after him. It is now known that the self-similarity is broken so the statistical description is presently modified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Still, a complete description of turbulence remains one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsolved_problems_in_physics" title="Unsolved problems in physics">unsolved problems in physics</a>.</p>
<p>According to an apocryphal story, <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a> was asked what he would ask <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>, given the opportunity. His reply was: "When I meet God, I am going to ask him two questions: Why <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a>? And why turbulence? I really believe he will have an answer for the first."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A similar witticism has been attributed to <a href="/wiki/Horace_Lamb" title="Horace Lamb">Horace Lamb</a> (who had published a noted text book on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamics" title="Hydrodynamics">Hydrodynamics</a>)—his choice being <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">quantum electrodynamics</a> (instead of relativity) and turbulence. Lamb was quoted as saying in a speech to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association for the Advancement of Science</a>, "I am an old man now, and when I die and go to heaven there are two matters on which I hope for enlightenment. One is quantum electrodynamics, and the other is the turbulent motion of fluids. And about the former I am rather optimistic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A more detailed presentation of turbulence with emphasis on high-Reynolds number flow, intended for a general readership of physicists and applied mathematicians, is found in the <a href="/wiki/Scholarpedia" title="Scholarpedia">Scholarpedia</a> articles by Benzi and Frisch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and by Falkovich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many scales of meteorological motions; in this context turbulence affects small-scale motions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Onset_of_turbulence">Onset of turbulence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turbulence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Onset of turbulence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2773" data-file-width="2152" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg/220px-Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg/330px-Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg/440px-Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laminar-turbulent_transition.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The plume from this candle flame goes from laminar to turbulent. The Reynolds number can be used to predict where this transition will take place</div>

<p>The onset of turbulence can be predicted by the <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> of inertial forces to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscous" title="Viscous">viscous</a> forces within a fluid which is subject to relative internal movement due to different fluid velocities, in what is known as a <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> in the case of a bounding surface such as the interior of a pipe. A similar effect is created by the introduction of a stream of higher velocity fluid, such as the hot gases from a flame in air. This relative movement generates fluid friction, which is a factor in developing turbulent flow. Counteracting this effect is the viscosity of the fluid, which as it increases, progressively inhibits turbulence, as more kinetic energy is absorbed by a more viscous fluid. The Reynolds number quantifies the relative importance of these two types of forces for given flow conditions, and is a guide to when turbulent flow will occur in a particular situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>This ability to predict the onset of turbulent flow is an important design tool for equipment such as piping systems or aircraft wings, but the Reynolds number is also used in scaling of fluid dynamics problems, and is used to determine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_similitude" title="Dynamic similitude">dynamic similitude</a> between two different cases of fluid flow, such as between a model aircraft, and its full size version. Such scaling is not linear and the application of Reynolds numbers to both situations allows scaling factors to be developed. A flow situation in which the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> is significantly absorbed due to the action of fluid molecular <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> gives rise to a <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar flow</a> regime. For this the dimensionless quantity the <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a> (<span class="texhtml">Re</span>) is used as a guide.</p>
<p>With respect to <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar</a> and turbulent flow regimes:</p>
<ul>
<li>laminar flow occurs at low Reynolds numbers, where viscous forces are dominant, and is characterized by smooth, constant fluid motion;</li>
<li>turbulent flow occurs at high Reynolds numbers and is dominated by inertial forces, which tend to produce chaotic <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a> and other flow instabilities.</li>
</ul>

<p>While there is no theorem directly relating the non-dimensional Reynolds number to turbulence, flows at Reynolds numbers larger than 5000 are typically (but not necessarily) turbulent, while those at low Reynolds numbers usually remain laminar. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hagen-Poiseuille_equation" title="Hagen-Poiseuille equation">Poiseuille flow</a>, for example, turbulence can first be sustained if the Reynolds number is larger than a critical value of about 2040;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recrit_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recrit-19">[19]</a></sup> moreover, the turbulence is generally interspersed with laminar flow until a larger Reynolds number of about 4000.</p>
<p>The transition occurs if the size of the object is gradually increased, or the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the fluid is decreased, or if the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of the fluid is increased.</p>

<h2><span id="Kolmogorov's_theory_of_1941"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kolmogorov.27s_theory_of_1941">Kolmogorov's theory of 1941<span id="Kolmogorov.27s_theory"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turbulence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Kolmogorov's theory of 1941">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Richardson's notion of turbulence was that a turbulent flow is composed by "eddies" of different sizes. The sizes define a characteristic length scale for the eddies, which are also characterized by flow velocity scales and time scales (turnover time) dependent on the length scale. The large eddies are unstable and eventually break up originating smaller eddies, and the kinetic energy of the initial large eddy is divided into the smaller eddies that stemmed from it. These smaller eddies undergo the same process, giving rise to even smaller eddies which inherit the energy of their predecessor eddy, and so on. In this way, the energy is passed down from the large scales of the motion to smaller scales until reaching a sufficiently small length scale such that the viscosity of the fluid can effectively dissipate the kinetic energy into internal energy.</p>
<p>In his original theory of 1941, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kolmogorov" title="Kolmogorov">Kolmogorov</a> postulated that for very high <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds numbers</a>, the small scale turbulent motions are statistically isotropic (i.e. no preferential spatial direction could be discerned). In general, the large scales of a flow are not isotropic, since they are determined by the particular geometrical features of the boundaries (the size characterizing the large scales will be denoted as <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">L</span>). Kolmogorov's idea was that in the Richardson's energy cascade this geometrical and directional information is lost, while the scale is reduced, so that the statistics of the small scales has a universal character: they are the same for all turbulent flows when the Reynolds number is sufficiently high.</p>
<p>Thus, Kolmogorov introduced a second hypothesis: for very high Reynolds numbers the statistics of small scales are universally and uniquely determined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity" title="Kinematic viscosity">kinematic viscosity</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">ν</span> and the rate of energy dissipation <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">ε</span>. With only these two parameters, the unique length that can be formed by dimensional analysis is</p>

<p>This is today known as the Kolmogorov length scale (see <a href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_microscales" title="Kolmogorov microscales">Kolmogorov microscales</a>).</p>
<p>A turbulent flow is characterized by a hierarchy of scales through which the energy cascade takes place. Dissipation of kinetic energy takes place at scales of the order of Kolmogorov length <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">η</span>, while the input of energy into the cascade comes from the decay of the large scales, of order <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">L</span>. These two scales at the extremes of the cascade can differ by several orders of magnitude at high Reynolds numbers. In between there is a range of scales (each one with its own characteristic length <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>) that has formed at the expense of the energy of the large ones. These scales are very large compared with the Kolmogorov length, but still very small compared with the large scale of the flow (i.e. <span class="texhtml"><i>η</i> ≪ <i>r</i> ≪ <i>L</i></span>). Since eddies in this range are much larger than the dissipative eddies that exist at Kolmogorov scales, kinetic energy is essentially not dissipated in this range, and it is merely transferred to smaller scales until viscous effects become important as the order of the Kolmogorov scale is approached. Within this range inertial effects are still much larger than viscous effects, and it is possible to assume that viscosity does not play a role in their internal dynamics (for this reason this range is called "inertial range").</p>
<p>Hence, a third hypothesis of Kolmogorov was that at very high Reynolds number the statistics of scales in the range <span class="texhtml"><i>η</i> ≪ <i>r</i> ≪ <i>L</i></span> are universally and uniquely determined by the scale <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span> and the rate of energy dissipation <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">ε</span>.</p>

<p>There is considerable evidence that turbulent flows deviate from this behavior. The scaling exponents deviate from the <span class="texhtml"><span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>n</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span></span> value predicted by the theory, becoming a non-linear function of the order <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">n</span> of the structure function. The universality of the constants have also been questioned. For low orders the discrepancy with the Kolmogorov <span class="texhtml"><span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>n</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span></span> value is very small, which explain the success of Kolmogorov theory in regards to low order statistical moments. <p>Since the experimental values obtained for the second order structure function only deviate slightly from the <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">2</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span> value predicted by Kolmogorov theory, the value for <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">p</span> is very near to <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">5</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span> (differences are about 2%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>). Thus the "Kolmogorov −<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">5</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span> spectrum" is generally observed in turbulence. However, for high order structure functions the difference with the Kolmogorov scaling is significant, and the breakdown of the statistical self-similarity is clear. This behavior, and the lack of universality of the <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">C<sub>n</sub></span> constants, are related with the phenomenon of intermittency in turbulence. This is an important area of research in this field, and a major goal of the modern theory of turbulence is to understand what is really universal in the inertial range.</p>
