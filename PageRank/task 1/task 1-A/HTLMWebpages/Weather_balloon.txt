<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Weather balloon</h1>

<p>A <b>weather</b> or <b>sounding balloon</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloon</a> (specifically a type of <a href="/wiki/High-altitude_balloon" title="High-altitude balloon">high-altitude balloon</a>) which carries instruments aloft to send back information on <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> and wind speed by means of a small, expendable measuring device called a <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a>. To obtain <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> data, they can be tracked by <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Direction_finding" title="Direction finding">radio direction finding</a>, or navigation systems (such as the satellite-based <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a>, GPS). Balloons meant to stay at a constant altitude for long periods of time are known as transosondes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_balloon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the first people to use weather balloons was <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Teisserenc_de_Bort" title="Léon Teisserenc de Bort">Léon Teisserenc de Bort</a>, the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologist</a>. Starting in 1896 he launched hundreds of weather balloons from his observatory in <a href="/wiki/Trappes" title="Trappes">Trappes</a>, France. These experiments led to his discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-eb-1">[1]</a></sup> Transosondes, weather balloons with instrumentation meant to stay at a constant altitude for long periods of time to help diagnose radioactive debris from atomic fallout, were experimented with in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_and_equipment">Materials and equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_balloon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Materials and equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The balloon itself produces the <a href="/wiki/Lifting_gas" title="Lifting gas">lift</a>, and is usually made of a highly flexible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latex_(rubber)" title="Latex (rubber)">latex</a> material, though <a href="/wiki/Chloroprene" title="Chloroprene">Chloroprene</a> may also be used. The unit that performs the actual measurements and <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">radio transmissions</a> hangs at the lower end of the string, and is called a <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a>. Specialized radiosondes are used for measuring particular parameters, such as determining the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone</a> concentration. An example of specialized measurements is found in the Tethered Balloon Measurements in Cyprus in 2003, as part of the Preliminary Evaluation of <a class="external text" href="http://www.airquality.dli.mlsi.gov.cy/default.aspx?pageid=1795" rel="nofollow">Air Quality in Cyprus</a>,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The balloon is usually filled with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> due to lower cost, though <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> can also be used. The ascent rate can be controlled by the amount of gas with which the balloon is filled. Weather balloons may reach altitudes of 40 km (25 miles) or more, limited by diminishing pressures causing the balloon to expand to such a degree (typically by a 100:1 factor) that it disintegrates. In this instance the instrument package is usually lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Above that altitude <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">sounding rockets</a> are used, and for even higher altitudes satellites are used.</p>
<h2><span id="Launch_time,_location,_and_uses"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_time.2C_location.2C_and_uses">Launch time, location, and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_balloon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch time, location, and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg/220px-Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg/330px-Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg/440px-Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogen_balloon_at_upper_air.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A hydrogen filled balloon at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Bay" title="Cambridge Bay">Cambridge Bay</a> Upper Air station, Nunavut, Canada</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Weather balloons are launched around the world for observations used to diagnose current conditions as well as by human forecasters and <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">computer models</a> for <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>. About 800<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> locations around the globe do routine releases, twice daily, usually at 0000 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> and 1200 UTC. Some facilities will also do occasional supplementary "special" releases when meteorologists determine there is a need for additional data between the 12-hour routine launches in which time much can change in the atmosphere. Military and civilian government meteorological agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> in the US typically launch balloons, and by international agreements almost all the data are shared with all nations.</p>
<p>Specialized uses also exist, such as for aviation interests, pollution monitoring, photography or videography and research. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Ceiling_balloon" title="Ceiling balloon">pilot balloons</a> (Pibal). Field research programs often use mobile launchers from land vehicles as well as ships and aircraft (usually <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsondes</a> in this case). In recent years weather balloons have also been used for scattering human ashes at high-altitude by companies such as Stardust Ashes, founded by <a href="/wiki/Chester_Mojay-Sinclare" title="Chester Mojay-Sinclare">Chester Mojay-Sinclare</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Weather balloon was also used to create the fictional entity 'Rover' during production of the 1960s TV series 'The Prisoner' in Portmeirion, Gwynedd, North Wales, UK in September 1966. This was retained in further scenes shot at MGM Borehamwood UK during 1966-67.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
