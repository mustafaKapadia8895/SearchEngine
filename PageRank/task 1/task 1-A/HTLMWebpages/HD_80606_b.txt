<p><b>HD 80606 b</b> (also <b>Struve 1341 B b</b> or <b>HIP 45982 b</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Eccentric_Jupiter" title="Eccentric Jupiter">eccentric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">hot Jupiter</a> 190 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from the Sun in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Major" title="Ursa Major">Ursa Major</a>. HD 80606 b was discovered orbiting the star <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Struve_1341" title="Struve 1341">Struve 1341</a> B in April 2001 by a team led by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Mayor" title="Michel Mayor">Michel Mayor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Didier_Queloz" title="Didier Queloz">Didier Queloz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eso0114_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-eso0114-6">[5]</a></sup> With a mass 4 times that of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, it is a <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giant</a>. Because the planet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transits</a> the host star its radius can be determined using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_method#Transit_method" title="Transit method">transit method</a> and was found to be slightly smaller than Jupiter's. Its density is slightly less than Earth's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fossey2009_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-fossey2009-2">[2]</a></sup> It has an extremely eccentric orbit like a comet, with its orbit taking it very close to its star and then back out very far away from it every 111 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_80606_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HD80606b_Orbit.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="350" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/HD80606b_Orbit.gif/220px-HD80606b_Orbit.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/HD80606b_Orbit.gif/330px-HD80606b_Orbit.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/HD80606b_Orbit.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HD80606b_Orbit.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<center>Orbital motion of HD 80606 b.</center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>HD 80606 b has the most eccentric orbit of any known planet after <a href="/wiki/HD_20782_b" title="HD 20782 b">HD 20782 b</a>. Its eccentricity is 0.9336, comparable to <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley's Comet">Halley's Comet</a>. The eccentricity may be a result of the <a href="/wiki/Kozai_mechanism" title="Kozai mechanism">Kozai mechanism</a>, which would occur if the planet's orbit is significantly inclined to that of the binary stars. This interpretation is supported by measurements of the <a href="/wiki/Rossiter%E2%80%93McLaughlin_effect" title="Rossiter–McLaughlin effect">Rossiter–McLaughlin effect</a>, which indicate that the planet's orbit may be significantly inclined (by approximately 50 degrees<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup>) to the rotational axis of the star, a configuration which would be expected if the Kozai mechanism were responsible for the orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moutou2009_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-moutou2009-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of this high eccentricity, the planet's distance from its star varies from 0.03 to 0.88 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apastron</a> it would receive an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> similar to that of Earth, while at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periastron" title="Periastron">periastron</a> the insolation would be around 800 times greater, far more than that experienced by Mercury in the Solar System. In 2009, the eclipse of HD 80606 b by its parent star was detected, allowing measurements of the planet's temperature to be made as the planet passed through periastron. These measurements indicated that the temperature rose from around 800 K (500 °C / 1000 °F) to 1500 K (1200 °C / 2200 °F) in just 6 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laughlin2009_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-laughlin2009-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>An observer above the cloud tops of the gas giant would see the parent star swell to 30 times the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_size" title="Apparent size">apparent size</a> of the Sun in our own sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-RAS-10">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The transit was detected using a <a href="/wiki/Celestron" title="Celestron">Celestron</a> 35-cm <a href="/wiki/Schmidt%E2%80%93Cassegrain_telescope" title="Schmidt–Cassegrain telescope">Schmidt–Cassegrain telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fossey2009_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-fossey2009-2">[2]</a></sup> Prior to the large data release of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_Mission" title="Kepler Mission">Kepler Mission</a> in February 2011, HD 80606 b has the longest orbital period of any known transiting planet. It takes 12.1 hours to transit its star.</p>
<p>The transit of 14 January 2010 was partially observed by MOST; but there were equipment failures over part of this time, and the 8 January secondary transit was entirely lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roberts2012_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-roberts2012-11">[10]</a></sup> The midpoint of the next transit is 1 February 2013 11:37 <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">UT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather">Weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HD_80606_b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The planet has wild variations in its weather as it orbits its parent star. Computer models predict the planet heats up 555 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (1,000 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>) in just a matter of hours triggering "shock wave storms" with winds that move 15 times faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>, at 3 miles per second (10800 mph / 17400 km/h / 4800 m/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laughlin2009_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-laughlin2009-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hothothot_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hothothot-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
