<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Indianola, Texas</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Indianola</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Ghost_town" title="Ghost town">ghost town</a> located on <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Calhoun_County,_Texas" title="Calhoun County, Texas">Calhoun County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The community, once the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of Calhoun County, is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Texas" title="Victoria, Texas">Victoria, Texas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_metropolitan_area" title="Victoria metropolitan area">Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>. In 1875, the city had a population of 5,000, but on September 15 of that year, a powerful <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a> struck, killing between 150 and 300 and almost entirely destroying the town. Indianola was rebuilt, only to be wiped out on August 19, 1886, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indianola_Hurricane_of_1886" title="Indianola Hurricane of 1886">another intense hurricane</a>, which was followed by a fire. Indianola was designated a <a href="/wiki/Recorded_Texas_Historic_Landmark" title="Recorded Texas Historic Landmark">Recorded Texas Historic Landmark</a> in 1963, marker number 2642.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indianola,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_immigration_port_of_entry">German immigration port of entry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indianola,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: German immigration port of entry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Prince_Carl_of_Solms-Braunfels" title="Prince Carl of Solms-Braunfels">Prince Carl of Solms-Braunfels</a>, representing the <a href="/wiki/Adelsverein" title="Adelsverein">Adelsverein</a>, selected Indian Point in December, 1844 as port of entry for the Verein colonists from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Prince Solms renamed the port Carlshafen in honor of himself, Count Carl of Castell-Castell<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Count Victor August of Leiningen-Westerburg-Alt-Leiningen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> whom Solms claimed had been christened Carl. Prince Solms' choice of Carlshafen and its inadequate accommodations as a port of entry, as well as the isolated route to New Braunfels, was to keep the Germans from interacting with any Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In February 1845 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Francis_Fisher" title="Henry Francis Fisher">Henry Francis Fisher</a> conspired with <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Armand_Strubberg" title="Friedrich Armand Strubberg">Dr. F. Schubbert</a> to coerce incoming immigrants to sign legal documents disassociating themselves from the Verein and to join Schubbert's colony in <a href="/wiki/Milam_County,_Texas" title="Milam County, Texas">Milam County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Armand_Strubberg" title="Friedrich Armand Strubberg">Friedrich Armand Strubberg</a>.</div>
<p>In May 1846, Meusebach received a letter from Count Castell informing him 4,304 colonists were on their way to Texas. With no funds and no new settlements, the mass of emigrants were stalled at Carlshafen. Meusebach's requests to the Verein for more money, and his warnings at pending bankruptcy for the Verein, brought no results. As a last resort, Meusebach instructed D.H. Klaener to publish the plight in the German news media. Embarrassed by the publicity, the Verein established an inadequate $60,000 letter of credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Meusebach had arranged with the Torrey Brothers for transporting the emigrants inland, but the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> hired the Torrey Brothers for use in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican-American_War" title="Mexican-American War">Mexican-American War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An epidemic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spinal_meningitis" title="Spinal meningitis">spinal meningitis</a> broke out at Carlshafen and spread with the emigrants to <a href="/wiki/New_Braunfels,_Texas" title="New Braunfels, Texas">New Braunfels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fredericksburg,_Texas" title="Fredericksburg, Texas">Fredericksburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Samuel Addition White and William M. Cook founded Indian Point, later Indianola, in August, 1846.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indianola.2C_Texas_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indianola.2C_Texas-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of these immigrants purchased land from Samuel White and established a settlement at Indian Point. The name of the settlement changed to Indianola in 1849 by combining the word "Indian" with "ola," the Spanish word for wave. The German immigrants continued to refer to the community as <i>Carlshaven</i> (Carl's Harbor) in honor of Prince Carl of Solms-Braunfels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wolff_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-wolff-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>From its founding in 1846, Indianola served as a major port, and before the 1875 storm was second only to <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston</a> as Texas's primary port. The town incorporated in 1853. In 1856, the port received cargoes of <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camels</a>, part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army_Camel_Corps" title="US Army Camel Corps">US Army Camel Corps</a> experiment to replace horses and mules as the primary pack animal in the southwestern parts of the country.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, Indianola was twice occupied by <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> troops, in October 1862 and November 1863. During the second occupation, part of a Union infantry regiment moving from Indianola to <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Island" title="Matagorda Island">Matagorda Island</a> <a href="/wiki/69th_Indiana_Infantry_drownings" title="69th Indiana Infantry drownings">drowned in Matagorda Bay</a>.</p>
<p>In 1869, the world's first mechanically-refrigerated shipment of beef left Indianola for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans</a>.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> constructed an anti-aircraft firing range along the Indianola shoreline to train gunners and the facility was used primarily by military personnel from <a href="/wiki/Camp_Hulen" title="Camp Hulen">Camp Hulen</a>, located outside of <a href="/wiki/Palacios,_Texas" title="Palacios, Texas">Palacios</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_storms">Impact of storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indianola,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact of storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Having survived severe storms before, Indianola was almost totally destroyed on 16 September 1875. <i>The New York Times</i> reported in detail on the extensive loss of life and property. Even the lighthouses were swept away<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse_keeper" title="Lighthouse keeper">keepers</a>, including Thomas H. Mayne and Edward Flick Jr. of the East Shoal Lighthouse, killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town was rebuilt but events were repeated in 1886. The destruction served as an object lesson for many residents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston" title="Galveston">Galveston</a>, 100 miles up the Texas coast. However, their calls for a <a href="/wiki/Seawall" title="Seawall">seawall</a> to protect that city went unheeded, and Galveston nearly shared Indianola's fate when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1900" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1900">Galveston Hurricane of 1900</a> struck the island.</p>
<p>A railroad was intended to connect the port of Indianola to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio</a>. After the two storms, discouraged investors abandoned the venture and made Galveston the port of choice. After Galveston's hurricane, shipping traffic recentered over time to inland <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a>.</p>
<p>After the 1886 storm, the county seat was moved to <a href="/wiki/Port_Lavaca,_Texas" title="Port Lavaca, Texas">Port Lavaca</a>. On October 4, 1887, the post office in Indianola was permanently closed and the town declared "dead".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wolff_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-wolff-10">[10]</a></sup> Today, almost nothing remains of the original Indianola, as, due to storm <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, most of the site of the city is now under water. A <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> marker was placed on the shore at the nearest point to the Indianola courthouse, now 300 feet (about 90 meters) away in Matagorda Bay. It reads, "Calhoun County Courthouse. Edward Beaumont, Architect 1859. During the Storms of 1875 and 1886, precious lives were saved within its walls of shell, concrete, and lime. Abandoned 1886." The site is also home to a statue of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle" title="René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle">René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</a>.</p>
<p>Indianola is now the site of a small <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated</a> fishing village.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indianola,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indianola,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Hand" title="Elizabeth Hand">Elizabeth Hand</a>'s novel <i>Aestival Tide</i>, a reconstructed Indianola is featured as the lowermost level of the central ziggurat in the dome city of Araboth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the novel <i>Matagorda</i> by author <a href="/wiki/Louis_L%27Amour" title="Louis L'Amour">Louis L'Amour</a>, much of the action took place in Indianola, Texas on the day it was destroyed by the 1875 hurricane. The courthouse, the site of which is now underwater out in the bay, was featured as the last refuge of the townspeople.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Part I of <i><a href="/wiki/Isaac%27s_Storm" title="Isaac's Storm">Isaac's Storm</a></i>, in the chapter "Galveston: An Absurd Delusion" (pages 79–84), <a href="/wiki/Erik_Larson_(author)" title="Erik Larson (author)">Erik Larson</a> describes the two storms that ravaged Indianola and the significance of these storms for <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a>, leading up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_storm_of_1900" title="Galveston storm of 1900">Galveston storm of 1900</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He notes: "At first, Galveston's leading men seemed to grasp the significance of the Indianola storms. Anyone who looked at a map could see that Galveston was even more vulnerable to destruction than Indianola. It had no picket of barrier islands to shelter it, no buffer of mainland prairie. The city faced the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf</a> head-on." He further observes: "Six weeks after the second Indianola storm, a group of thirty prominent Galveston residents calling themselves the Progressive Association met and resolved to build a seawall", and "The city's <i>Evening Tribune</i> endorsed the plan". However, although "The state eventually did authorize a bond to pay for the work," the city's engineer E.M. Hartwick observed, "this was some months after the flood, and by then the attitude was, Oh, we'll never get another one--and they didn't build."</p>
<p>Larson criticizes meteorologist <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Cline" title="Isaac Cline">Isaac Cline</a> (among others) for this failure. For example, Larson cites statements Cline published in the July 1891 Galveston <i>News</i> article, "West India Hurricanes":<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>If Galveston had any lingering anxiety about its failure to erect a seawall Isaac's 1891 article would have eased them. It was here that he belittled hurricane fears as the artifacts of 'an absurd delusion.' He was especially confident about storm surges. Galveston would escape harm, he argued, because the incoming water would spread first over the vast lowlands</i> behind <i>Galveston, on the Texas mainland north of the bay where the land was even closer to sea level.</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><i>'It would be impossible,' he wrote, 'for any cyclone to create a storm wave which could materially injure the city.'</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Further, in Part III of <i>Isaac's Storm</i>, in a chapter titled "Ritter's Cafe: <i>You Can't Frighten Me</i>", Larson publishes the recollections of Mollie Cohen, whose impressions of the Galveston Storm of 1900 reminded her of the second Indianola hurricane:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>'We had a storm like this in '86,' Mollie said, referring to the winds and rain that had reached Galveston from the last of the big Indianola hurricanes. 'My father's store on Market Street was flooded,' she said, casually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></i></dd>
</dl>

<p>Singer/songwriter, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Robison" title="Charlie Robison">Charlie Robison</a>, included a song titled "Indianola" on the 1998 album <i>Life of the Party</i> that is a specific reference to Indianola, Texas. The song begins from the perspective of a German immigrant approaching Indianola by sea and chronicling details of the narrator's family. The narrator and his cousin attempt to traverse the South to join the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, though they encounter Rebel resistance in Indianola, TX. The next scene of the folk song briefly addresses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_market_crash_of_1929" title="Stock market crash of 1929">stock market crash of 1929</a> in an indirect fashion, noting that little change occurred in their respective lives other than rust's accumulating on wagon wheels. While this verse is brief in regards to words and time, it denotes a subtle and increasing disaffection in the American/Texan experience, adversely affected by seemingly non-relevant incidences. The storyline then progresses to World War II, introducing an internal struggle between the call of duty of the United States of America and its armed conflict with Germany, the ancestral point of origin of many Americans. The overall sentiment of the song begins as a story of new promises followed by a series of fictional, though personally carved facts, that illustrate a common experience for the individualist. Texas exemplifies a spirit that is not only uniquely a Texan story, but an American one, thrusting the individual against the external influence that would assume its right to assert its own values. This, however, should not be confused with contemporary movements deviating from the spirit of this song.</p>
<p>Singer/songwriter <a class="external text" href="http://www.brianburnsmusic.com/about.php" rel="nofollow">Brian Burns</a> included the song "Indianola" on his 2004 album "<a class="external text" href="http://www.brianburnsmusic.com/music.php" rel="nofollow">Heavy Weather</a>". The <a class="external text" href="http://www.brianburnsmusic.com/song.php?songinfo=040515" rel="nofollow">lyrics</a> of the song chronicle the full life cycle of Indianola with verses from the points of view of a personified Matagorda bay, an immigrant who helped build the city (Johann), a worker who came to help "bury the lost souls who perished that day" (Isaac), and finally the songwriter himself visiting the site (Brian). It is unclear whether the "Johann" mentioned in verse three refers to the Johann Dethardt, the first ship to bring settlers into the area, or Johann Swartz, the owner/builder of the first home erected in what would become Indianola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
