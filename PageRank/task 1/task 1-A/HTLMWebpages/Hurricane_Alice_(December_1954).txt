<p><b>Hurricane Alice</b> is the only known <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> to span two calendar years and one of only two named Atlantic tropical cyclones, along with <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> of 2005, to do so. The twelfth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> and the eighth hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Alice developed on December 30, 1954 from a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> of low pressure in the central Atlantic Ocean in an area of unusually favorable conditions. The storm moved southwestward and gradually strengthened to reach hurricane status. After passing through the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> on January 2, 1955, Alice reached peak winds of 90 mph (150 km/h) before encountering cold air and turning to the southeast. It dissipated on January 6 over the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>.</p>
<p>Alice produced heavy rainfall and moderately strong winds across several islands along its path. <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">Saba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a> were affected the most, with total damage amounting to $623,500 (1955 USD). There was an earlier hurricane named <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(June_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (June 1954)">Alice</a> in the season. Operationally, lack of definitive data prevented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">U.S. Weather Bureau</a> from declaring the system a hurricane until January 2. It received the name <i>Alice</i> in early 1955, though re-analysis of the data supported extending its track to the previous year, resulting in two tropical cyclones of the same name in one season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alice2_1954_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1669" data-file-width="2700" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Alice2_1954_track.png/220px-Alice2_1954_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Alice2_1954_track.png/330px-Alice2_1954_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Alice2_1954_track.png/440px-Alice2_1954_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alice2_1954_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">cold front</a> moved southeastward from the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> on December 23, 1954, and passed over <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> two days later. A strong <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> developed behind the cold front, and on December 26 a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> extended southward from the cold front. The anticyclone moved southeastward as the trough and cold front moved eastward. The trough gradually decelerated as high pressures altered the upper level flow to that of an easterly flow. Convection increased over the trough as it moved over relatively warm waters, about 1 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> above normal, and began undergoing <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> after a circulation began to develop along the northern portion of the trough. The system continued to organize as it moved to the west, and based on three ship reports confirming a closed circulation, it is estimated the system developed into a tropical depression on December 31 while located 770 miles (1245 km) east-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbuda" title="Barbuda">Barbuda</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>With a narrow ridge of high pressure to its north, the depression moved west-southwestward. Atmospheric conditions were unusually favorable for the time of the year due to the ridge of high pressure preventing cold air from reaching the central Atlantic Ocean. This allowed the depression to intensify into a tropical storm later on December 30, based on direct observations. There were few direct observations for the following days, but it is estimated the storm intensified into a hurricane on December 31 while located about 475 miles (770 km) east-northeast of Barbuda. On January 1, 1955, a ship within 10 miles (16 km) of the center reported hurricane-force winds and a pressure of 987 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>. Based on this report and several other ship reports, the system was recognized as a tropical low by the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">Weather Bureau Office</a>. The hurricane continued to the southwest, and entered the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> on January 2 after passing between <a href="/wiki/Saint_Martin" title="Saint Martin">Saint Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Barth%C3%A9lemy" title="Saint Barthélemy">Saint Barthélemy</a>. A portion of the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewall</a> moved over Saint Martin and <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">Saba</a>. Based on near-hurricane-force winds from the affected islands, an information bulletin named the system as Hurricane Alice while in the northeastern Caribbean Sea. Throughout its track, Alice was a small tropical cyclone with a diameter of only around 60 miles (95 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Reconnaissance aircraft</a> flew into Hurricane Alice on January 3 and confirmed the existence of the tropical cyclone by reporting winds between 60 and 65 mph (95 to 105 km/h) and a warm core center. It is estimated Alice continued to intensify as it tracked southwestward in the Caribbean Sea, and attained peak winds of 80 mph (130 km/h) while located about 95 miles (150 km) west-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>. While near its peak the hurricane developed a ring of convection around its eye, and Alice maintained peak winds for about 24 hours until cold air from the north weakened it. Extratropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a> occurred north of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on January 3, which weakened the high pressure system to the north of Alice and turned the flow in the Caribbean Sea to that of cold northwesterly winds. Alice turned to the southeast on January 4 and weakened to a tropical storm shortly thereafter. Convection gradually became disorganized, and after weakening to a tropical depression on January 5, Alice dissipated on January 6 while located about 100 miles (160 km) west-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Impact,_naming_and_records"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact.2C_naming_and_records">Impact, naming and records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact, naming and records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:January_3,_1955_Alice.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="367" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/January_3%2C_1955_Alice.PNG/220px-January_3%2C_1955_Alice.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/January_3%2C_1955_Alice.PNG/330px-January_3%2C_1955_Alice.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/January_3%2C_1955_Alice.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:January_3,_1955_Alice.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Surface weather analysis of Hurricane Alice on January 3, 1955</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Subsequent to the confirmation of the existence of Hurricane Alice, the U.S. Weather Bureau Office in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a> warned the islands of the northern <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> to prepare for strong winds and rough seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> Hurricane Alice produced moderate to strong winds across the islands, peaking at 81 mph (130 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Barth%C3%A9lemy" title="Saint Barthélemy">Saint Barthélemy</a> and 75 mph (120 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">Saba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1955mwr_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-1955mwr-2">[2]</a></sup> The hurricane dropped moderate to heavy rainfall across several islands, including a peak of 11.27 inches (286 mm) in 48 hours recorded at Saba. Due to its small size, only a few islands received significant effects from the hurricane. Saint-Barthélemy, Saba, <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sint_Eustatius" title="Sint Eustatius">Sint Eustatius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts" title="Saint Kitts">Saint Kitts</a> reported damage from the hurricane, totaling $623,500 (1955 USD, $4.7 million 2006 USD). Damage was heaviest on Saba and Anguilla, occurring primarily to shipping facilities and crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> 626 houses were destroyed or severely damaged on Anguilla, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wama_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wama-3">[3]</a></sup> The damage was caused mostly by heavy rainfall and rough seas, not the direct action of the wind. The passage of Hurricane Alice greatly troubled the economies of the small affected islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, the outer <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> of the storm alleviated dry conditions on <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> which had persisted since the previous October. No deaths were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1955mwr_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-1955mwr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>This storm formed on December 30; however, in the days before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">satellites</a>, meteorologists had to rely on reports from ships and islands in the Atlantic to locate and track storms. This information was often sketchy; two cyclones, including a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 2</a> hurricane, operationally went undetected in 1954. It was officially recognized as a significant tropical cyclone on January 2, 1955. At the time, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> used the same naming list each year, so the name given to this storm was "Alice" and it was designated as a part of the <a href="/wiki/1955_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1955 Atlantic hurricane season">1955 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. However, it was found during post-storm analysis that the storm had actually formed on December 30, and was instead a part of the <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954 season</a>. Therefore, the season had two storms named "Alice" — the first storm of the season, and the last. Had Alice been discovered before the end of the calendar year, it would have been named Irene, the next name on the 1954 list. Some reports named this storm <i>Alice2</i> to avoid confusion with the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(June_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (June 1954)">earlier Alice</a> from June 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bonus-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alice formed later in the calendar year than any Atlantic tropical cyclone on record, forming six hours later in the season than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Zeta (2005)">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> in <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>. Alice was one of only eight Atlantic tropical cyclones to form in the month of December. Alice was the first of two Atlantic tropical cyclones to exist in two calendar years, the other being 2005's Zeta. The hurricane was also one of only six tropical or subtropical cyclones to exist in the month of January and the strongest to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
