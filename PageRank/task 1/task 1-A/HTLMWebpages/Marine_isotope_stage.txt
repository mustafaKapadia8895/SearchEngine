<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Marine isotope stage</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Marine isotope stages</b> (<b>MIS</b>), <b>marine oxygen-isotope stages</b>, or <b>oxygen isotope stages</b> (<b>OIS</b>), are alternating warm and cool periods in the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a>, deduced from <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope_ratio_cycle" title="Oxygen isotope ratio cycle">oxygen isotope data</a> reflecting changes in temperature derived from data from deep sea <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">core samples</a>. Working backwards from the present, which is MIS 1 in the scale, stages with even numbers have high levels of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-18" title="Oxygen-18">oxygen-18</a> and represent cold glacial periods, while the odd-numbered stages are troughs in the oxygen-18 figures, representing warm <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> intervals. The data are derived from <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a> (<a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>) remains in drilled marine <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> cores, <a href="/wiki/Sapropel" title="Sapropel">sapropels</a>, and other data that reflect historic climate; these are called <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxies</a>.</p>
<p>The MIS timescale was developed from the pioneering work of <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Emiliani" title="Cesare Emiliani">Cesare Emiliani</a> in the 1950s, and is now widely used in <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> and other fields to express dating in the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> period (the last 2.6 million years), as well as providing the fullest and best data for that period for <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a> or the study of the early climate of the Earth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> representing "the standard to which we correlate other Quaternary climate records".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Emiliani's work in turn depended on <a href="/wiki/Harold_Urey" title="Harold Urey">Harold Urey</a>'s prediction in a paper of 1947 that the ratio between oxygen-18 and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-16" title="Oxygen-16">oxygen-16</a> isotopes in <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, the main chemical component of the shells and other hard parts of a wide range of marine organisms, should vary depending on the prevailing water temperature in which the calcite was formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over 100 stages have been identified, going currently back some 6 million years, and the scale may in future reach back up to 15 mya. Some stages, in particular MIS 5, are divided into sub-stages, such as "MIS 5a", with 5 a, c, and e being warm and b and d cold. A numeric system for referring to "horizons" (events rather than periods) may also be used, with for example MIS 5.5 representing the peak point of MIS 5e, and 5.51, 5.52 etc. representing the peaks and troughs of the record at a still more detailed level. For more recent periods, increasingly precise resolution of timing continues to be developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_a_timescale">Developing a timescale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_isotope_stage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Developing a timescale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_glaciation" title="Timeline of glaciation">Timeline of glaciation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1714" data-file-width="1714" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg/220px-2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg/330px-2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg/440px-2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2000_core-repository02_hg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A store of core samples</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1957 Emiliani moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a> to have access to core-drilling ships and equipment, and began to drill in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and collect core data. A further important advance came in 1967, when <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Shackleton" title="Nicholas Shackleton">Nicholas Shackleton</a> suggested that the fluctuations over time in the marine isotope ratios that had become evident by then were caused not so much by changes in water temperature, as Emiliani thought, but mainly by changes in the volume of ice-sheets, which when they expanded took up the lighter oxygen-16 isotypes in preference to the heavier oxygen-18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The cycles in the isotope ratio were found to correspond to <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion#Terrestrial" title="Ecoregion">terrestrial</a> evidence of glacials and interglacials. A graph of the entire series of stages then revealed unsuspected advances and retreats of ice and also filled in the details of the <a href="/wiki/Stadial" title="Stadial">stadials and interstadials</a>.</p>
<p>More recent <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> samples of today's glacial ice substantiated the cycles through studies of ancient <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> deposition. Currently a number of methods are making additional detail possible. Matching the stages to named periods proceeds as new dates are discovered and new regions are explored geologically. The marine isotopic records appear more complete and detailed than any terrestrial equivalents, and have enabled a timeline of glaciation for the <a href="/wiki/Plio-Pleistocene" title="Plio-Pleistocene">Plio-Pleistocene</a> to be identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is now believed that changes in the size of the major <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> such as the historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurentide_ice_sheet" title="Laurentide ice sheet">Laurentide ice sheet</a> of North America are the main factor governing variations in the oxygen isotope ratios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MIS data also matches the astronomical data of <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> or the effects of variations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> caused by cyclical slight changes in the tilt of the earth's axis of rotation – the "orbital theory". Indeed, that the MIS data matched Milankovich's theory, which he formed during World War I, so well was a key factor in the theory gaining general acceptance, despite some remaining problems at certain points, notably the so-called <a href="/wiki/100,000-year_problem" title="100,000-year problem">100,000-year problem</a>. For relatively recent periods data from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiocarbon" title="Radiocarbon">radiocarbon</a> dating and <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a> also support the MIS data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The sediments also acquire <a href="/wiki/Rock_magnetism#Depositional_remanent_magnetization_.28DRM.29" title="Rock magnetism">depositional remanent magnetization</a> which allows them to be correlated with earth's <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">geomagnetic reversals</a>. For older core samples, individual annual depositions cannot usually be distinguished, and dating is taken from the geomagnetic information in the cores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other information, especially as to the ratios of gases such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the atmosphere, is provided by analysis of <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>.</p>
<p>The <b>SPECMAP</b> Project, funded by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>, has produced one standard chronology for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope" title="Oxygen isotope">oxygen isotope</a> records, although there are others. This high resolution chronology was derived from several isotopic records, the composite curve was then smoothed, filtered and tuned to the known cycles of the astronomical variables. The use of a number of isotopic profiles was designed to eliminate 'noise' errors, that could have been contained within a single isotopic record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Another large research project funded by the US government in the 1970s and 1980s was <a href="/wiki/Climate:_Long_range_Investigation,_Mapping,_and_Prediction" title="Climate: Long range Investigation, Mapping, and Prediction">Climate: Long range Investigation, Mapping, and Prediction</a> (CLIMAP), which to a large degree succeeded in its aim of producing a map of the global climate at the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, some 18,000 years ago, with some of the research also directed at the climate some 120,000 years ago, during the last interglacial. The theoretical advances and greatly improved data available by the 1970s enabled a "grand synthesis" to be made, best known from the 1976 paper <i>Variations in the earth’s orbit: pacemaker of the ice ages</i> (in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>), by J.D. Hays, Shackleton and <a href="/wiki/John_Imbrie" title="John Imbrie">John Imbrie</a>, which is still very widely accepted today, and covers the MIS timescale and the causal effect of the orbital theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010 the Subcommission on Quaternary Stratigraphy of the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Stratigraphy" title="International Commission on Stratigraphy">International Commission on Stratigraphy</a> dropped other lists of MIS dates and started using the Lisiecki &amp; Raymo (2005) LR04 Benthic Stack, as updated. This was compiled by <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_Lisiecki" title="Lorraine Lisiecki">Lorraine Lisiecki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maureen_Raymo" title="Maureen Raymo">Maureen Raymo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The following are the <b>start dates</b> of the most recent MIS, in <a href="/wiki/Year#Symbols_y_and_yr" title="Year">kya</a> (thousands of years ago). (1994), with the figures in parentheses alternative estimates from Martinson et al. for stage 4 and for the others the SPECMAP figures in Imbrie et al. (1984). For stages 1–16 the SPECMAP figures are within 5 kya of the figures given here. All figures up to MIS 21 are taken from Aitken &amp; Stokes, Table 1.4, except for the sub-stages of MIS 5, which are from Wright's Table 1.1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
