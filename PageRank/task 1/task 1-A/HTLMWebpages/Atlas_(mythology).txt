<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <b>Atlas</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἄτλας</span> ; <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>: <span lang="ber" xml:lang="ber">ⴰⵜⵍⴰⵙ</span> ) was a <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titan</a> condemned to hold up the sky for eternity after the <a href="/wiki/Titanomachy" title="Titanomachy">Titanomachy</a>. Although associated with various places, he became commonly identified with the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> in northwest Africa (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Atlas was the son of the Titan <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(mythology)" title="Iapetus (mythology)">Iapetus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Oceanid</a> <a href="/wiki/Asia_(mythology)" title="Asia (mythology)">Asia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Clymene_(mythology)" title="Clymene (mythology)">Clymene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He had many children, mostly daughters, the <a href="/wiki/Hesperides" title="Hesperides">Hesperides</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(mythology)" title="Hyades (mythology)">Hyades</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades_(Greek_mythology)" title="Pleiades (Greek mythology)">Pleiades</a>, and the nymph <a href="/wiki/Calypso_(mythology)" title="Calypso (mythology)">Calypso</a> who lived on the island <a href="/wiki/Ogygia" title="Ogygia">Ogygia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to the ancient Greek poet <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>, Atlas stood at the ends of the earth towards the <a href="/wiki/Hesperides" title="Hesperides">west</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Robert Graves's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Greek_Myths" title="The Greek Myths">The Greek Myths</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Pelasgians" title="Pelasgians">Pelasgians</a> believed the creator goddess <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Eurynome_(disambiguation)" title="Eurynome (disambiguation)">Eurynome</a> assigned Atlas and <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(mythology)" title="Phoebe (mythology)">Phoebe</a> to govern the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_(mythology)" title="Moon (mythology)">moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Hyginus" title="Gaius Julius Hyginus">Hyginus</a> emphasises the primordial nature of Atlas by making him the son of <a href="/wiki/Aether_(mythology)" title="Aether (mythology)">Aether</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>" means "Sea of Atlas", while "<a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantis</a>" means "island of Atlas".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of the name <i>Atlas</i> is uncertain. <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> took pleasure in translating etymologies of Greek names by combining them with adjectives that explained them: for Atlas his adjective is <i>durus</i>, "hard, enduring",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which suggested to George Doig<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> that Virgil was aware of the Greek τλῆναι "to endure"; Doig offers the further possibility that Virgil was aware of <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>'s remark that the native North African name for this mountain was <i>Douris</i>. Since the Atlas mountains rise in the region inhabited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berbers</a>, it has been suggested that the name might be taken from one of the <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>, from a word compounded by the Berber pronunciation "antel + as" Which means " The sun graveyard ".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditionally historical linguists etymologize the Ancient Greek word Ἄτλας (<a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a>: Ἄτλαντος) as comprised from copulative α- and the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> root <i>*telh₂-</i> 'to uphold, support' (whence also τλῆναι), and which was later reshaped to an nt-stem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beekes_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beekes-11">[11]</a></sup> However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Beekes" title="Robert Beekes">Robert Beekes</a> argues that it cannot be expected that this ancient Titan carries an Indo-European name, and that the word is of <a href="/wiki/Pre-Greek_substrate" title="Pre-Greek substrate">Pre-Greek</a> origin, and such words often end in <i>-ant</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beekes_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beekes-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Punishment">Punishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Punishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Atlas and his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menoetius_(mythology)" title="Menoetius (mythology)">Menoetius</a> sided with the Titans in their war against the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Olympians" title="Twelve Olympians">Olympians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Titanomachy" title="Titanomachy">Titanomachy</a>. When the Titans were defeated, many of them (including Menoetius) were confined to <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> condemned Atlas to stand at the western edge of <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia (the Earth)</a> and hold up the sky on his shoulders, to prevent the two from resuming their primordial embrace. Thus, he was <i>Atlas Telamon</i>, "enduring Atlas," and became a doublet of <a href="/wiki/Coeus" title="Coeus">Coeus</a>, the embodiment of the celestial axis around which the heavens revolve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common misconception today is that Atlas was forced to hold the Earth on his shoulders, but Classical art shows Atlas holding the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_spheres" title="Celestial spheres">celestial spheres</a>, not a <a href="/wiki/Globe" title="Globe">globe</a>; the solidity of the marble globe borne by the renowned <a href="/wiki/Farnese_Atlas" title="Farnese Atlas">Farnese Atlas</a> may have aided the conflation, reinforced in the 16th century by the developing usage of <i>atlas</i> to describe a corpus of <a href="/wiki/Atlas" title="Atlas">terrestrial maps</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In a late story,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> a giant named Atlas tried to drive a wandering <a href="/wiki/Perseus" title="Perseus">Perseus</a> from the place where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_mountains" title="Atlas mountains">Atlas mountains</a> now stand. In <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s telling,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Perseus revealed <a href="/wiki/Medusa" title="Medusa">Medusa</a>'s head, turning Atlas to stone (those very mountains) when Atlas tried to drive him away, because Perseus, who went there accidentally and asked Atlas for hospitality, named himself a son of Zeus and a prophecy said that a son of Zeus would steal the golden apples from Atlas' orchard. As is not uncommon in myth, this account cannot be reconciled with the far more common stories of Atlas' dealings with <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>, another son of Zeus, who was Perseus' great-grandson and who sought for the golden apples.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, the first king of <a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantis</a> was also named <b>Atlas</b>, but that Atlas was a son of <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> and the mortal woman Cleito.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">euhemerist</a> origin for Atlas was as a legendary Atlas, king of Mauretania, an expert astronomer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Encounter_with_Heracles">Encounter with Heracles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Encounter with Heracles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Labours" title="The Twelve Labours">Twelve Labors</a> of the hero <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> was to fetch some of the golden apples which grow in <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>'s garden, tended by Atlas' daughters, the <a href="/wiki/Hesperides" title="Hesperides">Hesperides</a>, and guarded by the dragon <a href="/wiki/Ladon_(mythology)" title="Ladon (mythology)">Ladon</a>. Heracles went to Atlas and offered to hold up the heavens while Atlas got the apples from his daughters.</p>
<p>Upon his return with the apples, however, Atlas attempted to trick Heracles into carrying the sky permanently by offering to deliver the apples himself, as anyone who purposely took the burden must carry it forever, or until someone else took it away. Heracles, suspecting Atlas did not intend to return, pretended to agree to Atlas' offer, asking only that Atlas take the sky again for a few minutes so Heracles could rearrange his cloak as padding on his shoulders. When Atlas set down the apples and took the heavens upon his shoulders again, Heracles took the apples and ran away.</p>
<p>In some versions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Heracles instead built the two great <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a> to hold the sky away from the earth, liberating Atlas much as he liberated <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etruscan_Aril">Etruscan Aril</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etruscan Aril">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The identifying name <i>Aril</i> is inscribed on two 5th-century Etruscan bronze items, a mirror from <a href="/wiki/Vulci" title="Vulci">Vulci</a> and a ring from an unknown site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Both objects depict the encounter with Atlas of <a href="/wiki/Hercle" title="Hercle">Hercle</a>, the Etruscan <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>, identified by the inscription; they represent rare instances where a figure from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> is imported into <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_mythology" title="Etruscan mythology">Etruscan mythology</a>, but the name is not. The Etruscan name <i>aril</i> is etymologically independent.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_influence">Cultural influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cultural influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Atlas_(disambiguation)" title="Atlas (disambiguation)">Atlas (disambiguation)</a></div>
<p>Atlas' best-known cultural association is in <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a>. The first publisher to associate the Titan Atlas with a group of maps was the print-seller <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Lafreri" title="Antonio Lafreri">Antonio Lafreri</a>, on the engraved title-page he applied to his <i>ad hoc</i> assemblages of maps, <i>Tavole Moderne Di Geografia De La Maggior Parte Del Mondo Di Diversi Autori</i> (1572);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> however, he did not use the word "atlas" in the title of his work, an innovation of <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">Gerardus Mercator</a>, who dedicated his "atlas" specifically "to honour the Titan, Atlas, King of <a href="/wiki/Mauretania" title="Mauretania">Mauretania</a>, a learned philosopher, mathematician, and astronomer"; he actually depicted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(King)" title="Atlas (King)">astronomer king</a>.</p>
<p>In psychology, Atlas is used metaphorically to describe <a href="/wiki/Atlas_personality" title="Atlas personality">the personality of someone whose childhood was characterized by excessive responsibilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
