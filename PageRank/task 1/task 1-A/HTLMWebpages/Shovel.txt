<p>A <b>shovel</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a> for digging, lifting, and moving bulk materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a>.</p>
<p>Most shovels are <a href="/wiki/Hand_tool" title="Hand tool">hand tools</a> consisting of a broad blade fixed to a medium-length handle. Shovel blades are usually made of <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet steel</a> or hard plastics and are very strong. Shovel handles are usually made of wood (especially specific varieties such as <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">ash</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass-reinforced_plastic" title="Glass-reinforced plastic">glass-reinforced plastic</a> (fibreglass).</p>
<p>Hand shovel blades made of sheet steel usually have a folded seam or hem at the back to make a socket for the handle. This fold also commonly provides extra rigidity to the blade. The handles are usually <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">riveted</a> in place. A T-piece is commonly fitted to the end of the handle to aid grip and control where the shovel is designed for moving soil and heavy materials. These designs can all be easily <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass-produced</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>shovel</i> also applies to larger excavating machines called <a href="/wiki/Power_shovel" title="Power shovel">power shovels</a>, which serve the same purpose—digging, lifting, and moving material. Modern power shovels descend from <a href="/wiki/Steam_shovel" title="Steam shovel">steam shovels</a>. <a href="/wiki/Loader_(equipment)" title="Loader (equipment)">Loaders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Excavator" title="Excavator">excavators</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Backhoe" title="Backhoe">backhoes</a>) perform similar work, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etic" title="Etic">etically</a> speaking, but are not classified as shovels <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emic" title="Emic">emically</a>.</p>
<p>Hand shovels have been adapted for many different tasks and environments. They can be optimized for a single task or designed as cross-over or compromise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multitasking" title="Multitasking">multitaskers</a>. It's very useful in agriculture. Examples are given under "<a href="#Types">Types</a>".</p>

<td><b><a href="/wiki/Spade" title="Spade">spade</a></b></td>
<td>A general category of shovels tailored to digging hard ground that must be broken with substantial force before it can be moved. Most spades have sharp or nearly sharp edges, often shaped as triangular points. (The typographical spade symbol, ♠, is a stylized icon derived from this idea.) Some spades lack triangular points but are fairly narrow. They are tailored to <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">lawn</a> cutting and to <a href="/wiki/Transplanting" title="Transplanting">transplanting</a> of bushes and small trees. In fact, their modern mechanized equivalent is also called a <a href="/wiki/Tree_spade" title="Tree spade">tree spade</a>.
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">usage prescriptions</a> prescribe that the words "spade" and "shovel" should be held in contradistinction (piercing and digging [spade] versus scooping and moving [shovel]). <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">Natural language</a> does not widely follow these prescriptions; it more often treats "spade" and "scoop" as contradistinguished subsets under "shovel".</p>
</td>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shovel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> times and earlier a large animal's <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapula</a> (shoulder blade) was often used as a crude shovel or <a href="/wiki/Spade" title="Spade">spade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConciseOxfordDictArchaeol_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConciseOxfordDictArchaeol-1">[1]</a></sup> It is via this connection between shoulder blades and digging blades that the words <i><a href="/wiki/Spatula" title="Spatula">spatula</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spade" title="Spade">spade</a></i> both have <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymologic</a> connection with scapulas.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aa_shoveling_00.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1000" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Aa_shoveling_00.jpg/100px-Aa_shoveling_00.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Aa_shoveling_00.jpg/150px-Aa_shoveling_00.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Aa_shoveling_00.jpg/200px-Aa_shoveling_00.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aa_shoveling_00.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical action when shoveling material from the ground</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Manual shoveling (often in combination with <a href="/wiki/Pickaxe" title="Pickaxe">picking</a>) was the chief means of excavation in construction until <a href="/wiki/Mechanization" title="Mechanization">mechanization</a> via <a href="/wiki/Steam_shovel" title="Steam shovel">steam shovels</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Hydraulics" title="Hydraulics">hydraulic</a> equipment (<a href="/wiki/Excavator" title="Excavator">excavators</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Backhoe" title="Backhoe">backhoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loader_(equipment)" title="Loader (equipment)">loaders</a>) gradually replaced most manual shoveling. The same is also true of the history of <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarrying</a> and of bulk <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_handling_equipment" title="Material handling equipment">materials handling</a> in industries such as <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steelmaking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stevedore" title="Stevedore">stevedoring</a>. <a href="/wiki/Railroad_car" title="Railroad car">Railroad cars</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hold_(ship)" title="Hold (ship)">cargo holds</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grains</a> were often loaded and unloaded this way. These industries did not always rely <i>exclusively</i> on such work, but such work was a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ubiquitous#Adjective" title="wikt:ubiquitous">ubiquitous</a> part of them. Until the 1950s, manual shoveling employed large numbers of workers. Groups of workers called <b>labor gangs</b> were assigned to whatever digging or bulk materials handling was needed in any given week, and dozens or hundreds of workers with hand shovels would do the kind of rapid excavating or materials handling that today is usually accomplished with powered excavators and loaders operated by a few skilled operators. Thus the <a href="/wiki/Labour_economics" title="Labour economics">cost of labor</a>, even when each individual worker was poorly paid, was a tremendous expense of operations. <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">Productivity</a> of the business was tied mostly to <a href="/wiki/Workforce_productivity" title="Workforce productivity">labor productivity</a>. It still often is even today; but in the past it was even more so. In industrial and commercial materials handling, hand shoveling was later replaced not only with loaders and backhoes.</p>
<p><span id="science_of_shoveling"></span>Given the central importance and cost of <a href="/wiki/Manual_labour" title="Manual labour">manual labour</a> in industry in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the [<a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied</a>] "science of shoveling" was something of great interest to developers of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_management" title="Scientific management">scientific management</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Winslow_Taylor" title="Frederick Winslow Taylor">Frederick Winslow Taylor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1911pp64-75_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor1911pp64-75-2">[2]</a></sup> Taylor, with his focus on <a href="/wiki/Time_and_motion_study" title="Time and motion study">time and motion study</a>, took an interest in differentiating the many motions of manual labor to a far greater degree than others tended to. Managers might not care to analyze it (<a href="/wiki/Manual_labour#simplistically_equating_manual_labour_to_low_skill_and_class" title="Manual labour">figuring that brute work is brute work, case closed</a>), and workers might not care to analyze it in any way that encouraged management to take away the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prerogative#Noun" title="wikt:prerogative">prerogative</a> in <a href="/wiki/Craft_production" title="Craft production">craft work</a> for the craftsman to decide the details of his methods. Taylor realized that failing to analyze shoveling practice represented a missed opportunity to discover or synthesize <a href="/wiki/Best_practice" title="Best practice">best practices</a> for shoveling, which could achieve highest productivity (value for dollar spent). It was Taylor and colleagues in the 1890s through 1910s that greatly expanded the existing idea of varied shovel designs with different-sized scoops, one for each material, based on the material's density. Under scientific management, it was no longer acceptable to use the same shovel for shoveling brown coal one day and gravel the next. Taylor advocated the higher <a href="/wiki/Capital_cost" title="Capital cost">capital cost</a> of maintaining two shovels as more than paying for itself through the increase in worker productivity that it would lead to, which would mean less money being spent on <a href="/wiki/Wage" title="Wage">wages</a> for each unit of shoveling work accomplished. Unfortunately, despite Taylor's valid insights about the possibility for applying science to manual labour (including shoveling and the lifting and carrying of heavy objects) and his valid insights about the inequality of talents between individuals, he did not really respect the human dignity of all workers, and tended to be contemptuous of people with low intelligence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1911p59_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor1911p59-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The science of shoveling [that is, hand shoveling] has long since mostly faded out of commercial concern thanks to extensive mechanization, such as bulldozers and other equipment. Humans are once again mostly content to let one coal scoop handle various bulk materials of differing densities.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_industrial_revolution" title="Second industrial revolution">Second industrial revolution</a> around year 1900, the shovel gave way to <a href="/wiki/Heavy_equipment" title="Heavy equipment">heavy equipment</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Excavator" title="Excavator">excavators</a>.</p>
