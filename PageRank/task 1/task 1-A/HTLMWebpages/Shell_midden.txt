<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Midden</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Shell_midden&amp;redirect=no" title="Shell midden">Shell midden</a>)</span></div>

<p>A <b>midden</b> (also <b>kitchen midden</b> or <b>shell heap</b>; from early Scandinavian; Danish: mødding, Swedish regional: mödding)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an old <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">dump for domestic waste</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which may consist of animal <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">human excrement</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanical" title="Botanical">botanical</a> material, <a href="/wiki/Mollusc_shell" title="Mollusc shell">mollusc shells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">sherds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithic_flake" title="Lithic flake">lithics</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Debitage" title="Debitage">debitage</a>), and other <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifacts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecofact" title="Ecofact">ecofacts</a> associated with past human occupation.</p>
<p>The word is of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> via <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> derivation, and is today used by <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeologists</a> worldwide to describe any kind of feature containing waste products relating to day-to-day human life. They may be convenient, single-use pits created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic" title="Nomadic">nomadic</a> groups or long-term, designated dumps used by <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> communities that accumulate over several generations.</p>
<p>These <a href="/wiki/Feature_(archaeology)" title="Feature (archaeology)">features</a>, therefore, provide a useful resource for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist">archaeologists</a> who wish to study the <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a> and habits of past societies. Middens with damp, <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic</a> conditions can even preserve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material">organic</a> remains in deposits as the debris of daily life are tossed on the pile. Each individual toss will contribute a different mix of materials depending upon the activity associated with that particular toss. During the course of deposition sedimentary material is deposited as well. Different mechanisms, from wind and water to animal digs, create a matrix which can also be analyzed to provide seasonal and climatic information. In some middens individual dumps of material can be discerned and analysed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shell_middens">Shell middens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Shell middens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG/220px-Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG/330px-Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG/440px-Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Whaleback_Shell_Midden_gully_-_20070722_07986.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Whaleback_Shell_Midden" title="Whaleback Shell Midden">Whaleback Shell Midden</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> resulted from oyster harvesting from 200 BC to 1000 AD.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>shell midden</b> or <b>shell mound</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> feature consisting mainly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk">mollusk</a> shells. The Danish term <i>køkkenmøddinger</i> (plural) was first used by <a href="/wiki/Japetus_Steenstrup" title="Japetus Steenstrup">Japetus Steenstrup</a> to describe shell heaps and continues to be used by some researchers. A midden, by definition, contains the debris of human activity, and should not be confused with wind or tide created beach mounds. Some shell middens are processing remains: areas where aquatic resources were processed directly after harvest and prior to use or storage in a distant location.</p>
<p>Some shell middens are directly associated with villages, as a designated village dump site. In other middens, the material is directly associated with a house in the village. Each household would dump its garbage directly outside the house. In all cases, shell middens are extremely complex and very difficult to excavate fully and exactly. The fact that they contain a detailed record of what food was eaten or processed and many fragments of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_tools" title="Stone tools">stone tools</a> and household goods makes them invaluable objects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> study.</p>
<p>Shells have a high <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> content, which tends to make the middens <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a>. This slows the normal rate of decay caused by soil acidity, leaving a relatively high proportion of organic material (food remnants, organic tools, clothing, human remains) available for archaeologists to find.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Sylvester_Morse" title="Edward Sylvester Morse">Edward Sylvester Morse</a> conducted one of the first archaeological excavations of shellmounds in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omori" title="Omori">Omori</a>, Japan in 1877, which led to the discovery of a style of pottery described as "cord-marked", translated as "<a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_pottery" title="Jōmon pottery">Jōmon</a>", which came to be used to refer to the early period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_prehistory" title="Japanese prehistory">Japanese history</a> when this style of pottery was produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall1988_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall1988-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Shell middens were studied in Denmark in the latter half of the 19th century. The Danish word <i>køkkenmødding</i> (kitchen mound) is now used internationally. The English word "midden" (waste mound) derives from the same Old Norse word that produced the modern Danish one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="478" data-file-width="600" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg/220px-TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg/330px-TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg/440px-TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TurtleMound1970_FPS1191.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Turtle_Mound" title="Turtle Mound">Turtle Mound</a> shell midden is the largest on the US East Coast.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Shell middens are found in coastal zones all over the world. Consisting mostly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">mollusc</a> shells, they are interpreted as being the waste products of meals eaten by nomadic groups or hunting parties. Some are small examples relating to meals had by a handful of individuals, others are many metres in length and width and represent centuries of shell deposition. In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, they are known as sambaquis, having been created over a long period between the 6th millennium BC and the beginning of European colonisation.</p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>'s west coast, there are shell middens that run for more than a kilometer along the coast and are several meters deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The midden in <a href="/wiki/Namu,_British_Columbia" title="Namu, British Columbia">Namu, British Columbia</a> is over 9 meters deep and spans over 10,000 years of continuous occupation.</p>
<p>Shell middens created in coastal regions of Australia by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">indigenous Australians</a> exist in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> today. Middens provide evidence of prior occupation and are generally protected from mining and other developments. One must exercise caution in deciding whether one is examining a midden or a beach mound. There are good examples on the <a href="/wiki/Freycinet_Peninsula" title="Freycinet Peninsula">Freycinet Peninsula</a> in Tasmania where wave action currently is combining charcoal from forest fire debris with a mix of shells into masses that storms deposit above high-water mark. Shell mounds near <a href="/wiki/Weipa,_Queensland" title="Weipa, Queensland">Weipa</a> in far north Queensland that are mostly less than 2 meters high (although ranging up to 10 meters high) and a few tens of meters long are claimed to be middens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but are in fact shell cheniers (beach ridges) re-worked by nest mound-building birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shell mounds are also attributed to the creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_hardwood_hammocks" title="Tropical hardwood hammocks">tropical hardwood hammocks</a>, one example being the <a href="/wiki/Otter_mound" title="Otter mound">Otter mound</a> preserve in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, where shell deposits from <a href="/wiki/Calusa" title="Calusa">Calusa</a> natives provided flood free high areas in otherwise large watered areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are instances in which shell middens may have doubled as areas of ceremonial construction or ritual significance. The <a href="/wiki/Woodland_period" title="Woodland period">Woodland period</a> <a href="/wiki/Crystal_River_Archaeological_State_Park" title="Crystal River Archaeological State Park">Crystal River site</a> provides an example of this phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_definitions">Other definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Midden_(disambiguation)" title="Midden (disambiguation)">Midden (disambiguation)</a></div>
<p>The word "midden" is still in everyday use in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a>, and has come by extension to refer to anything that is a mess, including people. This use was also taken to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> by Scottish plantation settlers. In <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, a midden is an outside lavatory, typically in the back yards of terraced houses. Often attached to this small building is an outhouse which houses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dustbin" title="Dustbin">dustbins</a>.</p>
<p>The word is used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farmers" title="Farmers">farmers</a> in Britain to describe the place where farm yard manure from cows or other animals is collected. Grants are sometimes available to protect these from rain to <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_wastewater_treatment" title="Agricultural wastewater treatment">avoid runoff and pollution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the animal kingdom, some species establish ground <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a>, also known as middens, that are used mostly for food storage. For example, the North <a href="/wiki/American_red_squirrel" title="American red squirrel">American red squirrel</a> (<i>Tamiasciurus hudsonicus</i>) usually has one large active midden in each territory with perhaps an inactive or auxiliary midden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A midden may be a regularly used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_toilet" title="Animal toilet">animal toilet</a> area or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunghill" title="Dunghill">dunghill</a>, created by many mammals, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hyrax" title="Hyrax">hyrax</a>, and also serving as a territorial marker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some animals, including some species of fishes, collect foodstuffs with heavy shells that are hard to remove. They may establish sites where rocks or similar items are available as natural <a href="/wiki/Anvil#Anvils_in_ethology_and_general_biology" title="Anvil">anvils</a> on which the animals habitually break open the shells. These discarded shells may accumulate around the anvils in sizable middens, sometimes for generations. Commonly such middens are sited where there is a convenient rock that is an unusual resource in the region.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">Octopus</a> middens are piles of debris that the octopus piles up to conceal the entrance of its den. Octopus middens are commonly made of rocks, shells, and the bones of prey, although they may contain anything the octopus finds that it can move.</p>
<p>Some more currently widespread used terms include garbage pile, garbage dump, waste pile, waste dump, and waste/garbage disposal site. The material (waste, garbage, refuse, rubbish, etc.) may be placed on a pile/mound on level ground or in a hole/pit dug into the ground which may be subsequently covered over with other material, often for sanitary, aesthetic or land reclamation reasons.</p>
