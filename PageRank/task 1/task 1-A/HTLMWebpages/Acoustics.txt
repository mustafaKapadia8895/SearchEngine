<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Acoustics</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Acoustics</b> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> that deals with the study of all <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_wave" title="Mechanical wave">mechanical waves</a> in gases, liquids, and solids including topics such as <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrasound" title="Infrasound">infrasound</a>. A scientist who works in the field of acoustics is an <b>acoustician</b> while someone working in the field of acoustics technology may be called an <a href="/wiki/Acoustical_engineering" title="Acoustical engineering">acoustical engineer</a>. The application of acoustics is present in almost all aspects of modern society with the most obvious being the audio and <a href="/wiki/Noise_control" title="Noise control">noise control</a> industries.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">Hearing</a> is one of the most crucial means of survival in the animal world, and <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a> is one of the most distinctive characteristics of human development and culture. Accordingly, the science of acoustics spreads across many facets of human society—music, medicine, architecture, industrial production, warfare and more. Likewise, animal species such as songbirds and frogs use sound and hearing as a key element of mating rituals or marking territories. Art, craft, science and technology have provoked one another to advance the whole, as in many other fields of knowledge. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bruce_Lindsay" title="Robert Bruce Lindsay">Robert Bruce Lindsay</a>'s 'Wheel of Acoustics' is a well accepted overview of the various fields in acoustics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word "acoustic" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> word ἀκουστικός (<i>akoustikos</i>), meaning "of or for hearing, ready to hear"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and that from ἀκουστός (<i>akoustos</i>), "heard, audible",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which in turn derives from the verb <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%BA%CE%BF%CF%8D%CF%89" title="wikt:ἀκούω">ἀκούω</a> (<i>akouo</i>), "I hear".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Latin synonym is "sonic", after which the term <b>sonics</b> used to be a synonym for acoustics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and later a branch of acoustics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">Frequencies</a> above and below the <a href="/wiki/Audio_frequency" title="Audio frequency">audible range</a> are called "<a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasonic</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrasonic" title="Infrasonic">infrasonic</a>", respectively.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_research_in_acoustics">Early research in acoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early research in acoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="590" data-file-width="620" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg/220px-Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg/330px-Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg/440px-Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental</a> and the first 6 <a href="/wiki/Overtone" title="Overtone">overtones</a> of a vibrating string. The earliest records of the study of this phenomenon are attributed to the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> in the 6th century BC.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 6th century BC, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> wanted to know why some <a href="/wiki/Interval_(music)" title="Interval (music)">combinations of musical sounds</a> seemed more beautiful than others, and he found answers in terms of numerical ratios representing the <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">harmonic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overtone_series" title="Overtone series">overtone series</a> on a string. He is reputed to have observed that when the lengths of vibrating strings are expressible as ratios of integers (e.g. 2 to 3, 3 to 4), the tones produced will be harmonious, and the smaller the integers the more harmonious the sounds. If, for example, a string of a certain length would sound particularly harmonious with a string of twice the length (other factors being equal). In modern parlance, if a string sounds the note C when plucked, a string twice as long will sound a C an octave lower. In one system of <a href="/wiki/Musical_tuning" title="Musical tuning">musical tuning</a>, the tones in between are then given by 16:9 for D, 8:5 for E, 3:2 for F, 4:3 for G, 6:5 for A, and 16:15 for B, in ascending order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (384-322 BC) understood that sound consisted of compressions and rarefactions of air which "falls upon and strikes the air which is next to it...",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> a very good expression of the nature of <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a> motion.</p>
<p>In about 20 BC, the Roman architect and engineer <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> wrote a treatise on the acoustic properties of theaters including discussion of interference, echoes, and reverberation—the beginnings of <a href="/wiki/Architectural_acoustics" title="Architectural acoustics">architectural acoustics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In Book V of his <i><a href="/wiki/De_architectura" title="De architectura">De architectura</a></i> (<i>The Ten Books of Architecture</i>) Vitruvius describes sound as a wave comparable to a water wave extended to three dimensions, which, when interrupted by obstructions, would flow back and break up following waves. He described the ascending seats in ancient theaters as designed to prevent this deterioration of sound and also recommended bronze vessels of appropriate sizes be placed in theaters to resonate with the fourth, fifth and so on, up to the double octave, in order to resonate with the more desirable, harmonious notes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amman_Roman_theatre.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Principles of acoustics have been applied since ancient times : A <a href="/wiki/Roman_theatre_(structure)" title="Roman theatre (structure)">Roman theatre</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Amman" title="Amman">Amman</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The physical understanding of acoustical processes advanced rapidly during and after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_Revolution" title="Scientific Revolution">Scientific Revolution</a>. Mainly <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> (1564–1642) but also <a href="/wiki/Marin_Mersenne" title="Marin Mersenne">Marin Mersenne</a> (1588–1648), independently, discovered the complete <a href="/wiki/Mersenne%27s_laws" title="Mersenne's laws">laws of vibrating strings</a> (completing what Pythagoras and Pythagoreans had started 2000 years earlier). Galileo wrote "Waves are produced by the <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibrations</a> of a sonorous body, which spread through the air, bringing to the tympanum of the <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a> a stimulus which the mind interprets as sound", a remarkable statement that points to the beginnings of physiological and psychological acoustics. Experimental measurements of the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> in air were carried out successfully between 1630 and 1680 by a number of investigators, prominently Mersenne. Meanwhile, Newton (1642–1727) derived the relationship for wave velocity in solids, a cornerstone of <a href="/wiki/Physical_acoustics" title="Physical acoustics">physical acoustics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Principia</a>, 1687).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_of_Enlightenment_and_onward">Age of Enlightenment and onward</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Age of Enlightenment and onward">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eighteenth century saw major advances in acoustics as mathematicians applied the new techniques of calculus to elaborate theories of sound wave propagation. In the nineteenth century the major figures of mathematical acoustics were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helmholtz" title="Helmholtz">Helmholtz</a> in Germany, who consolidated the field of physiological acoustics, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> in England, who combined the previous knowledge with his own copious contributions to the field in his monumental work <i>The Theory of Sound</i> (1877). Also in the 19th century, Wheatstone, Ohm, and Henry developed the analogy between electricity and acoustics.</p>
<p>The twentieth century saw a burgeoning of technological applications of the large body of scientific knowledge that was by then in place. The first such application was <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Clement_Sabine" title="Wallace Clement Sabine">Sabine</a>’s groundbreaking work in architectural acoustics, and many others followed. Underwater acoustics was used for detecting submarines in the first World War. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_recording" title="Sound recording">Sound recording</a> and the telephone played important roles in a global transformation of society. Sound measurement and analysis reached new levels of accuracy and sophistication through the use of electronics and computing. The ultrasonic frequency range enabled wholly new kinds of application in medicine and industry. New kinds of transducers (generators and receivers of acoustic energy) were invented and put to use.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Acoustics is defined by <a href="/wiki/ANSI/ASA_S1.1-2013" title="ANSI/ASA S1.1-2013">ANSI/ASA S1.1-2013</a> as "(a) Science of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a>, including its production, transmission, and effects, including biological and psychological effects. (b) Those qualities of a room that, together, determine its character with respect to auditory effects."</p>
<p>The study of acoustics revolves around the generation, propagation and reception of mechanical waves and vibrations.</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><b><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg" title="The fundamental acoustical process"><img alt="The fundamental acoustical process" data-file-height="85" data-file-width="680" height="85" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg/680px-Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg/1020px-Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg/1360px-Cause-effect_diagram_for_acoustics.svg.png 2x" width="680"/></a></b></dd>

<p>The steps shown in the above diagram can be found in any acoustical event or process. There are many kinds of cause, both natural and volitional. There are many kinds of transduction process that convert energy from some other form into sonic energy, producing a sound wave. There is one fundamental equation that describes sound wave propagation, the <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_wave_equation" title="Acoustic wave equation">acoustic wave equation</a>, but the phenomena that emerge from it are varied and often complex. The wave carries energy throughout the propagating medium. Eventually this energy is transduced again into other forms, in ways that again may be natural and/or volitionally contrived. The final effect may be purely physical or it may reach far into the biological or volitional domains. The five basic steps are found equally well whether we are talking about an <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a>, a submarine using sonar to locate its foe, or a band playing in a rock concert.</p>
<p>The central stage in the acoustical process is wave propagation. This falls within the domain of physical acoustics. In <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluids</a>, sound propagates primarily as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_wave" title="Pressure wave">pressure wave</a>. In solids, mechanical waves can take many forms including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longitudinal_waves" title="Longitudinal waves">longitudinal waves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transverse_waves" title="Transverse waves">transverse waves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_waves" title="Surface waves">surface waves</a>.</p>
<p>Acoustics looks first at the pressure levels and frequencies in the sound wave and how the wave interacts with the environment. This interaction can be described as either a <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">interference</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflection</a> or a mix of the three. If several <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">media</a> are present, a <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> can also occur. Transduction processes are also of special importance to acoustics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_propagation:_pressure_levels">Wave propagation: pressure levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wave propagation: pressure levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure" title="Sound pressure">Sound pressure</a></div>

<p>In fluids such as air and water, sound waves propagate as disturbances in the ambient pressure level. While this disturbance is usually small, it is still noticeable to the human ear. The smallest sound that a person can hear, known as the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_threshold_of_hearing" title="Absolute threshold of hearing">threshold of hearing</a>, is nine orders of magnitude smaller than the ambient pressure. The <a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">loudness</a> of these disturbances is related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_pressure_level" title="Sound pressure level">sound pressure level</a> (SPL) which is measured on a logarithmic scale in decibels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_propagation:_frequency">Wave propagation: frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wave propagation: frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Physicists and acoustic engineers tend to discuss sound pressure levels in terms of frequencies, partly because this is how our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ears" title="Ears">ears</a> interpret sound. What we experience as "higher pitched" or "lower pitched" sounds are pressure vibrations having a higher or lower number of cycles per second. In a common technique of acoustic measurement, acoustic signals are sampled in time, and then presented in more meaningful forms such as octave bands or time frequency plots. Both of these popular methods are used to analyze sound and better understand the acoustic phenomenon.</p>
<p>The entire spectrum can be divided into three sections: audio, ultrasonic, and infrasonic. The audio range falls between 20 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a> and 20,000 Hz. This range is important because its frequencies can be detected by the human ear. This range has a number of applications, including speech communication and music. The ultrasonic range refers to the very high frequencies: 20,000 Hz and higher. This range has shorter wavelengths which allow better resolution in imaging technologies. Medical applications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">ultrasonography</a> and elastography rely on the ultrasonic frequency range. On the other end of the spectrum, the lowest frequencies are known as the infrasonic range. These frequencies can be used to study geological phenomena such as earthquakes.</p>
<p>Analytic instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_analyzer" title="Spectrum analyzer">spectrum analyzer</a> facilitate visualization and measurement of acoustic signals and their properties. The <a href="/wiki/Spectrogram" title="Spectrogram">spectrogram</a> produced by such an instrument is a graphical display of the time varying pressure level and frequency profiles which give a specific acoustic signal its defining character.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transduction_in_acoustics">Transduction in acoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transduction in acoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg/220px-3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg/330px-3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg/440px-3.5_Inch_Speaker.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Transducer" title="Transducer">transducer</a> is a device for converting one form of energy into another. In an electroacoustic context, this means converting sound energy into electrical energy (or vice versa). Electroacoustic transducers include <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrophone" title="Hydrophone">hydrophones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> projectors. These devices convert a sound pressure wave to or from an electric signal. The most widely used transduction principles are <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrostatics" title="Electrostatics">electrostatics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectricity</a>.</p>
<p>The transducers in most common <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeakers</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Woofer" title="Woofer">woofers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tweeter" title="Tweeter">tweeters</a>), are electromagnetic devices that generate waves using a suspended diaphragm driven by an electromagnetic <a href="/wiki/Voice_coil" title="Voice coil">voice coil</a>, sending off pressure waves. <a href="/wiki/Electret_microphone" title="Electret microphone">Electret microphones</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condenser_microphone" title="Condenser microphone">condenser microphones</a> employ electrostatics—as the sound wave strikes the microphone's diaphragm, it moves and induces a voltage change. The ultrasonic systems used in medical ultrasonography employ piezoelectric transducers. These are made from special ceramics in which mechanical vibrations and electrical fields are interlinked through a property of the material itself.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustician">Acoustician</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Acoustician">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An acoustician is an expert in the science of sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many types of acoustician, but they usually have a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">Bachelor's degree</a> or higher qualification. Some possess a degree in acoustics, while others enter the discipline via studies in fields such as <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>. Much work in acoustics requires a good grounding in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>. Many acoustic scientists work in research and development. Some conduct basic research to advance our knowledge of the perception (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">hearing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">psychoacoustics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neurophysiology" title="Neurophysiology">neurophysiology</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noise" title="Noise">noise</a>. Other acoustic scientists advance understanding of how sound is affected as it moves through environments, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Underwater_acoustics" title="Underwater acoustics">Underwater acoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architectural_acoustics" title="Architectural acoustics">Architectural acoustics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Structural_acoustics" title="Structural acoustics">Structural acoustics</a>. Others areas of work are listed under subdisciplines below. Acoustic scientists work in government, university and private industry laboratories. Many go on to work in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustical_Engineering" title="Acoustical Engineering">Acoustical Engineering</a>. Some positions, such as <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(academic_staff)" title="Faculty (academic staff)">Faculty (academic staff)</a> require a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Archaeoacoustics" title="Archaeoacoustics">Archaeoacoustics</a> is the study of sound within archaeology. This typically involves studying the acoustics of archaeological sites and artefacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeroacoustics">Aeroacoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aeroacoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aeroacoustics" title="Aeroacoustics">Aeroacoustics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aeroacoustics" title="Aeroacoustics">Aeroacoustics</a> is the study of noise generated by air movement, for instance via turbulence, and the movement of sound through the fluid air. This knowledge is applied in <a href="/wiki/Acoustical_engineering" title="Acoustical engineering">acoustical engineering</a> to study how to quieten <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>. <a href="/wiki/Aeroacoustics" title="Aeroacoustics">Aeroacoustics</a> is important to understanding how wind <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instruments</a> work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustic_signal_processing">Acoustic signal processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Acoustic signal processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal_processing" title="Audio signal processing">Audio signal processing</a></div>
<p>Acoustic signal processing is the electronic manipulation of acoustic signals. Applications include: <a href="/wiki/Active_noise_control" title="Active noise control">active noise control</a>; design for <a href="/wiki/Hearing_aid" title="Hearing aid">hearing aids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cochlear_implant" title="Cochlear implant">cochlear implants</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echo_cancellation" title="Echo cancellation">echo cancellation</a>; <a href="/wiki/Music_information_retrieval" title="Music information retrieval">music information retrieval</a>, and perceptual coding (e.g. <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> or <a href="/wiki/Opus_(audio_format)" title="Opus (audio format)">Opus</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Architectural acoustics (also known as building acoustics) involves the scientific understanding of how to achieve a good sound within a building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It typically involves the study of speech intelligibility, speech privacy, music quality, and vibration reduction in the built environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioacoustics">Bioacoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Bioacoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioacoustics" title="Bioacoustics">Bioacoustics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bioacoustics" title="Bioacoustics">Bioacoustics</a> is the scientific study of the hearing and calls of animal calls, as well as how animals are affected by the acoustic and sounds of their habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electroacoustics">Electroacoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Electroacoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_Engineering" title="Audio Engineering">Audio Engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sound_reinforcement_system" title="Sound reinforcement system">Sound reinforcement system</a></div>
<p>This subdiscipline is concerned with the recording, manipulation and reproduction of audio using electronics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This might include products such as <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, large scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_address" title="Public address">public address</a> systems or <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> systems in research laboratories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_noise_and_soundscapes">Environmental noise and soundscapes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Environmental noise and soundscapes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_noise" title="Environmental noise">Environmental noise</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">Noise pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noise_control" title="Noise control">Noise control</a></div>
<p>Environmental acoustics is concerned with noise and vibration caused by railways,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> road traffic, aircraft, industrial equipment and recreational activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The main aim of these studies is to reduce levels of environmental noise and vibration. Research work now also has a focus on the positive use of sound in urban environments: <a href="/wiki/Soundscape" title="Soundscape">soundscapes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tranquility" title="Tranquility">tranquility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_acoustics">Musical acoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Musical acoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Musical_acoustics" title="Musical acoustics">Musical acoustics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
</div>
<p>Musical acoustics is the study of the physics of acoustic instruments; the <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal_processing" title="Audio signal processing">audio signal processing</a> used in electronic music; the computer analysis of music and composition, and the perception and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_neuroscience_of_music" title="Cognitive neuroscience of music">cognitive neuroscience of music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychoacoustics">Psychoacoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Psychoacoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">Psychoacoustics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">Psychoacoustics</a> explains how humans respond to sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speech">Speech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Speech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">Speech</a></div>
<p>Acousticians study the production, processing and perception of speech. <a href="/wiki/Speech_recognition" title="Speech recognition">Speech recognition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speech_synthesis" title="Speech synthesis">Speech synthesis</a> are two important areas of speech processing using computers. The subject also overlaps with the disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultrasonics">Ultrasonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Ultrasonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Ultrasonics deals with sounds at frequencies too high to be heard by humans. Specialisms include medical ultrasonics (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">medical ultrasonography</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sonochemistry" title="Sonochemistry">sonochemistry</a>, material characterisation and <a href="/wiki/Underwater_acoustics" title="Underwater acoustics">underwater acoustics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">Sonar</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Underwater_acoustics">Underwater acoustics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Underwater acoustics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Underwater_acoustics" title="Underwater acoustics">Underwater acoustics</a></div>
<p>Underwater acoustics is the scientific study of natural and man-made sounds underwater. Applications include <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> to locate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submarines" title="Submarines">submarines</a>, underwater communication by whales, climate change monitoring by measuring sea temperatures acoustically, sonic weapons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and marine <a href="/wiki/Bioacoustics" title="Bioacoustics">bioacoustics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vibration_and_dynamics">Vibration and dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Vibration and dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">Vibration</a></div>
<p>This is the study of how mechanical systems vibrate and interact with their surroundings. Applications might include: <a href="/wiki/Ground_vibrations" title="Ground vibrations">ground vibrations</a> from railways; <a href="/wiki/Vibration_isolation" title="Vibration isolation">vibration isolation</a> to reduce vibration in operating theatres; studying how vibration can damage health (<a href="/wiki/Vibration_white_finger" title="Vibration white finger">vibration white finger</a>); <a href="/wiki/Vibration_control" title="Vibration control">vibration control</a> to protect a building from <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, or measuring how structure-borne sound moves through buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
