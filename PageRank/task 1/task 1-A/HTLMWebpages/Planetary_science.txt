<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Planetary science</h1>

<p><b>Planetary science</b> or, more rarely, <b>planetology</b>, is the scientific study of <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>), <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary systems</a> (in particular those of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>) and the processes that form them. It studies objects ranging in size from <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">micrometeoroids</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>, aiming to determine their composition, dynamics, formation, interrelations and history. It is a strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> field, originally growing from <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth science</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1994_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor1994-1">[1]</a></sup> but which now incorporates many disciplines, including <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">planetary geology</a> (together with <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cosmochemistry" title="Cosmochemistry">cosmochemistry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_science" title="Atmospheric science">atmospheric science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_planetary_science" title="Theoretical planetary science">theoretical planetary science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Exoplanetology" title="Exoplanetology">exoplanetology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1994_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor1994-1">[1]</a></sup> Allied disciplines include <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">space physics</a>, when concerned with the effects of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> on the bodies of the Solar System, and <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">astrobiology</a>.</p>
<p>There are interrelated observational and theoretical branches of planetary science. Observational research can involve a combination of <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, predominantly with <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> missions using <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a>, and comparative, experimental work in Earth-based laboratories. The theoretical component involves considerable <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulation</a> and mathematical modelling.</p>
<p>Planetary scientists are generally located in the astronomy and physics or Earth sciences departments of universities or research centres, though there are several purely planetary science institutes worldwide. There are several major conferences each year, and a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">peer-reviewed journals</a>. In the case of some exclusive planetary scientists, many of whom are in relation to the study of dark matter, they will seek a private research centre and often initiate partnership research tasks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of planetary science may be said to have begun with the Ancient Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a>, who is reported by <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">Hippolytus</a> as saying</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The ordered worlds are boundless and differ in size, and that in some there is neither sun nor moon, but that in others, both are greater than with us, and yet with others more in number. And that the intervals between the ordered worlds are unequal, here more and there less, and that some increase, others flourish and others decay, and here they come into being and there they are eclipsed. But that they are destroyed by colliding with one another. And that some ordered worlds are bare of animals and plants and all water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philosophumena_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philosophumena-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In more modern times, planetary science began in astronomy, from studies of the unresolved planets. In this sense, the original planetary astronomer would be <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo</a>, who discovered the four largest moons of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, the mountains on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and first observed the <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">rings of Saturn</a>, all objects of intense later study. Galileo's study of the lunar mountains in 1609 also began the study of extraterrestrial landscapes: his observation "that the Moon certainly does not possess a smooth and polished surface" suggested that it and other worlds might appear "just like the face of the Earth itself".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1994b_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor1994b-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Advances in telescope construction and instrumental resolution gradually allowed increased identification of the atmospheric and surface details of the planets. The Moon was initially the most heavily studied, as it always exhibited details on its surface, due to its proximity to the Earth, and the technological improvements gradually produced more detailed lunar geological knowledge. In this scientific process, the main instruments were astronomical <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">optical telescopes</a> (and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_telescopes" title="Radio telescopes">radio telescopes</a>) and finally robotic exploratory <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">spacecraft</a>.</p>
<p>The Solar System has now been relatively well-studied, and a good overall understanding of the formation and evolution of this planetary system exists. However, there are large numbers of unsolved questions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SternPlanetarySoc09_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SternPlanetarySoc09-4">[4]</a></sup> and the rate of new discoveries is very high, partly due to the large number of interplanetary spacecraft currently exploring the Solar System.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disciplines">Disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_astronomy">Planetary astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Planetary astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is both an observational and a theoretical science. Observational researchers are predominantly concerned with the study of the small bodies of the Solar System: those that are observed by telescopes, both optical and radio, so that characteristics of these bodies such as shape, spin, surface materials and weathering are determined, and the history of their formation and evolution can be understood.</p>
<p>Theoretical planetary astronomy is concerned with <a href="/wiki/Analytical_dynamics" title="Analytical dynamics">dynamics</a>: the application of the principles of <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">celestial mechanics</a> to the Solar System and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planetary</a> systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_geology">Planetary geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Planetary geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">Planetary geology</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_solar_terrestrial_planets" title="Geology of solar terrestrial planets">Geology of solar terrestrial planets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icy_moon" title="Icy moon">Icy moon</a></div>
<p>The best known research topics of planetary geology deal with the planetary bodies in the near vicinity of the Earth: the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and the two neighbouring planets: <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. Of these, the Moon was studied first, using methods developed earlier on the Earth.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geomorphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</a></div>
<p>Geomorphology studies the features on planetary surfaces and reconstructs the history of their formation, inferring the physical processes that acted on the surface. Planetary geomorphology includes the study of several classes of surface features:</p>
<ul>
<li>Impact features (multi-ringed basins, craters)</li>
<li>Volcanic and tectonic features (lava flows, fissures, <a href="/wiki/Rille" title="Rille">rilles</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Space_weathering" title="Space weathering">Space weathering</a> - erosional effects generated by the harsh environment of space (continuous micro meteorite bombardment, high-energy particle rain, <a href="/wiki/Impact_gardening" title="Impact gardening">impact gardening</a>). For example, the thin dust cover on the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> is a result of micro meteorite bombardment.</li>
<li>Hydrological features: the liquid involved can range from water to <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, depending on the location within the Solar System.</li>
</ul>
<p>The history of a planetary surface can be deciphered by mapping features from top to bottom according to their <a href="/wiki/Law_of_superposition" title="Law of superposition">deposition sequence</a>, as first determined on terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Steno" title="Nicolas Steno">Nicolas Steno</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphic mapping</a> prepared the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> astronauts for the field geology they would encounter on their lunar missions. Overlapping sequences were identified on images taken by the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_program" title="Lunar Orbiter program">Lunar Orbiter program</a>, and these were used to prepare a lunar <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphic_column" title="Stratigraphic column">stratigraphic column</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geologic_map" title="Geologic map">geological map</a> of the Moon.</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">Geology of the Moon</a></div>
<h4><span id="Cosmochemistry,_geochemistry_and_petrology"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmochemistry.2C_geochemistry_and_petrology">Cosmochemistry, geochemistry and petrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cosmochemistry, geochemistry and petrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cosmochemistry" title="Cosmochemistry">Cosmochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">Geochemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">Petrology</a></div>
<p>One of the main problems when generating hypotheses on the formation and evolution of objects in the Solar System is the lack of samples that can be analysed in the laboratory, where a large suite of tools are available and the full body of knowledge derived from terrestrial geology can be brought to bear. Fortunately, direct samples from the Moon, <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> are present on Earth, removed from their parent bodies and delivered as <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>. Some of these have suffered contamination from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidise" title="Oxidise">oxidising</a> effect of Earth's atmosphere and the infiltration of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, but those meteorites collected in the last few decades from <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> are almost entirely pristine.</p>
<p>The different types of meteorites that originate from the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a> cover almost all parts of the structure of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">differentiated</a> bodies: meteorites even exist that come from the core-mantle boundary (<a href="/wiki/Pallasite" title="Pallasite">pallasites</a>). The combination of geochemistry and observational astronomy has also made it possible to trace the <a href="/wiki/HED_meteorite" title="HED meteorite">HED meteorites</a> back to a specific asteroid in the main belt, <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">4 Vesta</a>.</p>
<p>The comparatively few known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_meteorites" title="Martian meteorites">Martian meteorites</a> have provided insight into the geochemical composition of the Martian crust, although the unavoidable lack of information about their points of origin on the diverse Martian surface has meant that they do not provide more detailed constraints on theories of the evolution of the Martian <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As of July 24, 2013 65 samples of Martian meteorites have been discovered on Earth. Many were found in either Antarctica or the Sahara Desert.</p>
<p>During the Apollo era, in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, 384 kilograms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_sample" title="Lunar sample">lunar samples</a> were collected and transported to the Earth, and 3 <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna</a> robots also delivered <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> samples from the Moon. These samples provide the most comprehensive record of the composition of any Solar System body beside the Earth. The numbers of lunar meteorites are growing quickly in the last few years –<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as of April 2008 there are 54 meteorites that have been officially classified as lunar. Eleven of these are from the US Antarctic meteorite collection, 6 are from the Japanese Antarctic meteorite collection, and the other 37 are from hot desert localities in Africa, Australia, and the Middle East. The total mass of recognized lunar meteorites is close to 50 kg.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysics">Geophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">Space physics</a></div>
<p>Space probes made it possible to collect data in not only the visible light region, but in other areas of the electromagnetic spectrum. The planets can be characterized by their force fields: gravity and their magnetic fields, which are studied through geophysics and space physics.</p>
<p>Measuring the changes in acceleration experienced by spacecraft as they orbit has allowed fine details of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_field" title="Gravity field">gravity fields</a> of the planets to be mapped. For example, in the 1970s, the gravity field disturbances above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">lunar maria</a> were measured through lunar orbiters, which led to the discovery of concentrations of mass, <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(astronomy)" title="Mass concentration (astronomy)">mascons</a>, beneath the Imbrium, Serenitatis, Crisium, Nectaris and Humorum basins.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="1200" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/220px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/330px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/440px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> is deflected by the magnetosphere (not to scale)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>If a planet's <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> is sufficiently strong, its interaction with the solar wind forms a <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> around a planet. Early space probes discovered the gross dimensions of the terrestrial magnetic field, which extends about 10 Earth radii towards the Sun. The <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, a stream of charged particles, streams out and around the terrestrial magnetic field, and continues behind the magnetic tail, hundreds of Earth radii downstream. Inside the magnetosphere, there are relatively dense regions of solar wind particles, the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belts</a>.</p>
<p>Geophysics includes <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tectonophysics" title="Tectonophysics">tectonophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_fluid_dynamics" title="Geophysical fluid dynamics">geophysical fluid dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral_physics" title="Mineral physics">mineral physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geodynamics" title="Geodynamics">geodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_geophysics" title="Mathematical geophysics">mathematical geophysics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysical_surveying" title="Geophysical surveying">geophysical surveying</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">Geodesy</a>, also called <b>geodetics</b>, deals with the measurement and representation of the planets of the Solar System, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitational</a> fields and geodynamic phenomena (<a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">polar motion</a> in three-dimensional, time-varying space. The science of geodesy has elements of both astrophysics and planetary sciences. The shape of the Earth is to a large extent the result of its rotation, which causes its equatorial bulge, and the competition of geologic processes such as the collision of plates and of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">vulcanism</a>, resisted by the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> field. These principles can be applied to the solid surface of Earth (<a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogeny</a>; Few mountains are higher than 10 km (6 mi), few deep sea trenches deeper than that because quite simply, a mountain as tall as, for example, 15 km (9 mi), would develop so much <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> at its base, due to gravity, that the rock there would become <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>, and the mountain would slump back to a height of roughly 10 km (6 mi) in a geologically insignificant time. Some or all of these geologic principles can be applied to other planets besides Earth. For instance on Mars, whose surface gravity is much less, the largest volcano, <a href="/wiki/Olympus_Mons" title="Olympus Mons">Olympus Mons</a>, is 27 km (17 mi) high at its peak, a height that could not be maintained on Earth. The Earth <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">geoid</a> is essentially the figure of the Earth abstracted from its topographic features. Therefore, the Mars geoid is essentially the figure of Mars abstracted from its topographic features. <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">Surveying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">mapping</a> are two important fields of application of geodesy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_science">Atmospheric science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Atmospheric science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_science" title="Atmospheric science">Atmospheric science</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">Global climate model</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="840" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Jupiter.jpg/220px-Jupiter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Jupiter.jpg/330px-Jupiter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Jupiter.jpg/440px-Jupiter.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cloud bands clearly visible on <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is an important transitional zone between the solid planetary surface and the higher rarefied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionisation" title="Ionisation">ionizing</a> and radiation belts. Not all planets have atmospheres: their existence depends on the mass of the planet, and the planet's distance from the Sun — too distant and frozen atmospheres occur. Besides the four <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giant</a> planets, almost all of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planets" title="Terrestrial planets">terrestrial planets</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">Venus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">Mars</a>) have significant atmospheres. Two moons have significant atmospheres: <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>'s moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>'s moon <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Triton" title="Atmosphere of Triton">Triton</a>. A tenuous atmosphere exists around <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>.</p>
<p>The effects of the <a href="/wiki/Planet#Rotation" title="Planet">rotation rate</a> of a planet about its axis can be seen in atmospheric streams and currents. Seen from space, these features show as bands and eddies in the cloud system, and are particularly visible on Jupiter and Saturn.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_planetary_science">Comparative planetary science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comparative planetary science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Comparative_planetary_science" title="Comparative planetary science">Comparative planetary science</a></div>
<p>Planetary science frequently makes use of the method of comparison to give a greater understanding of the object of study. This can involve comparing the dense atmospheres of Earth and Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, the evolution of outer Solar System objects at different distances from the Sun, or the geomorphology of the surfaces of the terrestrial planets, to give only a few examples.</p>
<p>The main comparison that can be made is to features on the Earth, as it is much more accessible and allows a much greater range of measurements to be made. Earth analogue studies are particularly common in planetary geology, geomorphology, and also in atmospheric science.</p>
