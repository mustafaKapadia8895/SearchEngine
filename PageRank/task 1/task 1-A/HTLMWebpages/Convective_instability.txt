<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Convective instability</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <b>convective instability</b> or <b>stability</b> of an <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air mass</a> refers to its ability to resist vertical motion. A <i>stable</i> <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> makes vertical movement difficult, and small vertical disturbances <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damped_harmonic_motion" title="Damped harmonic motion">dampen</a> out and disappear. In an <i>unstable</i> atmosphere, vertical air movements (such as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_lifting" title="Orographic lifting">orographic lifting</a>, where an air mass is displaced upwards as it is blown by <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> up the rising slope of a mountain range) tend to become larger, resulting in turbulent airflow and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convective</a> activity. Instability can lead to significant <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>, extensive vertical <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convective_instability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic" title="Adiabatic">Adiabatic</a> cooling and heating are phenomena of rising or descending air. Rising air expands and cools due to the decrease in air pressure as altitude increases. The opposite is true of descending air; as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> increases, the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of descending air increases as it is compressed. Adiabatic heating and adiabatic cooling are terms used to describe this temperature change.</p>
<p>The adiabatic lapse rate is the rate at which the temperature of a rising or falling air mass lowers or increases per distance of vertical displacement. The ambient or <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate#Environmental_lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">environmental lapse rate</a> is the temperature change in the (non-displaced) air per vertical distance. Instability results from difference between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">adiabatic lapse rate</a> of an air mass and the ambient lapse rate in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allaby2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allaby2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>If the adiabatic lapse rate is <i>lower</i> than the ambient lapse rate, an air mass displaced upward cools <i>less</i> rapidly than the air in which it is moving. Hence, such an air mass becomes <i>warmer</i> relative to the atmosphere. As warmer air is less dense, such an air mass would tend to continue to rise.</p>
<p>Conversely, if the adiabatic lapse rate is <i>higher</i> than the ambient lapse rate, an air mass displaced upward cools <i>more</i> rapidly than the air in which it is moving. Hence, such an airmass becomes <i>cooler</i> relative to the atmosphere. As cooler air is more dense, the rise of such an airmass would tend to be resisted.</p>
<p>When air rises, moist air cools at a lower rate than dry air. That is, for the same vertical movement, a parcel of moist air will be warmer than a parcel of dry air. This is because of the <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> in the air parcel due to expansion cooling. As water vapor condenses, <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> is released into the air parcel. Moist air has more water vapor than dry air, so more latent heat is released into the parcel of moist air as it rises. Dry air does not have as much water vapor, therefore dry air cools at a higher rate with vertical movement than moist air. As a result of the latent heat that is released during water vapor condensation, moist air has a relatively lower adiabatic lapse rate than dry air. This makes moist air generally less stable than dry air (see <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">convective available potential energy</a> [CAPE]). The dry adiabatic lapse rate (for unsaturated air) is 3 °C (5.4 °F) per 1,000 vertical feet (300 m). The moist adiabatic lapse rate varies from 1.1 to 2.8 °C (2.0 to 5.0 °F) per 1,000 vertical feet (300 m).</p>
<p>The combination of <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> and temperature determine the stability of the air and the resulting weather. Cool, dry air is very stable and resists vertical movement, which leads to good and generally clear weather. The greatest instability occurs when the air is moist and warm, as it is in the tropical regions in the summer. Typically, thunderstorms appear on a daily basis in these regions due to the instability of the surrounding air.</p>
<p>The ambient lapse rate differs in different meteorological conditions, but, on average, is 2 °C (3.6 °F) per 1,000 vertical feet (300 m).</p>
