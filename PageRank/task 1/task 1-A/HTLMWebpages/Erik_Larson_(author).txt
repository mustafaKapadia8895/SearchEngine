<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Erik Larson (author)</h1>

<p><b>Erik Larson</b> (born January 3, 1954) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> journalist and author of nonfiction books. He has written a number of bestsellers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-booklist_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-booklist-1">[1]</a></sup> such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil_in_the_White_City" title="The Devil in the White City">The Devil in the White City</a></i> (2003), about the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">1893 World's Columbian Exposition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> and a series of murders by <a href="/wiki/H._H._Holmes" title="H. H. Holmes">H. H. Holmes</a> that were committed in the city around the time of the Fair; <i>The Devil in the White City</i> also won the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Award" title="Edgar Award">Edgar Award</a> in the Best Fact Crime category, among <a href="/wiki/The_Devil_in_the_White_City#Honors" title="The Devil in the White City">other awards</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Larson_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larson was born in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_New_York" title="Freeport, New York">Freeport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natbook_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-natbook-2">[2]</a></sup> He studied Russian history at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> and graduated <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/summa_cum_laude" title="wikt:summa cum laude">summa cum laude</a></i> in 1976. After a year off, he attended the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_Graduate_School_of_Journalism" title="Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism">Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism</a>, graduating in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natbook_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-natbook-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eriklarsonbooks_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-eriklarsonbooks-3">[3]</a></sup> He was inspired to go into journalism after seeing the movie <i><a href="/wiki/All_the_President%27s_Men_(film)" title="All the President's Men (film)">All the President's Men</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercury_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mercury-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism">Journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Larson_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larson's first newspaper job was with the <i>Bucks County Courier Times</i> in <a href="/wiki/Levittown,_Pennsylvania" title="Levittown, Pennsylvania">Levittown, Pennsylvania</a>, where he wrote about murder, witches, environmental poisons, and other "equally pleasant" things. He later became a features writer for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i> magazine</a>, where he is still a contributing writer. His magazine stories have appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Monthly" title="The Atlantic Monthly">The Atlantic Monthly</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harper%27s" title="Harper's">Harper's</a></i>, and other publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercury_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mercury-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Larson has written a number of books, mostly historical nonfiction. In a 2016 interview with the <i>Knoxville Mercury</i>, Larson stated he does all of his own research, asking, "why should I let anybody else have that fun?" He also rejected the idea of trying to imagine or take factual liberties with scenes and conversations from the past, stating that in his work, "anything that appears in quote is something that came from a historical document." He included among his literary inspirations <a href="/wiki/David_McCullough" title="David McCullough">David McCullough</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbara_Tuchman" title="Barbara Tuchman">Barbara Tuchman</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Halberstam" title="David Halberstam">David Halberstam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Lord" title="Walter Lord">Walter Lord</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercury_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-mercury-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching_and_public_speaking">Teaching and public speaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Larson_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Teaching and public speaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larson has taught non-fiction writing at <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_State_University" title="San Francisco State University">San Francisco State University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins Writing Seminars</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon" title="University of Oregon">University of Oregon</a>, and he has spoken to audiences across the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Larson_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larson has lived in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bristol,_Pennsylvania" title="Bristol, Pennsylvania">Bristol, Pennsylvania</a>; <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He and his wife have three daughters. They reside in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and maintain a home in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eriklarsonbooks_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-eriklarsonbooks-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
