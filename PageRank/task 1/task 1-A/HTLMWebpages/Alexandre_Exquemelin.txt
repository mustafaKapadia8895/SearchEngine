<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alexandre Exquemelin</h1>

<p><b>Alexandre Olivier Exquemelin</b> (also spelled <i>Esquemeling</i>, <i>Exquemeling</i>, or <i>Oexmelin</i>) (c. 1645–1707) was a French, Dutch or Flemish writer best known as the author of one of the most important sourcebooks of 17th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">piracy</a>, first published in Dutch as <i>De Americaensche Zee-Roovers</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, by Jan ten Hoorn, in 1678.</p>
<p>Born about 1645, it is likely that Exquemelin was a native of <a href="/wiki/Honfleur" title="Honfleur">Honfleur</a>, France, who on his return from <a href="/wiki/Buccaneer" title="Buccaneer">buccaneering</a> settled in <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">Holland</a>, possibly because he was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a>. In 1666 he was engaged by the <a href="/wiki/French_West_India_Company" title="French West India Company">French West India Company</a> and went to <a href="/wiki/Tortuga_(Haiti)" title="Tortuga (Haiti)">Tortuga</a>, where he worked as an indentured servant for three years. There he enlisted with the <a href="/wiki/Buccaneer" title="Buccaneer">buccaneers</a>, in particular with the band of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morgan" title="Henry Morgan">Henry Morgan</a>, whose confidante he was, probably as a <a href="/wiki/Barber_surgeon" title="Barber surgeon">barber-surgeon</a>, and remained with them until 1674. Shortly afterwards he returned to Europe and settled in Amsterdam where he qualified professionally as a surgeon, his name appearing on the 1679 register of the Dutch Surgeons' Guild. However, he was later once again in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> as his name appears on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muster-roll" title="Muster-roll">muster-roll</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgeon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Cartagena_(1697)" title="Raid on Cartagena (1697)">attack on Cartagena</a> in 1697.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bibliographic legacy of Exquemelin's <i>History of the Bouccaneers of America</i> is complex. It was first published in Dutch (1678), then translated into German (1679), Spanish (1681) and English (1684).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The German translation is a faithful translation of the original Dutch. The Spanish translation adds new material quite freely and without acknowledgment, and mistranslates the Dutch frequently, while the English translation appears to be as much a translation of the Spanish edition, including most of its deviations from the Dutch original. The French translation of 1686 is substantially a new work with many additions, including new pirate biographies and complete rearrangements in some sections incorporating new material of unknown source. Subsequent editions and translations added additional new material and whole biographies.</p>
<p>For a comparison of the 1678 Dutch edition and the 1686 French translation, see the 1974 translation and interpretation by the Danish author and historian Erik Kjærsgaard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For a contemporary reprinting, see Esquemeling, Alexander O., <i>The Buccaneers of America. A true account of the most remarkable assaults committed of late years upon the coasts of <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> by the Buccaneers of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tortuga_(Haiti)" title="Tortuga (Haiti)">Tortuga</a></i> (both English and French), containing also <a href="/wiki/Basil_Ringrose" title="Basil Ringrose">Basil Ringrose</a>’s account of the dangerous voyage and bold assaults of Captain <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Sharp" title="Bartholomew Sharp">Bartholomew Sharp</a> and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Benchley" title="Peter Benchley">Peter Benchley</a>, in his book <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Island_(1979_novel)" title="The Island (1979 novel)"><i>The Island</i></a>, referred to Exquemelin at length, having used his work in his research.</p>
