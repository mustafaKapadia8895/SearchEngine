<p>The <b>Federal Aviation Administration</b> (<b>FAA</b>) of the United States is a national authority with powers to regulate all aspects of <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil aviation</a>. These include the construction and operation of airports, <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_management" title="Air traffic management">air traffic management</a>, the certification of personnel and aircraft, and the protection of US assets during the launch or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Re-entry" title="Re-entry">re-entry</a> of commercial space vehicles.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_functions">Major functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The FAA's roles include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Regulating U.S. commercial space transportation</li>
<li>Regulating air navigation facilities' geometric and <a href="/wiki/Flight_inspection" title="Flight inspection">flight inspection</a> standards</li>
<li>Encouraging and developing civil aeronautics, including new aviation technology</li>
<li>Issuing, suspending, or revoking pilot certificates</li>
<li>Regulating civil aviation to promote <a href="/wiki/Transportation_safety_in_the_United_States" title="Transportation safety in the United States">Transportation safety in the United States</a>, especially through local offices called <a href="/wiki/Flight_Standards_District_Office" title="Flight Standards District Office">Flight Standards District Offices</a></li>
<li>Developing and operating a system of <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a> and navigation for both civil and military aircraft</li>
<li>Researching and developing the <a href="/wiki/National_Airspace_System" title="National Airspace System">National Airspace System</a> and civil aeronautics</li>
<li>Developing and carrying out programs to control aircraft noise and other environmental effects of civil aviation</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The FAA is divided into four "lines of business" (LOB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Each LOB has a specific role within the FAA.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Airports</b> (<b>ARP</b>) — plans and develops projects involving airports, overseeing their construction and operations. Ensures compliance with federal regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Air_Traffic_Organization" title="Air Traffic Organization">Air Traffic Organization</a></b> (<b>ATO</b>) — primary duty is to safely and efficiently move air traffic within the <a href="/wiki/National_Airspace_System" title="National Airspace System">National Airspace System</a>. ATO employees manage air traffic facilities including Airport Traffic Control Towers (ATCT) and Terminal Radar Approach Control Facilities (<a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control#Approach_and_terminal_control" title="Air traffic control">TRACONs</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>See also</i> <a href="/wiki/Airway_Operational_Support" title="Airway Operational Support">Airway Operational Support</a>.</li>
<li><b>Aviation Safety</b> (<b>AVS</b>) — Responsible for aeronautical certification of personnel and aircraft, including pilots, airlines, and mechanics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Commercial Space Transportation</b> (<b>AST</b>) — ensures protection of U.S. assets during the launch or reentry of commercial space vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_government_role_in_civil_aviation" title="United States government role in civil aviation">Air Commerce Act</a> of May 20, 1926, is the cornerstone of the federal government's regulation of civil aviation. This landmark legislation was passed at the urging of the aviation industry, whose leaders believed the airplane could not reach its full commercial potential without federal action to improve and maintain safety standards. The Act charged the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Commerce" title="United States Secretary of Commerce">Secretary of Commerce</a> with fostering air commerce, issuing and enforcing air traffic rules, <a href="/wiki/Pilot_certification_in_the_United_States" title="Pilot certification in the United States">licensing pilots</a>, certifying aircraft, establishing airways, and operating and maintaining aids to air navigation. The newly created Aeronautics Branch, operating under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a> assumed primary responsibility for aviation oversight.</p>
<p>In fulfilling its civil aviation responsibilities, the Department of Commerce initially concentrated on such functions as safety regulations and the certification of pilots and aircraft. It took over the building and operation of the nation's system of lighted airways, a task initiated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Post_Office_Department" title="United States Post Office Department">Post Office Department</a>. The Department of Commerce improved aeronautical radio communications — before the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> in 1934, which handles most such matters today — and introduced radio beacons as an effective aid to air navigation.</p>
<p>The Aeronautics Branch was renamed the Bureau of Air Commerce in 1934 to reflect its enhanced status within the Department. As commercial flying increased, the Bureau encouraged a group of airlines to establish the first three centers for providing <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a> (ATC) along the airways. In 1936, the Bureau itself took over the centers and began to expand the ATC system. The pioneer air traffic controllers used maps, blackboards, and mental calculations to ensure the safe separation of aircraft traveling along designated routes between cities.</p>
<p>In 1938, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_government_role_in_civil_aviation" title="United States government role in civil aviation">Civil Aeronautics Act</a> transferred the federal civil aviation responsibilities from the Commerce Department to a new independent agency, the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Board" title="Civil Aeronautics Board">Civil Aeronautics Authority</a>. The legislation also expanded the government's role by giving them the authority and the power to regulate airline fares and to determine the routes that air carriers would serve.</p>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> split the authority into two agencies in 1940, the Civil Aeronautics Administration (CAA) and the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Board" title="Civil Aeronautics Board">Civil Aeronautics Board</a> (CAB). CAA was responsible for ATC, airman and aircraft certification, safety enforcement, and airway development. CAB was entrusted with safety regulation, accident investigation, and economic regulation of the airlines. The CAA was part of the Department of Commerce. The CAB was an independent federal agency.</p>
<p>On the eve of America's entry into <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, CAA began to extend its ATC responsibilities to takeoff and landing operations at airports. This expanded role eventually became permanent after the war. The application of <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> to ATC helped controllers in their drive to keep abreast of the postwar boom in commercial air transportation. In 1946, meanwhile, Congress gave CAA the added task of administering the federal-aid airport program, the first peacetime program of financial assistance aimed exclusively at development of the nation's civil airports.</p>
<p>The approaching era of <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">jet</a> travel, and a series of midair collisions (most notable was the <a href="/wiki/1956_Grand_Canyon_mid-air_collision" title="1956 Grand Canyon mid-air collision">1956 Grand Canyon mid-air collision</a>), prompted passage of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Act_of_1958" title="Federal Aviation Act of 1958">Federal Aviation Act of 1958</a>. This legislation gave the CAA's functions to a new independent body, the Federal Aviation Agency. The act transferred air safety regulation from the CAB to the new FAA, and also gave the FAA sole responsibility for a common civil-military system of air navigation and air traffic control. The FAA's first administrator, <a href="/wiki/Elwood_Richard_Quesada" title="Elwood Richard Quesada">Elwood R. Quesada</a>, was a former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a> general and adviser to <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">President Eisenhower</a>.</p>
<p>The same year witnessed the birth of the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA), created in the wake of the Soviets launching of the first artificial satellite and assuming NACA's role of aeronautical research.</p>
<p>In 1967, a new <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">U.S. Department of Transportation</a> (DOT) combined major federal responsibilities for air and surface transport. The Federal Aviation Agency's name changed to the Federal Aviation Administration as it became one of several agencies (e.g., Federal Highway Administration, Federal Railroad Administration, the Coast Guard, and the Saint Lawrence Seaway Commission) within DOT (albeit the largest). The FAA administrator would no longer report directly to the president but would instead report to the Secretary of Transportation. New programs and budget requests would have to be approved by DOT, which would then include these requests in the overall budget and submit it to the president.</p>
<p>At the same time, a new <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Board" title="National Transportation Safety Board">National Transportation Safety Board</a> took over the Civil Aeronautics Board's (CAB) role of investigating and determining the causes of transportation accidents and making recommendations to the secretary of transportation. CAB was merged into DOT with its responsibilities limited to the regulation of commercial airline routes and fares.</p>
<p>The FAA gradually assumed additional functions. The hijacking epidemic of the 1960s had already brought the agency into the field of civil aviation security. In response to the hijackings on September 11, 2001, this responsibility is now primarily taken by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a>. The FAA became more involved with the environmental aspects of aviation in 1968 when it received the power to set aircraft noise standards. Legislation in 1970 gave the agency management of a new airport aid program and certain added responsibilities for airport safety. During the 1960s and 1970s, the FAA also started to regulate high altitude (over 500 feet) kite and balloon flying.</p>

<p>By the mid-1970s, the agency had achieved a semi-automated air traffic control system using both <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and computer technology. This system required enhancement to keep pace with air traffic growth, however, especially after the <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">Airline Deregulation Act of 1978</a> phased out the CAB's economic regulation of the airlines. A nationwide strike by the <a href="/wiki/Professional_Air_Traffic_Controllers_Organization_(1968)" title="Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization (1968)">air traffic controllers union</a> in 1981 forced temporary flight restrictions but failed to shut down the airspace system. During the following year, the agency unveiled a new plan for further automating its air traffic control facilities, but progress proved disappointing. In 1994, the FAA shifted to a more step-by-step approach that has provided controllers with advanced equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, Congress authorized the FAA to work with major commercial airports to define <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">contours</a> and investigate the feasibility of <a href="/wiki/Noise_control" title="Noise control">noise mitigation</a> by residential retrofit programs. Throughout the 1980s, these charters were implemented.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, satellite technology received increased emphasis in the FAA's development programs as a means to improvements in communications, navigation, and airspace management. In 1995, the agency assumed responsibility for safety oversight of commercial space transportation, a function begun eleven years before by an office within DOT headquarters. The agency was responsible for the decision to ground flights after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>.</p>
<p>In 2007, two FAA <a href="/wiki/Whistleblower" title="Whistleblower">whistleblowers</a>, inspectors Charalambe "Bobby" Boutris and Douglas E. Peters, alleged that Boutris said he attempted to ground Southwest after finding cracks in the <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, but was prevented by supervisors he said were friendly with the airline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes-faa_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-faa-10">[10]</a></sup> This was validated by a report by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">Department of Transportation</a> which found FAA managers had allowed <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> to fly 46 airplanes in 2006 and 2007 that were overdue for safety inspections, ignoring concerns raised by inspectors. Audits of other airlines resulted in two airlines grounding hundreds of planes, causing thousands of flight cancellations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ainonline_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ainonline-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Transportation_and_Infrastructure" title="United States House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure">House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee</a> held hearings in April 2008. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Oberstar" title="Jim Oberstar">Jim Oberstar</a>, former chairman of the committee said its investigation uncovered a pattern of regulatory abuse and widespread regulatory lapses, allowing 117 aircraft to be operated commercially although not in compliance with FAA safety rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes-faa_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-faa-10">[10]</a></sup> Oberstar said there was a "culture of coziness" between senior FAA officials and the airlines and "a systematic breakdown" in the FAA's culture that resulted in "malfeasance, bordering on corruption."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes-faa_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-faa-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2008 the FAA proposed to fine Southwest $10.2 million for failing to inspect older planes for cracks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap-mailtrib.2Fmsnbc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap-mailtrib.2Fmsnbc-12">[12]</a></sup> and in 2009 Southwest and the FAA agreed that Southwest would pay a $7.5 million penalty and would adopt new safety procedures, with the fine doubling if Southwest failed to follow through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2000, an organization within the FAA called the <a href="/wiki/Air_Traffic_Organization" title="Air Traffic Organization">Air Traffic Organization</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (ATO) was set up by presidential executive order. This became the <a href="/wiki/Air_navigation_service_provider" title="Air navigation service provider">air navigation service provider</a> for the airspace of the United States and for the New York (Atlantic) and Oakland (Pacific) oceanic areas. It is a full member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Air_Navigation_Services_Organisation" title="Civil Air Navigation Services Organisation">Civil Air Navigation Services Organisation</a>.</p>
<p>The FAA issues a number of awards to holders of its licenses. Among these are demonstrated proficiencies as an aviation mechanic, a flight instructor, a 50-year aviator, or as a safe pilot. The latter, the FAA "<a href="/wiki/Pilot_Proficiency_Award_Program" title="Pilot Proficiency Award Program">Wings Program</a>", provides a series of three badges for pilots who have undergone several hours of training since their last award. For more information see "FAA Advisory Circular 61-91H".</p>
<p>On March 18, 2008, the FAA ordered its inspectors to reconfirm that airlines are complying with federal rules after revelations that <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> flew dozens of aircraft without certain mandatory inspections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The FAA exercises surprise <a href="/wiki/Red_team#Important_cases" title="Red team">Red Team</a> drills on national airports annually.</p>
<p>On October 31, 2013, after outcry from media outlets, including heavy criticism <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bilton" title="Nick Bilton">Nick Bilton</a> of The New York Times,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the FAA announced it will allow airlines to expand the passengers use of portable electronic devices during all phases of flight, but <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> calls will still be prohibited. Implementation will vary among airlines. The FAA expects many carriers to show that their planes allow passengers to safely use their devices in <a href="/wiki/Airplane_mode" title="Airplane mode">airplane mode</a>, gate-to-gate, by the end of 2013. Devices must be held or put in the seat-back pocket during the actual takeoff and landing. Mobile phones must be in airplane mode or with mobile service disabled, with no signal bars displayed, and cannot be used for voice communications due to <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> regulations that prohibit any airborne calls using mobile phones. If an air carrier provides <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> service during flight, passengers may use it. Short-range <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> accessories, like <a href="/wiki/Wireless_keyboard" title="Wireless keyboard">wireless keyboards</a>, can also be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2014, in the wake of the downing of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia_Airlines_Flight_17" title="Malaysia Airlines Flight 17">Malaysia Airlines Flight 17</a>, the FAA suspended flights by U.S. airlines to <a href="/wiki/Ben_Gurion_Airport" title="Ben Gurion Airport">Ben Gurion Airport</a> during the <a href="/wiki/2014_Israel%E2%80%93Gaza_conflict" title="2014 Israel–Gaza conflict">2014 Israel–Gaza conflict</a> for 24 hours. The ban was extended for a further 24 hours, but was lifted about six hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IraqBA_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-IraqBA-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FAA_reauthorization_and_air_traffic_control_reform">FAA reauthorization and air traffic control reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: FAA reauthorization and air traffic control reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>U.S. law requires that the FAA’s budget and mandate be reauthorized on a regular basis. On July 18, 2016, President Obama signed a second short-term extension of the FAA authorization, replacing a previous extension that was due to expire that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2016 extension (set to expire itself in September 2017) left out a provision pushed by Republican House leadership, including <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Transportation_and_Infrastructure" title="United States House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure">House Transportation and Infrastructure (T&amp;I) Committee</a> Chairman <a href="/wiki/Bill_Shuster" title="Bill Shuster">Bill Shuster</a> (R-PA). The provision would have moved authority over <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a> from the FAA to a non-profit corporation, as many other nations, such as Canada, Germany and the United Kingdom have done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Shuster's bill, the <a href="/wiki/Aviation_Innovation,_Reform,_and_Reauthorization_(AIRR)_Act" title="Aviation Innovation, Reform, and Reauthorization (AIRR) Act">Aviation Innovation, Reform, and Reauthorization (AIRR) Act</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> expired in the House at the end of the 114th Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The House T&amp;I Committee began the new reauthorization process for the FAA in February 2017. It is expected that the committee will again urge Congress to consider and adopt air traffic control reform as part of the reauthorization package. Shuster has additional support from President Trump, who, in a meeting with aviation industry executives in early 2017 said the U.S. air control system is “….totally out of whack.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicting_roles">Conflicting roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conflicting roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The FAA has been cited as an example of <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_capture" title="Regulatory capture">regulatory capture</a>, "in which the airline industry openly dictates to its regulators its governing rules, arranging for not only beneficial regulation, but placing key people to head these regulators."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidoff-regcap_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-davidoff-regcap-25">[25]</a></sup> Retired NASA Office of Inspector General Senior Special Agent <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Gutheinz" title="Joseph Gutheinz">Joseph Gutheinz</a>, who used to be a Special Agent with the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Inspector_General_for_the_Department_of_Transportation" title="Office of Inspector General for the Department of Transportation">Office of Inspector General for the Department of Transportation</a> and with FAA Security, is one of the most outspoken critics of FAA. Rather than commend the agency for proposing a $10.2 million fine against Southwest Airlines for its failure to conduct mandatory inspections in 2008, he was quoted as saying the following in an <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> story: "Penalties against airlines that violate FAA directives should be stiffer. At $25,000 per violation, Gutheinz said, airlines can justify rolling the dice and taking the chance on getting caught. He also said the FAA is often too quick to bend to pressure from airlines and pilots."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap-mailtrib.2Fmsnbc_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap-mailtrib.2Fmsnbc-12">[12]</a></sup> Other experts have been critical of the constraints and expectations under which the FAA is expected to operate. The dual role of encouraging aerospace travel and regulating aerospace travel are contradictory. For example, to levy a heavy penalty upon an airline for violating an FAA regulation which would impact their ability to continue operating would not be considered encouraging aerospace travel.</p>
<p>On July 22, 2008, in the aftermath of the Southwest Airlines inspection scandal, a bill was unanimously approved in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House</a> to tighten regulations concerning airplane maintenance procedures, including the establishment of a whistleblower office and a two-year "cooling off" period that FAA inspectors or supervisors of inspectors must wait before they can work for those they regulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ainonline_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ainonline-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The bill also required rotation of principal maintenance inspectors and stipulated that the word "customer" properly applies to the flying public, not those entities regulated by the FAA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ainonline_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-ainonline-11">[11]</a></sup> The bill died in a Senate committee that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2009, the FAA administrator issued a directive mandating that the agency use the term "customers" only to refer to the flying public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_to_air_traffic_controller_application_process">Changes to air traffic controller application process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Changes to air traffic controller application process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, the FAA modified their approach to air traffic control hiring. They launched more "off the street bids", allowing anyone with either a 4-year degree or 5 years of full-time work experience to apply, rather than the closed college program or VRA bids, something that had last been done in 2008. Thousands have been picked up, including veterans, CTI grads, and people who are true "off the street" hires. The move was made to open the job up to more people who might make good controllers but did not go to a college that offered a CTI program. Before the change, candidates who had completed coursework at participating colleges and universities could be "fast-tracked" for consideration. However, the CTI program had no guarantee of a job offer, nor was the goal of the program to teach people to work actual traffic. The goal of the program was to prepare people for the FAA academy in Oklahoma City, OK. Having a CTI certificate allowed a prospective controller to skip the Air Traffic Basics part of the academy, about a 30- to 45-day course, and go right into Initial Qualification Training (IQT). All prospective controllers, CTI or not, have had to pass the FAA Academy in order to be hired as a controller. Failure at the academy means FAA employment is terminated. In January 2015 they launched another pipeline, a "prior experience" bid, where anyone with an FAA Control Tower Operator certificate (CTO) and 52 weeks of experience could apply. This was a revolving bid, every month the applicants on this bid were sorted out, and eligible applicants were hired and sent directly to facilities, bypassing the FAA academy entirely.</p>
<p>In the process of promoting diversity the FAA revised their hiring process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The FAA later issued a report that the "bio-data" was not a reliable test for future performance. However, the "Bio-Q" was not the determinating factor for hiring, it was merely a screening tool to determine who would take a revised Air Traffic Standardized Aptitude Test (ATSAT). Due to cost and time, it was not practical to give all 30,000 some applicants the revised ATSAT, which has since been validated. There were also charges that the FAA discriminated against qualified candidates and was helping minority candidates "cheat" to ensure they passed the test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, a reverse discrimination lawsuit was filed against the FAA seeking class action status for the thousands of men and women who spent up to $40,000 getting trained under FAA rules before they were abruptly changed. The prospects of the lawsuit are unknown, as the FAA is self-governing entity and therefore can alter and experiment with its hiring practices, and there was never any guarantee of a job in the CTI program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="FAA_process">FAA process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: FAA process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designated_Engineering_Representative">Designated Engineering Representative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Designated Engineering Representative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Designated Engineering Representative (DER) is an engineer who is appointed to act on behalf of a company or as an independent consultant (IC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Company DERs act on behalf of their employer and may only approve, or recommend the FAA approves, technical data produced by this company.</li>
<li>Consultant DERs are appointed to act as independent DERs to approve, or recommend the FAA approves, technical data produced by any person or organization.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="Designated_Airworthiness_Representative_(DAR)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Designated_Airworthiness_Representative_.28DAR.29">Designated Airworthiness Representative (DAR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Aviation_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Designated Airworthiness Representative (DAR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A DAR is an individual appointed in accordance with 14 CFR 183.33 who may perform examination, inspection, and testing services necessary to the issuance of certificates. There are two types of DARs: manufacturing, and maintenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Manufacturing DARs must possess aeronautical knowledge, experience, and meet the qualification requirements of Order 8100.8.</li>
<li>Maintenance DARs must hold:
<ol>
<li>a mechanic's certificate with an airframe and powerplant rating under 14 CFR part 65, Certification: Airmen Other Than Flight Crewmembers, or</li>
<li>a repairman certificate and be employed at a repair station certificated under 14 CFR part 145, or an air carrier operating certificate holder with an FAA-approved continuous airworthiness program, and must meet the qualification requirements of Order 8100.8, Chapter 14.</li>
</ol>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Specialized Experience – Amateur-Built and Light-Sport Aircraft DARs Both Manufacturing DARs and Maintenance DARs may be authorized to perform airworthiness certification of light-sport aircraft. DAR qualification criteria and selection procedures for amateur-built and light-sport aircraft airworthiness functions are provided in Order 8100.8.</p>
