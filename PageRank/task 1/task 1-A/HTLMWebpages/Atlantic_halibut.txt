<p>The <b>Atlantic halibut</b> (<i>Hippoglossus hippoglossus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Pleuronectidae" title="Pleuronectidae">Pleuronectidae</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Demersal_fish" title="Demersal fish">demersal fish</a> living on or near sand, gravel or clay bottoms at depths of between 50 and 2,000 m (160 and 6,560 ft). The <a href="/wiki/Halibut" title="Halibut">halibut</a> is among the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teleostei" title="Teleostei">teleost</a> (bony) fish in the world, and is an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a> due to a slow rate of growth and previous overfishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Halibut are strong swimmers and are able to <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migrate</a> long distances. Halibut size is not age-specific, but rather tends to follow a cycle related to halibut (and therefore food) abundance.</p>
<p>The native habitat of the Atlantic halibut is the temperate and arctic waters of the northern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a> and as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishbase_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fishbase-4">[4]</a></sup> It is the largest flatfish in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-5">[5]</a></sup> reaching lengths of up to 4.7 m (15 ft) and weights of 320 kg (710 lb). Its lifespan can reach 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishbase_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fishbase-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishGulfMaine_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FishGulfMaine-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Atlantic halibut is a right-eyed flounder. It is flattened sideways and habitually lies on the left side of its body with both eyes migrating to the right side of its head during development. Its upper surface is a uniformly dark chocolate, olive or slate colour, and can be almost black; the underside is pale. The end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a> is concave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishGulfMaine_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FishGulfMaine-6">[6]</a></sup> Young fish are paler with more mottled colouration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FGMI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FGMI-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Atlantic halibut has a relatively slow growth rate and late onset of sexual maturity, with males attaining maturity at seven to eight years old, females at 10 to 11 years, and individuals are thought to live up to 50 years. Little is known about their breeding except their <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> is seasonal, although its timing varies somewhat with location. In the eastern Atlantic, spawning occurs chiefly in March, April and May, although may span from January to June. Off the American coast, however, the spawning season appears to continue through the summer as late as September. After spawning, both sexes migrate northwards in search of food. Young Atlantic halibut individuals feed on <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prawn" title="Prawn">prawns</a>. These halibut lie motionless and invisible on the sea bed, capturing any fish that pass within reach, although they may also hunt for fish in open water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishbase_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fishbase-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FGMI_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FGMI-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This marine fish usually lives on the ocean floor at depths between 50 and 2,000 m (160 and 6,560 ft), but it occasionally comes closer to the surface. The larvae are <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic</a>, drifting relatively helplessly, but at around 4 cm, they migrate to the bottom. Young between the ages of two and four years live close to the shore, moving into deeper waters as they grow older.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishbase_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fishbase-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FGMI_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FGMI-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Found in both the eastern and western portions of the North Atlantic. In the western Atlantic, found from southwestern Greenland and Labrador, Canada to Virginia in the USA. They are found in the eastern Atlantic around Iceland, the United Kingdom, and northern Europe to Russia. A map of the Atlantic Halibut's geographic distribution can be found on the Official Website of Massachusetts Executive Office of Energy and Environmental Affairs. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_ecosystem">Role in ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role in ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Atlantic halibut occupies a relatively high <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The diet of the Atlantic halibut consists mainly of other fish, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haddock" title="Haddock">haddock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agonus_cataphractus" title="Agonus cataphractus">pogge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand_eel" title="Sand eel">sand eels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capelin" title="Capelin">capelin</a>, but it will also eat <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthos</a> organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishbase_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Fishbase-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishGulfMaine_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FishGulfMaine-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Atlantic halibut are eaten by <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, and are a <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staple food</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_shark" title="Greenland shark">Greenland shark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishGulfMaine_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-FishGulfMaine-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_fishing_of_wild_Atlantic_halibut">Commercial fishing of wild Atlantic halibut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Commercial fishing of wild Atlantic halibut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wild Atlantic halibut was formerly a very important food fish, but due to its slow rate of population growth, it is unable to recover quickly from overfishing, and the fishery has largely collapsed. Consequently, wild fish labelled as "halibut" are usually one of the other large flatfishes, including <a href="/wiki/Pacific_halibut" title="Pacific halibut">Pacific halibut</a>, <i>Hippoglossus stenolepis</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Farming">Farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its popularity as a food fish, Atlantic halibut has attracted investment in <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farming</a>. As of 2006, five countries - Canada, Norway, the UK, Iceland and Chile - were engaged in some form of Atlantic halibut <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishOceansCanada_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FishOceansCanada-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_halibut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature_and_Natural_Resources" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources">IUCN</a> rated it as <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">Endangered</a> and placed it on its <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">Red List</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Atlantic halibut is a US <a href="/wiki/National_Marine_Fisheries_Service" title="National Marine Fisheries Service">National Marine Fisheries Service</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species_of_Concern" title="Species of Concern">Species of Concern</a>, one of those species about which the U.S. Government’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> has some concerns regarding status and threats, but for which insufficient information is available to indicate a need to list the species under the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a> (ESA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The American Fisheries Society has classified the species as "Vulnerable". In 2010, Greenpeace International added the Atlantic halibut to its seafood red list of "fish that are commonly sold in supermarkets around the world, and which have a very high risk of being sourced from unsustainable fisheries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
