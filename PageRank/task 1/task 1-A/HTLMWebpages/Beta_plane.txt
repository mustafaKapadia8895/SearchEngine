<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Beta plane</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysical" title="Geophysical">geophysical</a> <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>, an approximation whereby the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_parameter" title="Coriolis parameter">Coriolis parameter</a>, <i>f</i>, is set to vary linearly in space is called a <b>beta plane approximation</b>. On a rotating sphere such as the earth, <i>f</i> varies with the sine of latitude; in the so-called <a href="/wiki/F-plane" title="F-plane">f-plane</a> approximation, this variation is ignored, and a value of <i>f</i> appropriate for a particular latitude is used throughout the domain. This approximation can be visualized as a tangent plane touching the surface of the sphere at this latitude. A more accurate model is a linear approximation to this variability about a given latitude (in the sense of a <a href="/wiki/Taylor_series" title="Taylor series">Taylor series</a> expansion). In analogy with the f-plane, this approximation is termed the beta plane, even though it no longer describes dynamics on a hypothetical tangent plane. The advantage of the beta plane approximation over more accurate formulations is that it does not contribute nonlinear terms to the dynamical equations; such terms make the equations harder to solve. The name 'beta plane' derives from the convention to denote the linear coefficient of variation with the Greek letter Î².</p>
<p>The beta plane approximation is useful for the theoretical analysis of many phenomena in geophysical fluid dynamics since it makes the equations much more tractable, yet retains the important information that the Coriolis parameter varies in space. In particular, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rossby_waves" title="Rossby waves">Rossby waves</a>, the most important type of waves if one considers large-scale atmospheric and oceanic dynamics, depend on the variation of <i>f</i> as a restoring force; they do not occur if the Coriolis parameter is approximated only as a constant.</p>
